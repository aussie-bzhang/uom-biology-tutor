Cells differ in their potency – Recap
Totipotent – potential to produce all cell types of an organism (e.g. zygote)
Pluripotent – can produce all the cell types of the body but not cells of
extraembryonic tissue like the placenta (e.g. embryonic stem cells, and induced
pluripotent stem cells)
Multipotent – can produce several cell types (e.g. stem cells)
Unipotent – can produce only one cell type – i.e. cells that can only produce
daughter cells of the same type (e.g. skin cells)

In vitro development (tissue engineering)
The ability of cells to divide and differentiate into multiple cell types and arrange themselves
into a complex tissue/organ is “built-in”. Cells grown in vitro will often spontaneously self-
organise into semi-functional organs/tissues.

Science Discovery: Human ex vivo regeneration
If a differentiated cell, such as a skin cell, has the complete genome, why can’t we turn it into
another cell type by changing the genes it expresses?
In other words, can we reverse the developmental programming of a mature cell to recover
potency? And can we then move these more potent cells down a new developmental path?
Yes, we can!
In fact, Gurdon and Yamanaka were jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in
2012 for exactly this discovery!
