Enzyme Catalysis
Enzyme: A biological catalyst
• Except for some RNAs that catalyse their own self-cleavage, all enzymes are proteins.
• Enzymes can increase the rate of a reaction by a factor of 109 to 1020 over an
uncatalyzed reaction.
• Some catalyse the reaction of only one compound.
• Others are stereoselective; for example, enzymes that catalyse the reactions of only L-
amino acids.
• Others catalyse reactions of specific types of compounds or bonds; for example, trypsin
catalyses hydrolysis of peptide bonds formed by the carboxyl groups of Lys and Arg.

Enzyme Names
The name of an enzyme
• is derived by replacing the end of the name of the reaction or reacting
compound with the suffix ase
• identifies the reacting substance—for example, sucrase catalyses the
reaction of sucrose
• describes the compound or the reaction that is catalysed — for example,
oxidase catalyses an oxidation reaction
• could be a common name, particularly for the digestion enzymes, such as
pepsin and trypsin

Enzyme active site binds the substrate:
Mechanism of action
• Both the lock-and-key model and the induced-fit model emphasize the shape of
the active site
• However, the chemistry of the active site is the more important than its shape
• Just five amino acids participate in the active site in more than 65 % of the
enzymes studied to date
• These five are His > Cys > Asp > Arg > Glu
• Four of these amino acids have either acidic or basic side chains; the fifth has a
sulfhydryl group (-SH)

Enzyme active site binds the substrate:
What is really happening
The active site
• is a region within an enzyme that fits the shape
of the reacting molecule called a substrate
• contains amino acid R groups that bind the
substrate
• the bonds between the R groups and the
substrate disrupt its stability reducing the energy
required to change it
• releases products when the reaction is complete

Reaction Rates
• We can measure the rate of reaction by two methods:
1. Measurement of the decreasing concentration of the substrate.
2. The increasing concentration of the products.
• When we use either of these methods, we are measuring a rate of
change.
• That is, the amount of products produced in a defined time.
Measurements of reaction rate are called kinetic measurements
• A kinetic measurement is a measurement of change in a defined time
interval (an example is the amount of NADH produced in 1 minute)

What affects Enzymes:
Temperature
Enzymes
• are most active at an optimum
temperature (usually 37 °C in
humans)
• show little activity at low
temperatures.
• lose activity at temperatures above
50 °C as denaturation occurs with
loss of catalytic activity

What affects Enzymes: pH
Enzymes:
• are most active at optimum pH
• contain R groups of amino acids
with proper charges at optimum pH
• lose activity in low or high pH as
tertiary structure is disrupted
Many of the enzymes in the body
have an optimum operating pH
between 7 and 8, for this reason, the
pH of the body fluids is maintained
very close to 7.40.

Enzyme Cofactors: Coenzymes
Coenzymes are complex non-protein organic molecules, which transfer
chemicals from the active site of one enzyme to the active site of another
enzyme
• Some coenzymes are permanently bound to the enzyme molecules.
• These are known as prosthetic groups.
