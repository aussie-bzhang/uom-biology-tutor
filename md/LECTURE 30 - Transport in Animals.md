Types of circulatory systems: Open

Open = fluid openly flowing through the tissues, i.e. empties into the body
cavity

• Extracellular fluid and transport fluid are the same (hemolymph)
• More energy efficient pump
• Larger blood volume & lower flow rates/pressures

Page [14]

Types of circulatory systems: Open

Found in arthropods (insects, arachnids, crustaceans) and non-cephalopod molluscs.

Page [15]

Types of circulatory systems: Closed

Closed = fluid enclosed within a network of vessels

• Separate transport fluid (blood) and fluid in tissues (interstitial fluid)
• Exchange with IF in capillary beds
• Vessels can also “pump” blood
• Evolution of chambered hearts
• Most vertebrates have a lymphatic system

Page [16]

Types of circulatory systems: Closed

Closed = fluid enclosed within a network of vessels

• More rapid flow through vessels> faster transport to tissue
• Ability to alter blood flow to selected tissues
• Large molecules are contained

Activity 1 – Open vs closed circulation

Summarise the advantage(s) of having a closed circulatory system.
• Fluid flow is more rapid in a closed system  – allows for a faster metabolism
• Control of flow – direct flow to where it is needed, e.g. gas exchange or heat exchange
• Specialised content – retain cells/large molecules – increased oxygen carrying capacity

But there are still many examples of organisms with open
systems that support high levels of activity…

How?

They don’t depend on their circulatory system for gas exchange!

Exchange occurs in the capillary bed

• What can be exchanged?

▪ Small lipid soluble items and nutrients

• Balance of hydrostatic pressures and osmotic pressure
• Four Starling forces

▪ Pressure out > pressure in = filtration
▪ Pressure out < pressure in = reabsorption
