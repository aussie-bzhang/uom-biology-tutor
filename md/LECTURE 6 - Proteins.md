Protein Denaturation
Proteins, although highly specific, can easily have
their structure changed, through disruption or
alteration of their side-chains.
When secondary or tertiary structure of a protein is
altered in this way, it is said to be denatured.
Conditions that lead to denaturation:
• High temperature
• pH changes
• High concentrations of polar molecules
• Nonpolar substances, via hydrophobic
interactions

Define denaturation
• Denaturation is the change to the secondary or tertiary structure of a protein.
• It causes the protein to either completely, or partially, stop functioning.
Page [19]

Conditions for denaturation
• pH change: Addition or removal of H+
‒ This causes changes to COOH and NH2 containing R-groups.
‒ COOH→COO- NH → NH +
2 3
‒ Disrupts ionic interactions
• Temperature change: Increasing temperature provides energy to disrupt
intermolecular interactions between R-groups.
• Addition of polar: Disrupts all polar R-groups by interfering with dipole-dipole
force and hydrogen bonding
• Addition of non-polar: Disrupts all non-polar R-groups by interfering with
dispersion force.

Summary
Proteins are the most varied group of macromolecules. They play a
host of roles in an organism; structure, defense, enzymes, hormones.
They are made from amino acid monomers.
All amino acids have;
• Carboxyl (-COOH, can donate H+)
• Amino (-NH , can receive H+)
2
• An R-group (20 of these)
Proteins are described in terms of their primary, secondary, tertiary or
quaternary structure.
Proteins can be denatured by disrupting their amino acid R-group
interactions. This leads to a loss of, or reduction in, function.
