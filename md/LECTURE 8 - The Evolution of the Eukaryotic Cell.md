Endosymbiosis and the Origin of
Chloroplasts
• Cyanobacteria and purple bacteria are
surrounded by two membranes not one
membrane.
• The cyanobacterium (or purple bacterium) enters
the host eukaryote via endocytosis and gets
surrounded by a food (phagocytic) vacuole.
• The cyanobacterium does not get eaten but
instead persists within the cell. Over time, the
outer membrane (food vacuole) breaks down
leaving an endosymbiont with its original two
membranes (outer and inner membrane).
• It transfers its genetic information to the nucleus.

Evidence for the endosymbiotic origin of
mitochondria and chloroplasts
1. These organelles appear morphologically similar to bacteria.
2. They are surrounded by an outer membrane similar to a cell membrane while
their inner membrane invaginates to form lamellae or cristae.
3. Mitochondria and chloroplasts are semi-autonomous, retaining their own genome
(DNA, RNA).
4. They also retain their own machinery for synthesizing proteins, including
ribosomes.
5. Their metabolism is like existing prokaryotic organisms (cyanobacteria for
chloroplast and a purple bacteria for mitochondrion).
6. The chloroplasts in some species still have the bacterial peptidoglycan wall
between the inner and outer membranes (for example, Cyanophora).

Summary
• The nucleus, mitochondria and chloroplasts are all membrane-bound
organelles with specific functions in a cell.
• The nucleus contains all of an organism’s genetic information – its DNA.
• It is likely that the nucleus evolved through invagination of the cell
membrane around a nucleoid.
• Mitochondria and chloroplasts are both double-membrane bound structures
that evolved through primary endosymbiosis (and potentially secondary
endosymbiosis for chloroplasts).
• Chloroplasts are responsible for photosynthesis in plant cells.
• Mitochondria are the site of the electron transport chain, essential for
aerobic respiration.
