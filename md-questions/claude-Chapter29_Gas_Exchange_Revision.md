## Key Concepts to Remember

**The problem:** because a large multicellular organism has a **small SA:V ratio** and a **large diffusion distance** to internal cells, simple diffusion with the external environment cannot meet its gas-exchange needs.

**Diffusion-only animals:** some keep every cell close to the environment instead — the **flatworm (flat)**, **Hydra (hollow)** and **sponge (porous)**: a central cavity/channels bring the external environment into the body.

**Three innovations larger organisms use:** (1) maintain an **internal aqueous environment** (extracellular fluid, kept stable by homeostasis; L32–33); (2) **specialised transport systems** (animal circulation = active pumps, plant transport = passive; bulk flow keeps steep gradients; L30–31); (3) **specialised exchange surfaces** (this lecture).

**Exchange surfaces have:** a **large surface area** (long, flat, folded, branched) and a **thin barrier** (short diffusion distance). The human lung ≈ 85 m² (a tennis court).

**Fick’s law:** Rate of diffusion = (surface area × partial-pressure gradient × diffusion coefficient) ÷ diffusion distance. So rate **rises** with surface area and gradient, and **falls** as diffusion distance grows. Partial pressures (mm Hg) add: P**total** = P**A** + P**B** + … (Dalton’s law).

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — SA:V, Diffusion Limits & Strategies

### Part A — Short-answer

1. A small SA:V ratio means there is too little surface (where exchange happens) relative to the volume of cells that must be supplied; a large diffusion distance means substances would take far too long to diffuse from the surface to the deepest cells (diffusion time scales with distance²). Together, the surface cannot supply enough material fast enough to reach all internal cells, so diffusion with the external environment alone is insufficient.

2. (a) Being flat (flatworm) or (b) being hollow with a central cavity (Hydra), or porous with channels (sponge). Both tricks keep the maximum distance from any cell to the environment very short and increase the surface available, so the central cavity/channels effectively bring the external environment into the body and every cell stays close to it.

3. (1) Maintain an internal aqueous environment (extracellular fluid; L32–33); (2) specialised transport systems (L30–31); (3) specialised exchange surfaces. This lecture focuses on (3) specialised exchange surfaces.

4. The internal environment is the extracellular (aqueous) fluid that bathes the cells; cells exchange substances with it rather than directly with the outside. A barrier (the body surface/epithelium) is needed to separate this internal fluid from the external environment so its composition can be controlled. It is kept stable by homeostasis.

5. Components: fibrous proteins (e.g. collagen) and a matrix of glycoproteins (proteoglycans). Functions (any two): holds cells together ('glue'); filters materials passing between tissues; orients cell movement in development and repair; allows chemical signalling between cells.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. Yes — smaller objects have a larger SA:V ratio. 2. Yes — its flatness keeps all cells near the surface, so diffusion suffices. 3. No — animal circulatory systems use active pumps; plant transport is passive. 4. Yes — the internal environment is kept stable by homeostasis. 5. Yes — bulk flow continually delivers/removes substances, keeping gradients steep for diffusion.

3. Internal aqueous environment → addresses the need to give cells a stable medium to exchange with / coordinate (Constraint 3) → L32–33. Specialised exchange surfaces → address the small SA:V ratio by providing extra surface (Constraint 1) → L29 (this lecture). They work together because a large exchange surface is useless unless a transport system delivers substances to/from deep cells and an internal environment lets every cell exchange locally.

## Set 2 — Fick’s Law & Exchange Surfaces

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Rate of diffusion = (surface area × partial-pressure gradient × diffusion coefficient) ÷ diffusion distance. Surface area = area available for exchange (↑ raises rate); partial-pressure gradient = difference in partial pressure across the surface (↑ raises rate); diffusion coefficient = how easily that gas moves through the medium (↑ raises rate); diffusion distance = thickness of the barrier (↑ LOWERS rate).

2. The partial pressure of a gas is the pressure that gas alone would exert in the mixture — a measure of its concentration. It is measured in mm Hg (millimetres of mercury). Dalton's law: the total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of the partial pressures of its component gases (P\_total = P\_A + P\_B + P\_C + …).

3. (a) A large surface area (long, flat, folded, branched) — the 'surface area' term in Fick's law (larger area → faster diffusion). (b) A thin membrane/barrier — the 'diffusion distance' term (shorter distance → faster diffusion).

4. Lung properties and their Fick's-law term: large surface area (~85 m², folded alveoli) → surface area; maximise the partial-pressure gradient (ventilation + blood flow) → partial-pressure gradient; thin barrier → (short) diffusion distance; minimise diffusion through an aqueous medium (keep the watery layer thin) → also reduces effective diffusion distance / favours the diffusion coefficient.

5. Gases diffuse slowly through water (an aqueous layer has a small diffusion coefficient for O₂ and adds to the distance). Keeping the aqueous diffusion path short means gases spend less distance/time in the slow medium, so the overall rate of gas exchange stays high.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. Yes — rate is proportional to surface area. 2. No — increasing diffusion distance DECREASES the rate (it is in the denominator). 3. Yes — a thin barrier shortens diffusion distance and raises the rate. 4. Yes — partial pressures are measured in mm Hg. 5. Yes — folding/branching increases surface area.

## Set 3 — Gas-Exchange Systems in Action

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Air enters the lung and fills the alveoli, which are wrapped in a network of fine capillaries. Gases diffuse across the thin alveolar wall and capillary endothelium. Incoming (deoxygenated) blood has a LOWER pO₂ than the alveoli, so O₂ diffuses from alveoli into blood; the same blood has a HIGHER pCO₂ than the alveoli, so CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli. Oxygenated blood then returns to the heart.

2. (1) External gills — e.g. a salamander/newt larva; (2) internal gills — e.g. a lobster (crustacean) / fish; (3) lungs — e.g. a rabbit (mammal); (4) tracheae — e.g. an insect (mantis).

3. CO₂ enters the leaf through the stomata (pores regulated by guard cells). The unavoidable cost is that water vapour (H₂O) diffuses out at the same time (and O₂ produced by photosynthesis also leaves). The specialised exchange surface inside the leaf blade is the spongy mesophyll, whose cells present a large, moist surface area to the internal air spaces.

4. The exchange surface is the lining of the internal water channels/chambers. The water current is generated by the beating flagella of choanocytes (collar cells), which draw water in through pores (ostia) and out through the osculum, bringing in food and O₂ and removing CO₂/waste.

5. Choanocytes are structurally very similar to choanoflagellates, which are free-living unicellular (and colony-forming) protists. This morphological similarity suggests sponges (simple metazoans) evolved from a choanoflagellate-like ancestor, supporting the proposed progression unicellular protists → colonial forms → metazoans.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. Yes — O₂ diffuses from high pO₂ (alveoli) to low pO₂ (blood). 2. Yes — blood has the higher pCO₂, so CO₂ diffuses out into the alveoli. 3. Yes — opening stomata for CO₂ inevitably lets water vapour escape. 4. Yes — choanocytes generate the water current. 5. Yes — tracheae are a specialised gas-exchange surface (in insects).

### Part D — Applied

1. (i) O₂ diffuses from alveolus (pO₂ 100) into blood (pO₂ 40) — down its gradient of 60 mm Hg. (ii) CO₂ diffuses from blood (pCO₂ 46) into alveolus (pCO₂ 40) — down its gradient of 6 mm Hg. (iii) Continuous blood flow carries oxygenated blood away and brings fresh deoxygenated, CO₂-rich blood, while ventilation continually replaces alveolar air with fresh air; both keep alveolar/blood partial pressures different, maintaining steep gradients — the 'partial-pressure gradient' term in Fick's law — so diffusion stays fast.

2. (i) Exchange surface = the lining of the internal water channels/chambers (the cells bordering the channels). (ii) Surface area is maximised by the extensive branching network of internal channels (porous body), giving a large area → 'surface area' term. (iii) Diffusion distance is minimised because the body wall/channel lining is very thin and water is brought right next to the cells → 'diffusion distance' term. (iv) Partial-pressure gradients are maintained by the continuous water current (driven by choanocyte flagella) constantly supplying fresh O₂-rich water and removing CO₂/waste → 'partial-pressure gradient' term.

3. (i) A flatworm is thin, so all its cells are within diffusion range of the surrounding water and its SA:V is large; a solid cube of the same cells has a small SA:V and a long path to its centre, so its inner cells cannot be supplied by diffusion. (ii) The stomata (with guard cells) let the plant open to admit CO₂ and close to conserve water; it would keep them closed when water is scarce / conditions are hot and dry (to limit water loss). (iii) Shared feature: a large surface area combined with a thin, moist barrier (short diffusion distance) — directly the surface-area and diffusion-distance terms of Fick's law.
