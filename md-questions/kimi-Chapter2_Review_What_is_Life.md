# Application Problem 1: Prebiotic Chemistry & the Miller-Urey Experiment

The Miller-Urey experiment (1953) simulated early Earth's reducing atmosphere to demonstrate the abiotic synthesis of organic compounds. The apparatus circulated a gas mixture of CH₄ (methane), NH₃ (ammonia), H₂O (water vapour), and H₂ (hydrogen) past electrical sparks simulating lightning.

In a modern replication of this experiment, a 2.0 L reaction vessel is charged with the following partial pressures at 300 K:

* P(CH₄) = 0.20 atm
* P(NH₃) = 0.10 atm
* P(H₂O) = 0.30 atm
* P(H₂) = 0.40 atm

(a) Calculate the total pressure in the vessel and determine the mole fraction of each gas. [3 marks]

(b) Using the ideal gas law (PV = nRT), calculate the total number of moles of gas in the vessel. (R = 0.0821 L·atm·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹) [3 marks]

(c) After 48 hours of continuous spark discharge, analysis reveals 1.2 × 10⁻³ mol of glycine (C₂H₅NO₂, M = 75.07 g/mol) was formed. Calculate the mass of glycine produced and its theoretical yield percentage if the limiting reagent (NH₃) was completely converted. [4 marks]

(d) The Murchison meteorite (1969) was found to contain over 90 different amino acids, including 8 of the 20 proteinogenic amino acids. Discuss how this finding supports or challenges the conclusions drawn from the Miller-Urey experiment regarding the origin of organic molecules on early Earth. [4 marks]

# Application Problem 5: The Great Oxygenation Event & Atmospheric Photochemistry

The accumulation of atmospheric O₂ from cyanobacterial photosynthesis triggered the formation of the ozone (O₃) layer, which was critical for the colonisation of land by life. The relevant photochemistry involves:

Step 1: O₂ + hν (λ < 240 nm) → 2O• (photodissociation)

Step 2: O• + O₂ + M → O₃ + M• (ozone formation, where M is a third body)

Step 3: O₃ + hν (λ < 320 nm) → O• + O₂ (ozone photolysis — UV absorption)

(a) Before the Great Oxygenation Event (~2.4 BYA), atmospheric O₂ levels were < 10⁻⁵ of present atmospheric level (PAL = 21% O₂ by volume). Calculate the partial pressure of O₂ in the pre-oxygenation atmosphere at sea level (P(total) = 1.0 atm). [2 marks]

(b) The Chapman cycle describes steady-state ozone concentration. At equilibrium, the rate of ozone formation equals the rate of ozone destruction. Given:

* Rate constant for Step 2: k₂ = 1.0 × 10⁻³³ cm⁶·molecule⁻²·s⁻¹
* Rate constant for Step 3: k₃ = 1.0 × 10⁻² s⁻¹ (at UV flux typical of early Earth)
* [O•] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹² molecules/cm³
* [M] = 2.5 × 10⁻¹⁹ molecules/cm³ (total atmospheric number density)

Derive the steady-state concentration of O₃ in molecules/cm³. [4 marks]

(c) The ozone layer currently absorbs approximately 97–99% of incoming UV-C (100–280 nm) and ~90% of UV-B (280–315 nm). Using the Beer-Lambert law (I = I₀e⁻εcl), calculate the ozone concentration column density (in mol/cm²) required to achieve 99% absorption of UV-C radiation, given ε(UV-C) = 3.0 × 10⁻¹⁸ cm²/molecule and path length l = 20 km (approximate thickness of ozone layer). [4 marks]

(d) The Cambrian Explosion (~541 MYA) coincided with atmospheric O₂ reaching ~10–20% PAL. Propose a mechanistic explanation connecting ozone layer development, UV radiation shielding, and the ecological diversification observed during the Cambrian Explosion. Your answer should integrate atmospheric photochemistry, cell biology (DNA damage/repair), and evolutionary ecology. [6 marks]

Application Problem 1: Prebiotic Chemistry & the Miller-Urey Experiment

(a) Total Pressure and Mole Fractions [3 marks]

Total pressure: P(total) = 0.20 + 0.10 + 0.30 + 0.40 = 1.00 atm [1 mark]

By Dalton's law, mole fraction Xᵢ = Pᵢ/P(total):

X(CH₄) = 0.20/1.00 = 0.20

X(NH₃) = 0.10/1.00 = 0.10

X(H₂O) = 0.30/1.00 = 0.30

X(H₂) = 0.40/1.00 = 0.40 [2 marks for all four correct]

(b) Total Moles of Gas [3 marks]

Using PV = nRT:

n = PV/RT = (1.00 atm × 2.0 L) / (0.0821 L·atm·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹ × 300 K)

n = 2.0 / 24.63 = 0.0812 mol [2 marks for correct substitution and calculation]

Therefore: n(CH₄) = 0.0162 mol, n(NH₃) = 0.00812 mol, n(H₂O) = 0.0244 mol, n(H₂) = 0.0325 mol [1 mark]

(c) Mass and Yield of Glycine [4 marks]

Mass of glycine = n × M = 1.2 × 10⁻³ mol × 75.07 g/mol = 0.0901 g = 90.1 mg [1 mark]

For theoretical yield: each glycine (C₂H₅NO₂) contains 1 N atom, requiring 1 NH₃.

Theoretical moles of glycine = n(NH₃) = 0.00812 mol [1 mark]

Theoretical mass = 0.00812 mol × 75.07 g/mol = 0.610 g [1 mark]

Percentage yield = (1.2 × 10⁻³ / 0.00812) × 100% = 14.8% [1 mark]

(Accept answers in range 14–15% due to rounding)

(d) Discussion: Miller-Urey vs. Murchison Meteorite [4 marks]

Key points (any 4 well-developed points):

* The Murchison meteorite findings SUPPORT the general conclusion that organic molecules can form abiotically under non-biological conditions, extending the principle beyond Earth. [1 mark]
* The diversity of amino acids in Murchison (90+) far exceeds Miller-Urey products, suggesting that prebiotic synthesis in space (interstellar media, cometary environments) may have contributed additional molecular diversity to early Earth via panspermia. [1 mark]
* The presence of proteinogenic amino acids in Murchison indicates that the molecular building blocks of life are not unique to Earth, supporting the hypothesis that life's precursors could have extraterrestrial origins. [1 mark]
* However, Murchison amino acids show equal proportions of L- and D-enantiomers (racemic), while terrestrial life uses predominantly L-amino acids. This suggests that the homochirality of biological amino acids required additional selective processes on Earth. [1 mark]

Application Problem 2: Photosynthesis Stoichiometry & Energy Accounting

(a) Total CO₂ Fixation Rate [2 marks]

Total cells = 2.0 × 10⁹ cells/mL × 5000 mL = 1.0 × 10¹³ cells [1 mark]

Total fixation rate = 1.0 × 10¹³ cells × 1.5 × 10⁻¹⁵ mol/cell/h = 1.5 × 10⁻² mol/h [1 mark]

(b) Time Until CO₂ Limitation [4 marks]

Initial CO₂ in bioreactor:

V(air) = 5.0 L, CO₂ at 0.04% = 4.0 × 10⁻⁴ fraction

V(CO₂) = 5.0 L × 4.0 × 10⁻⁴ = 2.0 × 10⁻³ L [1 mark]

n(CO₂) = 2.0 × 10⁻³ L / 24.5 L/mol = 8.16 × 10⁻⁵ mol [1 mark]

From the equation, 6 mol CO₂ produces 1 mol glucose.

Time = n(CO₂) / fixation rate = 8.16 × 10⁻⁵ mol / 1.5 × 10⁻² mol/h [1 mark]

= 5.44 × 10⁻³ h = 0.326 minutes ≈ 19.6 seconds [1 mark]

(Note: This extremely short time illustrates why closed bioreactors require CO₂ supplementation for continuous culture)

(c) Glucose Mass and O₂ Volume [4 marks]

After 24 h: n(CO₂ fixed) = 1.5 × 10⁻² mol/h × 24 h = 0.36 mol [1 mark]

From stoichiometry (6:1 ratio): n(glucose) = 0.36 / 6 = 0.060 mol [1 mark]

Mass of glucose = 0.060 mol × 180.16 g/mol = 10.8 g [1 mark]

n(O₂) = 0.36 mol (6:6 ratio); V(O₂) = 0.36 × 22.4 = 8.06 L at STP [1 mark]

(d) The 1.4 Billion Year Gap: Biochemical Significance [4 marks]

Key points (any 4 well-developed points):

* Oxygenic photosynthesis produces O₂ as a byproduct, which is highly reactive and toxic to anaerobic organisms. Early life lacked antioxidant defences, creating an evolutionary bottleneck. [1 mark]
* The accumulation of O₂ required sufficient geological sinks (e.g., dissolved Fe²⁺ in oceans) to be saturated before atmospheric O₂ could rise — this accounts for the ~1 billion year delay (banded iron formations). [1 mark]
* Aerobic respiration (using O₂ as terminal electron acceptor) yields ~36 ATP per glucose versus only 2 ATP from fermentation/anaerobic glycolysis. The oxygenation event enabled the evolution of metabolically demanding eukaryotic cells with larger genomes and complex organelles. [1 mark]
* The O₂-mediated formation of the ozone layer reduced UV radiation at Earth's surface, enabling life to colonise shallow waters and eventually land, driving the ecological diversification that led to multicellular eukaryotes. [1 mark]

Application Problem 3: Phylogenetic Tree Construction & Interpretation

(a) UPGMA Tree Construction [6 marks]

Step 1: Identify smallest distance = 0.08 (A-B). Merge A and B.

New node AB: distances to C = (0.18+0.20)/2 = 0.19; to D = (0.25+0.28)/2 = 0.265; to E = (0.30+0.32)/2 = 0.31 [1 mark]

Step 2: Smallest remaining distance = 0.15 (C-D). Merge C and D.

New node CD: distance to AB = (0.19+0.265)/2 = 0.2275; to E = (0.22+0.25)/2 = 0.235 [1 mark]

Step 3: Smallest remaining = 0.2275 (AB-CD). Merge AB and CD.

New node ABCD: distance to E = (0.31+0.235)/2 = 0.2725 [1 mark]

Step 4: Final merge: ABCD with E at distance 0.2725 [1 mark]

Branch lengths: A-B split at 0.08/2 = 0.04; C-D split at 0.15/2 = 0.075; AB-CD split at 0.2275 - 0.04 = 0.1875; ABCD-E split at 0.2725 - 0.1875 = 0.085 [1 mark for correct tree topology; 1 mark for correct branch lengths]

(b) Most Recent Common Ancestor & Divergence Time [3 marks]

A and B share the most recent common ancestor (shortest branch to their merge node). [1 mark]

Genetic distance between A and B = 0.08 substitutions/site.

Since each lineage accumulates mutations independently, divergence time t = d/(2r) [1 mark]

t = 0.08 / (2 × 1.5 × 10⁻⁹) = 0.08 / (3.0 × 10⁻⁹) = 2.67 × 10⁷ years = 26.7 million years ago [1 mark]

(c) Fossil and Molecular Integration [3 marks]

Key points:

* The 1.8 BYA stromatolite fossil provides a minimum age constraint for cyanobacterial diversification, anchoring the molecular clock calibration. [1 mark]
* Molecular phylogenetics of modern Oscillatoria can be extrapolated back to estimate divergence times, which when cross-validated with fossil dates increases confidence in the timeline. [1 mark]
* The combination of independent data sources (geological, morphological fossil, molecular) overcomes limitations of any single method — this is called ‘total evidence’ approach in phylogenetics. [1 mark]

(d) rRNA as Molecular Chronometer [4 marks]

Key points:

* rRNA genes are present in ALL cellular life (universal distribution), enabling comparison across all domains. [1 mark]
* rRNA has both highly conserved regions (suitable for deep phylogeny) and variable regions (suitable for fine-scale discrimination), providing resolution at multiple evolutionary scales. [1 mark]
* Ribosomal RNA is functionally constrained (essential for translation), so mutations are generally limited to neutral or nearly neutral changes, providing a relatively constant molecular clock. [1 mark]
* The structural differences between 70S (prokaryotic) and 80S (eukaryotic) ribosomes — particularly the additional proteins and larger rRNAs in eukaryotes — reflect the increased complexity of eukaryotic gene expression regulation. These differences are substantial enough to be targeted selectively by antibiotics, making ribosome structure clinically significant. [1 mark]

Application Problem 4: Ribosome Structure & Molecular Analysis

(a) Average Protein Mass and Composition [4 marks]

Prokaryotic 70S ribosome:

Average mass per protein = 2.3 × 10⁶ Da / 55 = 41,818 Da ≈ 41.8 kDa [1 mark]

Mass of proteins = 55 × 41,818 = 2.3 × 10⁶ Da (by definition); therefore protein mass fraction = 100% would be incorrect.

Correct approach: rRNA mass = total - protein mass. In 70S ribosomes, proteins constitute ~35% of mass, rRNA ~65%. [1 mark]

Eukaryotic 80S ribosome:

Average mass per protein = 3.3 × 10⁶ Da / 80 = 41,250 Da ≈ 41.3 kDa [1 mark]

Similar average protein size, but 25 additional proteins contribute to greater total mass. Protein mass fraction ~40–45%, rRNA ~55–60%. [1 mark]

(b) Sucrose Gradient Sedimentation [4 marks]

The 70S ribosome sediments at an intermediate position because:

* Sucrose gradient ultracentrifugation separates particles by SEDIMENTATION COEFFICIENT (S), not by density alone. [1 mark]
* The sedimentation coefficient depends on both mass AND shape (frictional coefficient): S = M(1 - v̄ρ)/Nₐf, where M = molar mass, v̄ = partial specific volume, ρ = solvent density, Nₐ = Avogadro's number, f = frictional coefficient. [1 mark]
* The intact 70S particle has a more compact shape than the dissociated subunits, reducing its frictional coefficient and causing it to sediment FASTER than either subunit alone, despite having intermediate mass. [1 mark]
* The 50S and 30S subunits, when separated, have more extended conformations with larger f values, causing them to sediment more slowly relative to their individual masses. This is why 50S + 30S ≠ 80S (the sum of S values is not additive). [1 mark]

(c) Antibiotic Selectivity [3 marks]

Structural basis for selectivity:

* The 10S difference reflects substantial architectural differences: the eukaryotic 80S ribosome has additional proteins (particularly in the 40S subunit) and expanded rRNA regions that alter the geometry of functional sites. [1 mark]
* Antibiotics like tetracycline bind to specific pockets in the 30S subunit (prokaryotic) that are structurally distinct in the 40S subunit (eukaryotic) due to different rRNA sequences and associated proteins. [1 mark]
* Mitochondrial 55S ribosomes in eukaryotes resemble prokaryotic 70S ribosomes (evolutionary endosymbiosis theory), which explains why some antibiotics have mitochondrial toxicity as a side effect. [1 mark]

(d) Experimental Design [5 marks]

Workflow:

* Step 1: Sucrose gradient ultracentrifugation (10–40%) separates 70S and 80S ribosomes based on sedimentation coefficients. Collect fractions and monitor A₂₆0 to locate ribosome peaks. [1 mark]
* Step 2: SDS-PAGE of proteins from each peak fraction. 80S ribosomes will show more protein bands (≅80 proteins vs 55). Compare band patterns to identify unique proteins. [1 mark]
* Step 3: Mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) of trypsin-digested protein bands to identify specific ribosomal proteins. Eukaryotic-specific proteins (e.g., eS1, eS6, RACK1) confirm 80S identity. [1 mark]
* Step 4: For plant vs. mitochondrial origin: PCR amplify and sequence rRNA genes. Cytosolic 80S rRNA sequences cluster with eukaryotic nuclear genomes; mitochondrial 55S rRNA sequences cluster with Alphaproteobacteria (α-proteobacterial origin of mitochondria). [1 mark]
* Step 5: Electron microscopy with negative staining to visualise ribosome morphology. 80S ribosomes are visibly larger than 70S. Plant cytosolic 80S can be distinguished from mitochondrial 55S by size and structural features. [1 mark]

Application Problem 5: The Great Oxygenation Event & Atmospheric Photochemistry

(a) Pre-Oxygenation O₂ Partial Pressure [2 marks]

PAL = 21% O₂ = 0.21 atm partial pressure

Pre-oxygenation: P(O₂) < 10⁻⁵ × 0.21 atm = 2.1 × 10⁻⁶ atm [1 mark for setup, 1 mark for answer]

This is approximately 0.002 ppm, compared to 210,000 ppm today.

(b) Steady-State Ozone Concentration [4 marks]

At steady state: rate of formation = rate of destruction

Formation: Rate = k₂[O•][O₂][M]

Destruction: Rate = k₃[O₃] [1 mark for correct rate expressions]

k₂[O•][O₂][M] = k₃[O₃]

[O₃] = k₂[O•][O₂][M] / k₃ [1 mark for correct rearrangement]

However, we need [O₂] from the photodissociation equilibrium. From Step 1 and 3 balance:

[O₃] = (1.0 × 10⁻³³)(1.0 × 10⁻¹²)(2.5 × 10¹⁹)[O₂] / (1.0 × 10⁻²) [1 mark]

Assuming [O₂] is maintained at photostationary state, [O₃] = 2.5 × 10⁻⁸ × [O₂] molecules/cm³

For early Earth with [O₂] ≈ 10¹³ molecules/cm³: [O₃] ≈ 2.5 × 10⁵ molecules/cm³ [1 mark]

(c) Ozone Column Density for 99% UV Absorption [4 marks]

Beer-Lambert law: I = I₀e⁻εcl

For 99% absorption: I/I₀ = 0.01, so e⁻εcl = 0.01

εcl = -ln(0.01) = 4.605 [1 mark]

l = 20 km = 20,000 m = 2.0 × 10⁶ cm [1 mark]

c = 4.605 / (ε × l) = 4.605 / (3.0 × 10⁻¹⁸ × 2.0 × 10⁶)

c = 4.605 / (6.0 × 10⁻¹²) = 7.68 × 10¹⁰ molecules/cm³ [1 mark]

Column density = c × l = 7.68 × 10¹⁰ × 2.0 × 10⁶ = 1.54 × 10¹⁷ molecules/cm²

= 1.54 × 10¹⁷ / (6.022 × 10²³) = 2.55 × 10⁻⁷ mol/cm² [1 mark]

(d) Ozone, UV Shielding, and the Cambrian Explosion [6 marks]

Integrated explanation (points must be connected mechanistically):

* Atmospheric photochemistry: O₂ accumulation from cyanobacterial photosynthesis enabled O₃ formation via UV photodissociation. By ~600 MYA, O₂ reached 10–20% PAL, sufficient to sustain a protective ozone layer absorbing >90% of DNA-damaging UV-B/C radiation. [1 mark]
* Cell biology: UV radiation causes pyrimidine dimer formation in DNA, leading to mutations and replication errors. Prokaryotes have efficient photolyase repair, but eukaryotes with larger genomes and nuclear envelopes are more vulnerable. The ozone layer reduced selective pressure for UV-resistance mechanisms and allowed organisms to invest resources in complexity rather than DNA repair. [2 marks]
* Ecological opportunity: Reduced UV enabled life to colonise shallow marine environments (photic zone expansion) and eventually intertidal zones. This expanded habitable volume and created new ecological niches. [1 mark]
* Evolutionary feedback: Larger organisms with higher surface-area-to-volume ratios (more vulnerable to UV) could now survive. This drove selection for multicellularity, tissue differentiation, and mineralised skeletons (which also protect against UV) — all hallmarks of Cambrian fauna. [1 mark]
* Synergistic factors: The ozone shield developed concurrently with other Cambrian triggers (increased dissolved oxygen enabling predation, Snowball Earth events creating genetic bottlenecks, Hox gene diversification). The UV shield was a NECESSARY but not SUFFICIENT condition, illustrating how environmental, biochemical, and genetic factors interact in macroevolution. [1 mark]
