# Chapter 20 — Mutations: The Source of Genetic Variation

*Topic 3: Genes and Heredity | Week 7, Lecture 20 | Lecturer: A/Prof Hayley Bugeja*

*University of Melbourne — First-Year Biology Revision Materials*

**Key idea to remember: A mutation is a change in the sequence, structure or composition of the genome. Mutations are the PRIMARY source of genetic variation. They are classified by how they occur (spontaneous vs induced), whether they are heritable (germline vs somatic), their scale (small-scale point/indel vs large-scale chromosomal), their location (intragenic exon/intron vs intergenic), and their effect on the polypeptide (silent / missense / nonsense / frameshift) and on gene function (loss / gain / no effect).**

This chapter has three question sets: Set 1 — What mutations are, why they matter, how they occur and heritability; Set 2 — Classification by scale and location (small-scale, large-scale chromosomal, intragenic/intergenic); Set 3 — Effects on the polypeptide and on gene function/fitness. Each set has 5 short-answer, 5 Yes/No, 10 multiple-choice and 3 applied (sequence-analysis / calculation) questions. All answers are in the Answer Key at the end.

*Note: For the sequence questions you may use the standard genetic code. You should know the START codon (AUG = Met) and the three STOP codons (UAA, UAG, UGA).*

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** A mutation is a change in the sequence, structure or composition of the genome of an organism. Two caveats: (1) it may or may not affect a gene (it can fall in non-coding/intergenic DNA), and (2) it may or may not be transmitted to offspring (only germline mutations are inherited).

**2.** Mutations introduce new sequence changes, creating the variation on which selection acts; they are the ultimate/primary source of all genetic variation. If DNA were perfectly stable, there would be no new variants, so populations could not adapt or evolve, and the diversity seen within and between species would not arise.

**3.** Spontaneous mutations arise without an external agent — e.g. errors during DNA replication (at most ~1 per billion nucleotides) or spontaneous chemical changes such as deamination. Induced mutations are caused by an external mutagen — chemical agents (e.g. nitrous acid causing deamination, benzopyrene adding a group to guanine) or radiation (X-rays/gamma-rays breaking the backbone, UV forming thymine dimers).

**4.** Deamination removes the amino (NH2) group from cytosine, converting it to uracil (U). U pairs with A. After replication, the strand carrying U templates an A opposite it, so on that daughter molecule the original C:G pair becomes T:A in the next round; the other daughter restores the normal C:G. Hence about half the descendants carry the permanent change (a C:G -> T:A transition).

**5.** Germline mutation: occurs in a gamete (or its precursor) BEFORE fertilisation; the whole organism that grows from that gamete carries it, about half the gametes pass it on, and it IS heritable. Somatic mutation: occurs in a body cell AFTER fertilisation; only that cell and its descendants carry it, the gametes do not, so it is NOT inherited by offspring.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — that is the definition of a mutation.

**2.** No — only germline (heritable) mutations are passed on; somatic ones are not.

**3.** Yes — UV causes covalent bonds between adjacent thymines (thymine dimers).

**4.** Yes — germline mutations are carried by the whole organism and can be inherited.

**5.** Yes — somatic mutations stay in specific cells and are not passed to offspring.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (i) After replication, the strand that contained U acts as a template and places A opposite it, while the original G-containing strand restores C:G. So one daughter helix becomes U:A (then T:A after the U is replaced by T in the next round), and the other stays C:G. (ii) The final mutant base-pair change is C:G -> T:A. Because both C->T and G->A keep purine-purine / pyrimidine-pyrimidine relationships, this is a TRANSITION. (iii) Replication of one double helix gives two daughters: one inherits the mutated strand (mutant) and one inherits the unaffected strand (normal), so roughly 50% mutant and 50% normal.

**2.** (i) Expected errors = (number of nucleotides copied) x (error rate) = 6 x 10^9 nucleotides x 1 mutation / 10^9 nucleotides = about 6 errors per genome copy. (Using 3 x 10^9 bp.) (ii) Most are tolerated because: DNA polymerase proofreading and mismatch repair correct the great majority of errors; much of the genome is non-coding/intergenic or in introns; and many coding changes are silent or neutral, so they rarely disrupt an essential function.

**3.** Least to most harmful (typical reasoning): (i) SSLP expansion in an INTERGENIC region during spermatogenesis is heritable (germline) but lies between genes, so it is least likely to disrupt a polypeptide or survival. (ii) An induced mutation in a single SKIN cell affecting a pigmentation gene is somatic (not inherited) and confined to a few cells; effect is local and usually minor for survival. (iii) A SNP that prevents a cell-cycle gene from being switched OFF is most dangerous: uncontrolled cell division (a hallmark of cancer) directly threatens the organism. Order: (i) < (ii) < (iii). Note heritability differs from harm — the germline change here is the least harmful, while the somatic cell-cycle change is the most dangerous to the individual.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Point mutation / SNP (single nucleotide polymorphism): a change affecting a single nucleotide. Indel: a small insertion or deletion of one or a few nucleotides. Microsatellite / SSLP (simple sequence length polymorphism): a short 1-6 nucleotide motif repeated a variable number of times, so the repeat number differs between individuals.

**2.** Translocation: a segment moves to a different (non-homologous) chromosome. Inversion: a segment is reversed end-to-end within the same chromosome. Deletion: a segment (and its genes) is lost. Duplication: a segment is copied so the chromosome carries an extra copy.

**3.** Intragenic = within a gene; intergenic = between genes. Within a gene, exons are the coding/expressed sequences and introns are intervening sequences that are transcribed into pre-mRNA but spliced out (removed) before translation, so introns are not represented in the final polypeptide.

**4.** Mutations are random changes to DNA and can occur at any position — in genes (exons or introns) or in intergenic regions — not just in coding sequence. A mutation in an intergenic region or deep within an intron is an example of a change that often has little or no effect on the polypeptide because that sequence is not translated.

**5.** A SNP is a single-nucleotide change/variant at one position. A microsatellite (SSLP) is a tandemly repeated short motif (1-6 nt) whose repeat number varies; these vary greatly between individuals because the repeats are prone to replication slippage (strand mis-alignment), which adds or removes whole repeat units.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — a point mutation/SNP affects a single nucleotide.

**2.** Yes — indels are small insertions or deletions.

**3.** Yes — a translocation moves a segment to a non-homologous chromosome.

**4.** No — introns are spliced out before translation and are not translated.

**5.** Yes — mutations can occur in intergenic (non-coding) regions.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Silent: nucleotide change but the codon still specifies the SAME amino acid, so the polypeptide is unchanged. Missense: a codon change that swaps ONE amino acid for a different one. Nonsense: a codon change that creates a premature STOP codon, truncating the polypeptide. Frameshift: an insertion/deletion not divisible by three shifts the reading frame, changing ALL downstream codons (usually with a premature stop), so most of the downstream sequence is wrong.

**2.** A silent mutation is usually a base substitution at the third (wobble) position of a codon, where the genetic code is degenerate so the amino acid stays the same. Likely effect on function: none / neutral, because the protein sequence is unchanged.

**3.** Nonsense: a premature stop ends translation early, producing a truncated (shortened) protein that is usually non-functional, so the effect is severe. Frameshift: shifting the reading frame changes every codon downstream of the indel (and typically introduces a premature stop), so a large part of the protein is altered/lost — far more disruptive than a single missense change, which alters only one amino acid.

**4.** Loss-of-function: protein is non-functional or not made; caused by many mutation types, especially nonsense, frameshift, disruptive missense, or 'no expression'. Gain-of-function: protein gains a new/altered activity; caused by specific (precise) missense changes or altered expression (often from a SNP). No-effect (neutral): protein still functional; caused by silent changes, in-frame indels of 3 nt, conservative substitutions, or changes at unimportant residues.

**5.** A randomly placed change is far more likely to disrupt a finely tuned protein than to improve it, so deleterious (loss-of-function) outcomes outnumber beneficial ones. A beneficial gain-of-function requires a very specific change that creates a new useful activity — such precise changes are rare, so beneficial mutations are uncommon.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — silent = DNA change, no amino-acid change.

**2.** Yes — missense changes a single amino acid.

**3.** Yes — nonsense creates a premature stop codon.

**4.** No — inserting/deleting a multiple of three keeps the reading frame (in-frame indel), so it is NOT a frameshift.

**5.** Yes — gain-of-function gives a new or altered activity.
