# Part II: Answer Key

## Section A: Conceptual Questions - Answers

**Question 1**

The three types of cytoskeletal filaments are:

• Microfilaments (actin filaments): Involved in cell movement, muscle contraction, and maintenance of cell shape. They are the thinnest filaments (~7 nm diameter).

• Intermediate filaments: Provide mechanical support and anchor organelles. They have a rope-like structure (~8-12 nm diameter) and include keratins, vimentin, and lamins.

• Microtubules: Hollow tubes (~25 nm diameter) composed of tubulin dimers. They serve as tracks for motor proteins, form the mitotic spindle, and are structural components of cilia and flagella.

**Question 2**

Semi-conservative replication means that each daughter DNA molecule contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesized strand. After replication, the two resulting double helices each consist of one old strand template and one new complementary strand.

This mechanism is advantageous for DNA repair because the parental strand serves as a template to correct errors in the newly synthesized strand. DNA repair mechanisms can distinguish the original strand from the new strand and use the original as the correct reference sequence.

**Question 3**

Key differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic DNA replication:

• Origin of replication: Prokaryotes have a single origin of replication (oriC) on their circular chromosome. Eukaryotes have multiple origins of replication (potentially thousands) distributed along each linear chromosome.

• Speed: Prokaryotic replication proceeds at approximately 1,000 bp/second. Eukaryotic replication is slower at about 50 bp/second per replication fork, but the multiple origins compensate for this.

• Timing: In prokaryotes, DNA replication can occur continuously as part of the cell cycle. In eukaryotes, replication is restricted to the S phase of interphase.

• Chromosome structure: Prokaryotic DNA is typically a single circular molecule. Eukaryotic DNA is organized into multiple linear chromosomes with telomeres at the ends, requiring special mechanisms to replicate chromosome ends.

**Question 4**

The three sub-phases of interphase are:

• G1 phase (First Gap): The cell grows, synthesizes proteins and RNA, and prepares for DNA replication. This is the primary growth phase and the decision point for cell division.

• S phase (Synthesis): DNA replication occurs. The entire genome is duplicated, resulting in sister chromatids joined at the centromere. Histone synthesis also occurs during this phase.

• G2 phase (Second Gap): The cell continues to grow and synthesize proteins necessary for mitosis. The cell checks for complete DNA replication and repairs any damage before entering mitosis.

**Question 5**

Cyclin levels oscillate throughout the cell cycle in a predictable pattern. Different cyclins are synthesized and degraded at specific phases:

• G1 cyclins (cyclin D) peak in G1 phase and activate CDKs to drive the cell past the restriction point.

• S cyclins (cyclin E, then cyclin A) peak during S phase and promote DNA replication initiation.

• M cyclins (cyclin B) accumulate during G2 and peak in M phase, triggering entry into mitosis.

CDKs are always present in the cell at relatively constant levels but are inactive on their own. They require binding to their specific cyclin partner to become active. The cyclin acts as a regulatory subunit that activates the CDK kinase and targets it to specific substrates. Cyclin degradation (via ubiquitin-proteasome pathway) inactivates the CDK, allowing the cell to exit that phase.

**Question 6**

Cell cycle checkpoints are surveillance mechanisms that ensure the fidelity of cell division by monitoring DNA integrity, replication completion, and chromosome attachment. They can halt the cell cycle if conditions are not met.

The ATR-mediated checkpoint in S phase is triggered by the presence of single-stranded DNA (ssDNA), which typically arises at stalled replication forks or sites of DNA damage. When ssDNA is detected, ATR (Ataxia Telangiectasia and Rad3-related) kinase is activated, which phosphorylates downstream targets to halt S phase progression. This gives the cell time to repair the damage or resolve the replication block before proceeding.

**Question 7**

The four main stages of mitosis in an animal cell:

• Prophase: Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids). The nuclear envelope breaks down. The mitotic spindle begins to form from the centrosomes.

• Metaphase: Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (equatorial plane). Spindle microtubules attach to the kinetochores of each chromosome. The cell checks that all chromosomes are properly attached before proceeding.

• Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate at the centromere and are pulled toward opposite poles by the shortening of kinetochore microtubules. Non-kinetochore microtubules elongate, pushing the poles apart. Once separated, each chromatid is considered an individual chromosome.

• Telophase: Chromosomes arrive at the poles and decondense. Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes. The spindle apparatus disassembles. Cytokinesis typically begins during late telophase.

**Question 8**

Three major differences between binary fission and mitosis:

• Chromosome structure and organization: Binary fission involves replication of a single circular chromosome attached to the cell membrane. Mitosis involves multiple linear chromosomes organized by a microtubule spindle apparatus.

• Spindle formation: Binary fission does not involve a mitotic spindle. The FtsZ protein forms a contractile ring at the division site. Mitosis requires an elaborate microtubule spindle to segregate chromosomes.

• Nuclear envelope: Prokaryotes lack a nucleus, so there is no nuclear envelope to break down and reform. In eukaryotic mitosis, the nuclear envelope disassembles in prophase and reforms in telophase.

**Question 9**

FtsZ (filament temperature sensitive Z) is a protein that polymerizes to form a ring-like structure (Z-ring) at the future division site of the bacterial cell. This Z-ring recruits other division proteins and serves as a scaffold for cell wall synthesis, ultimately constricting to divide the cell into two daughter cells.

FtsZ is a homologue of the eukaryotic cytoskeletal protein tubulin. Both proteins can hydrolyze GTP and polymerize into filamentous structures. This evolutionary relationship suggests that the mechanisms of cell division in prokaryotes and eukaryotes share a common ancestral origin. FtsZ is also involved in the division of chloroplasts and mitochondria (which evolved from bacteria).

**Question 10**

In animal cells, cytokinesis occurs by a cleavage furrow. A contractile ring of actin and myosin filaments assembles just inside the plasma membrane at the cell equator. This ring contracts, pinching the cell membrane inward until the cell is physically divided into two daughter cells.

In plant cells, cytokinesis occurs by cell plate formation. Vesicles containing cell wall materials are transported by microtubules to the center of the dividing cell, where they fuse to form a cell plate. The cell plate expands outward until it fuses with the existing plasma membrane, and a new cell wall is deposited.

The mechanisms differ because plant cells have rigid cell walls. A cleavage furrow would be ineffective against the stiff cell wall. The cell plate method allows new cell wall material to be deposited from the inside out, creating a new partition between the daughter cells.

## Section B: Application Problems - Answers

**Application Problem 1: DNA Replication Rate Calculation**

(a) Total length of DNA to be synthesized:

Since DNA replication is bidirectional (two replication forks) and the DNA is double-stranded, each fork synthesizes one new strand. However, the total amount of new DNA synthesized equals the length of one complete genome (both strands of the daughter molecule are newly synthesized when considering both daughter cells).

Total new DNA synthesized = 4.6 x 10^6 bp x 2 strands = 9.2 x 10^6 nucleotides. However, the more biologically relevant answer is 4.6 x 10^6 bp per daughter chromosome.

(b) Theoretical minimum time:

With bidirectional replication from a single origin, two replication forks move in opposite directions. Each fork must replicate half the chromosome:

Distance per fork = 4.6 x 10^6 bp / 2 = 2.3 x 10^6 bp

Time = Distance / Rate = 2.3 x 10^6 bp / 1,000 bp/s = 2,300 seconds = 38.3 minutes

This closely matches the observed ~40 minutes for E. coli replication.

(c) If a second round initiates 20 minutes after the first, and each round takes ~40 minutes:

At 20 minutes, the first round is halfway complete (2 forks active). When the second round initiates, it creates 2 new forks. Total forks = 4. By 40 minutes, a third round could initiate if conditions permit. This overlapping replication ensures that daughter cells receive complete chromosomes even in rapid growth conditions.

• Use fluorescence microscopy to examine individual chromosomes after the second S phase.

**Application Problem 3: Cyclin-CDK Regulation Analysis**

(a) Cyclin B levels remain low during G1 and S phases, begin rising in G2, peak at the G2/M transition, and then drop sharply during anaphase due to APC/C-mediated degradation. CDK1 activity closely mirrors cyclin B levels because CDK1 requires cyclin B binding for activation.

(b) The cyclin B-CDK1 inhibitor would cause cells to arrest at the G2/M checkpoint. Cells would have completed DNA replication (4N DNA content) but would be unable to enter mitosis. Morphologically, cells would appear larger than normal (due to continued growth in G2), with intact nuclear envelopes and condensed chromatin would not occur.

(c) Overexpression of cyclin B leads to premature or excessive activation of CDK1, causing the cell to enter mitosis before DNA damage is repaired or before DNA replication is complete. This can lead to chromosome missegregation and aneuploidy, hallmarks of cancer. A potential therapeutic strategy is to develop CDK1 inhibitors or drugs that promote cyclin B degradation (e.g., proteasome inhibitors or APC/C activators).

**Application Problem 4: Cell Cycle Drug Treatment**

(a) Drug analysis:

• Drug X: Accumulates cells in S phase (55% vs. 25% in control). This indicates the drug blocks DNA replication.

• Drug Y: Accumulates cells in G1 phase (65% vs. 50% in control). This suggests the drug prevents entry into S phase, possibly by inhibiting cyclin D-CDK4/6 complexes or blocking growth factor signaling.

• Drug Z: Accumulates cells in G2/M phase (40% vs. 25% in control). This indicates the drug blocks entry into or progression through mitosis, possibly by inhibiting cyclin B-CDK1 or microtubule polymerization.

(b) DNA polymerase III inhibition by Drug X prevents elongation of DNA strands during replication. Cells that were in S phase at the time of treatment cannot complete DNA synthesis. Cells that were in G1 when treated cannot enter S phase (due to checkpoint activation), but the observed accumulation in S phase suggests the drug primarily affects cells already engaged in replication, causing them to stall and accumulate.

(c) To confirm Drug Z affects mitosis, perform immunofluorescence staining with:

• Anti-alpha-tubulin antibody (to visualize microtubules/spindle)

• Anti-phospho-histone H3 antibody (a marker of mitotic chromosomes)

• DAPI (to stain DNA)

Expected results: Drug Z-treated cells would show condensed, phospho-H3 positive chromosomes but disorganized or absent mitotic spindle (instead of normal bipolar spindle), confirming the drug disrupts microtubule function required for chromosome segregation.

(b) Yes, the statement is correct. During anaphase, sister chromatids separate and each chromatid is now considered an individual chromosome. Therefore, 92 chromosomes are present (46 moving toward each pole), each consisting of a single chromatid. The key distinction is that before anaphase, 46 chromosomes each consist of 2 sister chromatids; after separation, 92 chromosomes each consist of 1 chromatid.

(c) Colchicine binds to free tubulin dimers and prevents their polymerization into microtubules. In metaphase, this prevents formation of the mitotic spindle. Without functional spindle microtubules, kinetochores cannot attach to spindle fibers, and chromosomes cannot align at the metaphase plate.

The spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC) detects unattached kinetochores and prevents activation of the anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C). This arrests the cell in metaphase because securin and cyclin B cannot be degraded. The affected cytoskeletal structure is the microtubule component of the cytoskeleton. Prolonged arrest typically triggers apoptosis.
