## Question 1: Mitochondrial Cristae Surface Area & ATP Synthesis

*Context: A liver cell contains approximately 2,000 mitochondria. Each mitochondrion is roughly spherical with a diameter of 0.5 µm. The inner membrane folds into cristae, which increases the total inner membrane surface area by a factor of 17 compared to the outer membrane surface area.*

**a)** Calculate the outer membrane surface area of a single mitochondrion (assume spherical geometry: A = 4πr²). [2 marks]

**b)** Calculate the total inner membrane surface area available for ATP synthesis in one mitochondrion. [2 marks]

**c)** If ATP synthase complexes are present at a density of 500 complexes per µm² of inner membrane, and each complex produces 100 ATP molecules per second, calculate the theoretical maximum ATP production rate (ATP molecules/second) for all mitochondria in one liver cell. [4 marks]

**d)** Explain why the actual ATP production rate in vivo may be significantly lower than your calculated theoretical maximum. Identify at least two limiting factors. [2 marks]

## Question 2: Nuclear Pore Transport Kinetics

*Context: The nuclear envelope of a eukaryotic cell contains approximately 2,000 nuclear pores. Each pore has a functional diameter of 50 nm and allows regulated transport between the nucleus and cytoplasm. You are studying the import of a specific transcription factor (protein X) with a molecular mass of 45 kDa and a diffusion coefficient (D) of 6.0 × 10⁻¹¹ m²/s in the cytoplasm. The concentration of protein X in the cytoplasm is maintained at 200 nM.*

**a)** Calculate the cross-sectional area of a single nuclear pore (A = πr²). Express your answer in m². [2 marks]

**b)** Using Fick's first law approximation: J = -D × (dC/dx), where dC/dx is the concentration gradient across the nuclear envelope (approximately 200 nM over 40 nm), calculate the diffusive flux (molecules per pore per second) of protein X through a single nuclear pore. Assume 1 nM = 6.022 × 10¹⁴ molecules/m³. [4 marks]

**c)** The actual measured import rate of protein X is only 0.1% of your calculated diffusive flux. Suggest two biological reasons why the actual rate is so much lower, considering the role of nuclear localization signals (NLS) and import receptors. [2 marks]

**d)** Design an experiment using fluorescent tagging to demonstrate that nuclear import of protein X requires an NLS and is energy-dependent. Include appropriate controls. [2 marks]

# Answer Key

## Question 1: Mitochondrial Cristae Surface Area & ATP Synthesis

a) Radius r = 0.25 µm = 0.25 × 10⁻⁶ m. Outer membrane surface area = 4πr² = 4 × π × (0.25 × 10⁻⁶)² = 7.85 × 10⁻¹³ m² = 0.785 µm². [2 marks]

b) Inner membrane surface area = 17 × outer membrane area = 17 × 0.785 = 13.35 µm² per mitochondrion. [2 marks]

c) Total inner membrane area in cell = 2,000 × 13.35 = 26,700 µm². Number of ATP synthase complexes = 26,700 × 500 = 1.335 × 10⁷ complexes. ATP production rate = 1.335 × 10⁷ × 100 = 1.335 × 10⁹ ATP molecules/second. [4 marks]

d) Limiting factors: (1) Substrate availability — NADH and FADH₂ supply may limit electron transport chain activity; (2) ADP and Pi concentration — ATP synthase requires ADP; if cellular ATP demand is low, ADP may be limiting; (3) Oxygen availability — terminal electron acceptor; (4) Membrane integrity and proton leak — reduces proton motive force efficiency. (Any two) [2 marks]

## Question 2: Nuclear Pore Transport Kinetics

a) Radius r = 25 nm = 25 × 10⁻⁹ m. Cross-sectional area A = πr² = π × (25 × 10⁻⁹)² = 1.96 × 10⁻¹⁵ m². [2 marks]

b) dC/dx = 200 nM / 40 nm = (200 × 6.022 × 10¹⁴) / (40 × 10⁻⁹) = 3.01 × 10²⁷ molecules/m⁴. J = D × dC/dx = 6.0 × 10⁻¹¹ × 3.01 × 10²⁷ = 1.81 × 10¹⁶ molecules/(m²·s). Molecules per pore per second = J × A = 1.81 × 10¹⁶ × 1.96 × 10⁻¹⁵ = 35.5 molecules/s ≈ 36 molecules/s per pore. [4 marks]

c) Reason 1: Protein X contains a nuclear localization signal (NLS) that must be recognized by importin receptors. Without NLS recognition, the protein cannot access the transport channel. Reason 2: Nuclear import is an active, energy-dependent process requiring GTP hydrolysis by Ran GTPase. Simple diffusion through the pore is blocked for macromolecules > 40-60 kDa; receptor-mediated transport is required and is rate-limited by receptor availability and RanGTP gradient. [2 marks]

d) Experimental design: (1) Express GFP-tagged protein X (wild-type with NLS) in cultured cells; (2) As a negative control, express GFP-tagged protein X with mutated NLS; (3) Treat cells with metabolic inhibitors (e.g., sodium azide + deoxyglucose) to deplete ATP/GTP; (4) Use live-cell fluorescence microscopy to monitor nuclear import over time. Expected results: WT protein accumulates in nucleus; NLS-mutant stays cytoplasmic; energy-depleted cells show blocked import. This demonstrates both NLS requirement and energy dependence. [2 marks]

## Question 3: Endosymbiotic Origin — Genomic Evidence Analysis

Evidence 1: Double membrane structure — The mitochondrion has an outer and inner membrane, consistent with an endosymbiotic origin where the inner membrane is derived from the ancestral bacterium and the outer membrane from the host's phagocytic vesicle. Evidence 2: Circular DNA — Mitochondrial DNA is circular like bacterial chromosomes, not linear like eukaryotic nuclear DNA. The genome size is dramatically reduced (16,569 bp vs 1,100,000 bp) due to massive gene transfer to the nucleus. Evidence 3: 70S ribosomes — Mitochondrial ribosomes are 70S, similar to bacterial ribosomes (70S), not 80S like eukaryotic cytoplasmic ribosomes. This is strong evidence of bacterial ancestry. (Any three of the six) [6 marks]

b) The remaining ~1,487 proteins are encoded by nuclear genes, synthesized on cytoplasmic ribosomes as precursor proteins with mitochondrial targeting signals (MTS), and imported into mitochondria via the TOM/TIM translocase complexes. The massive gene transfer from mitochondria to the nucleus over evolutionary time supports the endosymbiotic theory, as it demonstrates the dependency of the organelle on the host cell that developed after the initial endosymbiotic event. [2 marks]

c) Methodology: (1) Extract mitochondrial DNA from diverse eukaryotes (animals, fungi, plants, protists); (2) Sequence specific genes commonly retained in mitochondrial genomes (e.g., cytochrome c oxidase subunits, cytochrome b); (3) Align sequences with homologous genes from various α-proteobacteria (including Rickettsiales); (4) Construct a phylogenetic tree using maximum likelihood or Bayesian methods. Expected results: Mitochondrial sequences from all eukaryotes should form a monophyletic group nested within the α-proteobacteria, specifically sister to Rickettsiales. Interpretation: This would confirm a single origin of mitochondria from a purple bacterial ancestor. [2 marks]

## Question 4: Cellular Respiration ATP Yield Calculation

a) Glycolysis: 4 ATP (substrate-level) − 2 ATP (investment) = 2 net ATP + 2 NADH. Pyruvate dehydrogenase: 2 NADH. Citric acid cycle: 2 ATP (GTP) + 6 NADH + 2 FADH₂. Oxidative phosphorylation: (10 NADH × 3) + (2 FADH₂ × 2) = 30 + 4 = 34 ATP. Total = 2 + 2 + 34 = 38 ATP (or 36 if using the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle where cytoplasmic NADH yields only 2 ATP each). [4 marks]

b) NADH from glycolysis (via malate-aspartate shuttle): 2 × 2.5 = 5 ATP. NADH from pyruvate DH: 2 × 2.5 = 5 ATP. NADH from citric acid cycle: 6 × 2.5 = 15 ATP. FADH₂ from citric acid cycle: 2 × 1.5 = 3 ATP. Substrate-level: 2 (glycolysis net) + 2 (GTP) = 4 ATP. Total = 5 + 5 + 15 + 3 + 4 = 32 ATP per glucose. [3 marks]

c) Glucose molecules required = (5 × 10⁹) / (32 × 6.022 × 10²³) = 2.6 × 10⁻¹⁴ mol/min = 2.6 × 10⁻¹⁴ × 6.022 × 10²³ = 1.56 × 10¹⁰ glucose molecules per minute. [2 marks]

d) The malate-aspartate shuttle transfers NADH electrons to mitochondrial NAD⁺, producing 2.5 ATP per NADH. The glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle transfers electrons to FAD in the inner membrane, producing only 1.5 ATP per NADH. This difference can reduce total yield from 32 to 30 ATP per glucose if the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle is used. [1 mark]

## Question 5: Chloroplast Membrane Structure & Endosymbiosis

a) Chlamydomonas reinhardtii — Primary endosymbiosis. Two membranes indicate a direct engulfment of a cyanobacterium; no additional host membranes remain. Guillardia theta — Secondary endosymbiosis. Four membranes plus a nucleomorph indicate engulfment of a red alga (which itself had a chloroplast from primary endosymbiosis); the nucleomorph is the remnant nucleus of the red algal endosymbiont. Euglena gracilis — Secondary endosymbiosis. Three membranes suggest the phagosomal membrane broke down, leaving only the chloroplast's two membranes plus the euglenophyte's membrane. Dinophysis acuminata — Tertiary endosymbiosis. Multiple membrane layers and complex structure indicate sequential engulfment events. [4 marks]

b) The nucleomorph genome would be expected to contain genes essential for chloroplast function that were not transferred to the host nucleus, such as genes for chloroplast-targeted proteins involved in photosynthesis and plastid maintenance. These genes were retained because: (1) some proteins cannot be properly imported when synthesized in the cytoplasm due to complex membrane topology; (2) genes whose expression must be rapidly regulated in response to plastid metabolic state; (3) genes for which the host nuclear transcription/translation machinery is incompatible. [2 marks]

c) Genes transferred = 5,000 − 464 = 4,536 genes. Percentage transferred = (4,536 / 5,000) × 100 = 90.7%. This massive gene transfer demonstrates the extensive integration of the endosymbiont's genetic system into the host cell. [2 marks]

d) Experimental design: Use serial fluorescent labeling with membrane-specific dyes: (1) Label the outermost membrane with a fluorescent lectin (e.g., FITC-WGA) that binds to glycoproteins on the plasma membrane-derived outer membrane; (2) Label the second membrane with a lipophilic dye (e.g., DiO) that incorporates into membranes; (3) Label the chloroplast envelope with a chlorophyll autofluorescence signal (red fluorescence at ~680 nm); (4) Use confocal microscopy with Z-stack imaging to resolve all four membrane layers. Expected observation: Four distinct fluorescent rings surrounding the chloroplast, confirming the presence of four membranes derived from secondary endosymbiosis. [2 marks]
