# Answer Key

## Revision Set 1 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

1. The cytosol is the fluid part of the cytoplasm between organelles and the endomembrane system. It is called the metabolic heart because many biochemical processes, biochemical conversions, protein biosynthesis, and cytoskeletal activities occur there.

2. Microfilaments: actin filaments; maintain cell shape, allow cell movement, muscle contraction, cytoplasmic streaming, and cleavage furrow formation. Intermediate filaments: keratin-like fibrous cables; maintain cell shape, anchor nucleus/organelles, and form nuclear lamina. Microtubules: tubulin polymers; support cell shape, move organelles, form cilia/flagella, and move chromosomes during cell division.

3. Semi-conservative replication means each daughter DNA molecule contains one original parental strand and one newly synthesized strand. This helps repair because the old strand can guide correction of errors in the new strand.

4. Prokaryotes usually have one origin of replication on a circular DNA molecule. Eukaryotes have multiple origins of replication along each chromosome, allowing long chromosomes to be replicated efficiently.

5. Mitosis is nuclear division in eukaryotes that produces genetically identical nuclei. Binary fission is division of a prokaryotic cell into two cells and does not involve mitosis.

### D. Application / process-analysis answers

1. The new strand is synthesized in the 5' to 3' direction. DNA polymerase adds each incoming nucleotide to the 3' end of the growing strand.

2. Replication begins at the single origin. The DNA is copied as replication proceeds around the circular chromosome. Once the chromosome is duplicated, the cell elongates, FtsZ forms a constricting ring, and the cell divides into two daughter cells by binary fission.

3. Microtubules form the spindle and are required for chromosome attachment, alignment, and separation. If microtubules are disrupted, chromosomes may fail to attach to the spindle, fail to align at metaphase, or fail to separate properly in anaphase.

## Revision Set 2 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

1. Cell division rates reflect biological function. E. coli and embryos can divide rapidly; germinating plants require fast division for growth; neurons are highly specialized and generally do not divide.

2. Helicase unwinds the DNA double helix. Single-stranded binding proteins keep template strands separated. Primase synthesizes RNA primers. DNA polymerase extends DNA from primers in the 5' to 3' direction. Okazaki fragments are short DNA pieces formed on the lagging strand and later joined.

3. G1: growth and metabolic activity. S: DNA replication. G2: further growth and preparation for mitosis, including spindle-related preparation.

4. CDKs are generally present, but cyclin levels rise and fall through the cell cycle. When a cyclin binds a CDK, the complex becomes active and phosphorylates target proteins, regulating progression through checkpoints and phases.

5. Prophase: chromosomes condense and spindle assembly begins. Prometaphase: nuclear envelope breaks down and chromosomes attach to spindle microtubules. Metaphase: chromosomes align at the metaphase plate. Anaphase: sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles. Telophase: chromosomes decondense and nuclear envelopes re-form.

### D. Application / process-analysis answers

1. A G2 checkpoint would halt progression into mitosis if DNA damage is detected. This prevents damaged DNA from being passed to daughter nuclei and allows time for repair.

2. In animal cells, a contractile ring pinches the cell into two daughter cells. In plant cells, vesicles fuse at the center to form a cell plate, which becomes the new cell wall/membrane partition between daughter cells.

3. The stage is metaphase. This is identified by chromosomes aligned at the metaphase plate in the middle of the spindle before sister chromatids separate.
