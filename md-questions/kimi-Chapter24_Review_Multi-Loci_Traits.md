# Review Questions

The following five application problems are designed to reinforce your understanding of genetic analysis involving multiple gene loci. Each problem involves experimental design, calculations, or data analysis related to dihybrid crosses, test crosses, linked genes, and recombination. Show all your working.

## Question 1: Dihybrid Cross Analysis (Experimental Design & Calculation)

In a species of garden pea, flower colour is controlled by a single gene with two alleles: P (purple, dominant) and p (white, recessive). Pod length is controlled by a separate gene on a different chromosome with two alleles: L (long, dominant) and l (short, recessive). A true-breeding plant with purple flowers and long pods is crossed with a true-breeding plant with white flowers and short pods.

(a) State the genotypes and phenotypes of the P generation and F1 generation.

(b) If the F1 plants are self-fertilised, construct a Punnett square to show the F2 genotypic outcomes. What is the expected phenotypic ratio in the F2 generation?

(c) In an actual experiment, an F2 population of 640 plants was obtained. Calculate the expected number of plants for each phenotype.

(d) The observed results were: 365 purple long, 118 purple short, 121 white long, and 36 white short. Use a chi-square (X2) test to determine whether these results are consistent with Mendel's law of independent assortment. Use X2 = SUM((O-E)2/E) and the critical value of 7.815 for p = 0.05 with 3 degrees of freedom.

## Question 2: Test Cross Application (Problem Solving)

A guinea pig breeder has a male with black coat colour and long fur. Coat colour is controlled by gene B/b (B = black dominant, b = brown recessive), and fur length is controlled by gene S/s (S = long dominant, s = short recessive). These two genes are known to assort independently. The breeder wants to determine the genotype of this male.

(a) What is the most informative cross the breeder should perform to determine the genotype of the black, long-haired male? Explain your reasoning.

(b) Design the complete cross: state the genotype of the tester individual, and list all possible phenotypic outcomes of the offspring for each possible genotype of the unknown male (BBSS, BBSs, BbSS, or BbSs). Present your answer in a table.

(c) If the test cross produces offspring in a ratio of approximately 1 black long : 1 black short : 1 brown long : 1 brown short, what is the genotype of the original male?

(d) If instead all offspring from the test cross were black and long-haired, what would this indicate about the original male's genotype?

## Question 3: Linked Genes and Recombination Frequency (Data Analysis)

In Drosophila melanogaster, two genes are located on the same autosome: one controls body colour (G = grey dominant, g = ebony recessive) and the other controls wing shape (V = normal dominant, v = vestigial recessive). A dihybrid grey-bodied, normal-winged fly (GgVv) is test-crossed with an ebony-bodied, vestigial-winged fly. The following offspring were obtained from 1000 progeny:

| **Phenotype** | **Number** |
| --- | --- |
| Grey body, normal wings | 405 |
| Ebony body, vestigial wings | 415 |
| Grey body, vestigial wings | 92 |
| Ebony body, normal wings | 88 |

(a) Identify which phenotypic classes represent parental (non-recombinant) types and which represent recombinant types. Explain your reasoning.

(b) Calculate the recombination frequency between these two genes.

(c) Determine the map distance (in centiMorgans, cM) between the body colour gene and the wing shape gene.

(d) The G and V genes are linked. Draw a diagram showing the arrangement of alleles on the homologous chromosomes in the dihybrid parent, clearly indicating which alleles are on the same chromosome (cis or trans configuration).

(e) Explain how crossing over during meiosis I accounts for the production of recombinant offspring. In which stage of prophase I does this occur?

## Question 4: Interpreting Experimental Cross Data (Critical Analysis)

A plant breeder crossed pure-breeding lines of legumes: Line A has white flowers and long pods; Line B has purple flowers and short pods. The F1 generation all had pink flowers and long pods. Three different crosses were then performed with the F1 generation, and the results are shown below:

## Question 5: Experimental Design - Testing for Linkage

You are working in a genetics laboratory studying three genes in tomatoes: gene A/a controls fruit colour (A = red, a = yellow), gene B/b controls leaf texture (B = smooth, b = hairy), and gene C/c controls plant height (C = tall, c = dwarf). You suspect that all three genes may be on the same chromosome, but their order and distances are unknown.

(a) Describe the complete experimental procedure you would use to determine whether genes A and B are linked. Include: (i) the parental cross to produce the test individuals, (ii) the test cross itself, and (iii) how you would interpret the results.

| --- | --- |

(c) To map the three genes, a three-point test cross is performed using an AaBbCc triple heterozygote. Explain why a three-point test cross is more efficient than performing three separate two-point crosses for constructing a genetic map.

# Answer Key

Detailed solutions to all review questions are provided below.

## Question 1: Dihybrid Cross Analysis

### (a) P and F1 Generation

P generation: PP LL (purple, long) x pp ll (white, short)

F1 generation: All Pp Ll (purple, long) - heterozygous at both loci

### (b) F2 Punnett Square and Phenotypic Ratio

F1 self-cross: Pp Ll x Pp Ll. The F1 produces gametes PL, Pl, pL, pl in equal proportions (1:1:1:1).

F2 phenotypic ratio: 9 purple long (P-L-) : 3 purple short (P-ll) : 3 white long (ppL-) : 1 white short (ppll).

### (c) Expected Numbers in F2 (n = 640)

Expected ratio = 9:3:3:1 (total 16 parts)

Purple, long: 9/16 x 640 = 360

Purple, short: 3/16 x 640 = 120

White, long: 3/16 x 640 = 120

White, short: 1/16 x 640 = 40

### (d) Chi-Square Test

X2 = (365-360)2/360 + (118-120)2/120 + (121-120)2/120 + (36-40)2/40

X2 = 25/360 + 4/120 + 1/120 + 16/40 = 0.069 + 0.033 + 0.008 + 0.400 = 0.510

Since X2 (0.510) < critical value (7.815), we fail to reject the null hypothesis. The observed results are consistent with independent assortment (p > 0.05).

## Question 2: Test Cross Application

### (a) Most Informative Cross

A test cross with a homozygous recessive individual (bb ss, brown and short-haired). This is most informative because the tester can only contribute recessive alleles (b and s), so the phenotype of each offspring directly reveals which alleles the unknown male contributed in his gametes.

### (b) Test Cross Design and Expected Outcomes

| **Unknown Male Genotype** | **Offspring Phenotypes from Test Cross (x bb ss)** |
| --- | --- |
| BBSS | All black, long (100%) |
| BBSs | 1 black long : 1 black short |
| BbSS | 1 black long : 1 brown long |
| BbSs | 1 black long : 1 black short : 1 brown long : 1 brown short |

### (c) Genotype Determination

The 1:1:1:1 ratio indicates the male is heterozygous at both loci: BbSs. This is the only genotype that produces four gamete types (BS, Bs, bS, bs) in equal proportions.

### (d) All Offspring Black and Long

If all offspring were black and long, the male must be homozygous dominant at both loci (BBSS). He can only produce BS gametes, and all offspring would be BbSs (black, long).

## Question 3: Linked Genes and Recombination

### (a) Parental and Recombinant Types

Parental types (most numerous): Grey body, normal wings (405) and Ebony body, vestigial wings (415) - these match the phenotypes of the original parents.

Recombinant types (less numerous): Grey body, vestigial wings (92) and Ebony body, normal wings (88) - these result from crossing over between the linked genes.

### (b) Recombination Frequency

Recombinants = 92 + 88 = 180

Total offspring = 1000

Recombination frequency = 180/1000 = 0.18 = 18%

### (c) Map Distance

Map distance = 18 cM (1% recombination = 1 cM).

### (d) Allele Arrangement (cis configuration)

The dihybrid parent has the dominant alleles on one chromosome and recessive alleles on the homologous chromosome (cis/coupling configuration):

Chromosome 1: G --- V

Chromosome 2: g --- v

This arrangement produces parental gametes GV and gv most frequently, with recombinant gametes Gv and gV produced less frequently due to crossing over.

### (e) Crossing Over

During prophase I of meiosis, homologous chromosomes pair up and non-sister chromatids exchange segments at chiasmata. This physical exchange of DNA between chromatids results in recombinant chromosomes carrying new allele combinations (e.g., Gv or gV instead of the original GV or gv).

## Question 4: Interpreting Experimental Cross Data

### (a) Gene Loci and Dominance Relationships

Two gene loci are involved:

Flower colour shows INCOMPLETE DOMINANCE. Evidence: F1 x F1 cross produces purple:pink:white in a 1:2:1 ratio (1200:800 for long pods; or 400:800:400 across all pod types). The pink F1 phenotype is intermediate between white and purple. Let P = purple, P' = white (or use PP = purple, Pp = pink, pp = white notation).

Pod length shows COMPLETE DOMINANCE. Evidence: All F1 have long pods, and F1 x F1 gives approximately 3 long : 1 short (1200:400). Let S = long (dominant), s = short (recessive).

### (b) Genotypes

Line A (white, long): PP' SS (or pp SS if using W/w notation for white)

Line B (purple, short): PP ss (or PP ss)

F1 (pink, long): PP' Ss

### (c) Deviations from 9:3:3:1

The 9:3:3:1 ratio only applies when both genes show complete dominance. Here, flower colour shows incomplete dominance, producing a 1:2:1 ratio (purple:pink:white) instead of 3:1. Pod length shows 3:1 (long:short). Combined: (1:2:1) x (3:1) = 3:6:3:1:2:1 ratio for the six phenotypic classes observed.

Expected for F1 x F1 with n=1600:

Long purple: 3/16 x 1600 = 300; Long pink: 6/16 x 1600 = 600; Long white: 3/16 x 1600 = 300

Short purple: 1/16 x 1600 = 100; Short pink: 2/16 x 1600 = 200; Short white: 1/16 x 1600 = 100

These match closely with observed values (301, 599, 301, 99, 202, 98).

### (d) F1 x Short, White Test Cross Punnett Square

F1 (PP' Ss) gametes: PS, Ps, P'S, P's in 1:1:1:1 ratio

Tester (P'P' ss) gametes: P's only

Offspring: 1 PP' Ss (pink, long) : 1 PP' ss (pink, short) : 1 P'P' Ss (white, long) : 1 P'P' ss (white, short)

Expected with n=1600: 400 of each class. This matches the observed 404:399:397:400.

## Question 5: Experimental Design - Testing for Linkage

### (a) Experimental Procedure to Test for Linkage

Step 1 - Parental cross: Cross a true-breeding dominant parent (AA BB, red smooth) with a true-breeding recessive parent (aa bb, yellow hairy) to produce F1 AaBb individuals.

Step 2 - Test cross: Cross the F1 dihybrid (AaBb) with a double homozygous recessive tester (aa bb, yellow hairy).

Step 3 - Interpretation: If genes assort independently, expect 1:1:1:1 phenotypic ratio in offspring. If linked, parental phenotypes will exceed recombinant phenotypes.

### (b) Data Analysis and Conclusion

Parental types: Red smooth (230) + Yellow hairy (240) = 470

Recombinant types: Red hairy (28) + Yellow smooth (22) = 50

Total = 520

Recombination frequency = 50/520 = 0.096 = 9.6%

Yes, genes A and B ARE linked. The recombination frequency (9.6%) is much less than 50%, and parental phenotypes far outnumber recombinants, both indicating linkage.

### (c) Efficiency of Three-Point Test Cross

A three-point test cross is more efficient because:

1. It simultaneously determines the order of three genes and the distances between them in a single experiment.

2. It detects double crossovers, which three separate two-point crosses would miss. Double crossovers can make distant genes appear closer than they are.

3. It requires fewer experimental crosses and fewer offspring to achieve the same mapping resolution.

### (d) Genetic Map

Gene map: A ----(12 cM)---- C ----(18 cM)---- B

A to B distance from map: 12 + 18 = 30 cM

Observed A-B distance: 28 cM

The map distances are approximately additive (30 cM vs. 28 cM). The slight discrepancy (2 cM) is due to double crossovers between A and B that are not detected in the two-point data. This demonstrates that genetic map distances are additive only approximately, and the discrepancy increases with greater distances due to undetectable double crossovers.
