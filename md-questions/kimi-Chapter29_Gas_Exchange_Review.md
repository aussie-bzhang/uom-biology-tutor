# Problem 2: Applying Fick's Law (Calculation & Analysis)

Fick's Law describes the rate of gas diffusion across an exchange surface:

*Rate of diffusion = (Surface Area × Partial Pressure Gradient × Diffusion Coefficient) / Diffusion Distance*

In the human lung, the following parameters are measured at rest:

1. Alveolar surface area available for gas exchange: 85 m² (8.5 × 10⁵ cm²)
2. Partial pressure gradient for O₂ between alveolar air and pulmonary capillary blood: 60 mm Hg
3. Diffusion coefficient for O₂ through alveolar-capillary membrane: 2.5 × 10⁻⁶ cm²/s/mm Hg
4. Thickness of respiratory membrane (diffusion distance): 0.5 μm (5 × 10⁻⁵ cm)

**(a)** Calculate the rate of O₂ diffusion across the respiratory membrane under resting conditions. Include correct units in your answer. (3 marks)

**(b)** During strenuous exercise, the cardiac output increases and blood flow through pulmonary capillaries speeds up. This reduces the partial pressure of O₂ in capillary blood from 40 mm Hg to 25 mm Hg, increasing the partial pressure gradient to 75 mm Hg. At the same time, the effective alveolar surface area increases to 90 m² as more capillaries are recruited. Recalculate the diffusion rate and determine the percentage increase compared to rest. (3 marks)

**(c)** In a patient with pulmonary fibrosis, the diffusion distance increases from 0.5 μm to 2.5 μm due to tissue scarring, and the effective surface area decreases by 30%. Assuming the partial pressure gradient remains at 60 mm Hg, calculate the new diffusion rate as a percentage of the normal rate. Explain why patients with pulmonary fibrosis experience shortness of breath even at rest. (4 marks)

## Problem 1: Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio (10 marks)

**a) SA, V, and SA:V ratio calculations (3 marks)**

Cell A: SA = 4π(20)² = 5027 μm²; V = (4/3)π(20)³ = 33,510 μm³; SA:V = 0.15 μm²/μm³ (or 1:6.7)

Cell B: SA = 4π(40)² = 20,106 μm²; V = (4/3)π(40)³ = 268,083 μm³; SA:V = 0.075 μm²/μm³ (or 1:13.3)

Cell C: SA = 4π(80)² = 80,425 μm²; V = (4/3)π(80)³ = 2,144,661 μm³; SA:V = 0.0375 μm²/μm³ (or 1:26.7)

**b) Factor analysis (3 marks)**

Volume increases by 2³ = 8 times (from A to B). Surface area increases by 2² = 4 times. (2 marks)

This creates a diffusion problem because: metabolic demand (and therefore O₂ requirement) scales with volume (×8), but the surface area for diffusion only scales with surface area (×4). The surface area does not increase proportionally to meet the increased demand. As cells get larger, the relative surface area available for diffusion becomes insufficient. (1 mark)

**c) Minimum surface area calculation (4 marks)**

Required SA = (8×10⁻¹⁵ mol/s) / (2×10⁻¹⁸ mol/μm²/s) = 4000 μm² (2 marks)

Actual SA of Cell C = 80,425 μm². Yes, Cell C has sufficient surface area (80,425 > 4,000). (1 mark) However, in a multicellular tissue where many cells are internal and not directly exposed to the external environment, individual cells cannot rely on direct diffusion — specialised transport and exchange systems are needed. (1 mark)

## Problem 2: Fick's Law Application (10 marks)

**a) Resting diffusion rate (3 marks)**

Rate = (8.5×10⁵ cm² × 60 mm Hg × 2.5×10⁻⁶ cm²/s/mm Hg) / (5×10⁻⁵ cm)

Rate = (127.5) / (5×10⁻⁵) = 2.55×10⁶ cm³/s = 2.55×10⁶ / 22,400 mol/s ≈ 114 mol/s (or appropriate unit conversion)

(Full marks for correct substitution and calculation with correct units; accept answers in mL O₂/s or other valid units)

**b) Exercise diffusion rate (3 marks)**

New Rate = (9.0×10⁵ cm² × 75 mm Hg × 2.5×10⁻⁶) / (5×10⁻⁵) = 3.375×10⁶ (arbitrary diffusion units)

Percentage increase = ((3.375 - 2.55) / 2.55) × 100% = 32.4% increase (2 marks for correct calculation, 1 mark for correct method)

**c) Pulmonary fibrosis analysis (4 marks)**

New SA = 85 × 0.70 = 59.5 m²; New distance = 2.5 μm (×5 increase)

New Rate / Normal Rate = (0.70 × 1) / 5 = 0.14 = 14% (2 marks)

The diffusion rate drops to only 14% of normal because: (i) surface area is reduced by 30%, and (ii) the diffusion distance increases 5-fold. Since rate is inversely proportional to distance (Fick's Law), the 5-fold increase in distance causes a 5-fold decrease in rate. Combined, the effect is multiplicative: 0.70 × (1/5) = 0.14. This severely limits O₂ uptake, causing dyspnoea (shortness of breath) even at rest. (2 marks)

## Problem 3: Experimental Design (10 marks)

**a) Hypothesis (1 mark)**

As temperature increases, the rate of CO₂ diffusion across the dialysis membrane increases, because higher temperature increases the kinetic energy of gas molecules.

**b) Variables (4 marks)**

Independent variable: Temperature of the water bath (e.g., 5°C, 15°C, 25°C, 35°C, 45°C). (1 mark)

Dependent variable: Rate of CO₂ diffusion, measured as the change in CO₂ concentration inside the dialysis bag over time (or rate of pH change, since CO₂ forms carbonic acid). (1 mark)

Controlled variables (any 3, with reasons): (2 marks)

1. Volume of air/CO₂ inside the dialysis bag — affects the initial concentration gradient
2. Surface area of dialysis tubing — directly affects diffusion rate (Fick's Law)
3. Volume and initial CO₂ concentration of water — affects the partial pressure gradient
4. Time of measurement — ensures comparable data across replicates

**c) Method (5 marks)**

Award marks for any 5 of the following methodological points:

1. Set up 5 water baths at different temperatures (5, 15, 25, 35, 45°C) with thermometers to monitor temperature
2. Prepare identical dialysis tubing bags (same length, diameter = same SA) filled with the same volume of air containing a known CO₂ concentration (e.g., from a CO₂ cylinder or dry ice)
3. Seal the bags and submerge one in each water bath for a set equilibration period (e.g., 10 minutes)
4. Insert a CO₂ probe or pH meter into each bag and record initial and final readings at set time intervals (e.g., every 2 minutes for 10 minutes)
5. Calculate the rate of change (ΔCO₂ concentration / time or ΔpH / time) for each temperature
6. Repeat each temperature condition at least 3 times (n=3) to ensure reliability and enable calculation of mean and standard deviation
7. Plot a graph of diffusion rate vs. temperature and perform statistical analysis

## Problem 4: Comparative Analysis (10 marks)

**a) SA per gram calculations (3 marks)**

| **Parameter** | **Earthworm** | **Frog** | **Human** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| SA per gram | 10 cm²/g | 6 cm²/g | 0.0012 cm²/g (or 1.2 mm²/g) |

Earthworm: 0.05 m²/5g = 0.01 m²/g = 10 cm²/g; Frog: 0.3/50 = 0.006 m²/g = 6 cm²/g; Human: 85/70000 = 0.0012 m²/g = 12 mm²/g

**b) Explanation using Fick's Law (4 marks)**

Earthworms: The SA per gram is very high (10 cm²/g), meaning they have a relatively large surface area compared to their metabolic needs. Their body is small and flat, so internal cells are close to the external environment. Despite the large diffusion distance (200 μm), the low metabolic rate per gram and direct exposure to moist soil allow sufficient O₂ diffusion. (2 marks)

Humans: The SA per gram is extremely low (0.0012 cm²/g). If humans relied on skin diffusion alone, the surface area would be completely inadequate for our high metabolic rate. The lung solves this by having an enormous internal surface area (85 m², comparable to a tennis court) packed into a small thoracic volume through branching and folding (alveoli). The diffusion distance is minimised to 0.5 μm, and the partial pressure gradient is maintained by ventilation and circulation. (2 marks)

**c) Lung-to-skin diffusion rate ratio (3 marks)**

Rate is proportional to (SA × gradient) / distance.

Lung rate ∝ (0.3 × 60) / 5 = 3.6 (arbitrary units)

Skin rate ∝ (0.2 × 15) / 50 = 0.06 (arbitrary units)

Ratio = 3.6 / 0.06 = 60:1. The lung is approximately 60 times more efficient at gas exchange than the skin under these conditions. (1 mark for each calculation step)

## Problem 5: Plant Stomata and Trade-offs (10 marks)

**a) Stomatal pore area (2 marks)**

Number of stomata = 150 × 5000 = 750,000 stomata

Total pore area = 750,000 × 100 μm² = 75×10⁶ μm² = 75 mm² = 0.75 cm²

Percentage of leaf surface = (75 mm² / 5000 mm²) × 100% = 1.5% (1 mark for calculation, 1 mark for correct answer)

**b) Spongy mesophyll function (2 marks)**

The spongy mesophyll increases surface area by a factor of 30 (Fick's Law: rate ∝ SA). Total internal SA = 30 × 50 cm² × 2 (both sides) = 3000 cm². This massive increase in surface area compensates for the relatively small stomatal opening area, allowing efficient CO₂ absorption. (1 mark for explanation, 1 mark for calculation)

**c) Percentage change in diffusion rate (3 marks)**

New stomatal area = 75 mm² × 0.40 = 30 mm²; New distance = 25 μm

Rate change factor = (0.40) × (0.5/2.5) = 0.40 × 0.6 = 0.24 of original (or: SA factor = 0.40, distance factor = 15/25 = 0.6, combined = 0.40 × 0.6 = 0.24)

Percentage change = (0.24 - 1) × 100% = -76% (or 76% decrease). CO₂ diffusion rate decreases by 76%. (2 marks for correct method, 1 mark for final answer)

**d) Trade-off and adaptations (3 marks)**

Trade-off: Open stomata allow CO₂ entry for photosynthesis but also cause water loss through transpiration. In hot, dry conditions, the plant must balance carbon gain against water conservation to avoid wilting. (1 mark)

Two adaptations (any 2): (2 marks)

1. Sunken stomata: Stomata located in pits or grooves reduce the wind speed at the pore surface, reducing transpiration while still allowing CO₂ diffusion.
2. Waxy cuticle: A thick, waterproof cuticle on the leaf epidermis reduces water loss from non-stomatal surfaces.
3. CAM photosynthesis: Plants open stomata at night (cooler, more humid) to fix CO₂, then close them during the day — temporal separation of CO₂ uptake and photosynthesis.
4. Hairy leaves: Trichomes trap moist air near the leaf surface, reducing the water potential gradient and thus transpiration rate.
