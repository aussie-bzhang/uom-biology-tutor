# Practice Set 1 - Foundations: mutation, variation and classification

4. [Application: classification] A mutation occurs in a skin cell after fertilisation and affects a pigmentation gene. Classify it as somatic or germ-line, and state whether it is likely to be passed to offspring.

5. [Application: experimental reasoning] A bacterial culture is exposed to ultraviolet radiation. Explain what type of DNA damage UV can cause and propose one simple comparison group for testing whether UV increased mutation frequency.

# Answer Key

## Practice Set 1

### A. Short-answer questions

**1.** A mutation is a change in the sequence, structure or composition of an organism's genome. Mutations are the primary source of genetic variation because they create new DNA sequence variants and chromosomal changes that can be inherited or selected.

**2.** Spontaneous mutations arise without deliberate external mutagen exposure, for example DNA replication errors or deamination of cytosine. Induced mutations are caused by mutagens such as nitrous acid, benzopyrene, X-rays, gamma-rays or ultraviolet radiation.

**3.** 3 billion nucleotides / 1 billion nucleotides per mutation = at most about 3 replication errors, before considering DNA repair.

**4.** It is a somatic mutation because it occurs in a body cell after fertilisation. It is not normally passed to offspring because gametes do not carry that skin-cell mutation.

**5.** UV radiation can cause covalent bonds between adjacent thymine bases. A suitable comparison is an otherwise identical bacterial culture not exposed to UV, then compare mutation frequency or mutant colony counts between treated and control groups.

### B. Yes/No questions

1. Yes, 2. No, 3. Yes, 4. No, 5. Yes

### C. Multiple-choice questions

1. B, 2. A, 3. A, 4. A, 5. A, 6. A, 7. A, 8. A, 9. C, 10. A

## Practice Set 2

### A. Short-answer questions

**1.** A silent mutation changes DNA but not the amino-acid sequence. A missense mutation changes one amino acid. A nonsense mutation creates a premature stop codon and usually shortens the polypeptide.

**2.** Translation reads mRNA in triplets. If the inserted or deleted number of nucleotides is not a multiple of three, all downstream codons are regrouped, changing many amino acids and often creating an early stop codon.

**3.** The coding DNA codon changes from TGC, which corresponds to cysteine, to TGA, a stop codon. This is a nonsense mutation causing premature termination after the first codon, producing a shortened polypeptide.

**4.** This is a silent/synonymous mutation because both GAA and GAG encode glutamic acid. The polypeptide sequence is unchanged, so the likely direct functional effect is no effect, although rare expression-level effects are possible in real systems.

**5.** A one-nucleotide deletion changes the reading frame, so many downstream amino acids can be changed and a premature stop codon may appear. A single substitution often changes only one codon and may be silent, conservative or missense.

## Practice Set 3

### A. Short-answer questions

**1.** A loss-of-function mutation reduces or abolishes normal gene product function or expression. A gain-of-function mutation gives a gene product a new activity, inappropriate activity or altered expression that creates an additional functional effect.

**2.** Many mutations occur in locations or codons that do not strongly affect gene function, so they are neutral. Harmful outcomes are more common than beneficial ones because disrupting an existing functional system is usually easier than creating a useful new function.

**3.** This is likely loss-of-function because the enzyme activity is lost. If the enzyme is needed for a pathway, the phenotype may change because pathway output is reduced or absent.

**4.** This is best classified as gain-of-function or altered expression/regulation, because the cell-cycle gene remains active when it should be switched off. It may strongly affect cell behaviour and survival.

**5.** Likely lowest: (i), because an intergenic SSLP expansion may be neutral, although it is germ-line and could be inherited. Middle: (ii), because it affects only a skin cell lineage and is not normally inherited, though local effects are possible. Highest: (iii), because failure to turn off a cell-cycle gene can seriously disrupt cell division and survival.
