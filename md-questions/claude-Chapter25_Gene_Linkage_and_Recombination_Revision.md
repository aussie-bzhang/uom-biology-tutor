**Key idea for this chapter:** Genes on the same chromosome are **linked** and do not assort independently — **parental** allele combinations outnumber **recombinant** ones. Recombinants arise from **crossing over (chiasmata)** in Prophase I. The recombination frequency, **RF = (recombinant gametes / total) × 100**, is a proxy for the distance between loci (1% RF = 1 map unit = 1 centimorgan, cM). RF rises with distance up to a ceiling of **50%** (the value for unlinked genes). Map distances are additive, so test-cross data can be assembled into a linkage map.

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** Unlinked loci (different chromosomes): all four gamete types in 1:1:1:1. Linked loci (same chromosome): the four types are unequal — parental combinations are over-represented and recombinants are rare.

**2.** Parental gametes carry the allele combinations that entered the cross together (e.g. AB and ab); recombinant gametes carry new combinations (Ab and aB). When linked, parental types are more frequent because the two loci tend to travel together unless a crossover occurs between them.

**3.** During Prophase I, non-sister chromatids of paired homologues exchange segments at a chiasma (crossing over). A crossover between two loci swaps the alleles distal to it, converting parental chromatids into recombinant ones (e.g. AB and ab → Ab and aB).

**4.** The closer two loci are, the smaller the chance a crossover falls between them, so recombinants are rare and linkage is tight. The farther apart, the more often a crossover occurs between them, so recombination frequency rises (up to the 50% ceiling).

**5.** X-linked genes in males are hemizygous (one X, plus Y): a male’s single maternal X directly displays whichever allele combination it carries, with no second allele to mask it. So scoring F2 males reads the F1 female’s gametes directly, making parental vs recombinant classes easy to count.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** RF = (number of recombinant gametes/offspring ÷ total gametes/offspring) × 100. ‘Recombinant’ = offspring carrying new allele combinations; ‘total’ = all offspring scored. Map distance is expressed in map units (m.u.) or centimorgans (cM), where 1% recombination = 1 cM.

**2.** The chance of a crossover between two loci increases with the physical distance between them, so RF tracks distance. RF maxes out at 50%, the value for loci so far apart (or on different chromosomes) that they assort independently.

**3.** Cis: the two dominant alleles are on one homologue and the two recessives on the other (AB/ab) — parental gametes AB and ab. Trans: each homologue carries one dominant and one recessive (Ab/aB) — parental gametes Ab and aB.

**4.** Map distance reflects the physical separation of the loci, which is unchanged by which alleles happen to be together. Switching cis↔trans only swaps WHICH classes count as parental vs recombinant; the FREQUENCY of recombinants (hence RF) is the same.

**5.** The recessive tester contributes only recessive alleles, so it cannot mask anything: every offspring’s phenotype is a direct readout of the gamete the dihybrid contributed. This lets you count parental and recombinant gametes directly.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** Yes.

**3.** Yes.

**4.** No — RF cannot exceed 50%.

**5.** No — the map distance is unchanged.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** Measure pairwise recombination frequencies between loci by test crosses; convert each RF to a distance (1% = 1 cM); then arrange the loci in a line so that the distances are mutually consistent (using additivity and the order implied by which pairs are closest), giving a single linear map.

**2.** Additivity: the recombination distance between two outer loci equals the sum of the distances between intervening loci. E.g. w–r = w–m + m–r, so the position of each locus can be fixed relative to the others.

**3.** (i) Convert map distance to RF (cM → % recombinants). (ii) Split recombinants equally between the two recombinant gamete types and split the remaining (parental) gametes equally between the two parental types. (iii) Use the F1 arrangement (cis/trans) to decide WHICH allele combinations are parental vs recombinant. (iv) Multiply each gamete frequency by the offspring total.

**4.** Over long distances, double (and multiple) crossovers can restore parental arrangements and go undetected, so the observed RF underestimates the true distance and saturates near 50%. This is overcome by summing several SHORT intervals between closely spaced markers (additive mapping).

**5.** Trans, 20 cM: parental gametes Ab and aB (each 40%); recombinant gametes AB and ab (each 10%).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** Yes.

**3.** Yes — 12 cM means 88% parental, i.e. 44% each.

**4.** Yes — in trans the F1 is Ab/aB, so the PARENTAL gametes are Ab and aB; the recombinants are therefore AB (two dominants together) and ab (two recessives together).

**5.** Yes — 40 m.u. is still linkage (below the 50% independent-assortment limit), though weak.
