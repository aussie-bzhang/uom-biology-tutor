# Chapter 21 — Understanding How Genes Influence Traits

*Topic 3: Genes and Heredity | Week 7, Lecture 21 (Genetic Influence & Phenotype) | University of Melbourne, School of BioSciences*

Three sets of review questions (English). Each set: 5 Short-Answer + 5 Yes/No + 10 Multiple-Choice + 3 Applied (genetics-problem / reasoning) questions. Answer key for all three sets is at the end of the document.

*Key idea: A locus is the position of a gene; an allele is a variant form of the sequence at that locus. A diploid (2n) carries TWO copies of every autosomal locus, so its genotype at a locus is homozygous (AA, aa), heterozygous (Aa), or — for an X-linked gene in an XY individual — hemizygous (one copy only). The genotype produces a gene product, and the gene product (plus environment) produces the phenotype. The heterozygote (Aa) is the test case that tells you the type of dominance: complete dominance = one allele's phenotype only; incomplete dominance = an intermediate/blended phenotype; codominance = BOTH alleles' phenotypes shown together. Dominant alleles are written with a CAPITAL letter.*

*Concise model answers. Students should phrase short-answer responses in their own words; the points below are what each answer must contain.*

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** Ploidy = the number of complete sets of chromosomes in a cell. Haploid (n) = one set; diploid (2n) = two sets (one maternal, one paternal). Ploidy counts SETS, not the number of different chromosome types: whether an organism has 2 or 23 different chromosomes, being diploid simply means it has two of each, so ploidy is independent of chromosome-type number.

**2.** DNA replication copies the DNA so each chromosome ends up as two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. The amount of DNA doubles, but the chromosome NUMBER does not change — a chromosome with two chromatids is still counted as one chromosome until the chromatids separate at cell division. So pre- and post-replication the count is the same (e.g. 2 → still 2; 4 → still 4).

**3.** Locus = the specific location of a gene/DNA sequence on a chromosome. Allele = a variant form of the sequence at that locus. Many alleles can exist in a population because mutation generates new variants over time; but a diploid has only two homologous chromosomes, so any one individual carries at most two alleles (one per homologue) at a given locus.

**4.** Silent: base change does not alter the encoded amino acid (often in the 3rd codon position) → protein UNCHANGED. Missense: base change alters one amino acid → protein changed (function may be altered or retained). Indel: insertion/deletion; if not a multiple of three it causes a frameshift → protein usually drastically changed/non-functional. Each is a different DNA sequence at Locus A, hence a different allele.

**5.** A microsatellite (SSLP) locus varies in the NUMBER of short tandem repeats (e.g. [GCT]3 vs [GCT]7 vs [GCT]10). These differences are real genetic variation (different alleles), but the locus is non-coding, so the variation does not change a protein/gene product. This shows variation can be neutral with respect to gene products yet still useful (e.g. as DNA-fingerprinting markers).

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — haploid = one set of chromosomes.

**2.** Yes — a replicated chromosome = two sister chromatids joined at the centromere.

**3.** No — replication doubles the DNA/chromatids but the chromosome NUMBER stays the same.

**4.** No — homologues carry the same loci but may carry different alleles; they are non-identical.

**5.** Yes — e.g. a functional vs a non-functional (or altered) protein from different alleles.

### Part C. Multiple-Choice

**1.** B — number of complete chromosome sets.

**2.** B — 2n with two types = 4 chromosomes before replication.

**3.** C — still 4 chromosomes, each now with two sister chromatids.

**4.** B — matching pair, same loci, not necessarily same alleles.

**5.** B — a locus is a location.

**6.** B — same position on both homologues.

**7.** B — a variant form at a locus.

**8.** B — silent mutation.

**9.** B — differ in repeat number.

**10.** B — many in a population, at most two per diploid individual.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) 2n = 6 → 6 chromosomes before replication. (b) After replication still 6 chromosomes, but each has 2 chromatids → 12 chromatids in total. (c) Yes, still diploid: ploidy counts chromosome SETS (still two sets of 3), and replication does not change set number — it only duplicates each chromosome into sister chromatids.

**2.** a1 (silent): protein UNCHANGED → function unchanged. a2 (missense in active site): protein changed (one amino acid in the active site) → function likely LOST or strongly altered, because active-site residues are critical for catalysis. a3 (1-nt insertion): causes a FRAMESHIFT → downstream sequence and likely a premature stop → protein drastically changed → function lost. Justification rests on whether/how the change reaches the protein and whether it hits a functionally critical region.

**3.** (a) Longer = slower = higher on the gel. From top to bottom: [GCT]10 (top), [GCT]7 (middle), [GCT]3 (bottom). (b) Two bands = two different-length alleles = the individual is HETEROZYGOUS at this locus (one band would mean homozygous). (c) No protein change expected: a microsatellite is non-coding, so repeat-length differences do not alter a gene product (they change fragment length only).

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** Homozygous = the two alleles at a locus are the SAME (AA or aa). Heterozygous = the two alleles are DIFFERENT (Aa). ‘Homo’ = same; ‘hetero’ = different. A diploid has one allele on each homologue, so it is one of these three states (AA, aa, Aa) at any autosomal locus.

**2.** Hemizygous = having only ONE copy of a locus instead of two. An XY male has a single X chromosome, so for most X-linked genes (which have no counterpart on the small Y) he carries only one allele — hemizygous. The asterisk in ‘every\* locus’ flags this exception: the ‘two copies of every locus’ rule does not hold for X-linked (or Y-linked) loci in individuals with two different sex chromosomes.

**3.** XᴰY is HEMIZYGOUS at that X-linked locus (only one copy). A recessive X-linked allele is expressed more readily in XY individuals because there is no second X to carry a dominant functional allele that could mask it; in XX individuals a single recessive allele can be masked by a dominant allele on the other X.

**4.** Genotype = the genetic composition (alleles carried). Phenotype = a distinguishable, directly or indirectly observable/measurable biological trait. Genotype → gene product (protein) → phenotype. It is simplest in haploids because there is only ONE allele per locus, so each genotype maps directly to one gene product and one phenotype, with no dominance/masking by a second allele.

**5.** No — the genotype is still ‘Aa’. DNA replication increases the number of DNA copies/chromatids at the locus (each homologue now has two chromatids), but it does not change which ALLELES are present: there is still one A and one a. Genotype describes allele identity, not DNA copy number.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** Dominant = the allele whose phenotype is seen in the heterozygote; recessive = the allele whose phenotype is masked in the heterozygote. BB (black) × bb (brown) → F1 Bb is black, so BLACK is dominant and brown recessive. F1 genotype = Bb. Convention: the dominant allele is written with a CAPITAL letter (B), the recessive with the lower-case (b).

**2.** Incomplete dominance: R/R (red) × r/r (white) → R/r (pink), an intermediate phenotype. Molecularly, R encodes a functional pigment-producing allele; the heterozygote has only ONE ‘dose’ of R (vs two in R/R and none in r/r), so it makes an intermediate amount of pigment → pink. The phenotype is a blend reflecting allele dosage, not two separate colours.

**3.** Codominance: CᴿCᴿ (red) × CᵂCᵂ (white) → CᴿCᵂ (roan), which shows BOTH red and white hairs at the same time. It differs from incomplete dominance because the two phenotypes are seen SEPARATELY and simultaneously (red hairs + white hairs), rather than blended into a single intermediate (which would be pink-coated).

**4.** Iᴬ and Iᴮ are CODOMINANT with each other (genotype IᴬIᴮ gives group AB, showing both A and B antigens); i is RECESSIVE to both. Genotypes: A = IᴬIᴬ or Iᴬi; B = IᴮIᴮ or Iᴮi; AB = IᴬIᴮ; O = ii.

**5.** A dominance series is a ranked order of dominance among multiple alleles of one gene: C > cᵏ > cᵈ > cᵃ. Each allele is dominant to those listed after it. Albino (cᵃ) is the most recessive, so it is masked by any other allele; albino is therefore only seen when BOTH alleles are cᵃ — hence a single possible genotype (cᵃcᵃ). Black (C) is dominant to all, so it has the most possible genotypes.

**2.** Yes — incomplete dominance gives an intermediate heterozygote.

**3.** Yes — in codominance both alleles’ phenotypes are visible together.

**4.** Yes — group O is only ii.

**5.** Yes — black = CC, Ccᵏ, Ccᵈ or Ccᵃ, so it has more than one genotype.

**3.** Cross 1 = COMPLETE dominance (F1 shows only one parental phenotype → one allele masks the other); F2 ratio ≈ 3 full-colour : 1 no-colour.
