## Revision Set 1 — Forms of Energy and the Laws of Thermodynamics

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Energy is the capacity to cause change / the capacity to do work (work occurs when a force moves an object over a distance). Bioenergetics is the study of how organisms manage their energy resources.

**2.** Potential energy is the energy of state or position (stored energy) — e.g. energy stored in covalent bonds of glucose, in a concentration gradient, or in a charge imbalance. Kinetic energy is the energy of movement, the type that does work and makes things change — e.g. the heat that drives molecular motion, or a muscle/cell in motion.

**3.** The five forms: chemical-bond, electrical, heat, light, mechanical. Examples (any two): chemical-bond = energy in covalent bonds released during hydrolysis of polymers; electrical = electrical gradients across membranes driving ion movement through channels; heat = released by chemical reactions, altering body temperature; light = electromagnetic radiation captured by eye pigments and plant pigments in photosynthesis; mechanical = energy of motion used in muscle and intracellular movements.

**4.** First law: the energy of the universe is constant — energy can be transferred and transformed, but it cannot be created or destroyed. It is also called the principle of conservation of energy.

**5.** Second law: every energy transfer or transformation increases the entropy (disorder) of the universe; some energy is always made unusable (often lost as heat). A cell can decrease its own entropy by building ordered structures because it does so at the expense of a larger increase in the entropy of its surroundings (e.g. heat and small waste molecules released). Entropy may decrease in the organism, but the TOTAL entropy of the universe still increases — so order in cells does not violate the second law.

### B. Yes/No

**1.** No — energy can be transferred and transformed but cannot be created or destroyed.

**2.** Yes — organisms are open systems (they exchange both energy and matter with their surroundings).

**3.** Yes — every energy transfer or transformation increases the entropy of the universe.

**4.** No — chemical-bond energy is stored energy, i.e. potential energy.

**5.** No — it does not violate the second law; local order increases the disorder of the surroundings, so the universe's total entropy still rises.

### C. MCQ

**1.** A (the capacity to cause change / to do work).

**2.** B (potential energy).

**3.** C (kinetic energy).

**4.** B (the principle of conservation of energy).

**5.** C (disorder).

**6.** B (isolated from its surroundings).

**7.** D (light).

**8.** C (lost as heat / made unusable).

**9.** A (the study of how organisms manage their energy resources).

**10.** B (electromagnetic radiation stored as photons).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) PE = m\*g\*h = 4.0 x 9.81 x 0.80 = 31.4 J (about 31 J of work against gravity). (b) No, they are not the same. The CHEMICAL energy stored in the muscles is the same regardless of location (it is stored chemical-bond/potential energy). But the KINETIC/mechanical work needed to rise to the desk height depends on gravity through PE = m\*g\*h: as g falls toward zero in space, the gravitational PE to overcome becomes tiny, so far less work is needed — using the same muscular push the cat would massively overshoot. (c) For the lioness on Earth the energy she must expend to climb depends on her MASS (m) and the HEIGHT she climbs (h) (with g fixed at 9.81 m/s^2): a heavier cat or a taller tree means more gravitational PE, hence more chemical energy converted to mechanical work.

**2.** (a) Grows on the tree: LIGHT energy from the sun is captured by chlorophyll and converted (via photosynthesis) into CHEMICAL-BOND (potential) energy stored in sugars. (b) Falls to the ground: stored gravitational POTENTIAL energy is converted into KINETIC (mechanical) energy of motion as it falls; on impact some is converted to heat (and sound). (c) Digested by the astronaut: CHEMICAL-BOND potential energy in the sugars is released by catabolism / cellular respiration and captured in ATP (chemical energy), which is then used for mechanical work, active transport, etc.; as required by the second law, some energy is lost as HEAT at each step.

## Revision Set 2 — ATP and Free Energy

### A. Short-answer

**1.** ATP (adenosine triphosphate) consists of the base adenine, the sugar ribose, and a chain of three phosphate groups. Hydrolysis: ATP + H2O -> ADP + Pi (inorganic phosphate) + energy. (ATP can also phosphorylate other molecules by donating a phosphate group.)

**2.** Gibbs free energy (G) is the amount of energy available to do work (usable energy). The free-energy change of a reaction is delta-G = G(products) - G(reactants). delta-G < 0 means free energy is released by the reaction; delta-G > 0 means free energy must be supplied for the reaction to occur.

**3.** Exergonic: delta-G < 0 (negative), releases free energy, spontaneous — e.g. glucose + O2 -> CO2 + H2O (delta-G = -2870 kJ/mol), or ATP hydrolysis. Endergonic: delta-G > 0 (positive), absorbs free energy, nonspontaneous — e.g. dissolving salt in water, or building sucrose from glucose + fructose.

**4.** Free energy measures the instability of a system — its tendency to change to a more stable state. During a spontaneous change, free energy decreases and stability increases. Equilibrium is the state of maximum stability (lowest free energy). A process is spontaneous and can perform work only when it is moving toward equilibrium; at equilibrium it can do no work.

**5.** Chemical work (e.g. ATP phosphorylates a reactant such as glutamic acid or glucose, pushing an otherwise endergonic synthesis forward) and kinetic work, which includes transport work (ATP phosphorylates transport proteins, changing their shape to move solutes) and mechanical work (ATP binds and is hydrolysed by motor proteins, changing their shape so they 'walk' along cytoskeletal tracks).

### B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — ATP hydrolysis is exergonic (delta-G is negative, about -7.3 kcal/mol).

**2.** No — a positive delta-G means the reaction REQUIRES (absorbs) free energy; it does not release it.

**3.** Yes — forming ATP from ADP + Pi requires energy, so it is endergonic.

**4.** Yes — a process can perform work only while it is moving toward equilibrium.

**5.** Yes — about -7.3 kcal/mol (equivalently about -30.5 kJ/mol).

### C. MCQ

**1.** A (adenosine triphosphate).

**2.** B (ADP + Pi, releasing energy).

**3.** B (-7.3 kcal/mol, about -30.5 kJ/mol).

**4.** B (exergonic and spontaneous).

**5.** B (the amount of energy available to do work).

**6.** B (G(products) - G(reactants)).

**7.** B (absorbs free energy and is nonspontaneous).

**8.** C (maximum stability / minimum free energy).

**9.** B (transport / kinetic work).

**10.** B (exergonic).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) No. On its own, glucose + Pi -> G6P has delta-G = +4.0 kcal/mol (positive), so it is endergonic and nonspontaneous. (b) Net delta-G = (+4.0) + (-7.3) = -3.3 kcal/mol. Because the net delta-G is negative, the coupled reaction is exergonic and therefore spontaneous (it proceeds toward completion). (c) -7.3 kcal/mol x 4.184 kJ/kcal = -30.5 kJ/mol, which matches the stated value, so the two figures are consistent.

**2.** (a) R1: delta-G = -15 kJ/mol -> exergonic and spontaneous; it does not need ATP coupling (it already proceeds, and could itself supply energy). (b) R2: delta-G = +10 kJ/mol -> endergonic and nonspontaneous on its own; coupling to one ATP hydrolysis (-30.5 kJ/mol) gives net +10 + (-30.5) = -20.5 kJ/mol, which is negative, so one ATP is more than enough to make it spontaneous. (c) R3: delta-G = +45 kJ/mol -> endergonic and nonspontaneous; one ATP gives net +45 + (-30.5) = +14.5 kJ/mol, still positive, so a single ATP hydrolysis is NOT enough — more than one ATP (or another energy source) would be needed.

## Revision Set 3 — Metabolism: Anabolism, Catabolism, Coupling and Redox

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Anabolic reactions link smaller molecules to build larger, more complex ones; they require/consume free energy (delta-G > 0) and are endergonic — e.g. synthesis of sucrose from glucose and fructose. Catabolic reactions break larger, complex molecules into smaller ones and usually release energy (delta-G < 0) and are exergonic — e.g. hydrolysis of sucrose into glucose and fructose.

**2.** Coupling means using the free energy released by an exergonic reaction to drive an otherwise nonspontaneous endergonic reaction. ATP is the central coupling agent: its formation (from ADP + Pi) is endergonic and stores energy from exergonic processes (cell respiration, catabolism), while its hydrolysis is exergonic and supplies energy to endergonic processes (active transport, cell movement, anabolism). This coupling is very common in metabolism.

**3.** Oxidation is the LOSS of electrons (it may also involve the addition of oxygen or the loss of hydrogen). Reduction is the GAIN of electrons (it may also involve the loss of oxygen or the gain of hydrogen). (Memory aid: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.)

**4.** If A passes electrons to B: A loses electrons, so A is OXIDISED and acts as the reducing agent; B gains electrons, so B is REDUCED and acts as the oxidising agent.

**5.** Redox reactions transfer electrons, and the movement of electrons releases energy that cells capture (e.g. to make ATP). They are central to cellular respiration (oxidation of glucose, electrons passed to carriers and ultimately to oxygen) and to photosynthesis (reduction of CO2 to sugar) — so they are 'everywhere in biology'.

### B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — breaking sucrose into glucose and fructose breaks a larger molecule into smaller ones, so it is catabolic.

**2.** No — anabolic reactions are endergonic and have a POSITIVE delta-G.

**3.** Yes — oxidation is the loss of electrons.

**4.** Yes — gaining electrons is reduction.

**5.** Yes — ATP couples exergonic and endergonic reactions in metabolism.

### C. MCQ

**1.** B (break down molecules and release energy; exergonic).

**2.** B (build larger molecules and require energy; endergonic).

**3.** B (anabolic and endergonic).

**4.** B (loss of electrons).

**5.** B (gain of electrons).

**6.** B (donates electrons and is itself oxidised).

**7.** B (endergonic).

**8.** B (loss of hydrogen / addition of oxygen).

**9.** A (couple an exergonic reaction to an endergonic one).

**10.** B (catabolic and exergonic).

### D. Applied

**1.** Sucrose + H2O -> glucose + fructose: (a) delta-G is NEGATIVE (-). (b) Spontaneous. (c) Neither oxidation nor reduction — it is a hydrolysis reaction; no electrons are transferred between the products (carbon oxidation states are unchanged). (d) Exergonic (energy is released). (e) Catabolic (a larger molecule is broken into smaller ones). Brief explanation: water is added to split the glycosidic bond, releasing free energy, so the reaction proceeds on its own.

**2.** catechol + 1/2 O2 -> o-quinone + H2O: (a) Catechol is OXIDISED — it loses hydrogen atoms (and the associated electrons) as the two -OH groups become two C=O groups; loss of hydrogen/electrons indicates oxidation. (b) Oxygen (O2) is the oxidising agent — it accepts the electrons/hydrogens and is reduced to water. (c) The reaction is exergonic (delta-G negative) and spontaneous — which is why a cut apple browns by itself once exposed to air. (d) Polyphenol oxidase is an enzyme (a catalyst); it speeds the reaction up by lowering the activation energy but does NOT change the delta-G of the reaction (the energy difference between reactants and products is unchanged).
