## Section A — Biomolecules, Scale & Microscopy (Q1–Q6)

1. **Magnification & scale.** A mitochondrion measures 50 mm long in an electron micrograph taken at ×50,000. (a) Calculate its true length in µm. (b) A ribosome is 0.02 µm across; how large would it appear (in mm) on a print at the same magnification? (c) Explain quantitatively why a light microscope (resolution ≈ 200 nm) could not clearly resolve the ribosome but an electron microscope can.
2. **Peptide-bond bookkeeping.** A single polypeptide is 150 amino acids long. (a) How many peptide bonds does it contain? (b) How many molecules of water were released when it was assembled by condensation? (c) Taking the average mass of an amino acid residue as 110 Da, estimate the molecular mass of the polypeptide in kDa, and explain why you use the residue mass (not the free-amino-acid mass) in this calculation.
3. **Chargaff base composition.** A sample of double-stranded DNA is found to contain 20% adenine. (a) Calculate the percentage of thymine, guanine and cytosine. (b) A single strand of a different DNA is found to be 18% A and 22% T; explain why Chargaff’s rule (A = T, G = C) holds for the whole duplex but need not hold within one strand. (c) Outline an experiment (hydrolysis + separation) you could use to measure the base composition of an unknown DNA sample.
4. **DNA length & coding capacity.** A gene is 3,000 base pairs long. (a) How many nucleotides does the double-stranded gene contain in total? (b) Given that each base pair adds about 0.34 nm to the length of the helix, calculate the length of this gene in µm. (c) Calculate the maximum number of amino acids this sequence could encode, and explain why the real protein is usually shorter.
5. **Condensation & hydrolysis of carbohydrates.** (a) When n glucose monomers join to form one polysaccharide chain, derive a general expression for the number of water molecules released, and evaluate it for a chain of 1,000 glucose units. (b) A starch molecule built from 500 glucose residues is fully hydrolysed; calculate the total mass of water consumed (take the mass of one water molecule as 18 Da). (c) Describe how you would use Benedict’s reagent semi-quantitatively to compare the reducing-sugar content of two solutions.
6. **Lipid saturation.** (a) A triglyceride is assembled from one glycerol and three fatty acids; state how many ester bonds form and how many water molecules are released. (b) The ‘iodine number’ measures how much iodine a fat absorbs, and iodine adds across C=C double bonds. Oil A absorbs twice as much iodine per gram as oil B; what does this tell you about their relative numbers of double bonds, and which is likely to be liquid at room temperature? (c) Design a fair experiment to compare the melting points of a saturated and an unsaturated fat.

(c) The rate measured at 60 °C is far lower than your prediction; explain biochemically why.

5. **Water potential & osmosis.** A plant cell has a solute potential (Ψs) of −0.8 MPa and a pressure potential (Ψp) of +0.3 MPa. (a) Calculate the cell’s water potential (Ψ = Ψs + Ψp). # Answer Key — Worked Solutions

## Section A — Biomolecules, Scale & Microscopy

1. **Magnification & scale.** (a) Actual = image ÷ magnification = 50 mm ÷ 50,000 = 0.001 mm = 1 µm. (b) Image = actual × magnification = 0.02 µm × 50,000 = 1,000 µm = 1 mm. (c) Resolution is the smallest separation distinguishable. A light microscope resolves only down to ~200 nm, but the ribosome (20 nm) is ten times smaller, so it cannot be resolved; an electron microscope uses electrons of far shorter wavelength, giving resolution of ~0.5–1 nm, easily small enough to resolve a 20 nm ribosome.
2. **Peptide-bond bookkeeping.** (a) 150 − 1 = 149 peptide bonds. (b) 149 water molecules (one released per bond formed). (c) Mass ≈ 150 × 110 Da = 16,500 Da = 16.5 kDa. The residue mass (110 Da) is the mass of an amino acid AFTER it has lost water in forming the bond, so using it already accounts for the water removed; using the free-amino-acid mass would over-count by ~149 × 18 Da.
3. **Chargaff base composition.** (a) A = T = 20%, so T = 20%. G + C = 100 − (20 + 20) = 60%, and G = C, so G = 30% and C = 30%. (b) In a duplex every A pairs with a T on the opposite strand and every G with a C, forcing A = T and G = C overall. Within one strand there is no such pairing constraint, so a single strand can have unequal A and T. (c) Extract and hydrolyse the DNA to free bases (or nucleotides), separate them by chromatography (e.g. TLC/HPLC), and measure the relative amount of each by its absorbance — the proportions give the base composition.
4. **DNA length & coding capacity.** (a) 3,000 bp × 2 nucleotides per pair = 6,000 nucleotides. (b) 3,000 × 0.34 nm = 1,020 nm = 1.02 µm. (c) 3,000 bases ÷ 3 (per codon) = 1,000 codons → up to ~1,000 amino acids (fewer once a stop codon is counted). The real protein is usually shorter because the gene also contains non-coding introns and untranslated regions that are not translated.
5. **Condensation & hydrolysis of carbohydrates.** (a) Joining n monomers needs (n − 1) condensation reactions, so (n − 1) water molecules are released; for n = 1,000 that is 999 water molecules. (b) A 500-residue chain has 499 glycosidic bonds; full hydrolysis adds one water per bond = 499 × 18 Da = 8,982 Da of water. (c) Add excess Benedict’s reagent and heat both solutions for the same time; a higher reducing-sugar concentration gives a deeper colour / more precipitate (green → yellow → orange → brick-red), or compare against a colour series of known concentrations (a calibration scale).
6. **Lipid saturation.** (a) Three ester bonds form and three water molecules are released. (b) Iodine adds across C=C double bonds, so absorbing twice as much iodine means oil A has roughly twice as many double bonds (more unsaturated) as oil B; the more unsaturated oil A is more likely to be liquid at room temperature (kinks prevent tight packing). (c) Take equal masses of each fat, warm slowly in a water bath while stirring, record the temperature at which each becomes fully liquid; keep sample mass, heating rate and measurement method the same — the only variable is the fat type. Predict the saturated fat melts at the higher temperature.

## Section B — Enzymes & Membrane Transport

1. **Temperature coefficient (Q₁₀).** (a) 20 → 40 °C is a rise of 20 °C = two 10 °C steps, so rate × 2² = ×4; 4.0 × 4 = 16 µmol min⁻¹. (b) Q₁₀ over 25–35 °C = rate₃₅ ÷ rate₂₅ = 9.5 ÷ 5.0 = 1.9. (c) Above the optimum the enzyme denatures: heat disrupts the hydrogen bonds and other interactions holding the tertiary structure, the active site loses its shape, substrate no longer binds, so the rate falls instead of rising.
2. **Substrate concentration & Vₘₐₓ.** (a) Vₘₐₓ ≈ 91 units (the rate plateaus near 90–91 as [S] rises); it plateaus because at high [S] essentially all active sites are occupied at any instant — the enzyme is saturated, so adding more substrate cannot increase the rate. (b) Half of Vₘₐₓ ≈ 45.5 units, which is reached at about [S] = 3–4, so Kₘ ≈ 3–4 units. (c) At [S] = 10 the rate is 71; 71/91 × 100 ≈ 78% of Vₘₐₓ.
3. **Identifying an inhibitor.** (a) X is competitive: at very high [S] the substrate out-competes the inhibitor for the active site, so Vₘₐₓ is restored (only Kₘ rises). Y is non-competitive: it binds elsewhere and reduces the amount of working enzyme regardless of [S], so Vₘₐₓ stays depressed. (b) Control rises and plateaus at the highest Vₘₐₓ; X follows a lower curve that eventually reaches the same plateau; Y plateaus at a lower Vₘₐₓ that it never exceeds. (c) Run a parallel tube with no inhibitor but otherwise identical conditions, and hold pH and temperature constant (e.g. buffer + water bath) so any difference can be attributed only to the inhibitor.
4. **Surface-area-to-volume ratio.** (a) Side 2 mm: SA = 6 × 2² = 24 mm², V = 2³ = 8 mm³, SA:V = 24:8 = 3:1. Side 4 mm: SA = 6 × 4² = 96 mm², V = 4³ = 64 mm³, SA:V = 96:64 = 1.5:1. (b) As size increases, volume grows faster than surface area, so the SA:V ratio falls. (c) The larger cube has only half the relative surface (1.5:1 vs 3:1), so its interior is too far from the surface for diffusion to supply O₂/nutrients and remove waste fast enough; large organisms therefore need specialised exchange surfaces (lungs, gills) and transport systems (circulation) to maintain steep gradients.
5. **Water potential & osmosis.** (a) Ψ = Ψs + Ψp = −0.8 + 0.3 = −0.5 MPa. (b) The cell (−0.5 MPa) is more negative than the solution (−0.2 MPa); water moves down the water-potential gradient toward the more negative value, so water moves INTO the cell. (c) Cut equal potato cylinders, blot and weigh, immerse one in each of a sucrose series for a fixed time, reweigh and find the percentage mass change; the concentration at which mass does not change is where the cylinders’ water potential equals the solution’s — giving the tissue’s solute/water potential. Keep cylinder size, time and temperature constant.
6. **Active vs passive uptake experiment.** (a) Active transport needs ATP from respiration; cyanide (blocks respiration) or low temperature reduces ATP supply, so uptake against the gradient slows or stops. (b) Facilitated diffusion needs no ATP, so a respiratory inhibitor would NOT stop it (though very low temperature slightly slows all molecular movement). (c) Independent variable = presence/absence of cyanide (or a temperature series); dependent variable = rate of sugar uptake; control = identical cells with no inhibitor. If uptake collapses with the inhibitor it is active transport; if it continues it is facilitated diffusion. Keep cell number, sugar concentration and time constant.

## Section C — Energy: Respiration & Photosynthesis

1. **Respiratory quotient (RQ).** (a) C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O; RQ = 6 CO₂ ÷ 6 O₂ = 1.0. (b) RQ = 11 ÷ 16 = 0.69 ≈ 0.7, indicating that mainly lipid (fat) is being respired. (c) An RQ above 1.0 can arise when some respiration is anaerobic (extra CO₂ released without O₂ uptake) or when carbohydrate is being converted to fat, so CO₂ output exceeds O₂ uptake.
2. **Photosynthesis & limiting factors.** (a) 60 bubbles ÷ 5 min = 12 bubbles per minute. (b) Intensity ∝ 1/d²; moving from 10 to 20 cm gives (10/20)² = ¼ of the original intensity, so the rate would fall (toward about a quarter) if light is the limiting factor. (c) Hold light intensity high and temperature constant; supply different CO₂ concentrations (e.g. by adding sodium hydrogencarbonate) and measure the O₂-production rate — if the rate rises when more CO₂ is added, CO₂ was the limiting factor at high light.
3. **Net gas exchange & the compensation point.** (a) Net O₂ release = gross photosynthesis − respiration = 20 − 6 = 14 units h⁻¹. (b) The light compensation point is the light intensity at which gross photosynthesis exactly equals respiration, so net gas exchange is zero; here that requires gross photosynthesis = 6 units h⁻¹ (equal to respiration). (c) Measure net O₂ exchange at a range of light intensities; the compensation point is the intensity at which the measured net O₂ exchange crosses zero (below it the leaf is a net O₂ consumer, above it a net O₂ producer).

## Section D — Cell Division & DNA

1. **Mitotic index.** (a) MI = 60 ÷ 400 = 0.15 = 15%. (b) Time in mitosis ≈ fraction × cycle time = 0.15 × 24 h = 3.6 h. (c) Treat one batch of root tips with the chemical and leave an identical untreated batch as a control; after the same time, count the mitotic index in each. A higher MI than the control suggests the chemical promotes division; a lower MI suggests it inhibits division. Keep tissue type, temperature and staining method constant.
2. **Genetic variation from meiosis.** (a) Independent assortment gives 2ⁿ = 2²³ ≈ 8.39 × 10⁶ different gametes; the true number is far larger because crossing over recombines alleles within chromosomes, creating many more combinations. (b) 2n = 8 means n = 4, so 2⁴ = 16 different gametes. (c) Mitosis: 2 daughter cells, diploid, genetically identical to the parent. Meiosis: 4 daughter cells, haploid, genetically different from each other and the parent.
3. **Meselson–Stahl & semi-conservative replication.** (a) After one round: all molecules are hybrid (one ¹⁵N strand + one ¹⁴N strand), intermediate density. After two rounds: half hybrid, half fully light. (b) After three rounds there are 8 molecules; the 2 original heavy strands are still each paired with a new light strand (2 hybrid molecules), and the remaining 6 are fully light — so 2/8 = 25% hybrid and 6/8 = 75% light. (c) Semi-conservative predicts a single hybrid band after one round (seen). The conservative model predicts two bands (heavy + light) after one round (not seen); the dispersive model predicts the band would stay intermediate and gradually get lighter, never separating into distinct hybrid and light bands as observed.

## Section E — Genes & Heredity

1. **From mRNA to protein.** (a) 300 ÷ 3 = 100 codons; one is a stop codon, so ~99 amino acids are added to the polypeptide. (b) The gene (1,200 bp) is longer because it contains introns (and untranslated regions) that are transcribed but spliced out / not translated, leaving a shorter mature coding mRNA. (c) ~99 amino acids form 98 peptide bonds, releasing 98 water molecules.
2. **Chi-square test of a monohybrid ratio.** (a) Total = 400; expected 3:1 = 300 purple : 100 white. (b) χ² = Σ(O − E)²/E = (310 − 300)²/300 + (90 − 100)²/100 = 100/300 + 100/100 = 0.33 + 1.00 = 1.33. (c) 1.33 < 3.84, so we do NOT reject the null hypothesis; the deviation is small enough to be due to chance, and the data are consistent with a 3 : 1 ratio.
3. **Dihybrid ratios & epistasis.** (a) For 320 offspring, 9:3:3:1 of 16 parts = (9/16)×320 : (3/16)×320 : (3/16)×320 : (1/16)×320 = 180 : 60 : 60 : 20. (b) 180 : 60 : 80 = 9 : 3 : 4 (out of 16); this is recessive epistasis. (c) One gene’s recessive (homozygous) genotype blocks an early step in the pathway, so its product is never made regardless of the second gene; the two classes that depended on that later step therefore look identical and merge — collapsing the expected 3 + 1 into a single class of 4.
4. **Recombination frequency & gene mapping.** (a) Recombinants = 95 + 105 = 200; total = 410 + 390 + 95 + 105 = 1,000; RF = 200/1,000 × 100 = 20%. (b) 20% recombination = 20 centimorgans (map units). (c) Unlinked genes (different chromosomes) assort independently and give ~50% recombinants; because the observed value (20%) is well below 50%, the parental types are over-represented, showing the two genes are linked on the same chromosome.
5. **Measuring gene induction.** (a) Fold-induction = 200 ÷ 4 = 50×. (b) Grow two identical cultures, one with inducer and one without (the control), under the same conditions; after the same time assay the enzyme activity (or mRNA level) in each. Much higher activity only in the +inducer culture shows the gene is switched on by the inducer. (c) With a transcription inhibitor present, no new mRNA can be made, so adding inducer would NOT raise enzyme levels — confirming that induction acts by switching on transcription of the gene.

## Section F — Multicellular Physiology

1. **Sliding-filament shortening.** (a) Shortening = (2.2 − 1.6)/2.2 = 0.6/2.2 = 0.273 = 27.3%. (b) Contracted length = 4.0 cm × (1.6/2.2) = 4.0 × 0.727 = 2.9 cm (a 27.3% decrease). (c) Each myosin head binds actin, performs a power stroke that pulls the thin filament inward, then needs ATP to detach and re-cock for the next stroke; repeated shortening therefore consumes ATP continuously — without ATP the heads cannot release (rigor) and sliding stops.
2. **Fick’s law of diffusion.** (a) Factor = (×2 area) × (×1.5 gradient) ÷ (×0.5 distance) = (2 × 1.5)/0.5 = 3/0.5 = ×6. (b) Surface area — millions of alveoli give a huge total area (~70–85 m²); concentration gradient — continuous ventilation and a dense capillary blood flow keep the O₂ gradient steep; diffusion distance — the alveolar + capillary walls are extremely thin (~0.5 µm). (c) A thin surface keeps the diffusion distance in the denominator small (raising the rate); a moist surface lets gases dissolve so they can diffuse, again consistent with maximising the Fick rate.
3. **Oxygen unloading & the dissociation curve.** (a) O₂ unloaded = (98% − 75%) × 20 mL = 0.23 × 20 = 4.6 mL O₂ per 100 mL blood. (b) In active muscle (higher CO₂, lower pH, higher temperature) the curve shifts right, so at a given Pₒ₂ haemoglobin holds less O₂ and releases more to the tissues — increasing O₂ delivery where demand is greatest. (c) 5 L min⁻¹ = 5,000 mL; O₂ delivered = 5,000 × (20/100) = 1,000 mL O₂ per minute.
4. **Water potential & ascent of sap.** (a) Ψ = −0.6 + 0.2 = −0.4 MPa; the cell is more negative than the soil water (−0.1 MPa), so water moves from soil into the root cell. (b) Gravitational pressure = 0.01 MPa/m × 30 m = 0.30 MPa that must be overcome (i.e. about −0.30 MPa contribution). (c) Transpiration removes water from the leaves, making leaf water potential the most negative; this pulls water up the cohesive column, so each step is more negative than the one below — soil (least negative) > root > stem > leaf > air (most negative) — and water flows down this gradient toward the air.
5. **Transpiration rate from a potometer.** (a) Volume = πr² × distance = 3.14 × (0.5)² × 60 = 3.14 × 0.25 × 60 = 47.1 mm³ in 10 min; rate = 47.1 ÷ 10 = 4.71 mm³ per minute. (b) (i) A fan steepens the water-vapour gradient by removing humid air, increasing transpiration and uptake rate; (ii) blocking the lower (stoma-rich) surface with petroleum jelly closes most stomata, greatly reducing transpiration and uptake. (c) Assumption: the volume of water taken up equals the volume transpired (i.e. negligible water is used in photosynthesis or stored / for growth).
6. **Blood-glucose homeostasis.** (a) Fall = 9.0 − 5.4 = 3.6 mmol L⁻¹ over 60 min; average rate = 3.6 ÷ 60 = 0.06 mmol L⁻¹ per minute. (b) Receptors in the pancreatic β-islet cells detect the high glucose (above the set point); they act as control + effector by releasing insulin; insulin makes the liver (and muscle) take up glucose and store it as glycogen, lowering blood glucose back toward the set point — a negative-feedback loop because the response opposes the original rise. (c) In untreated type-1 diabetes little/no insulin is made, so the glucose curve rises higher and stays elevated for far longer (no rapid return). Fair test: give both people the same glucose dose per kg body mass after the same fasting period, measure blood glucose at identical time intervals with the same method, and plot both curves.
7. **Cleavage divisions in early development.** (a) After 7 divisions = 2⁷ = 128 cells. (b) Each cell occupies 1/128 of the original volume; radius scales as volume^{1/3}, so each cell’s radius = (1/128)^{1/3} ≈ 0.198 of the original — about one-fifth the egg’s radius (cleavage increases cell number without growth, so cells get smaller). (c) Potency, highest → lowest: zygote (totipotent) > inner-cell-mass cell (pluripotent) > haematopoietic stem cell (multipotent) > skin cell (essentially unipotent/differentiated). Yamanaka took an adult (differentiated) cell and introduced four transcription factors — OCT4, SOX2, KLF4 and c-MYC — reprogramming it into an induced pluripotent stem (iPS) cell; pluripotency is confirmed by showing the cell can give rise to derivatives of all three germ layers.

## Quick reminders for the calculations (Wentao’s checklist)

* Magnification: actual = image ÷ magnification — always convert units first (mm ↔ µm ↔ nm).
* n monomers → (n − 1) bonds → (n − 1) water molecules released by condensation (and the same number consumed by full hydrolysis).
* Chargaff: A = T and G = C in a duplex; remember %G + %C = 100 − (%A + %T).
* DNA: 0.34 nm per base pair; coding capacity = bases ÷ 3 amino acids (minus introns/UTRs/stop codon).
* Q₁₀ = rate at (T+10) ÷ rate at T; a 20 °C rise multiplies the rate by Q₁₀² (within the working range only).
* Vₘₐₓ = the plateau rate; Kₘ = the [S] giving ½ Vₘₐₓ. Competitive inhibitor: Vₘₐₓ unchanged, Kₘ up. Non-competitive: Vₘₐₓ down.
* SA:V = surface area ÷ volume — it FALLS as size rises; this drives the need for exchange surfaces and transport systems.
* Water potential Ψ = Ψs + Ψp; water always moves toward the MORE NEGATIVE Ψ.
* RQ = CO₂ out ÷ O₂ in: ≈1.0 carbohydrate, ≈0.7 lipid, ≈0.9 protein.
* Light intensity ∝ 1/distance² (inverse-square law).
* Meiosis variation = 2ⁿ (independent assortment); crossing over makes it far larger.
* Meselson–Stahl after k rounds: still only 2 hybrid molecules; light fraction = (2ᵏ − 2)/2ᵏ.
* Chi-square: χ² = Σ(O − E)²/E; compare with 3.84 (1 df, p = 0.05). χ² < 3.84 → keep the expected ratio.
* 9:3:3:1 splitting into 9:3:4 (or 12:3:1, 15:1, etc.) signals epistasis — classes have merged.
* Recombination frequency (%) = recombinants ÷ total × 100 = map distance in cM; 50% means unlinked.
* Fick’s law: rate ∝ (area × gradient) ÷ distance — distance is in the DENOMINATOR.
