## Key Concepts to Remember

**Origins & timing:** Eukaryotes emerged ~2.1 billion years ago (Bya); the last common ancestor of plants and animals (~1.6 Bya) was **unicellular**. Combining the **molecular clock** (divergence times estimated from DNA mutation rate) with the fossil record shows multicellularity arose **independently at least 6 times** — so it is a case of *convergent* evolution, not a single event.

**The transition (several steps):** aggregation of cells → intercellular communication → specialisation/cooperation of some cells → organisation of specialised cells into tissues. A key trade-off: cells **lose the ability to live independently**. The Volvocine green algae (Chlamydomonas → Pandorina → Eudorina → Pleodorina → Volvox) illustrate this gradient.

**Benefits:** larger size (shifts ecological position, e.g. escaping predators), and **division of labour / functional specialisation** — cells dedicate energy to ONE task (e.g. Volvox: outer somatic cells for motility/phototaxis, big inner cells for reproduction; no 'phase switching' as in single-celled Chlamydomonas) → greater efficiency.

**Consequences (4 constraints):** (1) small **surface-area-to-volume (SA:V) ratio** — as volume rises, SA:V falls; (2) large distance from internal cells to the surface; together these mean simple diffusion can no longer meet exchange needs → specialised **transport/gas-exchange systems** (L29–31). (3) Need to coordinate all cells (= dependency) → communication/homeostasis (L32–33). (4) Higher energy needs and complexity → more regulation and development (L34–36).

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Origins & Evolution

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Multicellularity is the condition in which an organism is composed of many cells that live together, communicate and (in complex forms) specialise and form tissues, rather than living as independent single cells. The last common ancestor of plants and animals (~1.6 Bya) is thought to have been unicellular.

2. A molecular clock is a technique that estimates the time at which two or more lineages diverged, based on the rate at which DNA accumulates mutations over time. Assuming a roughly constant mutation rate, the amount of sequence difference between two lineages is proportional to the time since they shared a common ancestor.

3. It means multicellularity evolved independently in several separate lineages (e.g. animals, fungi, land plants, brown algae, red algae, slime moulds) rather than once in a single ancestor. This is convergent evolution — the same general feature (multicellularity) arising repeatedly and independently.

4. (1) Aggregation of cells into a cluster; (2) intercellular communication within the cluster; (3) specialisation of some cells (cooperation/division of labour); (4) organisation of specialised cells into groups (tissues).

5. The Volvocine algae form a gradient: Chlamydomonas (single-celled) → Pandorina (16-celled cluster) → Eudorina (larger cluster) → Pleodorina (large cluster with somatic + reproductive cells) → Volvox (even larger, with somatic and reproductive cells organised into tissue-like arrangement). Trade-off: individual cells lose the ability to live independently (they become dependent on the colony).

### Part B — Yes/No

1. No — it arose independently at least 6 times. 2. Yes — the molecular clock is based on DNA mutation rate. 3. Yes — every eukaryotic kingdom except plants and animals contains both unicellular and multicellular examples (plants and animals are all multicellular). 4. Yes — eukaryotes emerged ~2.1 Bya, long before the first multicellular fossils (~600 Mya). 5. No — they generally lose the ability to live independently.

### Part D — Applied

1. (i) At 1% per 100 My, an 8% difference ≈ 800 million years since divergence. (ii) The assumption is that the molecular clock is calibrated for total accumulated difference between the two lineages (the stated rate already accounts for change along both branches), and that the mutation rate has been roughly constant. (iii) Inaccuracy can arise from a non-constant mutation rate, selection (non-neutral changes), saturation of mutations at a site (multiple hits), or different rates in different lineages/genes.

2. (i) ~2.1 Bya − ~0.6 Bya ≈ 1.5 billion years. (ii) Possible reasons: appropriate environmental conditions (e.g. rising oxygen) took time; the genetic/cell-adhesion and signalling 'toolkit' needed to evolve; or there was little selective advantage to grouping until certain ecological conditions arose. (iii) Convergent evolution (independent/parallel evolution of the same feature).

3. (i) Least → most: A (Chlamydomonas, 1) → B (Pandorina, 16) → C (Eudorina, ~32) → D (Pleodorina, ~64, two cell types) → E (Volvox, ~2000, somatic + reproductive, organised). (ii) Division of labour (distinct cell types) first appears at D (Pleodorina), where somatic and reproductive cells are distinguished. (iii) The series shows living organisms spanning the whole range from single cells to complex colonies with specialised cells and tissue-like organisation, so it serves as a 'snapshot' model of the stepwise transition to multicellularity.

## Set 2 — Benefits & Cell Specialisation

### Part A — Short-answer

1. (a) Size — becoming larger shifts the organism's position in the ecological system (e.g. too big for small predators to eat). (b) Functional specialisation (division of labour) — cells perform dedicated tasks such as motility or reproduction, increasing efficiency.

2. Chlamydomonas is a single cell with one 'job' at a time, so it must switch between a swimming phase (using its flagellum) and a non-swimming phase associated with cell division. Volvox is a multicellular colony with (at least) two cell types and no phase switching: outer somatic cells handle coordinated flagellar movement while large inner cells are dedicated to reproduction, so movement and reproduction can occur simultaneously because different cells do each job.

3. Division of labour means different cells specialise in different tasks instead of every cell doing everything. Because a specialised cell can devote all its energy and structure to one function (rather than juggling many), each task is performed more efficiently, raising the overall efficiency and capability of the whole organism — 'the whole is greater than the sum of its parts'.

4. (Any three) Dorsal epithelial cell — sensing predators; ventral epithelial cell — cilia for movement and absorption of digested food; gland cell — secrete communication molecules; lipophil cell — secrete digestive molecules; crystal cell — gravity sensing; fibre cell — churning movement when feeding; shiny sphere cell — venom.

5. Specialised cells of the same type group together to form tissues; different tissues are organised together to form functional organs; organs work together in systems to make up the whole multicellular organism. (Hierarchy: specialised cells → tissues → organs → organism.)

No — larger size generally REDUCES vulnerability to small predators (it can become too big to ingest). 4. Yes — the large inner cells of Volvox are specialised for reproduction. 5. Yes — specialisation lets a cell dedicate its energy to a single task.

## Set 3 — Consequences & Constraints

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Cells must take in materials they need and remove the products of metabolism to stay alive. The four key substances exchanged are oxygen (O₂) and nutrients (in) and carbon dioxide (CO₂) and waste products (out).

2. The opportunity for exchange depends on surface area, while the demand for materials depends on volume. As an object gets larger, volume (n³) grows faster than surface area (6n²), so the SA:V ratio decreases. Consequently, proportionally less surface is available to supply each unit of internal volume.

3. Constraint 1: the SA:V ratio of a large multicellular organism is small. Constraint 2: the distance from internal cells to the external environment is large. Overall consequence: simple diffusion across the body surface cannot meet the exchange needs of most multicellular organisms, so specialised gas-exchange and transport systems are required.

4. Specialisation makes cells dependent on one another: each cell type does only part of what is needed, so cells rely on other cells (and on transport systems) for the functions they no longer perform themselves. Coordinating these interdependent cells requires multiple types of communication — both chemical (e.g. signalling molecules/hormones) and electrical (e.g. nerve signals).

5. Larger mass means a larger total metabolic requirement. Specialisation helps by making each task more efficient and by allowing complex transport systems to maintain high metabolism; but specialisation and large complex bodies also create new requirements — genetic regulation and differentiation to produce the right cells in the right place and time (temporal and spatial control), and therefore more regulation overall.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. No — SA:V ratio DECREASES as volume increases. 2. Yes — exchange opportunity is related to surface area. 3. No — diffusion alone cannot meet the needs of most large multicellular organisms. 4. Yes — transport and gas-exchange systems are adaptations to these constraints. 5. Yes — larger mass requires greater metabolic input.
