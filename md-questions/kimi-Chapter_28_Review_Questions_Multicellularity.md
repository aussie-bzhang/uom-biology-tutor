# Question 1: Surface Area-to-Volume Ratio and Diffusion Limits

**Background:** Lecture 28 emphasizes that as organisms become multicellular and larger, the surface area-to-volume ratio (SA:V) decreases dramatically. This creates Constraint 1 for multicellular life. Chlamydomonas (unicellular) has a diameter of approximately 10 μm, while Volvox (multicellular) forms a spherical colony with a diameter of approximately 500 μm.

**Part A (Calculation):** Calculate the SA:V ratio for a spherical Chlamydomonas cell (d = 10 μm) and a spherical Volvox colony (d = 500 μm). For a sphere, SA = 4πr² and V = (4/3)πr³. Express your answers as ratios (e.g., X:1). (4 marks)

**Part B (Analysis):** If each cell in the Volvox colony has the same metabolic rate as a single Chlamydomonas cell, by what factor has the total metabolic demand increased relative to the surface area available for gas/nutrient exchange? Explain why this creates a physiological constraint that natural selection must solve. (4 marks)

**Part C (Experimental Design):** Propose a simple experiment using the volvocine green algae series (Chlamydomonas → Pandorina → Eudorina → Pleodorina → Volvox) to test whether larger colony size correlates with reduced oxygen diffusion efficiency. State your independent and dependent variables, and one controlled variable. (4 marks)

# Question 2: Diffusion Distance and Internal Cell Survival

**Background:** Constraint 2 states that the distance from internal cells to the external environment is large in multicellular organisms. The diffusion coefficient (D) of oxygen in water at 25°C is approximately 2.0 × 10⁻⁵ cm²/s. The diffusion time t can be estimated by the relation t ≈ x²/(2D), where x is the diffusion distance.

**Part A (Calculation):** A small multicellular animal embryo is roughly spherical with a radius of 0.5 mm. Calculate the approximate time required for oxygen to diffuse from the surface to the centre of the embryo. Show your working and state your answer in seconds. (3 marks)

**Part B (Calculation):** Now consider a larger multicellular organism with a radius of 5 cm (e.g., a small flatworm). Calculate the diffusion time to the centre under the same conditions. Convert your answer to hours. (3 marks)

**Part C (Biochemical Interpretation):** A typical mammalian cell consumes oxygen at a rate of approximately 10⁻¹⁶ mol/cell/s. If the centre cell in the 5-cm organism from Part B relies solely on diffusion, and the surface oxygen concentration is 200 μM, explain quantitatively why diffusion alone cannot sustain aerobic metabolism for the interior cells. (Hint: Consider that diffusion time exceeds the rate of oxygen consumption and the concentration gradient would collapse.) (6 marks)

**Part A (Calculation):** Calculate the total metabolic power (in watts) of the Volvox colony. (4 marks)

**Part B (Comparison):** If the same number of cells (2,016) existed as independent, unicellular Chlamydomonas organisms, what would be their total metabolic power? By what percentage does the colonial, multicellular organisation increase the total energy demand? (4 marks)

If protein synthesis costs 4 ATP per amino acid (average) and the ECM contains 5 × 10⁹ amino acid residues, calculate the total ATP cost of ECM synthesis per generation. (Assume 1 ATP ≈ 1 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.) Convert this energy cost to joules and discuss whether this represents a significant overhead compared to the colony’s total metabolic power from Part A. (6 marks)

# Question 5: Division of Labour and Metabolic Efficiency

**Background:** Volvox exhibits division of labour: somatic cells are terminally differentiated for motility (flagella beating) and die after a few days, while gonidia are specialized for reproduction. This represents an evolutionary trade-off. In a hypothetical ancestral undifferentiated colony (like Pleodorina), every cell spends 40% of its energy budget on motility and 40% on reproductive machinery, with 20% on housekeeping. In Volvox, somatic cells spend 70% on motility and 30% on housekeeping; gonidia spend 80% on reproduction and 20% on housekeeping.

**Part A (Calculation):** Consider a colony of 2,000 cells. In the undifferentiated ancestral state, all cells are equal. In the specialized Volvox state, there are 1,980 somatic cells and 20 gonidia. If the total ATP budget per cell per day is 1 × 10¹⁰ ATP molecules in all cell types, calculate: (i) the total ATP "wasted" on reproductive machinery in somatic cells of the ancestral undifferentiated colony, and (ii) the total ATP "saved" by Volvox somatic cells not investing in reproduction. Express your answer as a percentage of the colony’s total daily ATP budget. (6 marks)

**Part B (Biochemical Trade-off):** Somatic cells in Volvox lack reproductive machinery (no chloroplast division for daughter colonies, no DNA replication for gametes). However, they must produce large amounts of ATP for flagellar dynein motors. Each dynein ATPase consumes ~200 ATP/s per flagellum. If each somatic cell has 2 flagella and beats them for 12 hours per day, calculate the total ATP consumed by flagellar motility per somatic cell per day. Compare this to the 40% reproductive cost in the ancestral cell. Which strategy is more energetically expensive per cell? (5 marks)

**Part C (Evolutionary Implications):** The lecture states that multicellularity enables cells to "dedicate their energy to one task rather than multiple tasks – increased efficiency." Using your calculations from Parts A and B, discuss whether the efficiency gain comes from reduced total energy consumption per colony or from improved functional performance per unit energy. Relate your answer to Constraint 4 (increased energy needs and specialization). (3 marks)

## Question 1 Answers

**Part A:** For Chlamydomonas (r = 5 μm = 5 × 10⁻⁴ cm):
SA = 4π(5 × 10⁻⁴)² = 3.14 × 10⁻⁵ cm²; V = (4/3)π(5 × 10⁻⁴)³ = 5.24 × 10⁻⁷ cm³
SA:V = (3.14 × 10⁻⁵) / (5.24 × 10⁻⁷) ≈ 60:1

For Volvox (r = 250 μm = 0.025 cm):
SA = 4π(0.025)² = 7.85 × 10⁻³ cm²; V = (4/3)π(0.025)³ = 6.54 × 10⁻⁵ cm³
SA:V = (7.85 × 10⁻³) / (6.54 × 10⁻⁵) ≈ 120:1 for the whole colony.
However, if we consider the SA:V of the constituent cells (each ~10 μm), the internal cells have effectively zero direct surface contact. The "available" surface for exchange is only the outer monolayer. With ~2,000 cells, only ~200 cells are on the surface (approx. 10% of cells). Thus, the effective SA:V for internal cells is drastically reduced compared to a free-living unicellular organism.

**Part B:** If Volvox has 2,000 cells, total metabolic demand is ~2,000× that of one Chlamydomonas. However, the surface area has only increased by (500/10)² = 2,500×. The metabolic demand per unit surface area has increased by a factor of ~2,000/2,500 = 0.8, but the critical issue is that the internal cells are not directly exposed to the surface. The diffusion path length has increased from ~5 μm to ~250 μm (50×). This creates a severe oxygen/nutrient deficit for interior cells, necessitating specialized transport systems (e.g., cytoplasmic streaming, circulatory systems in animals, vascular tissue in plants).

**Part C:** Independent variable: Colony size/species (Chlamydomonas, Pandorina, Eudorina, Pleodorina, Volvox). Dependent variable: Oxygen concentration at the centre of the colony (measured by microelectrode or fluorescence lifetime imaging using an oxygen-sensitive dye such as Ru(dpp)₃). Controlled variable: Light intensity, temperature, CO₂ concentration, and culture medium. Expected result: Larger colonies show steeper oxygen gradients from surface to centre, with Volvox showing near-anoxic conditions in the centre unless actively rotating (which is a behavioural adaptation to enhance exchange).

## Question 2 Answers

**Part A:** r = 0.5 mm = 0.05 cm. Using t ≈ x²/(2D):
t = (0.05)² / (2 × 2.0 × 10⁻⁵) = 0.0025 / (4.0 × 10⁻⁵) = 62.5 s ≈ 1.04 minutes.

**Part B:** r = 5 cm. t = (5)² / (2 × 2.0 × 10⁻⁵) = 25 / (4.0 × 10⁻⁵) = 6.25 × 10⁵ s.
Convert to hours: 6.25 × 10⁵ / 3600 ≈ 173.6 hours (≈ 7.2 days).

**Part C:** A cell at the centre of a 5-cm organism would require ~7 days to receive oxygen by diffusion alone. However, a mammalian cell consumes 10⁻¹⁶ mol O₂/cell/s. Over 7 days, it would need 10⁻¹⁶ × 6.25 × 10⁵ ≈ 6.25 × 10⁻¹¹ mol O₂ just to survive the diffusion wait time, which is impossible because the oxygen would be consumed by intermediate layers long before reaching the centre. The concentration gradient from surface (200 μM) to centre would collapse as surface cells deplete the oxygen. The flux J = -D(dC/dx) is insufficient when x is large. Quantitatively, the diffusion time constant (L²/D) exceeds the metabolic turnover time by orders of magnitude, making diffusion alone incompatible with aerobic life at this scale. This is why circulatory systems (blood/hemolymph) and respiratory pigments (hemoglobin) evolved.

## Question 3 Answers

**Part A:** Somatic cell power = 1.5 × 10⁻¹² × 1.20 = 1.8 × 10⁻¹² W.
Gonidium power = 1.5 × 10⁻¹² × 1.50 = 2.25 × 10⁻¹² W.
Total power = (2,000 × 1.8 × 10⁻¹²) + (16 × 2.25 × 10⁻¹²)
= 3.6 × 10⁻⁹ + 3.6 × 10⁻¹¹ = 3.636 × 10⁻⁹ W ≈ 3.64 nW.

**Part B:** Unicellular total = 2,016 × 1.5 × 10⁻¹² = 3.024 × 10⁻⁹ W ≈ 3.02 nW.
Percentage increase = [(3.636 - 3.024) / 3.024] × 100 ≈ 20.2%.
The colonial organization increases energy demand by ~20% due to the higher metabolic costs of specialized functions (flagellar beating and biosynthesis).

**Part C:** Total ATP cost = 5 × 10⁹ amino acids × 4 ATP/amino acid = 2 × 10¹⁰ ATP.
Energy = 2 × 10¹⁰ × 1 × 10⁻¹⁹ J = 2 × 10⁻⁹ J per generation.
If a generation time is ~48 hours (1.728 × 10⁵ s), the power overhead = 2 × 10⁻⁹ / 1.728 × 10⁵ ≈ 1.16 × 10⁻¹⁴ W.
Compared to the colony total metabolic power (3.64 × 10⁻⁹ W), the ECM synthesis overhead is only ~0.3% of the total budget. However, this is a one-time capital cost per generation. The more significant recurring cost is the 20% increase in per-cell metabolic rate due to specialization. The ECM cost is small in energetic terms but essential for structural integrity, demonstrating that multicellularity requires both material and energy investments.

## Question 4 Answers

**Part A:** Hypothesis: In the presence of a copepod predator, Volvox will have higher relative fitness (measured as population growth rate or survival) compared to Chlamydomonas because the larger size and ECM of Volvox make it physically resistant to ingestion by the predator, whereas single-celled Chlamydomonas will be preferentially consumed.

**Part B:** (i) Treatments: (1) Chlamydomonas alone (control), (2) Volvox alone (control), (3) Mixed culture without predator, (4) Mixed culture with predator, (5) Mixed culture with predator + high nutrient supplementation.
(ii) Conditions: Standard Bold’s basal medium, 25°C, 16:8 light:dark cycle, 100 μmol photons/m²/s.
(iii) Fitness metric: Cell density measured by hemocytometer counts at days 0, 7, 14, 21, 28; r (intrinsic rate of increase) = (ln(Nt) - ln(N₀))/t.
(iv) Molecular assay: Measure reactive oxygen species (ROS) using dichlorofluorescein diacetate (DCFDA) fluorescence as a proxy for oxidative stress induced by predation pressure. Alternatively, quantify expression of stress-response genes (e.g., HSP70) by RT-qPCR.

**Part C:** Percentage changes (With predator vs No predator):
Chlamydomonas: (800 - 5200)/5200 × 100 = -84.6%
Volvox: (1650 - 1800)/1800 × 100 = -8.3%
Pandorina: (2400 - 3100)/3100 × 100 = -22.6%
Volvox benefits most from multicellularity under predation, showing only an 8.3% decline versus 84.6% for Chlamydomonas.

Biochemical explanation for Pandorina’s intermediate response: Pandorina forms a 16-cell cluster embedded in a sparse ECM composed of glycoproteins. While the cluster is larger than a single Chlamydomonas cell, it is still small enough to be ingested by the copepod. The partial protection likely comes from the increased effective size (reducing ingestion efficiency) and the presence of cell-cell adhesion molecules (e.g., pherophorins) that make the colony more difficult to separate into individual edible units. However, unlike Volvox, it lacks the massive ECM and large size that would render it physically invulnerable to the predator’s feeding apparatus.

## Question 5 Answers

**Part A:** In the ancestral undifferentiated colony (2,000 cells), each cell wastes 40% of its ATP on reproductive machinery that will never be used (since all cells are equivalent and only some will actually reproduce).
Total "wasted" ATP = 2,000 cells × (0.40 × 1 × 10¹⁰) = 8 × 10¹² ATP/day.
Total colony budget = 2,000 × 1 × 10¹⁰ = 2 × 10¹³ ATP/day.
Waste = 40% of total budget.

In Volvox, somatic cells (1,980) spend 0% on reproduction.
ATP saved = 1,980 × (0.40 × 1 × 10¹⁰) = 7.92 × 10¹² ATP/day.
As a percentage of the Volvox total budget (2,016 × 1 × 10¹⁰ = 2.016 × 10¹³), the saving is 7.92 × 10¹² / 2.016 × 10¹³ ≈ 39.3%.

**Part B:** ATP per flagellum per day = 200 ATP/s × 2 flagella × 12 h × 3600 s/h = 1.728 × 10⁷ ATP/day.
This is only 0.17% of the cell’s daily ATP budget (1 × 10¹⁰).
Wait — correction: The question states 40% of the ancestral cell budget was for reproduction. 40% of 1 × 10¹⁰ = 4 × 10⁹ ATP/day.
Flagellar cost = 1.728 × 10⁷ ATP/day.
Comparison: The reproductive cost (4 × 10⁹) is ~230× higher than the flagellar motility cost (1.728 × 10⁷). Therefore, eliminating the reproductive machinery from somatic cells saves vastly more energy than is spent on enhanced motility. The somatic cells are energetically "cheaper" than ancestral undifferentiated cells despite increased flagellar activity.

**Part C:** The efficiency gain comes primarily from improved functional performance per unit energy, not from reduced total energy consumption. From Part B, the colony still spends nearly the same total ATP (the saved reproductive ATP is reallocated to gonidia, which have higher biosynthetic costs). However, the functional output is dramatically improved: (1) Motility is enhanced because 1,980 cells are dedicated swimmers with 70% of their energy directed to flagella, creating coordinated phototaxis; (2) Reproduction is enhanced because 20 gonidia can each invest 80% of their energy into producing daughter colonies. The "efficiency" is therefore a ratio of functional performance (movement speed + reproductive output) to energy input. This aligns with Constraint 4: specialization increases complexity and energy needs, but the return on investment (in terms of survival and reproduction) is higher than in an undifferentiated colony where each cell is a generalist.
