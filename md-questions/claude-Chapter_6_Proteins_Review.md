# Answer Key – Set 1

### Part A – Short-answer

1. Every amino acid has a central (α) carbon bonded to: (1) a hydrogen atom (-H); (2) a basic amino group (-NH2); (3) an acidic carboxyl group (-COOH); and (4) a variable side chain (-R). The carboxyl group is acidic (can donate H+); the amino group is basic (can accept H+).
2. A zwitterion is a molecule that carries both a positive and a negative charge at the same time while being overall neutral. In an amino acid the amino group is protonated (-NH3+) and the carboxyl group is deprotonated (-COO-). At low pH (excess H+) the carboxyl stays/returns to -COOH; at high pH (few H+) the amino group loses its proton to -NH2 (so -COOH → -COO- at high pH and -NH2 → -NH3+ at low pH).
3. A peptide bond forms by a condensation (dehydration) reaction: the carboxyl group of one amino acid reacts with the amino group of the next, releasing one molecule of water (H2O). The peptide bond is an amide bond. The end with the free amino group is the N-terminus; the end with the free carboxyl group is the C-terminus.
4. Examples (any five): structural – keratin (hair); defence – antibodies; hormone – insulin; contractile – myosin (muscle); enzyme – RUBISCO; transport – membrane transport proteins; storage – gluten; structural matrix – proteoglycans (cartilage).
5. Primary structure is the specific order (sequence) of amino acids in a polypeptide chain. It is held together (stabilised) by peptide (covalent amide) bonds.

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. The backbone is identical; only the R group differs.
2. Yes. Carboxyl is acidic (donates H+); amino is basic (accepts H+).
3. Yes. There are 20 common amino acids.
4. No. A peptide bond forms by condensation (dehydration), which removes a water molecule; hydrolysis is the reverse (breaking) reaction.
5. Yes. The peptide bond is also called an amide bond.

# Answer Key – Set 2

### Part A – Short-answer

1. Primary: the sequence/order of amino acids – stabilised by peptide bonds. Secondary: local folding into alpha helices and beta pleated sheets – stabilised by hydrogen bonds between non-adjacent backbone peptide groups. Tertiary: the overall 3-D folding of a single polypeptide – stabilised by R-group interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic/electrostatic interactions, hydrophobic/dispersion interactions, and disulfide bridges). Quaternary: the assembly of two or more polypeptide chains (subunits) – stabilised by the same kinds of interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions, disulfide bridges).
2. The two main types are the alpha helix and the beta pleated sheet. Both are formed and stabilised by hydrogen bonds between non-adjacent peptide groups (-CONH-) along the backbone.
3. (1) Hydrogen bonds (between polar uncharged R groups, dipole-dipole); (2) ionic/electrostatic interactions (between oppositely charged R groups); (3) hydrophobic/dispersion-force interactions (between non-polar R groups); (4) disulfide bridges (covalent S-S bonds between cysteine residues).
4. Non-polar (hydrophobic) R groups can only form weak dispersion-force (hydrophobic) interactions. Polar, charged R groups can form strong electrostatic (ionic) interactions. Polar, uncharged R groups can form dipole-dipole interactions and hydrogen bonds. So the chemistry of the R group dictates which interactions are possible.
5. A disulfide bridge is a covalent bond (strong S-S bond), whereas the other tertiary interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, hydrophobic/dispersion forces) are non-covalent and weaker. It is formed between two cysteine residues (via their -SH groups).

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. Secondary structure is held together by backbone hydrogen bonds.
2. Yes. Tertiary structure comes from R-group interactions across the chain.
3. No. Quaternary structure requires two or more polypeptide chains.
4. Yes. Disulfide bridges form between two cysteine residues.
5. No. Alpha helices and beta sheets are secondary structure, not tertiary.

# Answer Key – Set 3

### Part A – Short-answer

1. Denaturation is a change to the secondary and/or tertiary structure of a protein (the primary sequence stays intact). The altered 3-D shape causes the protein to partially or completely lose its function.
2. A pH change adds or removes H+. This changes the protonation of acidic and basic R groups (-COOH ⇌ -COO-, and -NH2 ⇌ -NH3+), which disrupts the ionic (electrostatic) interactions between charged R groups and so unfolds the protein.
3. Increasing temperature gives the molecule more kinetic/thermal energy, which disrupts the relatively weak intermolecular interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic, dispersion) between R groups, causing the protein to unfold (denature).
4. Specificity needs (1) shape – there must be a general geometric “fit” between the protein and the other molecule; and (2) chemistry – surface R groups must be able to form complementary interactions (ion-ion, ion-dipole, hydrogen bonds, dispersion forces, dipole-dipole). Without both a matching shape and matching surface chemistry, the binding is not specific.
5. Any four: high temperature; pH change; high concentration of polar molecules; non-polar substances (acting via hydrophobic interactions).

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. Denaturation alters secondary and/or tertiary structure.
2. No. Primary structure (peptide bonds) remains intact; only secondary/tertiary structure changes.
3. Yes. Function is reduced or lost.
4. Yes. Excess polar molecules interfere with dipole-dipole and hydrogen-bond R-group interactions.
5. No. Heat disrupts (does not stabilise) these interactions.

### Part D – Applied / Exam-style

1. Model answer:

(a) pH change – adding or removing H+ changes charged R groups (-COOH ⇌ -COO-, -NH2 ⇌ -NH3+), disrupting ionic (electrostatic) interactions; the protein unfolds.

(b) Increased temperature – extra thermal energy disrupts the weak intermolecular interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic, dispersion) between R groups, unfolding the protein.

(c) High concentration of a polar molecule – the added polar molecules compete for and interfere with the dipole-dipole interactions and hydrogen bonds between polar R groups.

(d) High concentration of a non-polar molecule – the non-polar molecules interfere with the dispersion (hydrophobic) interactions between non-polar R groups, disrupting the hydrophobic core and unfolding the protein.
