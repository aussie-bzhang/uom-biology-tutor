## Question 2: Na+/K+ Pump and Resting Membrane Potential (20 points)

**(a) Calculate the net charge movement per ATP molecule hydrolysed by the Na+/K+ pump. Is the neuron becoming more negative or less negative inside with each pumping cycle? (4 points)**

## Question 1 Answer: Hormone Solubility and Receptor Binding

(a) Epinephrine is WATER-SOLUBLE. (1 point) The catechol ring contains two hydroxyl (-OH) groups and the amine group (-NH2), both of which are polar and can form hydrogen bonds with water. (1 point) These polar groups make epinephrine hydrophilic and unable to cross the lipid bilayer. (1 point) Additionally, epinephrine is stored in chromaffin granules within adrenal medulla cells, which indicates it is released via exocytosis rather than diffusing through membranes. (1 point)

(b) Because epinephrine is water-soluble (hydrophilic), it CANNOT pass through the lipid bilayer of the plasma membrane. (1 point) Therefore, epinephrine binds to CELL SURFACE RECEPTORS located on the extracellular face of the target cell membrane. (1 point) The receptor is typically a G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR). (1 point) When epinephrine binds, it activates a heterotrimeric G-protein (Gs), which in turn activates the enzyme adenylyl cyclase. (1 point) Adenylyl cyclase converts ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP), a SECOND MESSENGER. (1 point) cAMP activates protein kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates various target proteins in the cell, producing the cellular response (e.g., glycogen breakdown, increased heart rate). (1 point) This signal amplification cascade allows a small amount of hormone to produce a large intracellular response. (1 point) The hormone itself does not enter the cell; the signal is relayed by the second messenger system. (1 point)

(c) Experimental design (8 points distributed as follows):

Materials: Two test tubes containing distilled water and olive oil (lipid solvent); epinephrine solution; UV spectrophotometer or visual observation. (2 points)

Procedure: (1) Add equal volumes of epinephrine solution to each of the two test tubes. (2) Vortex both tubes for 1 minute. (3) Allow to settle for 5 minutes. (4) Observe which layer the epinephrine partitions into, or use spectrophotometry to measure epinephrine concentration in each phase. (3 points)

Expected results: If epinephrine is water-soluble, it will remain in the aqueous (water) layer; the oil layer will remain colourless/clear. If lipid-soluble, it would partition into the oil layer. (2 points)

Conclusion: Observation of epinephrine remaining in the aqueous phase confirms that it is water-soluble and therefore must bind to cell surface receptors rather than intracellular receptors. (1 point)

## Question 2 Answer: Na+/K+ Pump and Resting Membrane Potential

(a) Per ATP hydrolysis: 3 Na+ are pumped OUT and 2 K+ are pumped IN. (1 point) Net charge movement = 3 positive charges out - 2 positive charges in = +1 net positive charge exported per cycle. (1 point) This makes the inside of the neuron MORE NEGATIVE (electrogenic effect). (1 point) The pump is electrogenic because it creates a small negative potential with each cycle. (1 point)

(b) Calculation: The pump brings 2 K+ in per ATP. (1 point) To compensate for 1.0 x 10⁶ K+ leaking out per second, the pump must bring the same number back in. (1 point) ATP required = (1.0 x 10⁶ K+ ions/s) / (2 K+ per ATP) = 5.0 x 10⁵ ATP molecules per second. (3 points) This represents a significant energy cost; neurons consume approximately 20% of the body's total ATP despite comprising only 2% of body mass, largely due to Na+/K+-ATPase activity. (1 point)

(c) The resting membrane potential (-70 mV) is closer to E\_K (-90 mV) than to E\_Na (+60 mV) because the membrane is far more permeable to K+ than to Na+ at rest. (1 point) At rest, there are many more open K+ channels (leak channels) than Na+ channels. (1 point) The Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation describes the resting potential as a weighted average of the equilibrium potentials of all permeant ions, weighted by their relative permeabilities. (1 point) Since P\_K >> P\_Na at rest, the resting potential is dominated by the K+ equilibrium potential. (1 point) If Na+ channels were equally permeable at rest, the potential would be closer to 0 mV. (1 point) The Na+/K+ pump indirectly contributes by maintaining the concentration gradients that drive K+ efflux through leak channels. (1 point)

(d) If the Na+/K+ pump is blocked, the resting potential will gradually DEPOLARISE (become less negative) over time. (1 point) This occurs because: (1) The concentration gradients for Na+ and K+ will gradually dissipate as Na+ leaks in and K+ leaks out without being pumped back. (1 point) (2) The direct electrogenic contribution of the pump (+1 net charge out per cycle) is lost. (1 point) Eventually, the membrane potential would approach 0 mV as the concentration gradients collapse, and the neuron would lose its ability to generate action potentials. (1 point)

## Question 3 Answer: Action Potential Ion Channel Analysis

(a) Summary table of ion channel states and ion movements (12 points = 2 points per phase):

(i) Resting state: Voltage-gated Na+ channels = CLOSED; Voltage-gated K+ channels = CLOSED. (1 point) Only leak channels are open. K+ efflux dominates, establishing -70 mV. (1 point)

(ii) Threshold and rapid depolarisation: A depolarising stimulus opens some voltage-gated Na+ channels; Na+ influx causes further depolarisation, opening more Na+ channels (positive feedback). (1 point) Voltage-gated K+ channels begin to open slowly. Net ion flow: Na+ rushes IN. (1 point)

(iii) Peak of action potential: Voltage-gated Na+ channels reach maximum open probability; then INACTIVATION begins (inactivation gate closes). (1 point) Voltage-gated K+ channels are now fully open. At the peak, Na+ influx and K+ efflux are briefly balanced. (1 point)

(iv) Repolarisation: Voltage-gated Na+ channels INACTIVATE (close and unresponsive). (1 point) Voltage-gated K+ channels are fully open; K+ efflux drives membrane potential back toward negative values. (1 point)

(v) Hyperpolarisation (undershoot): K+ channels remain open and are slow to close; excessive K+ efflux drives membrane potential below resting level (toward E\_K, ~-90 mV). (1 point) Na+ channels are still in the closed (recovering) state. (1 point)

(vi) Return to resting potential: K+ channels finally close. (1 point) K+ leak channels and the Na+/K+ pump restore the resting membrane potential to -70 mV. (1 point)

(b) The positive feedback mechanism: A small depolarisation opens some voltage-gated Na+ channels. (1 point) Na+ influx further depolarises the membrane, opening MORE Na+ channels. (1 point) This creates a self-amplifying cycle that drives rapid depolarisation. (1 point) The feedback terminates because Na+ channels have an inactivation gate that closes shortly after opening (time-dependent inactivation), and the slower-activating K+ channels open to drive repolarisation. (1 point)

(c) During the absolute refractory period, most voltage-gated Na+ channels are inactivated (inactivation gate closed) and CANNOT reopen regardless of stimulus strength. (1 point) This means no new action potential can be generated in the recently depolarised region. (1 point) By the time the Na+ channels recover from inactivation, the action potential has moved forward due to local current flow. (1 point) This ensures the action potential can only propagate in one direction (away from the stimulus origin). (1 point)

## Question 4 Answer: Synaptic Transmission and Drug Mechanisms

(a) Diagram should include and correctly label: (1) presynaptic terminal with mitochondria, (2) synaptic vesicles containing neurotransmitter, (3) voltage-gated Ca2+ channels in presynaptic membrane, (4) synaptic cleft (~20-40 nm gap), (5) ligand-gated receptor channels on postsynaptic membrane, (6) neurotransmitter molecules crossing the cleft. (4 points: 0.5-1 point per label depending on completeness)

(b) Sequence of events: (1) Action potential arrives at presynaptic terminal. (1 point) (2) Depolarisation opens voltage-gated Ca2+ channels. (1 point) (3) Ca2+ influx triggers exocytosis of synaptic vesicles via SNARE proteins. (1 point) (4) Neurotransmitter is released into the synaptic cleft by vesicle fusion with the presynaptic membrane. (1 point) (5) Neurotransmitter diffuses across the cleft and binds to ligand-gated receptors on the postsynaptic membrane. (1 point) (6) Receptor binding opens ion channels (e.g., Na+ channels for excitatory postsynaptic potentials), causing depolarisation (EPSP) or hyperpolarisation (IPSP). (1 point)

(c)(i) Compound A (SERT blocker) functions similarly to an SSRI. (1 point) SSRIs specifically inhibit the serotonin reuptake transporter, increasing serotonin concentration in the synaptic cleft and prolonging the postsynaptic signal. (1 point) Blocking reuptake is the mechanism used by clinically prescribed SSRIs such as fluoxetine (Prozac). (1 point)

(c)(ii) Compound A (SERT blocker): Serotonin concentration in the cleft INCREASES (stays elevated). (1 point) The postsynaptic signal is PROLONGED because serotonin remains bound to receptors longer. (1 point) Compound B (MAO enhancer): Serotonin concentration in the cleft DECREASES MORE RAPIDLY. (1 point) MAO breaks down serotonin in the presynaptic terminal, so enhancing MAO activity would reduce available serotonin for release, shortening and weakening the postsynaptic signal. (1 point) Compound B would likely worsen depression rather than treat it.

(c)(iii) Hypothesis for delayed therapeutic effect: Although SSRIs immediately increase synaptic serotonin, clinical antidepressant effects take 2-4 weeks. (1 point) Possible explanations: (1) Downregulation of presynaptic autoreceptors (5-HT1A) is required; initially, increased serotonin activates these autoreceptors, actually REDUCING serotonin release until they desensitise. (1 point) (2) Neuroplastic changes including increased BDNF expression, neurogenesis in the hippocampus, and remodelling of neural circuits occur over weeks. (1 point) The delay suggests depression involves circuit-level adaptations, not simply low synaptic serotonin.

## Question 5 Answer: Myelination and Conduction Velocity

(a) Number of Nodes = Total distance / Internodal distance = 5.0 cm / 1.0 mm = 50 mm / 1.0 mm = 50 Nodes of Ranvier. (2 points for correct calculation and answer, 1 point for unit conversion) The action potential is regenerated at each Node (saltatory conduction).

(b)(i) Original velocity = 50 m/s. Continuous conduction is 10x slower: New velocity = 50 / 10 = 5.0 m/s. (2 points: 1 for method, 1 for answer)

(b)(ii) Time = Distance / Velocity. Before demyelination: t = 0.050 m / 50 m/s = 0.001 s = 1.0 ms. (1.5 points) After demyelination: t = 0.050 m / 5.0 m/s = 0.010 s = 10 ms. (1.5 points) The signal takes 10 times longer to travel the same distance, which would severely impair motor coordination and sensory processing. (1 point)

(c) Saltatory conduction is faster because: (1) Myelin acts as an electrical insulator, preventing ion leakage across the internodal membrane and reducing membrane capacitance. (1 point) (2) Local currents generated at a Node spread passively (much faster than ion channel gating) through the myelinated segment to the next Node. (1 point) (3) The action potential is only regenerated at the Nodes of Ranvier, where voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels are concentrated. (1 point) (4) Glial cells (oligodendrocytes in the CNS, Schwann cells in the PNS) wrap multiple layers of membrane around the axon, creating myelin. (1 point) (5) This 'jumping' from Node to Node (saltare = to leap) avoids the time-consuming process of sequentially opening ion channels along the entire axon length. (1 point) Nodes of Ranvier are essential because they contain the high density of ion channels needed to regenerate the action potential. (1 point)

(d) The student's hypothesis is INCORRECT. (1 point) Closer spacing of Nodes would NOT increase conduction velocity and may actually decrease it. (1 point) Reasons: (1) The passive spread of local current in myelinated segments depends on the length constant; if Nodes are too close, less passive spread occurs before regeneration, and more time is spent on channel gating relative to passive spread. (1 point) (2) Each Node has high membrane capacitance (no myelin); bringing Nodes closer together increases the overall capacitance per unit length, slowing conduction. (1 point) (3) Evolution has optimised internodal distance for each axon diameter; the ratio of internode length to axon diameter is typically ~100:1. (1 point) Optimal myelination balances the speed of passive spread with the need for regeneration at Nodes.
