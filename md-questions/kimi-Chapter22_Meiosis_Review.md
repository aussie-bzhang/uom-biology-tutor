# Answer Key

Detailed solutions and marking guide for all five questions.

## Question 1: Answer

**(a) Stage Identification [4 marks]**

The cells are in Metaphase I of meiosis. [1 mark]

* Homologous chromosome pairs (bivalents/tetrads) are aligned at the metaphase plate/equator. This is characteristic of Metaphase I, where homologous pairs (not individual chromosomes) line up. [1 mark]
* In contrast, during metaphase of MITOSIS, individual chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids) line up singly at the equator, NOT as homologous pairs. [1 mark]
* The presence of intact sister chromatids and the attachment of spindle fibres to homologous chromosomes (from opposite poles) confirms this is Metaphase I, not Metaphase II. [1 mark]

**(b) Diagram [4 marks]**

* Correct depiction of 2n = 4 chromosomes as TWO homologous pairs. [1 mark]
* Each chromosome shown with two sister chromatids joined at centromere. [1 mark]
* Homologous pairs aligned at the cell equator (metaphase plate). [1 mark]
* Spindle fibres shown attached to kinetochores, pulling from opposite poles. Labels: homologous pairs, sister chromatids, centromeres, spindle fibres. [1 mark]

**(c) Anaphase I Events [4 marks]**

* In Anaphase I, homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles of the cell. [1 mark]
* Sister chromatids REMAIN attached at the centromere and move together as a unit. [1 mark]
* This separation of homologous chromosomes is the physical basis of Mendel's Law of Segregation: the two alleles of a gene (located on homologous chromosomes) are separated into different gametes. [1 mark]
* After this division, each daughter cell has a haploid number of chromosomes (n), though each chromosome still consists of two chromatids. [1 mark]

**(d) Meiosis I vs Meiosis II Identification [3 marks]**

* The cells are in Meiosis II (specifically Anaphase II). [1 mark]
* Sister chromatids separate during Anaphase II, not during Anaphase I. In Anaphase I, homologous chromosomes separate while sister chromatids remain joined. [1 mark]
* This distinction is critical: Meiosis I is the REDUCTIVE division (reduces chromosome number from 2n to n), while Meiosis II is an EQUATIONAL division (similar to mitosis, separates sister chromatids). [1 mark]

* Its purpose is to determine whether the dominant-phenotype organism is homozygous dominant (RR) or heterozygous (Rr) by examining the offspring ratios. [1 mark]

**(b)(ii) Test Cross Punnett Square [3 marks]**

* F1 genotype: Rr; Test cross parent: rr [1 mark]
* Punnett square: 1/2 Rr (round) and 1/2 rr (wrinkled) [1 mark]

## Question 3: Answer

**(a) Hypothesis and Prediction [2 marks]**

* Hypothesis: If Mendel's Law of Segregation is correct, then the two alleles (w+ and w) for eye colour will separate during gamete formation in heterozygous individuals. [1 mark]
* Prediction: A cross between two heterozygous red-eyed flies (w+/w) will produce offspring in a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (red:white) and a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio (w+/w+ : w+/w : w/w). [1 mark]

**(b) Parental Cross Description [3 marks]**

* P generation: True-breeding wild-type red-eyed fly (w+/w+) crossed with white-eyed fly (w/w). [1 mark]
* P phenotypes: Red eyes x White eyes [1 mark]
* Rationale: Using true-breeding parents ensures the P generation is homozygous, making the genetic analysis of offspring straightforward. [1 mark]

**(c) Genetic Diagram [5 marks]**

* F1 cross: w+/w x w+/w [1 mark]
* Punnett square: Gametes w+ and w from each parent. Offspring: 1/4 w+/w+ (red), 2/4 w+/w (red), 1/4 w/w (white) [2 marks]
* Genotypic ratio F2: 1 w+/w+ : 2 w+/w : 1 w/w [1 mark]
* Phenotypic ratio F2: 3 red-eyed : 1 white-eyed [1 mark]

**(d) Test Cross Confirmation [4 marks]**

* Cross the F1 red-eyed fly (w+/w) with a white-eyed fly (w/w). [1 mark]
* If the F1 fly is heterozygous (w+/w), the expected offspring ratio is 1 red-eyed (w+/w) : 1 white-eyed (w/w). [1 mark]
* If the F1 fly were homozygous (w+/w+), ALL offspring would be red-eyed (w+/w). [1 mark]
* Observing approximately equal numbers of red-eyed and white-eyed offspring confirms the F1 parent was heterozygous, supporting segregation. [1 mark]

**(e) Controlled Variables [2 marks]**

* Temperature: Maintain at constant 25 degrees C throughout development, as temperature affects Drosophila development rate and viability. [1 mark]
* Sample size: Use sufficient number of crosses (e.g., 10-20 female flies per cross) to ensure statistically reliable offspring counts. [1 mark]
* (Alternative acceptable answers: food medium consistency, humidity, age of flies at crossing, genetic background of parental strains)

**(f) Cellular Mechanism of Segregation [4 marks]**

* During Meiosis I, homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) in prophase I and align at the metaphase plate in metaphase I. [1 mark]
* The w+ and w alleles are located on homologous chromosomes (one carrying w+, the other carrying w in a heterozygote). [1 mark]
* In anaphase I, homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles, physically segregating the two alleles into different daughter cells. [1 mark]
* This ensures that each gamete receives only ONE allele (either w+ or w), which is the cellular basis of Mendel's Law of Segregation. [1 mark]

## Question 4: Answer

**(a) Expected Phenotypic Ratio [2 marks]**

* Punnett square: Ww x Ww produces 1/4 WW : 2/4 Ww : 1/4 ww [1 mark]
* Expected phenotypic ratio: 3 white (WW + Ww) : 1 yellow (ww) [1 mark]

**(b)(i) Null Hypothesis [1 mark]**

* H0: The observed phenotypic ratio of white to yellow fruits is not significantly different from the expected 3:1 Mendelian ratio. (Or: Seed colour follows Mendelian inheritance with complete dominance.) [1 mark]

**(b)(ii) Expected Numbers [2 marks]**

* Total offspring = 200; Expected white = 3/4 x 200 = 150 [1 mark]
* Expected yellow = 1/4 x 200 = 50 [1 mark]

**(b)(iii) Chi-Square Calculation [3 marks]**

* White: (142 - 150)^2 / 150 = 64 / 150 = 0.427 [1 mark]
* Yellow: (58 - 50)^2 / 50 = 64 / 50 = 1.280
* chi2 = 0.427 + 1.280 = 1.707 [1 mark]
* Working shown clearly with formula: chi2 = sum of [(O-E)^2/E] [1 mark]

**(b)(iv) Degrees of Freedom and Critical Value [2 marks]**

* Degrees of freedom = number of phenotypic categories - 1 = 2 - 1 = 1 [1 mark]
* Critical value at p = 0.05, df = 1: 3.841 [1 mark]

**(b)(v) Conclusion [3 marks]**

* Since calculated chi2 (1.707) < critical value (3.841), we ACCEPT the null hypothesis. [1 mark]
* The difference between observed and expected values is not statistically significant. [1 mark]
* The data are consistent with Mendelian inheritance with a 3:1 phenotypic ratio, supporting the hypothesis that fruit colour is controlled by a single gene with complete dominance. [1 mark]

**(c) Sample Size Effect [2 marks]**

* With a smaller sample size (n=50), the expected numbers would be smaller (37.5 white, 12.5 yellow), making the chi-square value more sensitive to random deviations. [1 mark]
* The same proportional deviation would produce a larger chi2 value relative to expected values, potentially leading to rejection of the null hypothesis even if the underlying genetics are Mendelian. Larger samples provide more reliable estimates of true ratios. [1 mark]

## Question 5: Answer

**(a) Genotype Determination [6 marks]**

* I-1 (unaffected): Tt (carrier). Must carry t allele since child II-3 is affected (tt), but I-1 is phenotypically normal. [1 mark]
* I-2 (affected): tt (homozygous recessive). Affected individuals must have two disease alleles. [1 mark]
* II-1 (unaffected): Tt. Child of I-1 (Tt) and I-2 (tt). From I-2 must receive t; from I-1 could receive T or t. Since II-1 is unaffected, must have received T from I-1. Genotype = Tt. [1 mark]
* II-2 (unaffected): Tt. Same reasoning as II-1. Must be Tt since father (I-2) is tt and II-2 is phenotypically normal. [1 mark]
* II-3 (affected): tt. Received t from both parents. [1 mark]
* II-4 (unaffected): Tt or TT. Child of I-1 (Tt) and I-2 (tt). Must receive t from I-2. Since unaffected, receives T from I-1. However, if information from II-5 is not given, most likely Tt (50% probability). Accept Tt. [1 mark]

**(b)(i) Probability II-2 is a Carrier [2 marks]**

* Since II-2's father (I-2) is tt (affected), II-2 MUST have inherited t from his father. [1 mark]
* Since II-2 is phenotypically normal, he must have inherited T from his mother (I-1, who is Tt). Therefore, P(II-2 = Tt) = 1 (or 100%). [1 mark]

**(b)(ii) Probability of Affected Child [4 marks]**

* P(II-2 passes t) = 1/2 (since II-2 = Tt) [1 mark]
* P(partner is carrier, Tt) = 1/27; P(partner passes t | partner is Tt) = 1/2 [1 mark]
* P(child is tt) = P(II-2 passes t) x P(partner is Tt) x P(partner passes t | Tt) [1 mark]
* = 1/2 x 1/27 x 1/2 = 1/108 = 0.00926 or approximately 0.93% [1 mark]

**(c)(i) Metaphase I Diagram [3 marks]**

* Diagram shows ONE pair of homologous chromosomes (since n=1 for this gene in haploid state, 2n=2 in diploid). [1 mark]
* Both chromosomes in the homologous pair carry the t allele (since I-2 is tt). Label shows t allele on both homologs. [1 mark]
* Chromosomes aligned at metaphase plate with spindle fibres attached. Sister chromatids visible for each chromosome. [1 mark]

**(c)(ii) Gamete Allele Distribution [3 marks]**

* In anaphase I, homologous chromosomes separate. Since BOTH homologs carry the t allele, it does not matter which homolog goes to which daughter cell. [1 mark]
* After meiosis I, each secondary spermatocyte has one chromosome (with two t-carrying chromatids). [1 mark]
* After meiosis II (sister chromatid separation), ALL four gametes carry the t allele. This is because I-2 is homozygous recessive (tt) - there is no T allele to segregate. [1 mark]

**(c)(iii) Meiosis vs Mitosis for Genetic Variation [2 marks]**

* Meiosis produces haploid gametes through reductional division, allowing fusion of gametes from two different parents during fertilisation. This combines genetic material from two individuals. [1 mark]
* Mitosis produces genetically identical diploid daughter cells, which cannot contribute to new genetic combinations in offspring. Meiosis also involves independent assortment and crossing over, further increasing genetic diversity. [1 mark]
