## Part A: Short Answer & Concept Questions

**Question 1**

The six most abundant elements are: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Sulfur (S). Carbon is the primary backbone of biological macromolecules because it can form up to 4 covalent bonds simultaneously, allowing diverse molecular structures.

**Question 2**

Electronegativity is the attraction of an atomic nucleus for the bonding electrons. When the electronegativity difference is >1.9, the bond is ionic; when 0.4-1.9, it is polar covalent (unequal electron sharing); when <0.4, it is non-polar covalent (equal electron sharing).

**Question 3**

Water is polar because oxygen is much more electronegative (3.5) than hydrogen (2.1), creating polar O-H bonds. The bent molecular geometry means the partial negative charge on oxygen and partial positive charges on hydrogens are asymmetrically distributed. This polarity allows the partial positive hydrogen of one water molecule to be attracted to the partial negative oxygen (lone pair electrons) of another molecule, forming hydrogen bonds.

**Question 4**

Cohesion is the attraction between water molecules (hydrogen bonding between H2O molecules), e.g., surface tension allowing water striders to walk on water. Adhesion is the attraction of water molecules to other substances, e.g., water climbing up xylem vessels in plants due to adhesion to cellulose walls.

**Question 5**

(1) Dipole-dipole forces: between polar molecules (hydrophilic interaction). (2) Hydrogen bonding: between H bonded to F, O, or N and a lone pair of electrons. (3) London dispersion forces: between non-polar molecules (hydrophobic interaction); can exist between any molecules but is the only force between non-polar molecules.

**Question 6**

Non-polar molecules lack permanent dipole moments because their electron distributions are symmetrical. Without partial charges, they cannot form dipole-dipole interactions or hydrogen bonds. However, temporary fluctuations in electron density create instantaneous dipoles, which induce dipoles in neighboring molecules. These weak, transient attractions are London dispersion forces. Larger non-polar molecules with more electrons have stronger dispersion forces.

**Question 7**

The autoionization equation: H2O(l) + H2O(l) <=> H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq). In pure water at 25 degree C, [H+] = [OH-] = 1.0 x 10^-7 mol/L. Water acts as both a weak acid (donating H+) and a weak base (accepting H+).

**Question 8**

Hydrogen bonds between water molecules require substantial energy to break. Before water molecules can move faster (increase temperature), hydrogen bonds must be disrupted. This means a large amount of heat energy is absorbed with only a small temperature change, giving water its high specific heat capacity. This property stabilizes temperatures in aquatic environments and organisms.

## Part B: Application Problems

**Problem 1: Electronegativity and Bond Type Analysis**

a) Electronegativity differences and classifications:

| **Bond** | **EN Difference** | **Bond Type** | **Explanation** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| C-O | 3.5 - 2.5 = 1.0 | Polar covalent | 0.4 < 1.0 < 1.9 |
| N-H | 3.0 - 2.1 = 0.9 | Polar covalent | 0.4 < 0.9 < 1.9 |
| Na-Cl | 3.1 - 0.9 = 2.2 | Ionic | 2.2 > 1.9 |
| C-C | 2.5 - 2.5 = 0 | Non-polar covalent | 0 < 0.4 |

b) Mg-O: electronegativity difference = 3.5 - 1.2 = 2.3. Since 2.3 > 1.9, the bond is ionic, not non-polar covalent. The student is incorrect. Magnesium loses 2 electrons to oxygen, forming Mg2+ and O2- ions.

c) (i) 4 single bonds: tetrahedral arrangement (e.g., methane CH4). (ii) 2 single + 1 double: trigonal planar (e.g., ethene C2H4 has one C=C double bond and 2 C-H single bonds per carbon). This versatility allows carbon to form chains, rings, and complex 3D structures essential for carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

**Problem 2: pH Calculations and Biological Relevance**

a) (i) pH = -log(1.0 x 10^-4) = 4.0. Acidic. (ii) pH = -log(2.5 x 10^-9) = -log(2.5) + 9 = -0.40 + 9 = 8.60. Basic. (iii) [H+] = Kw/[OH-] = 10^-14 / 10^-3 = 10^-11 mol/L. pH = 11. Basic.

b) At pH 7.35: [H+] = 10^-7.35 = 4.47 x 10^-8 mol/L. At pH 7.45: [H+] = 10^-7.45 = 3.55 x 10^-8 mol/L.

c) At pH 5.2: [H+] = 10^-5.2 = 6.31 x 10^-6 mol/L. At pH 4.8: [H+] = 10^-4.8 = 1.58 x 10^-5 mol/L. Factor increase = 1.58 x 10^-5 / 6.31 x 10^-6 = 2.5 times (or use the shortcut: 10^(5.2-4.8) = 10^0.4 = 2.5).

d) pH affects enzyme activity and protein structure (3-D conformation). Enzymes have optimal pH ranges; deviation causes denaturation or reduced catalytic efficiency. For example, extreme pH can protonate or deprotonate amino acid side chains, disrupting ionic bonds that maintain protein structure.

**Problem 3: Specific Heat Capacity and Thermal Biology**

a) q = m x c x delta T = 500 g x 4.186 J g-1 degree C-1 x (37 - 20) degree C = 500 x 4.186 x 17 = 35,581 J = 35.6 kJ.

b) q = 500 g x 2.44 J g-1 degree C-1 x 17 degree C = 500 x 2.44 x 17 = 20,740 J = 20.7 kJ.

c) Water requires 35.6 kJ while ethanol requires only 20.7 kJ for the same temperature change. Water absorbs more heat per degree of temperature change, meaning aquatic environments experience smaller temperature fluctuations. This thermal stability protects aquatic organisms from temperature shock and maintains stable conditions for biochemical reactions.

d) m = 2000 kg = 2,000,000 g. q = 2,000,000 x 4.186 x (38 - 25) = 2,000,000 x 4.186 x 13 = 108,836,000 J = 108.8 MJ.

e) When sweat evaporates from skin, water molecules with the highest kinetic energy escape as vapor. Breaking hydrogen bonds requires significant energy (high heat of vaporization). This removes heat from the body surface, cooling the skin and helping maintain core body temperature during exercise.

**Problem 4: Intermolecular Forces and Molecular Properties**

a) (i) Two water molecules: Hydrogen bonding (H bonded to O interacts with lone pair on another O). (ii) Two methane molecules: London dispersion forces only (both non-polar, no permanent dipole). (iii) Ammonia and water: Hydrogen bonding (N-H and O-H both can participate in H-bonding). (iv) Na+ and water: Ion-dipole interaction (Na+ is attracted to the partial negative charge on oxygen).

b) Increasing boiling point order: CH4 < NH3 < CH3OH < H2O. CH4 has only weak London dispersion forces. NH3 can form hydrogen bonds but N is less electronegative than O, so H-bonds are weaker. CH3OH can form hydrogen bonds but only has one O-H group per molecule. H2O has two O-H groups and can form an extensive 3D hydrogen bond network, giving it the highest boiling point despite being the smallest molecule.

c) Non-polar amino acid side chains cannot form hydrogen bonds with water. When exposed to water, they disrupt the hydrogen-bonding network of surrounding water molecules. To minimize this disruption (entropy decrease), water molecules push non-polar groups together, forming a hydrophobic core. This hydrophobic effect drives protein folding, burying non-polar residues inside and placing polar residues on the surface where they can hydrogen-bond with water.

**Problem 5: Water Properties and Biological Applications**

a) At the water surface, water molecules are surrounded by other water molecules below and to the sides, but not above (air interface). This creates a net inward pull on surface molecules due to cohesive hydrogen bonds, minimizing surface area and creating surface tension. This 'elastic skin' effect is strong enough to support small insects. Cohesion (H-bonding between water molecules) is essential because without it, molecules would not be held together at the surface.

b) Cohesion holds water molecules together in a continuous column within xylem vessels. As water evaporates from leaf stomata (transpiration), the cohesive water column is pulled upward. Adhesion between water molecules and the hydrophilic cellulose walls of xylem vessels counters gravity by attracting water to the vessel walls. Together, cohesion (pulling) and adhesion (anchoring) enable water to rise to great heights in trees.

c) In water (polar solvent): Glucose (polar, many -OH groups) is soluble because it can form hydrogen bonds with water (dipole-dipole interactions). Olive oil (non-polar) is insoluble because it cannot form hydrogen bonds with water; hydrophobic effect causes oil to aggregate. In hexane (non-polar solvent): Glucose is insoluble because its polar -OH groups cannot interact favorably with non-polar hexane (only weak dispersion forces). Olive oil is soluble because both are non-polar and interact through London dispersion forces ('like dissolves like').
