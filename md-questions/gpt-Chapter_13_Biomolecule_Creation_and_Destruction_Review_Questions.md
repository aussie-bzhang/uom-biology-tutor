# Answer Key

## Revision Set 1 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

1. 1. Metabolism is the set of biochemical reactions in living systems. It is described as a network because reactions occur in linked pathways, where intermediates from carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins can feed into shared routes such as glycolysis, acetyl-CoA formation, and the citric acid cycle.
2. 2. Catabolism breaks large molecules into smaller common metabolites, often using hydrolysis and oxidation, and tends to be exergonic with negative ΔG. Anabolism builds larger molecules from smaller metabolites, often using condensation and reduction, and tends to be endergonic with positive ΔG.
3. 3. They receive carbon and electrons from many kinds of organic molecules. Carbohydrates, fats, and amino acids can be converted into intermediates that enter glycolysis or the citric acid cycle, where their energy is harvested.
4. 4. Glycogenesis is the synthesis of glycogen for storage, usually from glucose. Gluconeogenesis is the production of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors such as glycerol, lactate, or some amino acids.
5. 5. Food molecules can be oxidised to produce ATP, but their carbon skeletons can also be redirected into biosynthetic pathways to make cellular molecules such as carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

### B. Yes/No questions

1. Yes.
2. No. Several steps are bypassed or use different enzymes because some glycolysis steps are not reversible.
3. Yes. Fatty acids undergo beta-oxidation to produce acetyl-CoA.
4. No. Excess amino acids are not stored as a long-term protein reserve; carbon skeletons can be converted and nitrogen must be eliminated.
5. Yes. If substrate is depleted or diverted, the pathway can slow or stop.

### D. Application / calculation answers

1. Glycerol can enter glycolysis or gluconeogenesis. Fatty acids can enter mitochondria, be activated to fatty acyl-CoA, and undergo beta-oxidation to produce acetyl-CoA, NADH, and FADH2; acetyl-CoA can enter the citric acid cycle or, if abundant, ketogenesis/lipogenesis depending on conditions.
2. Five beta-oxidation cycles yield about 5 × 4 = 20 ATP from the cycles. If five acetyl-CoA molecules enter the citric acid cycle, they yield about 5 × 10 = 50 ATP. Estimated total = 70 ATP. Simplifying assumption: exactly five acetyl-CoA molecules are counted and each follows the lecture rule.
3. Glucose from polysaccharides can pass through glycolysis to pyruvate and then acetyl-CoA. Amino acids can be converted after deamination/transamination into pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, or citric acid cycle intermediates. When energy is abundant, carbon skeletons can be redirected to lipogenesis and stored as triglycerides.

## Revision Set 2 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

1. 1. Pathway rate can be regulated by changing enzyme abundance through gene expression, altering enzyme activity through covalent modification such as phosphorylation, feedback inhibition by pathway products, and substrate availability.
2. 2. Some glycolytic steps are strongly favoured in one direction and require different enzymes or bypass routes in gluconeogenesis. This prevents futile cycling and allows independent regulation of glucose breakdown and glucose production.
3. 3. Glycerol from triglycerides can enter glycolysis or gluconeogenesis. Fatty acids are converted through beta-oxidation to acetyl-CoA, which enters the citric acid cycle; acetyl-CoA can also contribute to lipid synthesis when energy and carbon are abundant.
4. 4. When acetyl-CoA accumulates beyond the immediate capacity of the citric acid cycle or metabolic needs, it can enter ketogenesis, forming ketone bodies such as acetoacetate.
5. 5. Essential amino acids cannot be synthesised by the body in sufficient amounts. They must be supplied in food so that proteins and other nitrogen-containing molecules can be built.

### B. Yes/No questions

1. Yes.
2. Yes. Anabolism often requires energy input and/or reducing power.
3. No. Lipids are energy-rich but generally cannot provide ATP as quickly as glucose.
4. Yes. Amino acids can feed into several central metabolic intermediates after nitrogen handling.
5. Yes. This is the principle of feedback inhibition.

### D. Application / calculation answers

1. Carbon is likely to be stored or used for biosynthesis rather than oxidised only for immediate ATP. With abundant ATP and glucose, cells may make glycogen, fatty acids/triglycerides, or other biomolecules depending on tissue and regulatory state.
2. Seven cycles yield about 7 × 4 = 28 ATP from beta-oxidation cycles alone. Total yield is higher because each acetyl-CoA produced can enter the citric acid cycle, generating additional reduced coenzymes that support ATP production through electron transport.
3. Scott is not correct. There is no dedicated storage form of amino acids equivalent to glycogen or adipose fat. Amino acids above immediate need are metabolised: amino groups must be removed and nitrogen eliminated through urea, while carbon skeletons may be used for energy or converted to carbohydrate or fat.
