# Answer Key

## Practice Set 1 Answers

SAQ1. An enzyme is a catalyst because it increases reaction rate without being permanently consumed. It binds substrates, stabilises the transition state, releases products, and can be used again.

SAQ2. Activation energy is the energy needed to bring reactants to the transition state. Enzymes lower this barrier by providing an alternative pathway; they do not change Delta G.

SAQ3. Specific enzymes act on specific substrates, bonds, stereochemical forms, or reaction types. This lets cells regulate individual pathway steps.

SAQ4. Lock-and-key treats the enzyme as relatively rigid with a matching active site. Induced-fit says substrate binding changes the active site conformation to improve binding and catalysis.

SAQ5. Active-site chemistry determines how R groups bind, orient, strain, donate/accept protons or charges, and stabilise transition states. Shape matters, but chemical interactions often explain catalysis more directly.

Yes/No: Y/N1 Yes; Y/N2 No; Y/N3 Yes; Y/N4 Yes; Y/N5 No.

MCQ: MCQ1 B; MCQ2 C; MCQ3 B; MCQ4 C; MCQ5 A; MCQ6 B; MCQ7 B; MCQ8 D; MCQ9 A; MCQ10 B.

APP1. Catalase provides a lower-activation-energy pathway for the same decomposition reaction. It changes rate, not product identity or overall free energy.

APP2. The reaction reaches equilibrium faster, but the final equilibrium ratio is not changed by the enzyme. Products and reactants remain nearly equal at equilibrium.

APP3. The plateau is Vmax. Most or all enzyme active sites are occupied, so adding more substrate alone cannot greatly increase rate.

## Practice Set 2 Answers

SAQ1. At low temperature, molecular motion is low and activity is low. At the optimum, structure and motion are suitable. Above the optimum, heat disrupts non-covalent interactions and can denature the enzyme.

SAQ2. pH changes alter protonation and charges of amino-acid R groups, disrupting ionic interactions, hydrogen bonds, active-site chemistry, and tertiary structure.

SAQ3. Cofactors are non-protein helpers needed by some enzymes. Coenzymes are organic molecules that transfer chemical groups. Prosthetic groups are tightly or permanently attached built-in coenzymes.

SAQ4. Histidine often participates in acid-base catalysis because it can donate or accept protons near physiological pH. Alanine lacks this reactive group, reducing catalysis.

SAQ5. Enzymes control metabolism through specificity, localisation, substrate availability, cofactors, activators, inhibitors, pH and temperature sensitivity, and regulation of pathway steps.

Yes/No: Y/N1 Yes; Y/N2 Yes; Y/N3 Yes; Y/N4 No; Y/N5 Yes.

MCQ: MCQ1 D; MCQ2 B; MCQ3 B; MCQ4 A; MCQ5 A; MCQ6 A; MCQ7 B; MCQ8 A; MCQ9 A; MCQ10 A.

APP1. At 60 C, many human enzymes lose activity because heat disrupts tertiary structure and the active site.

APP2. v = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]) = 100 x 5/(5 + 5) = 50 units/min. When [S] = Km, v = 1/2 Vmax.

APP3. Lower activation energy means more substrate molecules reach the transition state per unit time. Therefore rate increases dramatically even though Delta G and equilibrium are unchanged.

# One-page Revision Checklist

* Enzymes are biological catalysts; most are proteins, but some RNAs can be catalytic.
* Enzymes lower activation energy / Delta G double dagger, not overall Delta G.
* Enzymes do not change equilibrium constants; they help equilibrium be reached faster.
* Active sites bind substrates through shape and especially through chemical interactions involving R groups.
* Common active-site residues include His, Cys, Asp, Arg and Glu.
* Reaction rate can be measured by substrate disappearance or product appearance over time.
* Michaelis-Menten behaviour: rate increases with substrate concentration then approaches Vmax as enzymes saturate.
* Temperature and pH affect enzyme shape, active-site charge, and catalytic activity.
* Cofactors may be inorganic ions or organic helpers; coenzymes and prosthetic groups are important examples.
* Competitive, noncompetitive and irreversible inhibitors affect rate; they are introduced here and developed later.
