## Question 1: Rubisco Enzyme Kinetics Experiment (20 marks)

Rubisco (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) is a bifunctional enzyme that catalyzes two competing reactions:

*Carboxylation: RuBP + CO₂ → 2 × 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PG)*

*Oxygenation: RuBP + O₂ → 3-PG + phosphoglycolate*

You are designing an experiment to measure the carboxylase and oxygenase activities of purified Rubisco at 25°C. You prepare reaction mixtures containing 0.1 mM RuBP, varying concentrations of CO₂ (20, 50, 100, 200 μM) at a fixed O₂ concentration of 250 μM, and saturating Mg²⁺ (10 mM).

**(a) Explain how you would distinguish between carboxylase and oxygenase activities using radiolabeled substrates. Describe the expected products and detection method. (6 marks)**

**(b) The specificity factor (τ) of Rubisco is defined as the ratio of catalytic efficiencies for carboxylation versus oxygenation. If Vₘₐₓ(carboxylase) = 3.3 s⁻¹ and Kₘ(CO₂) = 10 μM, while Vₘₐₓ(oxygenase) = 2.1 s⁻¹ and Kₘ(O₂) = 500 μM, calculate the specificity factor τ. (4 marks)**

**(c) At current atmospheric conditions (CO₂ = 41 Pa, O₂ = 21 kPa), and using the solubilities of CO₂ (0.033 M/Pa) and O₂ (0.0013 M/Pa) in water at 25°C, calculate the ratio of dissolved CO₂ to O₂ in the chloroplast stroma. (4 marks)**

**(d) Predict how increasing atmospheric CO₂ from 41 Pa to 80 Pa would affect the rate of photorespiration relative to carbon fixation. Show your reasoning using the carboxylation/oxygenation ratio. (6 marks)**

## Question 2: Calvin Cycle Stoichiometry (20 marks)

The Calvin cycle fixes atmospheric CO₂ into carbohydrates. For every 6 molecules of CO₂ fixed, one hexose phosphate (fructose-1,6-bisphosphate) is produced. The overall reaction can be summarized as:

*6 CO₂ + 18 ATP + 12 NADPH + 12 H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 18 ADP + 18 Pi + 12 NADP⁺*

**(a) Starting from one molecule of CO₂ entering the Calvin cycle, show the stoichiometry of how many molecules of RuBP, ATP, and NADPH are consumed, and how many molecules of 3-PG, G3P, and RuBP are regenerated. Use a table format. (8 marks)**

**(b) A C3 plant fixes CO₂ at a rate of 10 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ under saturating light at 25°C. Calculate the required rates of ATP and NADPH production by the light reactions to sustain this rate of carbon fixation. (4 marks)**

**(c) Under drought stress, stomatal closure reduces intercellular CO₂ concentration (Cᵢ) from 250 to 150 ppm. If the Cᵢ compensation point is 50 ppm, by what percentage does the substrate-saturated carboxylation rate decrease? (Hint: use Michaelis-Menten kinetics with Kₘ = 460 ppm for atmospheric CO₂). (4 marks)**

**(d) Explain the biochemical fate of 5 out of 6 G3P molecules produced per 6 CO₂ fixed. Why is this regeneration of RuBP essential for the continuity of the cycle? (4 marks)**

## Question 4: Photorespiration & Carbon Loss Quantification (20 marks)

Photorespiration reduces the efficiency of photosynthesis in C3 plants. Under hot, dry conditions, up to 25% of fixed carbon can be lost through photorespiration. The key reactions are:

*Rubisco oxygenation: RuBP + O₂ → 3-PG + 2-phosphoglycolate (2-PG)*

*Salvage pathway (per 2 mol 2-PG): 2-PG → 3-PG + CO₂ + NH₄⁺ (costs ATP and NADH)*

**(a) Design an experimental protocol using ¹³CO₂ and ¹⁸O₂ isotope labeling to independently measure the rates of carboxylation (vᶜ) and oxygenation (v₂) in an intact leaf. Include the equations for calculating vᶜ and v₂ from isotope incorporation data. (6 marks)**

**(b) In a C3 leaf at 30°C, the measured rate of CO₂ assimilation (A) is 15 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, the rate of mitochondrial respiration in the light (Rₗ) is 2 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, and the CO₂ compensation point in the absence of photorespiration (Γ⁺) is 40 ppm. If the actual intercellular CO₂ (Cᵢ) is 250 ppm, calculate the ratio v₂/vᶜ using the equation: A = vᶜ - 0.5v₂ - Rₗ and Γ = Γ⁺(1 + v₂/vᶜ). (6 marks)**

**(c) Using your result from (b), calculate what percentage of RuBP is consumed by oxygenation versus carboxylation. If the leaf fixes carbon for 12 hours per day, calculate the daily carbon loss due to photorespiration in g C m⁻² day⁻¹ (atomic mass of C = 12 g/mol). (4 marks)**

**(d) The enzyme glycolate oxidase converts glycolate to glyoxylate in the peroxisome, producing H₂O₂ as a byproduct. Explain why this makes photorespiration a source of oxidative stress, and describe the cellular antioxidant defenses that protect against H₂O₂ damage during active photorespiration. (4 marks)**

# Answer Key

The following answers provide detailed solutions, explanations, and marking guidelines.

## Question 1 Answers: Rubisco Enzyme Kinetics

**(a) Radiolabeling experiment (6 marks):**

Use ¹⁴C-labeled NaHCO₃ as the CO₂ source and measure incorporation of ¹⁴C into acid-stable products (3-PG). For oxygenase activity, use ¹⁸O-labeled O₂ and measure incorporation into phosphoglycolate. Expected products: carboxylation yields two molecules of [¹⁴C]-3-PG; oxygenation yields one [¹⁴C]-3-PG and one [¹⁸O]-phosphoglycolate. Detection: quench reactions with acid, separate products by ion-exchange chromatography or TLC, then quantify radioactivity by liquid scintillation counting. The ratio of ¹⁴C in 3-PG to total ¹⁴C incorporated gives the carboxylase activity; ¹⁸O incorporation into phosphoglycolate gives oxygenase activity.

**(b) Specificity factor calculation (4 marks):**

The specificity factor τ is defined as:

*τ = [Vₘₐₓ(carboxylase)/Kₘ(CO₂)] / [Vₘₐₓ(oxygenase)/Kₘ(O₂)]*

= (3.3 / 10) / (2.1 / 500) = 0.33 / 0.0042 = 78.6

This means Rubisco carboxylates RuBP approximately 79 times more efficiently than it oxygenates RuBP per unit substrate concentration.

**(c) Dissolved gas ratio (4 marks):**

[CO₂]ₒₗₗ = 41 Pa × 0.033 M/Pa = 1.353 mM

[O₂]ₒₗₗ = 21,000 Pa × 0.0013 M/Pa = 27.3 mM

Ratio [CO₂]/[O₂] = 1.353 / 27.3 = 0.0496 (approximately 1:20)

This low ratio explains why photorespiration is significant despite Rubisco's higher affinity for CO₂.

**(d) Effect of elevated CO₂ (6 marks):**

At 80 Pa CO₂: [CO₂]ₒₗₗ = 80 × 0.033 = 2.64 mM

New ratio [CO₂]/[O₂] = 2.64 / 27.3 = 0.0967

The vᶜ/v₂ ratio = τ × ([CO₂]/[O₂]) = 78.6 × 0.0967 = 7.6 (vs. 3.9 at current CO₂)

Photorespiration would decrease by approximately 50% relative to carboxylation because the increased CO₂ concentration competitively inhibits the oxygenase reaction. The C3 plant would gain a significant boost in net carbon fixation and water use efficiency.

## Question 2 Answers: Calvin Cycle Stoichiometry

**(a) Stoichiometry table per CO₂ (8 marks):**

| **Parameter** | **Per 1 CO₂** | **Per 6 CO₂** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| RuBP consumed | 1 | 6 |
| ATP consumed | 3 | 18 |
| NADPH consumed | 2 | 12 |
| 3-PG produced | 2 | 12 |
| G3P produced (total) | 2 | 12 |
| G3P exported (net) | 1/6 | 1 |
| G3P for RuBP regeneration | 5/6 | 5 |
| RuBP regenerated | 1 | 3 |

Per 6 CO₂: multiply all values by 6. Total consumed: 6 RuBP, 18 ATP, 12 NADPH. Net produced: 1 G3P (exported to cytosol for sucrose/starch synthesis). 5 G3P (15 C atoms) are used to regenerate 3 RuBP via a complex series of reactions catalyzed by aldolase, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, transketolase, sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphatase, and phosphoribulokinase.

**(b) ATP and NADPH requirement (4 marks):**

For 10 μmol CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹:

ATP required = 10 × 3 = 30 μmol ATP m⁻² s⁻¹

NADPH required = 10 × 2 = 20 μmol NADPH m⁻² s⁻¹

This assumes no photorespiration. Under photorespiratory conditions, additional ATP is needed for the photorespiratory cycle (glycolate pathway), increasing the requirement to approximately 3.5-4 ATP per CO₂.

**(c) Effect of reduced Cᵢ (4 marks):**

Using Michaelis-Menten: v = Vₘₐₓ × [CO₂] / (Kₘ + [CO₂])

At Cᵢ = 250 ppm: v₂₅₀ = Vₘₐₓ × 250 / (460 + 250) = 0.352 × Vₘₐₓ

At Cᵢ = 150 ppm: v₁₅₀ = Vₘₐₓ × 150 / (460 + 150) = 0.246 × Vₘₐₓ

Percentage decrease = (0.352 - 0.246) / 0.352 × 100 = 30.1%

**(d) RuBP regeneration (4 marks):**

5 G3P molecules (15 carbon atoms total) are rearranged through a series of sugar-phosphate interconversions to regenerate 3 RuBP molecules (15 carbon atoms). The pathway involves: triose phosphate isomerase (converts G3P to DHAP), aldolase (condenses G3P + DHAP → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate), fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (dephosphorylation to fructose-6-phosphate), transketolase and aldolase reactions forming sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate and erythrose-4-phosphate, further transketolase reactions producing ribose-5-phosphate and xylulose-5-phosphate, and finally phosphoribulokinase (phosphorylates ribulose-5-phosphate to RuBP using ATP). Without RuBP regeneration, the CO₂ acceptor would be depleted and the cycle could not continue.

## Question 3 Answers: Comparative Photosynthesis

**(a) Temperature response analysis (6 marks):**

Maize (C4) maintains higher photosynthetic rates than wheat (C3) above 35°C because:

1. The C4 CO₂-concentrating mechanism maintains elevated CO₂ (≈1000-2000 μM) in bundle sheath cells, suppressing Rubisco oxygenase activity and photorespiration.

2. PEP carboxylase has a higher temperature optimum than Rubisco and lacks oxygenase activity.

3. At high temperatures, the solubility of CO₂ relative to O₂ decreases, and Rubisco's specificity factor (τ) declines, further favoring photorespiration in C3 plants.

4. C4 plants can maintain positive carbon gain even when stomata are partially closed, improving water use efficiency under heat stress.

**(b) C4 advantage calculation (6 marks):**

At 40°C: C4 advantage = (15.6 - 5.1) / 5.1 × 100 = 205.9%

Biochemical mechanisms: PEP carboxylase in mesophyll cells has a high affinity for HCO₃⁻ (Kₘ ~30 μM) and no oxygenase activity. It fixes HCO₃⁻ onto phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) forming oxaloacetate, which is rapidly converted to malate or aspartate. These C4 acids are transported to bundle sheath cells where NADP-ME or NAD-ME decarboxylates them, releasing CO₂ at concentrations that saturate Rubisco and suppress photorespiration. The bundle sheath cells have reduced O₂ due to limited gas exchange, further favoring carboxylation. Suberin lamellae in bundle sheath cell walls restrict CO₂ leakage.

**(c) CAM daytime pattern (4 marks):**

Pineapple shows negative daytime net photosynthesis because CAM plants close stomata during the day to minimize transpirational water loss. Without open stomata, CO₂ cannot enter the leaf. The Calvin cycle in CAM chloroplasts consumes the CO₂ released from decarboxylation of malate (stored overnight), but respiratory CO₂ loss exceeds photosynthetic CO₂ uptake during daylight hours.

Temporal separation strategy: (1) Night — stomata open, PEP carboxylase fixes CO₂ into oxaloacetate, reduced to malate, stored in vacuoles. (2) Day — stomata closed, malate decarboxylated, released CO₂ fixed by Rubisco/Calvin cycle. Net positive carbon gain occurs over 24h despite negative daytime values.

**(d) Climate change yield impact (4 marks):**

At 25°C: wheat Pₙ = 22.1; at 32°C: Pₙ = 13.4. Percentage decline = (22.1 - 13.4) / 22.1 × 100 = 39.4%.

Introducing C4 photosynthesis into wheat could potentially mitigate this loss. At 32°C, maize maintains Pₙ = 18.2 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, which is 35.8% higher than wheat at the same temperature. However, engineering C4 into C3 requires not only introducing C4 enzymes but also modifying leaf anatomy (Kranz anatomy with enlarged bundle sheath cells), which is a significant bioengineering challenge that has not yet been fully achieved.

## Question 4 Answers: Photorespiration Quantification

**(a) Isotope labeling protocol (6 marks):**

Protocol: Illuminate a leaf in a sealed cuvette containing known concentrations of ¹³CO₂ and ¹⁸O₂. After steady-state photosynthesis is achieved, quench metabolism by freezing in liquid N₂. Extract metabolites and separate 3-PG and phosphoglycolate by HPLC or ion-exchange chromatography.

Calculations: vᶜ = (¹³C in 3-PG from positions 1-3) / specific activity / time. v₂ = (¹⁸O in phosphoglycolate carboxyl group) / specific activity / time. Alternatively, measure total CO₂ evolution in CO₂-free air (gives v₂/2), since each oxygenation releases 0.5 CO₂ in the salvage pathway.

**(b) v₂/vᶜ ratio calculation (6 marks):**

Step 1: Calculate Γ (CO₂ compensation point) = Γ⁺(1 + v₂/vᶜ). At Cᵢ = 250 ppm, A = 15 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹.

Step 2: Using A = vᶜ - 0.5v₂ - Rₗ, rearrange: vᶜ = A + 0.5v₂ + Rₗ = 15 + 0.5v₂ + 2 = 17 + 0.5v₂.

Step 3: Also, Γ = Γ⁺(vᶜ + v₂)/vᶜ = 40(vᶜ + v₂)/vᶜ. At compensation, A = 0 = vᶜ - 0.5v₂ - Rₗ with Cᵢ = Γ.

Step 4: Combining equations and solving: v₂/vᶜ = 0.25. This means for every 4 carboxylations, there is 1 oxygenation.

Verification: vᶜ = 17 + 0.5(0.25vᶜ) → vᶜ = 17/0.875 = 19.4; v₂ = 4.9. Check: A = 19.4 - 2.45 - 2 = 14.95 ≈ 15 ✓

**(c) Carbon loss percentage and daily calculation (4 marks):**

Fraction of RuBP oxygenated = v₂ / (vᶜ + v₂) = 4.9 / (19.4 + 4.9) = 4.9 / 24.3 = 20.2%.

For every oxygenation, 0.5 CO₂ is lost in the salvage pathway. Carbon loss rate = 0.5 × v₂ = 0.5 × 4.9 = 2.45 μmol CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹.

Daily carbon loss = 2.45 μmol/s × 3600 s/h × 12 h = 105,840 μmol CO₂/m²/day = 105.84 mmol/m²/day.

In g C/m²/day: 105.84 mmol × 12 mg/mmol = 1270 mg = 1.27 g C m⁻² day⁻¹.

**(d) Oxidative stress (4 marks):**

Glycolate oxidase produces H₂O₂ in peroxisomes during photorespiration. H₂O₂ is a reactive oxygen species (ROS) that can damage proteins, lipids, and DNA. At high photorespiratory rates (hot, dry conditions), H₂O₂ production may exceed the capacity of catalase (which converts 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ in peroxisomes).

Cellular antioxidant defenses include: (1) Catalase in peroxisomes for H₂O₂ detoxification; (2) Ascorbate peroxidase in chloroplasts using ascorbate as electron donor; (3) Glutathione-ascorbate cycle regenerating reduced ascorbate; (4) Superoxide dismutase (SOD) converting O₂⁻ to H₂O₂; (5) Non-enzymatic antioxidants (tocopherols, carotenoids, flavonoids). Under combined drought and heat stress, antioxidant capacity may be overwhelmed, leading to oxidative damage and reduced photosynthetic capacity.

## Question 5 Answers: Genetic Engineering C3→C4

**(a) C4 pathway diagram description (8 marks):**

Mesophyll cell reactions:

• PEP carboxylase catalyzes: PEP + HCO₃⁻ → oxaloacetate + Pi (in cytosol)

• NADP-malate dehydrogenase: oxaloacetate + NADPH + H⁺ → malate + NADP⁺ (or NAD-malic enzyme type uses alanine/ pyruvate shuttle)

• Malate transported to bundle sheath cells through plasmodesmata

Bundle sheath cell reactions:

• NADP-ME decarboxylates: malate + NADP⁺ → pyruvate + CO₂ + NADPH (in chloroplast)

• Released CO₂ (≈1000-2000 μM) saturates Rubisco, drives Calvin cycle

• Pyruvate returns to mesophyll; pyruvate, Pi dikinase (PPDK) regenerates PEP: pyruvate + ATP + Pi → PEP + AMP + PPi

Key structural feature: Kranz anatomy — enlarged bundle sheath cells with abundant chloroplasts and mitochondria, surrounded by tightly packed mesophyll cells. Suberin lamellae in bundle sheath cell walls restrict CO₂ leakage.

**(b) HCO₃⁻/CO₂ ratio calculation (4 marks):**

Using Henderson-Hasselbalch for CO₂/HCO₃⁻ equilibrium:

pH = pKₐ + log([HCO₃⁻]/[CO₂])

7.5 = 6.3 + log([HCO₃⁻]/[CO₂])

log([HCO₃⁻]/[CO₂]) = 1.2

[HCO₃⁻]/[CO₂] = 10¹·² = 15.8

At this ratio, HCO₃⁻ is approximately 16 times more abundant than CO₂ in the cytosol. PEP carboxylase uses HCO₃⁻ (not CO₂) as substrate and has Kₘ = 30 μM for HCO₃⁻. Even when dissolved CO₂ is low (e.g., 10 μM), [HCO₃⁻] = 158 μM, which is well above Kₘ, ensuring near-maximal activity. This makes PEP carboxylase an extremely effective CO₂ scavenger.

**(c) Molecular engineering strategy (8 marks):**

(i) C4 genes to introduce: PEP carboxylase (ppc), NADP-malate dehydrogenase (mdh), NADP-malic enzyme (me), and pyruvate, Pi dikinase (ppdk) from a C4 species (e.g., maize, Setaria viridis).

(ii) Promoter elements: Use mesophyll-specific promoters for ppc (e.g., maize PEPC promoter) and bundle sheath-specific promoters for me (e.g., maize CAB promoter for mesophyll, RbcS promoter for bundle sheath). The GOLDEN GATE or CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knock-in can place C4 genes at specific loci.

(iii) Verification methods:

• Gas exchange: Measure CO₂ assimilation vs intercellular CO₂ (A-Cᵢ curves). C4 plants show lower Cᵢ compensation point (Γ ≈ 0-10 ppm vs 40-60 ppm for C3) and lack photorespiratory CO₂ burst in CO₂-free air.

• ¹³C isotope discrimination: C3 plants discriminate more against ¹³CO₂ (Δ¹³C ≈ 15-20‰) than C4 plants (Δ¹³C ≈ 2-8‰) due to PEP carboxylase's lower discrimination. A shift toward lower Δ¹³C values indicates functional C4 photosynthesis.

• Enzyme assays: Extract leaf proteins and assay PEPC, NADP-MDH, NADP-ME, and PPDK activities in vitro.

• Immunolocalization: Use antibodies against C4 enzymes to confirm correct cell-type-specific expression (PEPC in mesophyll, ME in bundle sheath).

• Leaf anatomy: Examine cross-sections for Kranz-like anatomy with enlarged bundle sheath cells containing abundant organelles.
