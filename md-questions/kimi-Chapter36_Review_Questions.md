# Application Questions (5 Problems)

The following five application problems cover experimental design, quantitative analysis, and integrative biochemistry from Lecture 36. Show all calculations and reasoning.

## Question 1: Muscle Contraction — ATP Hydrolysis & Sarcomere Shortening

During skeletal muscle contraction, each myosin head undergoes a cross-bridge cycle that hydrolyses 1 ATP molecule. A single sarcomere contains approximately 20,000 myosin heads and shortens from an initial length of 2.2 um to 1.6 um during a typical contraction.

(a) Calculate the total number of ATP molecules hydrolysed if all myosin heads in one sarcomere complete exactly one cross-bridge cycle.

(b) If a muscle fibre contains 5,000 serially arranged sarcomeres and contracts at a velocity of 2.0 um/ms, calculate the time required for complete contraction (express your answer in milliseconds).

(c) Explain how the structure of the thick (myosin) and thin (actin) filaments relates to the striated appearance of skeletal muscle, and predict what would happen to the H-zone and I-band during the contraction described above.

## Question 2: Epithelial Tissue — Surface Area & Nutrient Absorption

The small intestine is lined with simple columnar epithelium featuring villi and microvilli (brush border). A segment of small intestine has the following dimensions: length = 30 cm, inner diameter = 2.5 cm. Each enterocyte (absorptive cell) has approximately 3,000 microvilli, each 1.0 um long and 0.1 um in diameter. The villi increase the surface area by a factor of 10, and the microvilli increase it by a further factor of 20.

(a) Calculate the basic (smooth) inner surface area of the intestinal segment.

(b) Calculate the total functional absorptive surface area after accounting for villi and microvilli.

(c) A patient has a genetic condition that reduces microvilli density by 50%. Calculate the percentage reduction in total functional surface area and predict the clinical consequence for nutrient absorption.

(d) Name the specific germ layer that gives rise to this epithelial lining and justify your answer with reference to its function.

## Question 3: Germ Layer Contributions — Organ Development Analysis

The small intestine is a complex organ composed of multiple tissue types, each derived from specific germ layers during embryonic development.

(a) Identify which of the three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) contributes to EACH of the following tissue components of the small intestine:

1. **Epithelial lining (including secretory cells and absorptive cells)**
2. **Smooth muscle layers (muscularis externa)**
3. **Nervous tissue (enteric nervous system, e.g., Auerbach's and Meissner's plexuses)**
4. **Connective tissue (lamina propria, submucosa)**
5. **Blood vessels and lymphatic vessels**

(b) Explain why the coordinated development of all three germ layers is essential for the intestine to perform its dual functions of digestion and absorption. Include specific examples of how tissues derived from different germ layers interact during these processes.

(c) Describe an experimental approach (e.g., lineage tracing) that could be used to confirm the germ layer origin of each tissue type in the adult intestine.

## Question 4: Plant Vascular Tissue — Transport Efficiency

A transpiring leaf has the following water potential values at different locations: soil (-0.3 MPa), root xylem (-0.5 MPa), leaf xylem (-1.2 MPa), and atmospheric air (-50 MPa). The leaf is 15 m above the ground. Phloem sap contains 0.4 M sucrose at the source (leaf) and 0.1 M sucrose at the sink (root). The temperature is 25 degC (RT = 2.48 L.MPa/mol).

(a) Calculate the total water potential gradient driving water movement from the soil to the leaf xylem. Explain the direction of water flow based on your calculation.

(b) Using the pressure-flow hypothesis, calculate the pressure potential difference (Delta Psi\_p) that drives phloem sap translocation from source to sink. Use the formula: Psi = Psi\_p + Psi\_s, where Psi\_s = -iCRT (i = 1 for sucrose).

(c) Design an experiment to measure the rate of water transport in the xylem using a potometer. Include the independent variable, dependent variable, and at least two controlled variables.

(d) Predict how the water potential values would change under drought conditions (dry soil) and explain the adaptive significance of these changes for the plant.

## Question 5: Integrated Organ Systems — Digestive & Endocrine Coordination

After consuming a carbohydrate-rich meal, blood glucose concentration rises from a fasting level of 5.0 mM to a peak of 8.5 mM. The following organs and tissues are involved in glucose homeostasis: pancreas (islets of Langerhans), liver, skeletal muscle, adipose tissue, and the small intestine epithelium.

(a) Identify the specific tissue types (epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous) found in each of the following organs, and for each tissue, describe its specific role in the glucose homeostasis response:

1. **Pancreas**
2. **Liver**
3. **Small intestine**

(b) Calculate the percentage increase in blood glucose concentration after the meal. If a patient's fasting glucose is 7.0 mM (diabetic range) and rises to 12.0 mM after the same meal, calculate the additional insulin (in relative units) that would theoretically be required to restore normal glucose levels, assuming insulin requirement is proportional to glucose excess above 5.0 mM.

(c) Describe the nervous tissue involvement (parasympathetic and sympathetic innervation) in coordinating digestive function after a meal. Include the specific nerve (vagus nerve) and its effect on target tissues.

(d) Explain how ALL FOUR animal tissue types (epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous) work together in the small intestine to achieve efficient nutrient absorption after a meal. This should be an integrated explanation demonstrating the functional hierarchy from tissue to organ.

# Answer Key

Detailed solutions and marking guide for the application problems.

## Answer to Question 1: Muscle Contraction

(a) Total ATP hydrolysed = 20,000 myosin heads x 1 ATP/head = 20,000 ATP molecules per sarcomere per cycle.

(b) Each sarcomere shortens by: 2.2 um - 1.6 um = 0.6 um. With 5,000 sarcomeres in series, total shortening = 5,000 x 0.6 um = 3,000 um. Time = distance / velocity = 3,000 um / 2.0 um/ms = 1,500 ms (or 1.5 s).

(c) Myosin (thick) filaments and actin (thin) filaments are arranged in repeating sarcomere units, creating the characteristic banding pattern: A-band (entire thick filament), I-band (thin filaments only), H-zone (thick filaments only), Z-discs (boundaries). During contraction, myosin heads pull actin filaments toward the M-line, causing the I-band and H-zone to shorten or disappear, while the A-band remains constant in width.

## Answer to Question 2: Epithelial Surface Area

(a) Basic surface area = circumference x length = pi x d x L = 3.14 x 2.5 cm x 30 cm = 235.5 cm^2.

(b) Total functional area = 235.5 cm^2 x 10 (villi) x 20 (microvilli) = 47,100 cm^2 = 4.71 m^2.

(c) With 50% reduced microvilli, the microvilli enhancement factor drops to 10 (instead of 20). New total area = 235.5 x 10 x 10 = 23,550 cm^2. Reduction = (47,100 - 23,550) / 47,100 x 100% = 50%. Clinical consequence: malabsorption syndrome, leading to nutrient deficiency, diarrhoea, and weight loss.

(d) Endoderm. The endoderm gives rise to the epithelial linings of the digestive tract, respiratory tract, and associated glands. This is consistent with its function in absorption and secretion, as endoderm-derived tissues are specialized for exchange with the external environment (lumen).

## Answer to Question 3: Germ Layer Contributions

(a) Germ layer origins:

1. **Epithelial lining — ENDODERM (forms the mucosal lining of the digestive tract)**
2. **Smooth muscle — MESODERM (specifically splanchnic mesoderm)**
3. **Nervous tissue — ECTODERM (neural crest cells migrate to form enteric ganglia)**
4. **Connective tissue — MESODERM (forms lamina propria and submucosa)**
5. **Blood/lymphatic vessels — MESODERM (angiogenesis from mesodermal precursors)**

(b) All three germ layers are required because digestion and absorption involve coordinated mechanical, chemical, and regulatory processes. The endoderm-derived epithelium secretes digestive enzymes (e.g., brush border enzymes) and absorbs nutrients. The mesoderm-derived smooth muscle creates peristaltic movements to mix and propel food, while connective tissue provides structural support and contains blood vessels for nutrient transport. The ectoderm-derived enteric nervous system (the 'second brain') autonomously regulates secretion and motility, coordinating responses to luminal contents. Without neural input from ectoderm, the gut could not respond adaptively to different types of meals.

(c) Lineage tracing using Cre-lox recombination: (i) Generate transgenic mice expressing Cre recombinase under germ layer-specific promoters (e.g., Foxa2 for endoderm, Brachyury for mesoderm, Sox1 for ectoderm). (ii) Cross with reporter mice (e.g., ROSA26-LSL-tdTomato). (iii) In offspring, cells derived from each germ layer will be permanently labeled with fluorescent protein. (iv) Examine adult intestine sections by fluorescence microscopy to identify germ layer origin of each tissue type.

## Answer to Question 4: Plant Vascular Transport

(a) Total water potential gradient = Psi\_leaf - Psi\_soil = (-1.2) - (-0.3) = -0.9 MPa. Water flows from higher to lower water potential (soil to leaf), driven by this gradient. The negative gradient indicates passive water movement from soil (-0.3 MPa) to leaf (-1.2 MPa).

(b) At source (leaf): Psi\_s = -(1)(0.4 mol/L)(2.48 L.MPa/mol) = -0.992 MPa. At sink (root): Psi\_s = -(1)(0.1)(2.48) = -0.248 MPa. Assuming equal pressure potentials at equilibrium loading/unloading, the pressure gradient driving flow is proportional to the solute potential difference: Delta Psi\_p approximately equals |Delta Psi\_s| = 0.992 - 0.248 = 0.744 MPa.

(c) Potometer experiment: Independent variable = environmental conditions (e.g., light intensity, wind speed, humidity). Dependent variable = rate of water uptake (measured by movement of water meniscus in capillary tube). Controlled variables: (1) temperature (constant via water bath or controlled room), (2) plant species and size, (3) initial water potential of soil. Procedure: Cut stem underwater, attach to potometer, allow equilibration, then measure meniscus displacement over time under different conditions.

(d) In drought: soil water potential becomes more negative (e.g., -1.5 MPa), increasing the gradient but reducing water availability. The leaf may respond by closing stomata (increasing resistance), which reduces transpiration but also limits photosynthesis. Some plants produce compatible solutes to lower cell water potential (osmotic adjustment), maintaining water uptake despite dry soil. These changes represent a trade-off between water conservation and carbon gain.

## Answer to Question 5: Integrated Organ Systems

(a) Tissue types and roles:

Pancreas: Epithelial (islet beta cells secrete insulin, alpha cells secrete glucagon); Connective (blood vessels transport hormones, extracellular matrix supports islets); Nervous (autonomic innervation modulates secretion).

Liver: Epithelial (hepatocytes store glucose as glycogen, perform gluconeogenesis); Connective (sinusoids allow blood-hepatocyte exchange, Kupffer cells); Muscle (no significant muscle tissue in liver for glucose homeostasis).

Small intestine: Epithelial (enterocytes absorb glucose via SGLT1 and GLUT2 transporters); Muscle (smooth muscle for peristalsis and mixing); Connective (lamina propria contains capillaries for glucose transport); Nervous (enteric plexuses regulate motility and secretion).

(b) Percentage increase = (8.5 - 5.0) / 5.0 x 100% = 70%. For diabetic patient: normal meal excess = 8.5 - 5.0 = 3.5 mM; diabetic excess = 12.0 - 5.0 = 7.0 mM. Additional insulin required = (7.0 - 3.5) / 3.5 = 100% more insulin (or double).

(c) The vagus nerve (parasympathetic, cranial nerve X) stimulates digestive activity post-prandially: it increases acetylcholine release, enhancing gastric acid secretion, pancreatic enzyme release, and gallbladder contraction. It also stimulates insulin secretion (cephalic and early phase). Sympathetic innervation (via splanchnic nerves) inhibits digestion during stress, reducing motility and secretion. After a meal, parasympathetic dominance promotes nutrient absorption.

(d) Integrated function: (1) Epithelial tissue — enterocytes with microvilli absorb glucose, fructose, and amino acids; goblet cells secrete protective mucus; enteroendocrine cells secrete hormones (GIP, GLP-1) that signal the pancreas. (2) Muscle tissue — smooth muscle in the muscularis externa generates peristaltic waves that mix chyme with digestive enzymes and propel it along the tract; muscularis mucosae increases surface contact. (3) Connective tissue — lamina propria supports the epithelium and contains blood capillaries (for nutrient transport) and lymphatic lacteals (for lipid absorption); the submucosa houses Brunner's glands (duodenum) that secrete alkaline mucus. (4) Nervous tissue — the enteric nervous system (Meissner's submucosal plexus regulates secretion, Auerbach's myenteric plexus regulates motility) coordinates local responses; extrinsic innervation (vagus nerve) provides CNS integration. This hierarchy of specialized tissues (each with distinct cell types) combines to form the functional organ (small intestine), which then integrates with other organs (pancreas, liver) in the digestive system to achieve complete nutrient processing and metabolic homeostasis.
