# Reference Answers

## Set 1 Answers

### Short-answer questions

1. Totipotent cells can form all embryonic and extraembryonic cell types, e.g. a zygote. Pluripotent cells can form all body cell types but not extraembryonic tissues, e.g. embryonic stem cells or iPSCs. Multipotent cells can form several related cell types, e.g. adult stem cells. Unipotent cells form one cell type, e.g. mature skin-lineage cells.

2. Cleavage consists of rapid cell divisions with little or no growth phase, so the embryo is partitioned into smaller cells rather than increasing in total volume.

3. A blastula is a relatively simple hollow or fluid-filled cell arrangement; a gastrula is a multilayered embryo formed by cell movements and contains germ layers and body axes.

4. Ectoderm, mesoderm and endoderm. They are early determined cell populations that give rise to broad categories of adult tissues.

5. Fertilisation combines haploid sperm and oocyte genomes to restore diploidy and triggers developmental activation while protective mechanisms help block polyspermy.

### Yes/No questions

1. Yes; 2. No; 3. Yes; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

### Multiple-choice questions

1. B; 2. C; 3. A; 4. B; 5. C; 6. B; 7. B; 8. B; 9. B; 10. A

### Applied problems

1. From 2 cells to 16 cells requires three doublings: 2→4→8→16. The interval is about 3 days, so the average is about 1 day per cleavage division. Mammalian cleavage is slower than frog cleavage because mammalian development and maternal/uterine context differ, and early divisions are not as rapid.

2. Blue outer cells most likely represent ectoderm if they form skin and nervous tissue. Red inward-moving cells include cells contributing to endoderm and mesoderm. The result shows that cell position and movement during gastrulation are linked to determination of broad tissue fate.

3. Yes, fertilisation is possible. Sperm from day 10 could survive until about day 14 or 15, overlapping with an oocyte released on day 14 and viable for 12-24 hours.

## Set 2 Answers

### Short-answer questions

1. Radial symmetry: many planes through a central axis produce similar halves, e.g. cnidarians. Bilateral symmetry: one plane through the anterior-posterior midline produces mirror-image halves, e.g. frogs or humans.

2. Triploblastic animals have a mesoderm in addition to ectoderm and endoderm, allowing development of muscles, blood vessels, bones and other internal tissues, increasing structural and functional complexity.

3. Determination establishes cell fate; differentiation produces specialised cell features; morphogenesis organises cells and tissues into body form; growth increases size and cell number.

4. After birth, tissues still need maintenance, cell replacement, repair, immune responses and sometimes regeneration or reproductive cell production.

5. Adult intestinal stem cells self-renew and produce daughter cells that differentiate into cell types such as enterocytes, goblet cells and enteroendocrine cells.

## Set 3 Answers

### Short-answer questions

1. During repair and regeneration, adult tissues can reactivate developmental programmes controlling proliferation, migration, differentiation and patterning.

2. Repair restores tissue integrity, often with scar formation. Regeneration restores lost structure more completely, e.g. zebrafish fin regeneration or salamander limb regeneration.

3. Cancer can arise when pathways controlling proliferation, migration, survival or differentiation are mis-regulated, causing uncontrolled growth and tissue disruption.

4. iPSCs are differentiated cells reprogrammed back to a pluripotent-like state. This showed that mature cell identity can be reversed and redirected, with major implications for disease modelling and regenerative medicine.

5. Organoid formation shows that cells contain developmental instructions and, under suitable culture conditions, can divide, differentiate and organise into semi-functional tissue-like structures.

### Yes/No questions

1. Yes; 2. Yes; 3. Yes; 4. No; 5. Yes

### Multiple-choice questions

1. B; 2. B; 3. B; 4. B; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. B; 10. A

### Applied problems

1. Excessive pathway activation may cause too much stem-cell proliferation, excess daughter-cell production and abnormal tissue architecture. Rapidly renewing tissues are vulnerable because they already rely on frequent controlled cell division.

2. Basic steps: obtain fibroblasts, introduce reprogramming factors to generate iPSCs, verify pluripotency markers and differentiation potential, guide differentiation toward osteoblast-like cells, compare disease and control cells, and test candidate drugs or rescue treatments in vitro.

3. Skin repair mainly involves clotting, inflammation, cell migration, proliferation and scar formation, often without perfect restoration. Zebrafish fin regeneration involves formation of a regenerative cell mass, proliferation, pattern restoration and differentiation to rebuild fin structures.
