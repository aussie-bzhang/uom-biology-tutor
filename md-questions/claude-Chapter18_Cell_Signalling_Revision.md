**Key idea to remember: Every signalling event has THREE stages — (1) signal reception (perception), (2) signal transduction (relay through the cell), and (3) the cellular response. A target cell only responds if it carries the correct receptor; cells lacking that receptor ignore the signal.**

This chapter has three question sets: Set 1 — Cell communication, signal types and the three stages; Set 2 — Signal detection and receptor types; Set 3 — Signal transduction pathways, second messengers, amplification and specificity. Each set contains 5 short-answer, 5 Yes/No, 10 multiple-choice and 3 applied (experiment / calculation) questions. All answers are in the Answer Key at the end.

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** (i) Signal reception/perception — the signal molecule (ligand) binds a receptor on or in the target cell. (ii) Signal transduction — the bound receptor relays and converts the message through one or more intracellular signalling steps. (iii) Cellular response — the cell carries out a change (enzyme activity, movement, gene expression, etc.).

**2.** Autocrine — acts on the SAME cell that made it. Juxtacrine (contact-dependent) — acts only on a directly TOUCHING/adjacent cell via a membrane-bound signal. Paracrine — acts on NEARBY cells a short distance away through extracellular fluid. Endocrine (hormones) — travel to DISTANT cells, usually via the bloodstream/circulatory system.

**3.** Even a single free-living cell must sense and respond to its environment: find nutrients, distinguish light from dark, avoid toxins and predators, and (for a 'social life') communicate with other cells. Signalling lets it convert environmental information into appropriate behaviour.

**4.** The impala senses the cheetah; its brain signals the adrenal glands to release epinephrine into the blood. RECEPTION: an epinephrine molecule binds a receptor on a muscle cell. TRANSDUCTION: relay molecules transmit and amplify the signal, ultimately activating an enzyme. RESPONSE: the enzyme breaks down glycogen, releasing glucose to fuel the leg muscles.

**5.** A target cell is one that bears the specific receptor for a given signal and can therefore respond. Two neighbouring cells can respond differently (or one not at all) because they express different receptors and/or different intracellular relay proteins; a cell with no receptor for the signal simply does not respond.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — juxtacrine (contact-dependent) signalling needs the cells to be in direct physical contact.

**2.** Yes — autocrine signals act on the secreting cell itself.

**3.** Yes — hormones generally reach distant targets via the circulatory system.

**4.** No — without the appropriate receptor the cell cannot detect or respond to the signal.

**5.** Yes — transduction is the internal relay/processing stage between reception and response.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (i) Histamine = PARACRINE (acts on nearby blood vessels a short distance away). (ii) Testosterone = ENDOCRINE/hormone (made in testis, travels body-wide via blood). (iii) Delta = JUXTACRINE/contact-dependent (membrane protein binding a touching neighbour). (iv) Cytokine acting on the same T cell = AUTOCRINE. ## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Large or polar ligands cannot cross the hydrophobic lipid bilayer, so their receptors must sit on the cell surface with an extracellular binding domain. Small, nonpolar (lipid-soluble) ligands diffuse straight through the bilayer and bind receptors inside the cell (cytoplasm or nucleus).

**2.** Cortisol (a lipid-soluble steroid) diffuses across the membrane and enters the cytoplasm. It binds a cortisol receptor held in a complex with a chaperone protein; the receptor–chaperone complex cannot enter the nucleus. Cortisol binding changes the receptor's shape and releases the chaperone, allowing the receptor + cortisol to enter the nucleus and influence transcription (gene expression).

**3.** Ion-channel-coupled: ligand binding opens (or closes) a channel, changing ion flow across the membrane. Enzyme-coupled (e.g. receptor tyrosine kinase): ligand binding activates the receptor's enzymatic (kinase) activity, which phosphorylates intracellular substrates. G-protein-coupled (GPCR): ligand binding activates an associated G protein (GTP replaces GDP), which then activates an effector protein and downstream messengers.

**4.** Acetylcholine binds two of the five AChR subunits, causing the channel to change shape and open. The channel is lined with negatively charged amino acids, which favour passage of the positively charged Na+; Na+ flows into the cell and the resulting build-up/depolarisation initiates muscle contraction.

**5.** The insulin receptor has two alpha subunits (extracellular) and two beta subunits (membrane-spanning, with intracellular kinase domains). Insulin binds the alpha subunits; a conformational change in the beta subunits transmits the signal to the cytoplasm and activates the receptor's protein-kinase (tyrosine kinase) domain, which phosphorylates insulin-response substrates, triggering a cascade of intracellular responses.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — lipid-soluble steroids diffuse across the membrane to intracellular receptors.

**2.** Yes — insulin is a large protein that cannot cross the membrane, so it binds a transmembrane (cell-surface) receptor.

**3.** Yes — receptor activation causes GTP to replace GDP on the G protein.

**4.** Yes — that is the general architecture of a transmembrane receptor.

**5.** No — the chaperone must be RELEASED; the receptor–chaperone complex cannot enter the nucleus.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** Insulin is a large, polar protein and cannot diffuse through the hydrophobic lipid bilayer, so it must bind a membrane receptor with an extracellular domain that then signals inward. Oestrogen and cortisol are small, nonpolar steroids that dissolve in and cross the bilayer, so they can reach intracellular receptors. Prediction: a small, uncharged, lipid-soluble hormone would be expected to have an INTRACELLULAR receptor, because it can diffuse across the membrane.

**2.** (i) The salty signal depends on Na+ continuing to leak through ion channels because the external salt concentration is higher than inside; once the mouth is rinsed and the salt is removed, the concentration gradient collapses, the channels no longer carry net Na+, and the signal stops almost immediately — it is directly and continuously dependent on the ligand being present. (ii) Glutamate binds a GPCR that launches an intracellular signalling cascade with second messengers and amplification; once started, the cascade and its activated downstream molecules can persist for a time even after the glutamate is rinsed away, so the umami taste lingers. The key contrast is direct/continuous (ion channel) vs amplified/self-sustaining cascade (GPCR).

**3.** GPCRs amplify because the cascade has several catalytic (one-to-many) steps: (1) one activated receptor can switch on MANY G-protein molecules; (2) each activated G protein/effector (e.g. adenylyl cyclase) can produce MANY second-messenger molecules (e.g. cAMP); and (3) each second messenger activates kinases that each phosphorylate many substrates. So a few hormone molecules evoke a large response. A simple ligand-gated ion channel has no such enzymatic multiplication — roughly one channel opening per ligand-bound receptor — so it provides little amplification.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** RELAY — pass the signal onward to spread it through the cell. AMPLIFY — make the signal stronger so a few molecules produce a large response. INTEGRATE — combine inputs from more than one pathway before relaying onward. DISTRIBUTE (branch) — send the signal to more than one effector protein, creating branches and a complex/coordinated response.

**2.** A second messenger is a small intracellular molecule made/released in response to receptor activation that spreads the signal inside the cell. cAMP is made from ATP by adenylyl cyclase after the GPCR/G protein is activated; cAMP then activates protein kinase A, leading to the response. The FIRST messenger (the hormone, e.g. epinephrine) acts OUTSIDE the cell at the surface receptor; the SECOND messenger (cAMP) acts INSIDE the cell.

**3.** A phosphorylation cascade is a chain of protein kinases in which each activated kinase phosphorylates and activates the next, amplifying the signal at each step. Kinases switch target proteins ON by adding phosphate; protein phosphatases switch them OFF by removing the phosphate. Together they form a reversible on/off switch that also lets the cell terminate the signal.

**4.** Short-term responses are fast and do not need new gene expression — e.g. activating an existing enzyme or rearranging the cytoskeleton for movement. Long-term responses involve activating transcription regulators that enter the nucleus and change which genes are transcribed into mRNA and translated into protein; these take longer because they require new protein synthesis.

**5.** Specificity comes from which receptors a cell expresses (a cell only responds if it has the receptor) and which intracellular relay/effector proteins it contains. The same signal can therefore activate different pathways and different end-effectors in different cell types, producing different responses; the matched 'lock-and-key' set of proteins ensures the correct pathway is switched on.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — adenylyl cyclase converts ATP to cAMP.

**2.** Yes — that one-to-many step is the basis of cascade amplification.

**3.** Yes — phosphatases remove phosphates and reverse kinase action.

**4.** Yes — activated transcription regulators alter gene expression (long-term response).

**5.** Yes — different receptors and/or relay proteins explain different responses to the same signal.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (a) About 10^8 (one hundred million) glucose 1-phosphate molecules per single bound hormone molecule. (b) The signal has been amplified roughly 10^8-fold (eight orders of magnitude). (c) In fight-or-flight, a tiny amount of epinephrine must mobilise a large, rapid burst of glucose to fuel muscles; the cascade's enormous amplification lets a small hormone signal produce a massive, fast energy response.

**2.** A single GPCR-triggered cascade can control ~200 outcomes because: (i) DISTRIBUTION/branching — the activated pathway splits to many different effector proteins, each controlling a different process (DNA-synthesis block, directional growth, membrane fusion); (ii) AMPLIFICATION — each step activates many downstream molecules, so the response is strong enough to reorganise the whole cell; (iii) TRANSCRIPTION REGULATORS — part of the cascade activates regulators that switch on/off the ~200 genes, producing coordinated long-term changes. One receptor input is thus fanned out and amplified into a whole genetic and physiological programme.
