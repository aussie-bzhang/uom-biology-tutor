# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Metabolism Overview

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** Metabolism is the sum of all chemical (biochemical) reactions in a living system, usually organised into pathways. Catabolism breaks larger molecules down into smaller common metabolites and releases energy; anabolism combines smaller metabolites into larger molecules and requires energy.

**2.** Catabolism commonly involves hydrolysis and oxidation reactions; these are typically exergonic (−ΔG, release energy).

**3.** Anabolism commonly involves condensation and reduction reactions; these are typically endergonic (+ΔG, require energy).

**4.** (i) Changing the amount of active enzyme (gene expression); (ii) changing enzyme activity by covalent modification (e.g. phosphorylation by a protein kinase); (iii) feedback inhibition (allosteric binding of a product to an early enzyme); (iv) substrate availability (if substrate is used up by another pathway, the enzyme stops).

**5.** Food must also provide carbon skeletons (building blocks) that cells use to make their own molecules — not just calories/energy.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — catabolic reactions are generally exergonic (release energy).

**2.** Yes — condensation is typical of anabolic pathways.

**3.** Yes — phosphorylation by a protein kinase is a covalent modification that can change enzyme activity.

**4.** Yes — they funnel electrons from many organic molecules toward oxygen.

**5.** No — anabolic reactions are generally endergonic (require energy).

## Set 2 — Carbohydrate and Lipid Metabolism

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** Glycolysis is the series of reactions that breaks glucose down to produce energy. Gluconeogenesis is the production of glucose within a cell. It is “almost a reversal” of glycolysis because 7 of its steps use the same enzymes running in reverse; only the few irreversible steps require different enzymes/route.

**2.** The irreversible steps (e.g. pyruvate → phosphoenolpyruvate via oxaloacetate; the fructose-1,6-bisphosphate and glucose-6-phosphate conversions) require separate enzymes in each direction. This separation lets the cell control each direction independently — it enables regulation of the pathway.

**3.** Beta-oxidation occurs in the mitochondria (after the fatty acid is linked to coenzyme A and transported in). Each cycle removes a 2-carbon unit, producing a shorter fatty acyl-CoA, one acetyl-CoA, and the reduced coenzymes NADH and FADH2.

**4.** Ketone bodies (e.g. acetoacetate) are formed by ketogenesis when acetyl-CoA levels in the cell are high. Their build-up can cause ketosis and acidosis.

**5.** Lipogenesis is the synthesis of lipids; almost any organic substrate can be used to form glycerol. Essential fatty acids are those the body cannot synthesise, so they must be supplied in the diet.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — gluconeogenesis makes glucose within the cell.

**2.** No — glycerol enters the glycolytic pathways while the fatty acids enter the mitochondria for beta-oxidation (different points).

**3.** No — the steps of beta-oxidation cannot be reversed.

**4.** Yes — PFK is inhibited by ATP and citrate (and activated by AMP/F-2,6-BP).

**5.** No — lipids cannot provide large amounts of ATP quickly; they are used when glucose is limited.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** Glycogenesis = synthesis of glycogen from glucose (storing glucose as the polymer glycogen). Gluconeogenesis = synthesis of NEW glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (e.g. lactate, glycerol, amino acids/pyruvate). Starting material: glycogenesis starts from glucose; gluconeogenesis starts from non-sugar precursors. (Both make/store glucose-related molecules but from different starting points.)

**2.** Using the lecture’s figures: (a) 7 oxidation cycles × 4 ATP = 28 ATP. (b) 8 acetyl-CoA × 10 ATP = 80 ATP. (c) Combined total ≈ 28 + 80 = 108 ATP. (These are the lecture’s simplified per-cycle and per-acetyl-CoA estimates, not the more detailed textbook stoichiometry.)

**3.** Both the amino-acid carbons and the glucose can be converted to acetyl-CoA (glucose → pyruvate → acetyl-CoA; amino acids → pyruvate/acetyl-CoA after losing their amino group). The 2-carbon acetyl units from acetyl-CoA are then linked together by fatty-acid synthesis to build fatty acids, which combine with glycerol to form triglycerides (fat). The key shared intermediate is acetyl-CoA.

## Set 3 — Protein Metabolism and Metabolic Integration

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** (1) There is no storage form of protein — protein eaten beyond need is turned into carbohydrate or fat and the nitrogen is eliminated through the urea cycle. (2) Essential amino acids must be consumed daily for proteins to be made. (3) Proteins are constantly being degraded, so there is a constant need for quality protein to maintain body structure.

**2.** Transamination: the amino group of one amino acid is transferred to a keto acid (an exchange reaction), catalysed by a transaminase — no free ammonia released. Deamination: the amino group is removed entirely and an ammonium ion (NH4+) is released, catalysed by a deaminase.

**3.** The urea cycle disposes of nitrogen safely: it combines two ammonium ions (NH4+) with carbon dioxide (CO2) to make urea, a relatively harmless, soluble compound excreted in the urine.

**4.** Amination is the attachment of an amino group (from NH4+) to a carbon framework (e.g. α-ketoglutarate → glutamic acid). It is the key step in synthesising nonessential amino acids.

**5.** Because there is no protein store, mobilising protein means breaking down functional/structural proteins; the carbon skeletons must be processed and the nitrogen safely removed (transamination/deamination + urea cycle). This makes protein a slow, costly, last-resort fuel rather than a quick energy source.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** No — there is no dedicated protein store; excess is converted to carbohydrate/fat.

**2.** Yes — essential amino acids must come from the diet.

**3.** Yes — deamination releases NH4+.

**4.** Yes — amino-acid carbon skeletons can enter the citric acid cycle to make ATP when other fuels are inadequate.

**5.** Yes — proteins are continually degraded and resynthesised (turnover).

### Part D. Applied

**1.** Scott is INCORRECT. There is no storage form of amino acids/protein. Amino acids consumed beyond what is needed for protein synthesis are not stored: their amino groups are removed (transamination/deamination) and disposed of as urea via the urea cycle, while the carbon skeletons are converted to carbohydrate or fat (which can be stored or oxidised). Building muscle requires the right training stimulus plus adequate (not merely excessive) protein — surplus amino acids do not simply wait in reserve.

**2.** (a) Protein → amino acids → (transamination/oxidative deamination removes –NH2 as NH4+ → urea) → carbon skeletons become pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, or citric-acid-cycle intermediates (e.g. α-ketoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, fumarate, oxaloacetate). (b) Glucose → glycolysis → pyruvate → acetyl-CoA → citric acid cycle. (c) Triacylglycerol → glycerol (→ glycolysis) + fatty acids (→ beta-oxidation → acetyl-CoA). All converge on acetyl-CoA / the citric acid cycle, producing NADH and FADH2 for the electron transport chain and ATP synthesis.
