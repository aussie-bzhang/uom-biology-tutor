## Core concepts to remember before you start

* **Why a transport system?** Most multicellular animals are too large for diffusion alone: small SA:V ratio + large diffusion distance to internal cells. A circulatory system uses **bulk flow** (mass movement of extracellular fluid) to carry substances near every cell, and this **maintains high concentration gradients** so diffusion across the final short distance stays fast.
* **Exceptions (no circulatory system):** sponges, cnidarians (jellyfish, corals, hydra), flatworms and tardigrades — they are flat, thin or hollow (small or thin body), so every cell is close enough to the environment for diffusion to suffice.
* **Common components of any circulatory system:** a pump; a circulatory fluid; exchange surfaces; a system of tubular vessels; a mechanism to return fluid to the pump; and elements that structure/direct flow (valves, vessel diameter).
* **Open vs closed:** Open = fluid (hemolymph) empties into the body cavity; transport fluid = interstitial fluid; energy-efficient pump, large volume, low pressure (arthropods, non-cephalopod molluscs). Closed = fluid enclosed in vessels, blood separate from interstitial fluid, exchange in capillary beds; faster flow, flow can be directed, large molecules retained (annelids, cephalopods, vertebrates).
* **Open systems can still be very active —** e.g. insects — because they do *not* depend on the circulatory system for gas exchange (they use a separate tracheal system).
* **Single → double circuit:** fish (2-chambered, single circuit) → amphibian (3-chambered, 2 atria + 1 ventricle, some mixing) → crocodilian/bird/mammal (4-chambered, fully separated pulmonary and systemic circuits, no mixing).
* **Capillary exchange = Starling forces.** CHP (capillary hydrostatic pressure, pushes fluid OUT) vs BOP (blood/colloid osmotic pressure from plasma proteins, pulls fluid IN). IFOP and IFHP are negligible. **Net pressure out > in → filtration** (arteriole end); **net pressure in > out → reabsorption** (venule end). Balance = homeostasis; imbalance = oedema. Excess filtered fluid is returned by the lymphatic system.

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short answer

**1.** As an animal gets larger, volume (and number of internal cells with metabolic demand) grows faster than surface area, so the SA:V ratio falls — there is too little surface to supply too much volume. At the same time the diffusion distance from the surface to deep internal cells becomes large, and diffusion is far too slow over long distances. Together (small SA:V + large distance) these mean diffusion with the external environment cannot meet exchange needs in most multicellular animals.

**2.** Bulk flow is the mass (active) movement of an extracellular/transport fluid through the body, driven by pressure from a pump — not by random diffusion. The circulatory system uses bulk flow to carry O₂, nutrients and wastes rapidly to within a short distance of every cell; the final step across the cell membrane is still diffusion. By continually delivering fresh fluid (high O₂/nutrients, low waste) and carrying away exchanged substances, bulk flow keeps the concentration gradients across the exchange surface steep, which keeps diffusion fast.

**3.** These animals are flat, thin or hollow (and small), so no cell is far from the external environment or from water in a central cavity. With a short diffusion distance and a favourable SA:V, diffusion alone meets the exchange needs of every cell, so no internal transport system is required (e.g. a flat flatworm; a hollow cnidarian polyp; a porous sponge that draws water through itself).

**4.** Pump (drives bulk flow); circulatory fluid (carries dissolved substances and, in many animals, cells/proteins); exchange surfaces (where substances move between fluid and tissues, e.g. capillary beds); a system of tubular vessels (conduits for the fluid); a mechanism to return fluid to the pump (e.g. veins/lymphatics or ostia); and elements that structure flow and direct fluid (e.g. arteries/arterioles, valves).

**5.** Animal circulatory systems involve active mechanisms — muscular pumps that use ATP to generate the considerable force needed to move fluid through highly branched vessels. Plant transport relies largely on passive processes (e.g. evaporation-driven and osmotic movement). A high-metabolism animal needs rapid delivery of O₂ and nutrients and rapid waste removal, which requires fast, forced (pressurised) bulk flow — hence an active pump.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1. Yes** — for the same shape, increasing size lowers SA:V (volume grows with the cube of length, surface area only with the square).

**2. No** — bulk flow is mass movement driven by pressure from the pump, not random diffusion (it complements diffusion).

**3. Yes** — the lecture tree dates the first blood vascular system to ~600–700 mya and the first endothelium later, to ~540–510 mya (in the vertebrate ancestor).

**4. No** — flatworms have no heart or circulatory system; their flat, thin body allows diffusion to supply all cells.

**5. Yes** — moving fluid through long, branched, narrow vessels of an active high-metabolism animal requires considerable force from a pump.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short answer

**1.** Open system: the transport fluid flows openly through the tissues, emptying into the body cavity, so the transport fluid and the fluid bathing the tissues are the same (hemolymph). Closed system: the fluid is enclosed within a network of vessels, so the transport fluid (blood) is kept separate from the fluid in the tissues (interstitial fluid); exchange between them occurs across capillary beds.

**2.** (1) More rapid flow → faster transport to tissues, supporting a faster metabolism. (2) Control of flow → blood can be directed to where it is needed (e.g. to gas-exchange or heat-exchange surfaces). (3) Specialised, contained contents → cells and large molecules (e.g. oxygen-carrying proteins) are retained in the vessels, increasing oxygen-carrying capacity.

**3.** Insects do not depend on the circulatory system for gas exchange. They use a separate tracheal system that delivers air directly to the tissues, so the limitations of an open circulatory system for O₂ transport do not constrain their activity level.

**4.** Fish: single circuit, 2-chambered heart (one atrium, one ventricle), blood passes once through the heart per circuit. Amphibians: double circuit beginning, 3-chambered heart (2 atria, 1 ventricle) with some mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood in the single ventricle. Birds and mammals (and crocodilians): fully double circuit, 4-chambered heart (2 atria, 2 ventricles) with complete separation of pulmonary and systemic blood (no mixing).

**5.** Pump = chambered heart; circulatory fluid = blood; exchange surface = capillary beds; mechanism to return fluid to the pump = veins & lymphatics; elements that structure/direct flow = arteries and arterioles plus one-way valves (in the heart and veins).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1. Yes** — in an open system the extracellular (interstitial) fluid and the transport fluid are one and the same: hemolymph.

**2. Yes** — closed systems generally have higher pressure and faster flow, giving faster delivery to tissues.

**3. Yes** — because the fluid is enclosed in vessels, large molecules (e.g. plasma proteins) are retained within the system.

**4. No** — most fish have a 2-chambered heart and a single circuit; full four-chambered separation is seen in birds, mammals and crocodilians.

**5. Yes** — arthropod hearts are tubular with ostia (pores) through which hemolymph returns from the body cavity into the heart.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short answer

**1.** Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure of the fluid in the contained network, created by the force of the pump (heart). It is highest as the fluid is first driven out of the pump into the arteries. Mean blood pressure then falls progressively along the system — highest in large arteries, dropping steeply across arterioles, and lowest in the veins.

**2.** Plasma proteins are large, so they cannot cross the capillary wall and stay within the blood vessels. Because solutes draw water by osmosis, these retained proteins generate blood (colloid) osmotic pressure (BOP) that pulls water from the interstitial fluid back into the capillary — the main inward force opposing filtration.

**3.** CHP (capillary hydrostatic pressure) — pushes fluid OUT. BOP (blood/colloid osmotic pressure) — pulls fluid IN. IFOP (interstitial fluid osmotic pressure) — would draw fluid OUT, but is negligible. IFHP (interstitial fluid hydrostatic pressure) — would push fluid IN, but is negligible. The two negligible forces are IFOP and IFHP.

**4.** Filtration predominates at the arteriole (upstream) end, where it supplies O₂, nutrients and water to cells; it occurs because outward pressure (CHP + IFOP) exceeds inward pressure (BOP + IFHP). Reabsorption predominates at the venule (downstream) end, where it removes wastes, CO₂ and excess fluid; here inward pressure exceeds outward pressure. Hydrostatic pressure is higher at the arteriole end because it is closer to the heart (less pressure has been lost along the vessel).

**5.** Starling forces are normally balanced so that net filtration roughly equals reabsorption plus lymphatic drainage (homeostasis). If they become imbalanced — e.g. raised CHP or lowered BOP — more fluid is filtered out than is returned, and it accumulates in the tissues as oedema (swelling). Some filtered fluid is always returned to the circulation by the lymphatic system, so lymphatic failure can also cause oedema.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1. Yes** — hydrostatic (blood) pressure is highest as fluid leaves the pump into the arteries, then falls along the network.

**2. No** — plasma proteins are too large to cross the capillary wall and normally remain in the blood.

**3. Yes** — at the arteriole end, CHP is high, so outward pressure exceeds inward pressure and filtration dominates.

**4. No** — IFOP is treated as a negligible force (as is IFHP).

**5. Yes** — lower plasma protein → lower BOP → less reabsorption → fluid accumulates in tissues (oedema).

### Part D — Applied

**1. Net filtration pressure.** (a) Arteriole end: NFP = CHP − BOP = 40 − 25 = +15 mmHg. Venule end: NFP = 16 − 25 = −9 mmHg. (b) Positive NFP (+15) at the arteriole end → fluid moves OUT (filtration); negative NFP (−9) at the venule end → fluid moves IN (reabsorption). (c) Hydrostatic pressure falls from arteriole to venule end because pressure is lost as the fluid travels along the high-resistance capillary (and the arteriole end is closer to the heart), so CHP drops from 40 to 16 mmHg.

**2. Oedema scenario.** (a) With BOP = 12: arteriole end NFP = 40 − 12 = +28 mmHg; venule end NFP = 16 − 12 = +4 mmHg. (b) Both ends now have a net OUTWARD pressure (no longer negative at the venule end), so filtration exceeds reabsorption all along the capillary and fluid accumulates in the tissues → oedema. (c) Even with normal Starling forces, some fluid is always filtered out and must be drained by lymphatics; if a lymphatic vessel is blocked, this excess fluid cannot be returned and builds up, causing oedema.

**3. Experimental design — capillary filtration.** (a) IV = downstream (venule-end) pressure; DV = tissue fluid volume (e.g. tissue weight gain or interstitial volume); controlled variables = perfusion fluid composition (incl. protein/BOP), arteriole-end pressure, temperature, tissue type. (b) Raising venule-end hydrostatic pressure toward 25 mmHg reduces (then abolishes) the inward NFP, so reabsorption falls — less fluid returns to the blood. (c) Prolonged standing or venous obstruction raises venous (downstream) hydrostatic pressure in the legs, reducing reabsorption so filtered fluid accumulates → swelling (oedema) in the lower limbs.
