# Review Questions

This document contains 5 application-style review questions for Chapter 10: Energy & Bioenergetics. These questions cover ATP energetics, thermodynamics, redox reactions, metabolic coupling, and cellular respiration calculations. Answer all questions showing your working. Answers are provided at the end of the document.

## Question 1: ATP Hydrolysis & Energy Calculation

During a sprint, a 70 kg athlete hydrolyses ATP at a rate of 0.50 mol per minute to power muscle contraction. The hydrolysis reaction is:

*ATP + H₂O → ADP + Pᵢ ΔG = −30.5 kJ/mol*

(a) Calculate the rate of heat released (in kJ/min) if muscle contraction operates at only 35% efficiency. Where does the remaining energy go?

(b) The athlete sprints for 12 seconds. Calculate the total number of ATP molecules hydrolysed during this sprint. (Avogadro’s constant = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹)

(c) Creatine phosphate (CP) serves as a rapid ATP reserve via the reaction: CP + ADP → ATP + Creatine, with ΔG = −43.1 kJ/mol. Calculate the total free energy change if the athlete uses 0.10 mol of CP to regenerate ATP during the sprint. Is this coupled reaction spontaneous? Explain your reasoning.

## Question 2: Thermodynamics & Experimental Design

A biology student designs an experiment to investigate the relationship between entropy and biological order using yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) fermentation:

“I set up two flasks at 25°C. Flask A contains 100 mL of glucose broth with yeast. Flask B (control) contains the same broth but has been autoclaved (killed yeast). I measured the temperature of each flask every 10 minutes for 1 hour using a digital thermometer.”

(a) The student observes that Flask A rises from 25.0°C to 27.4°C, while Flask B stays at 25.0°C. Calculate the total entropy change of the surroundings given that the broth has a specific heat capacity of 4.18 J/g·K and a density of 1.02 g/mL.

(b) Explain how this result illustrates the Second Law of Thermodynamics, even though yeast cells are creating highly ordered biological structures (new cells, proteins, and organised organelles) during fermentation.

(c) Suggest TWO improvements to the experimental design that would strengthen the student’s conclusion about the relationship between biological order and entropy.

## Question 3: Redox Reactions & Energy Efficiency

The complete oxidation of glucose in cellular respiration follows:

*C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O ΔG°’ = −2870 kJ/mol*

(a) Identify which species is oxidised and which is reduced. Show your reasoning by assigning oxidation numbers to the carbon atoms in glucose and carbon dioxide.

(b) Under cellular conditions, aerobic respiration captures approximately 40% of this free energy as ATP (32 ATP per glucose). The remaining 60% is released as heat. Calculate:

(i) The total energy captured as ATP per mole of glucose (in kJ).

(ii) The total heat released per mole of glucose (in kJ).

(iii) The overall thermodynamic efficiency of cellular respiration as a percentage.

(c) A marathon runner burns approximately 2400 kJ of energy per hour from glucose oxidation. Calculate the mass of glucose consumed per hour (Molar mass of glucose = 180 g/mol). How many moles of CO₂ are produced in this process?

## Question 4: Coupled Reactions & Equilibrium

The hydrolysis of sucrose into glucose and fructose is catalysed by the enzyme sucrase:

*Sucrose + H₂O → Glucose + Fructose ΔG°’ = +27 kJ/mol*

(a) Explain why this reaction is non-spontaneous under standard conditions. Calculate the equilibrium constant (K’eq) for this reaction at 37°C (body temperature). (R = 8.314 J/mol·K)

(b) In cells, this reaction is driven by coupling with ATP hydrolysis (ΔG°’ = −30.5 kJ/mol). Write the overall coupled reaction equation and calculate the combined ΔG°’. Is the coupled reaction spontaneous? Explain.

(c) In an experiment, you add purified sucrase to a solution containing 0.10 M sucrose and 0.050 M ATP at 37°C (pH 7). Using the coupled reaction from (b), predict the direction of the reaction and calculate the maximum theoretical concentration of glucose that could form at equilibrium, assuming ATP remains in excess. Show your reasoning.

## Question 5: Metabolic Pathway Analysis

The diagram below summarises ATP production from one molecule of glucose through glycolysis, the citric acid (TCA) cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation.

Glycolysis: Glucose → 2 Pyruvate (net: 2 ATP, 2 NADH)

Pyruvate oxidation: 2 Pyruvate → 2 Acetyl-CoA (2 NADH)

TCA cycle: 2 Acetyl-CoA → 4 CO₂ (6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 ATP)

Oxidative phosphorylation: each NADH → 2.5 ATP; each FADH₂ → 1.5 ATP

(a) Construct a complete ATP accounting table showing the total ATP yield from ONE molecule of glucose via aerobic respiration. Include both substrate-level phosphorylation and oxidative phosphorylation.

(b) A rare genetic mutation reduces the efficiency of Complex I in the electron transport chain by 60%, meaning each NADH now produces only 1.0 ATP instead of 2.5 ATP. Recalculate the total ATP yield per glucose. By what percentage is the overall efficiency reduced?

(c) A 25-year-old human at rest consumes approximately 8,400 kJ of energy per day, with 40% derived from glucose oxidation. Given that ATP hydrolysis provides ~30.5 kJ/mol of usable energy, calculate the minimum number of glucose molecules the body must oxidise daily to meet this energy demand under normal conditions (from part a).

# Answer Key

Detailed solutions for all review questions.

## Answer 1: ATP Hydrolysis & Energy Calculation

**(a)** Heat released at 35% efficiency:

At 35% efficiency, 65% of the energy is lost as heat.

*Heat released = 0.50 mol/min × 30.5 kJ/mol × 0.65 = 9.91 kJ/min*

The remaining 35% (5.34 kJ/min) powers muscle contraction (mechanical work). The ‘lost’ energy dissipates as heat due to the Second Law of Thermodynamics — no energy transfer is 100% efficient.

**(b)** ATP molecules hydrolysed in 12 seconds:

*Time = 12/60 = 0.20 min*

*Moles of ATP = 0.50 mol/min × 0.20 min = 0.10 mol*

*Molecules = 0.10 mol × 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹ = 6.0 × 10²² molecules*

**(c)** Creatine phosphate coupling:

*ΔG\_total = ΔG\_CP + ΔG\_ATP\_hydrolysis\_reversed*

*ΔG\_total = (−43.1) + (+30.5) = −12.6 kJ/mol*

For 0.10 mol CP used:

*Total ΔG = 0.10 mol × (−12.6 kJ/mol) = −1.26 kJ*

The negative ΔG°’ confirms the coupled reaction is spontaneous. The highly exergonic breakdown of CP drives the endergonic rephosphorylation of ADP to ATP, a classic example of reaction coupling in bioenergetics.

## Answer 2: Thermodynamics & Experimental Design

**(a)** Entropy change of surroundings:

*Mass of broth = 100 mL × 1.02 g/mL = 102 g*

*Heat released (q) = m × c × ΔT = 102 g × 4.18 J/g·K × (27.4 − 25.0)K = 102 × 4.18 × 2.4 = 1023 J*

*ΔS\_surroundings = q/T = 1023 J / 298 K = +3.43 J/K*

**(b)** Second Law explanation:

The positive ΔS\_surroundings confirms that the total entropy of the universe increases, satisfying the Second Law. Although yeast cells decrease their internal entropy by creating ordered structures (new cells, proteins, organelles), they release MORE entropy into the surroundings as heat than they consume in building order. The net effect is always ΔS\_universe > 0. This demonstrates that living organisms are open systems that maintain local order at the expense of increasing global disorder.

**(c)** Experimental improvements:

1. ​Include a replicate set of flasks (n ≥ 3) to enable statistical analysis and account for biological variability between yeast cultures.

2. ​Measure glucose concentration over time using a spectrophotometer or glucose assay kit to quantify the metabolic rate and correlate it with entropy production, rather than relying solely on temperature change.

## Answer 3: Redox Reactions & Energy Efficiency

**(a)** Oxidation states:

In glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆): Each carbon has an average oxidation state of 0. In CO₂: Carbon is +4. Since carbon’s oxidation number increases from 0 to +4, glucose is OXIDISED.

In O₂: Oxygen is 0. In H₂O and CO₂: Oxygen is −2. Since oxygen’s oxidation number decreases from 0 to −2, O₂ is REDUCED.

Glucose is the reducing agent; O₂ is the oxidising agent.

**(b)** Energy calculations:

(i) Energy captured as ATP:

*32 ATP × 30.5 kJ/mol = 976 kJ per mole of glucose*

(ii) Heat released:

*2870 kJ − 976 kJ = 1894 kJ per mole of glucose*

(iii) Overall efficiency:

*Efficiency = (976 / 2870) × 100% = 34.0%*

Note: The stated 40% capture is a theoretical maximum; the actual efficiency based on 32 ATP is ~34%. The difference reflects energy costs of transport and proton leak.

**(c)** Marathon runner calculations:

*Energy from glucose = 2400 kJ/hr × 40% / 34% = 2824 kJ/hr of glucose oxidation*

*Moles of glucose = 2824 kJ/hr / 2870 kJ/mol = 0.984 mol/hr*

*Mass of glucose = 0.984 mol × 180 g/mol = 177 g/hr*

*Moles of CO₂ = 0.984 mol glucose × 6 = 5.90 mol CO₂/hr*

## Answer 4: Coupled Reactions & Equilibrium

**(a)** Non-spontaneous reaction:

The positive ΔG°’ (+27 kJ/mol) indicates the reaction requires energy input and is endergonic (non-spontaneous) under standard conditions.

*K’eq = e^(−ΔG°’/RT) = e^(−27000 / (8.314 × 310)) = e^(−10.47) = 2.83 × 10⁻⁵*

The very small K’eq confirms the reaction strongly favours reactants.

**(b)** Coupled reaction:

*Sucrose + H₂O + ATP → Glucose + Fructose + ADP + Pᵢ*

*ΔG°’\_coupled = (+27) + (−30.5) = −3.5 kJ/mol*

The negative ΔG°’ means the coupled reaction is spontaneous. Although ATP hydrolysis releases only slightly more energy than sucrose hydrolysis requires, this small net negative ΔG drives the reaction forward. The cell maintains a high ATP/ADP ratio, making the actual ΔG even more negative under cellular conditions.

**(c)** Reaction direction and equilibrium:

Since ΔG°’\_coupled is negative, the reaction proceeds forward (toward glucose and fructose formation).

*K’eq\_coupled = e^(−(−3500)/(8.314 × 310)) = e^(+1.36) = 3.90*

At equilibrium with excess ATP:

*K’eq = [Glucose][Fructose][ADP][Pᵢ] / ([Sucrose][ATP]) ≈ [Glucose]² / [Sucrose] (since [ADP], [Pᵢ] remain low)*

Assuming sucrose is limiting and ATP remains constant:

*Maximum [Glucose] ≈ √(3.90 × 0.10) = 0.624 M*

## Answer 5: Metabolic Pathway Analysis

**(a)** Complete ATP accounting table:

| **Stage** | **Substrate ATP** | **Oxidative ATP** | **Total ATP** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Glycolysis | 2 | 5 (2 NADH × 2.5) | 7 |
| Pyruvate oxidation | 0 | 5 (2 NADH × 2.5) | 5 |
| TCA cycle | 2 | 18 (6 NADH×2.5 + 2 FADH₂×1.5) | 20 |
| Grand Total | 4 | 28 | 32 |

Total = 32 ATP per glucose

**(b)** Complex I mutation:

Total NADH produced = 10 (2 from glycolysis + 2 from pyruvate oxidation + 6 from TCA)

*Lost ATP from NADH = 10 × (2.5 − 1.0) = 15 ATP*

*New total = 32 − 15 = 17 ATP per glucose*

*% reduction = (32 − 17) / 32 × 100% = 46.9%*

**(c)** Daily glucose requirement:

*Energy from glucose = 8400 kJ/day × 40% = 3360 kJ/day*

*ATP energy needed = 3360 kJ / 30.5 kJ/mol = 110.2 mol ATP*

*Glucose molecules = 110.2 mol ATP / 32 mol ATP/mol glucose = 3.44 mol glucose*

*Glucose molecules = 3.44 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 2.07 × 10²´ molecules*

Alternatively, in grams: 3.44 mol × 180 g/mol = 619 g glucose per day.
