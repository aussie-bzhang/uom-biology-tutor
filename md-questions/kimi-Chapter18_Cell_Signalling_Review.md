## Question 1: Signal Amplification Calculation

**(a) Signal Amplification Factor**

Step 1: One activated receptor activates 100 Gs proteins/second.

Step 2: Each Gs activates 100 AC enzymes. So: 100 × 100 = 10,000 AC molecules activated.

Step 3: Each AC produces 1,000 cAMP molecules/second. So: 10,000 × 1,000 = 10,000,000 cAMP molecules.

Step 4: cAMP activates PKA. Each PKA tetramer phosphorylates 100 targets/second. The number of activated PKA complexes equals cAMP/4 (since 4 cAMP bind per tetramer). However, for simplicity, if we consider catalytic efficiency: 10,000,000 cAMP molecules lead to phosphorylation of 10,000,000 × 100 = 1,000,000,000 target proteins (if each cAMP effectively activates one PKA catalytic subunit).

**More precise calculation (accepted answer):**

Amplification at each step:

* Receptor → G protein: 100×
* G protein → AC → cAMP: 100 × 1,000 = 100,000×
* cAMP → PKA → targets: ~100× (since each freed catalytic subunit phosphorylates ~100 targets)

**Total amplification factor = 100 × 100,000 × 100 = 1,000,000,000 (10⁹)**

Alternative accepted: If calculating [100 Gs] × [1000 cAMP/Gs] × [100 targets/PKA] = 10⁷, or stepwise product of all amplification steps. Full marks for clear reasoning with correct multiplication.

**(b) Total Phosphorylated Targets after 5 seconds**

Activated receptors = 10,000 × 10% = 1,000 receptors

Targets per receptor per second = 10⁹ (from part a)

Total after 5 seconds = 1,000 × 10⁹ × 5 = 5 × 10¹² phosphorylated target proteins

Note: This enormous number demonstrates why amplification is necessary — even a few active receptors can trigger massive cellular responses.

**(c) Biological Advantage and Regulation**

Advantage: Massive amplification allows cells to respond to extremely low concentrations of hormones (sometimes picomolar or less). This ensures sensitivity to faint signals and enables rapid, robust cellular responses. Without amplification, physiological hormone concentrations would be insufficient to trigger observable cellular changes.

Regulation mechanism (any one accepted):

* GTPase activity of Gα: Intrinsic GTP hydrolysis inactivates G proteins within seconds, providing automatic shutoff.
* Phosphodiesterases: Enzymes that rapidly degrade cAMP to AMP, terminating the signal.
* Receptor desensitization: Phosphorylation by GRKs and arrestin binding prevents overstimulation.
* Negative feedback: PKA can phosphorylate and inhibit upstream components.

## Question 2: Classifying a Novel Signalling Molecule

**(a) Experimental Design**

Experiment 1 — Autocrine vs. Non-autocrine (Transwell assay):

* Setup: Place fibroblasts in the upper chamber of a Transwell insert (permeable membrane, 0.4 μm pores). Place fresh fibroblasts in the lower chamber. Add conditioned medium (containing Signal-X) to fresh fibroblasts in a separate well.
* Expected: If autocrine — only the upper chamber cells proliferate (they produce and respond to Signal-X). If paracrine — both chambers show proliferation (Signal-X diffuses through). If endocrine — proliferation occurs even with diluted medium from distant sources.
* Controls: No-conditioned-medium control; heat-denatured Signal-X (negative control); known autocrine/paracrine positive controls.

Experiment 2 — Distance-dependent signalling (Agarose spot assay):

* Setup: Place fibroblast-secreting Signal-X in an agarose gel spot. Place reporter cells at varying distances.
* Expected: Paracrine shows distance-dependent response (closer cells respond more). Endocrine shows uniform response regardless of distance. Autocrine shows only self-response.

Experiment 3 — Systemic circulation test:

* Setup: Inject Signal-X into a model organism and measure whether it affects distant tissues.
* Expected: Endocrine would show effects at distant sites; autocrine/paracrine would not.

**(b) Receptor Location and Membrane Permeability**

Signal-X (MW = 3,200 Da, hydrophilic) CANNOT freely cross the plasma membrane. The lipid bilayer is hydrophobic and presents a barrier to polar/hydrophilic molecules, especially those >500 Da. Therefore, Signal-X requires a MEMBRANE-BOUND receptor — most likely a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) or an enzyme-coupled receptor (e.g., receptor tyrosine kinase) with an extracellular ligand-binding domain.

This is analogous to insulin (MW = 5,808 Da, protein hormone) which binds to the insulin receptor (a receptor tyrosine kinase) on the cell surface.

**(c) Effect of Lipid Solubility Modification**

If Signal-X becomes lipid-soluble:

* It would diffuse across the plasma membrane without a membrane receptor.
* It would bind to an INTRACELLULAR receptor (e.g., nuclear receptor like steroid hormone receptors).
* The receptor would function as a transcription factor — hormone binding causes translocation to the nucleus and direct regulation of gene expression.
* Response would be slower (hours) compared to membrane receptor pathways (seconds to minutes), as it requires transcription and translation.

## Question 3: Receptor Analysis and Drug Design

**(a) Target Receptor Types**

Condition A (Insulin, MW = 5,808 Da, hydrophilic/protein): MEMBRANE-BOUND receptor required. Insulin cannot cross the lipid bilayer. It binds the insulin receptor (a receptor tyrosine kinase) on the cell surface. The receptor has extracellular α-subunits (ligand binding) and transmembrane β-subunits (kinase activity).

Condition B (Testosterone, MW = 288 Da, lipid-soluble/steroid): INTRACELLULAR receptor. Testosterone diffuses across the plasma membrane and binds the androgen receptor in the cytoplasm. The hormone-receptor complex translocates to the nucleus and acts as a transcription factor.

**(b) GPCR Structural Features and Signal Amplification**

An insulin receptor agonist must bind the extracellular domain because:

* Insulin is hydrophilic and cannot enter the cell.
* The receptor's ligand-binding domain is extracellular.
* Agonists mimicking insulin must replicate the natural binding pattern to trigger conformational change.

GPCR features enabling superior amplification:

* Seven-transmembrane structure allows allosteric activation of heterotrimeric G proteins — one receptor activates many G proteins before desensitization.
* GDP/GTP exchange on Gα acts as a molecular switch — each G protein activates multiple effectors (e.g., adenylyl cyclase molecules).
* Second messenger production (cAMP, Ca²⁺, IP₃) amplifies the signal further — one enzyme produces many second messenger molecules.
* Multiple G protein types (Gs, Gi, Gq) enable signal branching to different downstream pathways from the same receptor.

**(c) Ki Calculation**

Given data: IC50 of Drug-B = 4.0 nM (from table, 50% inhibition between 3 and 10 nM; ~4.0 nM accepted)

Cheng-Prusoff equation: Ki = IC50 / (1 + [L]/Kd)

Substituting: Ki = 4.0 / (1 + 1.0/2.0) = 4.0 / (1 + 0.5) = 4.0 / 1.5 = 2.67 nM

Interpretation: Drug-B binds the androgen receptor with ~2.7× higher affinity than testosterone (Ki < Kd), making it an effective competitive antagonist for treating prostate cancer.

## Question 4: Signal Transduction Pathway Analysis

**(a) From Single Receptor to 200 Genes**

The G protein-linked receptor pathway achieves this through three key features:

**Amplification:**

One active G protein activates multiple effector enzymes. Each produces many second messengers. Each kinase in the MAPK cascade phosphorylates many downstream kinases. By the MAP kinase (Fus3) stage, enormous amplification has occurred.

**Distribution (Branching):**

The pathway branches at multiple points: Gβγ subunits activate separate pathways from Gα; different MAPKKKs may be activated; multiple transcription factors (Ste12, etc.) are activated. Each transcription factor regulates different sets of target genes.

**Integration:**

Multiple signals converge on shared pathway components. The cell integrates pheromone signals with nutrient and stress signals at the MAPK level, ensuring coordinated responses.

Transcriptional cascade: Activated transcription factors induce early genes (e.g., transcription factors themselves), which then activate late genes, exponentially expanding the response.

**(b) Mutant Phenotypes**

Mutant 1 (Constitutively active Gα): PERMANENT signalling regardless of pheromone. Cells would constitutively arrest in G1, form shmoos, and attempt mating even without partner. This demonstrates the importance of GTPase activity as an "off switch."

Mutant 2 (Defective MAP kinase cascade): NO response to pheromone. Cells would continue normal cell division, not arrest, not form shmoos. This shows the MAPK cascade is essential for transducing the signal to nuclear targets.

**(c) Epistasis Analysis**

Method: Create double mutants combining the G protein mutant with the MAPK mutant. If the G protein mutant is upstream of MAPK:

* Gα(constitutively active) + MAPK(-): The double mutant would show the MAPK(-) phenotype (no response), because even though Gα is always on, the downstream MAPK is defective.
* This places MAPK downstream of G protein. The downstream mutant's phenotype masks the upstream mutant's phenotype.

If G protein were downstream of MAPK, the Gα active phenotype would dominate (constitutive signalling). The observed result (MAPK(-) phenotype wins) confirms G protein → MAPK ordering.

## Question 5: Plant Cell Signalling — Stomatal Guard Cells

**(a) Experimental Design**

Objective: Determine if mutant-1 has defect in blue light pathway or ABA pathway.

**Experimental Design:**

Four treatment groups (wild-type and mutant-1, each with ± blue light and ± ABA in factorial design):

* Group 1: WT, darkness, no ABA (baseline open)
* Group 2: WT, blue light, no ABA (blue light induces opening)
* Group 3: WT, darkness + 10 μM ABA (ABA induces closing)
* Group 4: WT, blue light + 10 μM ABA (competing signals)
* Groups 5-8: Same as 1-4 but with mutant-1

Measurements: Stomatal aperture width (μm) using light microscopy; measure 30 stomata per treatment; calculate mean ± SEM.

**Predicted Results:**

If blue light pathway defect: mutant-1 will NOT open in response to blue light (Groups 2 vs 6 differ), but WILL close in response to ABA (Groups 3 vs 7 similar).

If ABA pathway defect: mutant-1 will open normally with blue light (Groups 2 vs 6 similar), but will NOT close in response to ABA (Groups 3 vs 7 differ).

**(b) Statistical Analysis**

Calculating t-statistic for WT + ABA vs mutant-1 + ABA:

Mean difference = 2.1 - 6.8 = -4.7 μm

SEM₁ = 0.3, SEM₂ = 0.4

t = |2.1 - 6.8| / √(0.3² + 0.4²) = 4.7 / √(0.09 + 0.16) = 4.7 / √0.25 = 4.7 / 0.5 = 9.4

**t = 9.4**

Since 9.4 >> 2.0 (t-critical), the difference is HIGHLY statistically significant (p < 0.001).

Interpretation: mutant-1 fails to respond to ABA (stomata remain open at 6.8 μm vs WT closing to 2.1 μm). This indicates a defect in the ABA signalling pathway.

**(c) Signal Integration Advantage**

Multiple converging pathways controlling stomatal aperture provide several advantages:

* Redundancy and robustness: If one sensor fails, others can still regulate stomatal movement.
* Context-dependent decision-making: The plant integrates light (photosynthesis opportunity), CO2 (carbon availability), and water status (ABA) to optimize the stomatal aperture for maximum carbon gain with minimal water loss.
* Rapid response to priority threats: During drought, ABA overrides the blue light "open" signal to prevent dehydration — this is hierarchical signal integration.
* Fine-tuning: Small changes in multiple inputs can produce graded responses rather than all-or-none behaviour.

Signal integration at the guard cell level ensures the plant makes optimal trade-offs between photosynthesis and water conservation in fluctuating environments.
