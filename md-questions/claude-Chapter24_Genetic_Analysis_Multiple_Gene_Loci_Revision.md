**Key idea for this chapter:** Mendel’s law of independent assortment says that alleles of genes on **different homologous chromosome pairs** segregate into gametes independently of one another. A dihybrid (Y/y R/r) therefore makes four gamete types in equal frequency — **YR : Yr : yR : yr = 1 : 1 : 1 : 1** — and a dihybrid × dihybrid cross gives the classic **9 : 3 : 3 : 1** phenotypic ratio in F2. When two loci sit **on the same chromosome (linked)**, they do NOT assort independently: parental gamete types are more frequent than recombinants, which arise only by crossing over (chiasmata) in Prophase I.

# Answer Key

*Concise model answers. For short-answer and applied questions, key marking points are given; equivalent wording is acceptable.*

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** Independent assortment: during meiosis, alleles of one gene segregate into gametes independently of the alleles of another gene, so all allele combinations are equally likely. It holds when the two genes are on DIFFERENT homologous chromosome pairs (or, more loosely, far apart on the same chromosome).

**2.** Gametes: YR, Yr, yR, yr, in a 1 : 1 : 1 : 1 ratio.

**3.** Each homologous pair aligns independently at metaphase I; the orientation of the Y/y pair is independent of the orientation of the R/r pair. The two equally likely alignments send (YR + yr) in one case and (Yr + yR) in the other; pooled over many meioses this gives the four gamete types in equal frequency.

**4.** 9 round/yellow (Y/– R/–) : 3 round/green (Y/–... here Y = yellow so: 9 yellow round (Y/– R/–) : 3 yellow wrinkled (Y/– r/r) : 3 green round (y/y R/–) : 1 green wrinkled (y/y r/r). [Naming the two dominant + two single-dominant + double-recessive classes is the key point.]

**5.** Each locus alone gives 3 dominant : 1 recessive. Multiplying the two independent ratios: (3 + 1)(3 + 1) expanded by phenotype = 9 (both dominant) : 3 (dominant×recessive) : 3 (recessive×dominant) : 1 (both recessive) = 9:3:3:1.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** No. A single meiosis yields gametes of only TWO of the four types (either YR + yr, or Yr + yR), depending on the alignment; all four types appear only across many meioses.

**2.** Yes (this is the physical basis of the law).

**3.** Yes — Y/Y R/R appears once (top-left box) out of 16.

**4.** Yes — with incomplete dominance the heterozygote is distinguishable, so the ratio changes (e.g. to 1:2:1 patterns).

**5.** No — 1:1:1:1 is the gamete ratio of one dihybrid; 9:3:3:1 is the F2 offspring phenotype ratio of dihybrid × dihybrid.

Each alignment is equally likely, so pooled gametes = YR : Yr : yR : yr = 1:1:1:1. (A sketch of two metaphase-I plates with the second locus’s homologue reversed earns full marks.)

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** A test cross is a cross of an individual of unknown genotype to a homozygous recessive “tester” (e.g. a/a b/b). The tester contributes only recessive alleles, so it cannot mask anything: the offspring phenotypes directly reveal which gametes (and therefore which alleles) the unknown parent contributed.

**2.** A back cross is a cross of an F1 to an individual with one of the PARENTAL genotypes. It is also a test cross only when that parental genotype happens to be homozygous recessive at the loci examined.

**3.** A/a B/b × a/a b/b → 1 A/a B/b : 1 A/a b/b : 1 a/a B/b : 1 a/a b/b (= 1:1:1:1 of the four phenotypes). Because the tester adds only ‘ab’, each offspring’s phenotype is set by the dihybrid’s gamete — so the offspring ratio is a direct readout of the gamete ratio.

**4.** (a) Flower colour: F1 are pink, intermediate between white and purple → incomplete dominance (heterozygote distinct). (b) Pod length: F1 are long like one parent → long is completely dominant over short.

**5.** B/– S/– could be BB SS, BB Ss, Bb SS or Bb Ss — the dominant phenotype hides whether a recessive allele is present. A test cross to b/b s/s resolves it most efficiently (offspring phenotypes reveal each parental allele).

**2.** Yes — a 1:1:1:1 ratio shows the unknown parent contributed all four gamete types equally, i.e. it was Bb Ss (with the loci unlinked).

**3.** Yes.

**4.** No — the white-short tester (WW ss) contributes a W allele, so offspring are either white (WW) or pink (WP); purple (PP) cannot form.

**5.** No — a dihybrid test cross gives 1:1:1:1; only a dihybrid self-cross gives 9:3:3:1.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** Loci on the same chromosome tend to travel together into the same gamete because they are physically joined; they cannot align/separate independently. The consequence is that the gamete ratio departs from 1:1:1:1 — parental allele combinations are over-represented.

**2.** Parental gametes carry the allele combinations that entered the cross together (e.g. AB and ab for an AB/ab dihybrid). Recombinant gametes carry new combinations (Ab and aB). When linked, the parental types are more frequent.

**3.** Crossing over is the reciprocal exchange of segments between non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes; it occurs during Prophase I of meiosis. A chiasma is the visible X-shaped point where this exchange occurs.

**4.** A crossover between the two loci on one pair of non-sister chromatids swaps the alleles distal to the crossover, converting an AB chromatid into Ab and an ab chromatid into aB (the two chromatids not involved remain parental). This generates recombinant gametes.

**5.** In a test cross the recessive tester contributes only recessive alleles, so every offspring phenotype directly reports the dihybrid’s gamete type. This lets you count parental vs recombinant gametes directly; a self-cross blurs the gamete origins (dominant alleles from both parents overlap), making linkage harder to quantify.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** No — with no crossing over, only parental gamete types (AB and ab) are produced.

**3.** Yes.

**4.** Yes.

**5.** Yes.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** Unlinked prediction: 1:1:1:1 (250 each of 1000). (a) Parental classes are the two large ones: AaBb (410) and aabb (390); recombinant classes are the two small ones: Aabb (95) and aaBb (105). (b) Yes, the data indicate LINKAGE: the four classes are not 1:1:1:1 — there is a large excess of parental types (≈ 800) over recombinants (≈ 200), which independent assortment cannot produce. (c) The two smaller classes were produced by crossing over (recombination) between the two loci during Prophase I.

**2.** (a) Recombinants = 70 + 60 = 130. (b) Recombination frequency = 130 / 1000 × 100 = 13%. (c) About 13% recombination corresponds to roughly 13 map units (cM): the larger the recombination frequency, the farther apart the loci; 13% indicates the genes are linked but a moderate distance apart (a 50% frequency would mean effectively unlinked).

**3.** (a) Cell 1 (no crossover) → gametes AB and ab (parental). Cell 2 (single crossover between loci) → AB, ab, Ab, aB (two parental + two recombinant). (b) Most meiocytes have no crossover between two given loci (or the crossover lies elsewhere), so parental gametes dominate; recombinants come only from the minority of meioses with a crossover between the loci. (c) If the loci are extremely close, crossovers between them essentially never happen, so the recombinant classes Ab and aB would be absent (only AB and ab gametes form).
