## Revision Set 1 — Enzymes as Catalysts: Activation Energy and Energetics

### A. Short-answer

**1.** As well as speeding reactions, enzymes (i) CONTROL reactions and (ii) provide SPECIFICITY, with a specific enzyme controlling each individual reaction. Because over 1,000 different reactions occur in a cell, having one enzyme per reaction lets the cell regulate its very complex metabolism in small, gentle, controllable steps (and many enzymes have important clinical applications).

**2.** Activation energy (E\_A) is the energy that reactants must absorb from their surroundings to reach the unstable transition state where bonds can break. The transition state is the intermediate stage of a reaction in which old bonds break and new bonds form; it sits at the maximum (highest free energy) of the curve connecting reactants and products.

**3.** An enzyme increases the rate by lowering the activation energy (delta-G double-dagger) needed to reach the transition state, i.e. it provides an alternative pathway; this increases the concentration of the transition state so more substrate is converted per unit time. Enzymes can increase reaction rates by a factor of about 10^9 to 10^20 over the uncatalysed reaction.

**4.** delta-G (and the equilibrium constant) depends only on the free-energy difference between reactants and products, which the enzyme does not change — the start and end energies are the same with or without the catalyst. The enzyme only lowers delta-G double-dagger (the activation energy / barrier height), so it speeds the reaction and the approach to equilibrium without changing the position of equilibrium or the overall energy released.

**5.** Carbonic anhydrase lowers the activation energy for CO2 + H2O <-> HCO3- + H+, so it greatly speeds up this (reversible) reaction. It does NOT change the equilibrium position, the equilibrium constant, or the overall delta-G — it just lets equilibrium be reached far faster.

### B. Yes/No

**1.** No — enzymes do not change the delta-G between reactants and products.

**2.** Yes — enzymes lower the activation energy (delta-G double-dagger).

**3.** No — almost all enzymes are proteins, but some RNAs catalyse their own self-cleavage (ribozymes).

**4.** Yes — enzymes provide an alternative (lower-barrier) pathway for the reaction.

**5.** No — a catalyst speeds up a reaction but cannot make a thermodynamically nonspontaneous reaction spontaneous; the sign of delta-G is unchanged.

### C. MCQ

**1.** B (lowering the activation energy).

**2.** B (old bonds break and new bonds form, the highest-free-energy state).

**3.** C (10^9 to 10^20).

**4.** C (proteins).

**5.** B (the activation energy, delta-G double-dagger).

**6.** B (the difference between reactant and product energies under standard conditions).

**7.** C (the equilibrium constant / the overall delta-G).

**8.** C (substrates).

**9.** B (CO2 + H2O <-> HCO3- + H+).

**10.** B (an alternative pathway with lower activation energy).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Catalase lowers E\_A by 75.2 - 23.0 = 52.2 kJ/mol; platinum lowers it by 75.2 - 48.9 = 26.3 kJ/mol. (b) Catalase vs platinum: 6.51 x 10^8 / 2.77 x 10^4 = 2.35 x 10^4, so catalase is about 23,500 times faster than platinum (and 6.51 x 10^8 times faster than uncatalysed). (c) No catalyst changes delta-G (the energy released); catalysts only lower the activation barrier, so the energy difference between reactants and products is identical in all three cases. (d) 18.0 kcal/mol x 4.184 = 75.3 kJ/mol, which matches 75.2 kJ/mol, so the values are consistent.

**2.** Adding the enzyme will make the reaction REACH equilibrium much faster (it speeds both the forward and reverse reaction by lowering the activation energy). It does NOT change the equilibrium constant (still 0.95), the final equilibrium concentrations of products and reactants, or the delta-G of the reaction. In short: same destination, reached sooner.

## Revision Set 2 — Enzyme Structure, Specificity, Classification and Mechanism

### A. Short-answer

**1.** An enzyme name is usually formed by adding the suffix '-ase' to the name of the reacting substance or the reaction: it may identify the substrate (e.g. sucrase acts on sucrose) or describe the reaction (e.g. oxidase catalyses an oxidation). Examples: sucrase, oxidase, urease. Exception: some enzymes, especially digestive ones, keep common names, e.g. pepsin and trypsin.

**2.** (1) Oxidoreductases — transfer of electrons; (2) Transferases — transfer of C-, N- or P-containing groups; (3) Hydrolases — bond cleavage by addition of water; (4) Lyases — cleavage of C-C, C-S, C-N bonds, often forming a double bond; (5) Isomerases — formation of optical or geometric isomers; (6) Ligases — form new bonds between molecules using ATP energy.

**3.** Lock-and-key model: the enzyme is a rigid three-dimensional body whose surface contains an active site that the substrate fits exactly (like a key in a lock). Induced-fit model: the active site is not rigid but becomes modified (changes shape) to accommodate the substrate as it binds. Difference: rigid pre-formed fit (lock-and-key) vs flexible shape change on binding (induced fit).

**4.** The CHEMISTRY of the active site is more important than its shape. Just five amino acids participate in the active site of more than 65% of enzymes studied: His > Cys > Asp > Arg > Glu. Four have acidic or basic side chains (His and Arg basic; Asp and Glu acidic); the fifth, Cys, has a sulfhydryl (-SH) group.

**5.** The active site lowers the E\_A barrier by: orienting substrates correctly; straining/distorting substrate bonds; providing a favourable microenvironment; and (in some cases) covalently bonding to the substrate. (Binding the substrate via R groups disrupts its stability, reducing the energy needed to change it.)

### B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — the suffix '-ase' usually indicates an enzyme.

**2.** Yes — in the lock-and-key model the enzyme is treated as a rigid 3-D body.

**3.** Yes — in the induced-fit model the active site changes shape to accommodate the substrate.

**4.** No — the chemistry of the active site is more important than its shape.

**5.** Yes — the enzyme is unchanged by the reaction and is free to bind another substrate molecule.

### C. MCQ

**1.** A (sucrose).

**2.** B (bond cleavage by the addition of water).

**3.** A (transfer of electrons).

**4.** C (hydrolase).

**5.** A (form new bonds between molecules using ATP energy).

**6.** B (formation of optical or geometric isomers).

**7.** C (substrate).

**8.** B (Cys).

**9.** B (lysine and arginine).

**10.** B (the active site changes shape to fit the substrate).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Form bonds using ATP -> LIGASE (e.g. pyruvate carboxylase). (b) Rearrange atoms to form an isomer -> ISOMERASE (e.g. maleate isomerase). (c) Transfer a group between compounds -> TRANSFERASE (e.g. hexokinase). (d) Hydrolysis / bond cleavage by adding water -> HYDROLASE (e.g. trypsin).

**2.** Best mechanism: activation by limited (auto)proteolytic CLEAVAGE that removes an inhibitory N-terminal propeptide (a zymogen / covalent-modification mechanism). The zymogen still has a functional catalytic pair (His48 assisted by Asp102, like a serine-protease catalytic triad/dyad) that lets it slowly cleave its OWN single peptide bond between residues 45 and 46; removing the 45-residue N-terminal fragment lets the catalytic domain adopt its fully active conformation, giving high activity. Justification from each observation: (i) low activity of the intact zymogen shows the active site is not yet fully formed/accessible; (ii) the single 45/46 cleavage producing an N-terminal fragment + catalytic domain identifies the propeptide that is removed on activation; (iii) His48->Ala abolishing both cleavage and activation shows His48 is the essential catalytic residue performing the chemistry, while Asp102->Asn only reducing autoproteolysis shows Asp102 assists (orients/polarises His48) but is not absolutely essential. So His48 = key catalytic residue; Asp102 = supporting catalytic residue.

## Revision Set 3 — Enzyme Kinetics and Factors Affecting Enzymes

### A. Short-answer

**1.** A kinetic measurement is a measurement of change over a defined time interval (e.g. the amount of NADH produced in 1 minute). Reaction rate can be measured by (1) the decreasing concentration of substrate, or (2) the increasing concentration of product.

**2.** Rate rises steeply with substrate concentration at low [S], then levels off and approaches a plateau (the maximum rate, Vmax) at high [S]. Saturation is the point at which all enzyme molecules are bound to substrate, so the enzyme is working at its maximum rate and adding more substrate cannot increase the rate further.

**3.** v = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S]). Vmax is the maximum reaction velocity, reached when the enzyme is saturated with substrate. Km (the Michaelis constant) is the substrate concentration at which the velocity is half of Vmax (1/2 Vmax); a low Km means high apparent affinity for the substrate.

**4.** Enzymes are most active at an optimum temperature (usually about 37 degrees C in humans). They show little activity at low temperatures (molecules move slowly, few collisions). Above about 50 degrees C they lose activity because denaturation disrupts the tertiary structure and the active site, abolishing catalytic activity.

**5.** Enzymes are most active at an optimum pH, where the R groups of active-site amino acids carry the correct charges. At pH values that are too low or too high, the charges change and the tertiary structure is disrupted, so activity falls. Example optima: pepsin (stomach) about 1.5-2.0, while trypsin (small intestine) is about 7.7-8.0. (Most body enzymes work best around pH 7.4.)

### B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — at saturation all enzyme molecules are bound to substrate and the enzyme works at maximum rate.

**2.** No — activity rises with temperature only up to the optimum; beyond it (above ~50 degrees C) denaturation causes activity to fall.

**3.** No — cofactors are NONprotein enzyme helpers (inorganic metal ions or organic molecules).

**4.** Yes — a prosthetic group is a coenzyme/cofactor permanently (tightly) bound to the enzyme.

**5.** Yes — when [S] = Km, v = 1/2 Vmax (this is the definition of Km).

### C. MCQ

**1.** B (v = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S])).

**2.** C (half of Vmax).

**3.** C (approaches Vmax, a plateau).

**4.** C (37 degrees C).

**5.** B (denaturation / loss of tertiary structure).

**6.** C (7 to 8, around 7.4).

**7.** A (1.5 to 2.0).

**8.** C (inorganic, e.g. metal ions, or organic).

**9.** B (prosthetic group).

**10.** A (it resembles the substrate and competes for the active site).

### D. Applied

**1.** Using v = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S]) with Vmax = 60 micromol/min and Km = 4 mM: (a) [S] = 4 mM: v = 60 x 4 / (4 + 4) = 240/8 = 30 micromol/min. (b) [S] = 12 mM: v = 60 x 12 / (4 + 12) = 720/16 = 45 micromol/min. (c) [S] = 36 mM: v = 60 x 36 / (4 + 36) = 2160/40 = 54 micromol/min. (d) At [S] = 4 mM, v = 30 = 1/2 Vmax; this is exactly what Km means — Km is the substrate concentration that gives half-maximal velocity (here Km = 4 mM).

**2.** (a) No — pepsin would be essentially inactive in the small intestine (pH about 8). Pepsin's optimum is pH 1.5-2.0; at pH 8 the wrong charges on its active-site R groups and disruption of its tertiary structure prevent proper substrate binding/catalysis. (Trypsin, with optimum pH 7.7-8.0, is the one suited to the small intestine.) (b) The rise from 10 to ~37 degrees C occurs because higher temperature gives molecules more kinetic energy, so substrate and enzyme collide more often and more substrate molecules can reach the transition state, increasing the rate. The sharp fall above ~50 degrees C is due to denaturation: heat disrupts the bonds maintaining the enzyme's tertiary structure, destroying the active site. This high-temperature loss of activity is usually NOT reversible (denaturation is generally permanent), unlike the reversible slowing seen at low temperature.
