## Part A: Answers

**Answer 1**

The complete balanced equation for oxygenic photosynthesis is:

6 CO2 + 12 H2O + light energy -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2 + 6 H2O

In the light-dependent reactions specifically:

- Reactants: H2O, light energy, NADP+, ADP, Pi

- Products: O2, ATP, NADPH

Note: Water is split (photolysis) to provide electrons and protons, releasing O2 as a byproduct.

**Answer 2**

The chloroplast has a three-membrane system:

- Outer membrane: Permeable to small molecules

- Inner membrane: Selectively permeable; contains transport proteins

- Thylakoid membrane: The site of the light reactions; contains chlorophyll, photosystems, and electron transport chain components

Thylakoids are flattened sac-like structures that form grana (stacks). The thylakoid membrane houses the protein complexes for light absorption and electron transport. The stroma is the fluid-filled space surrounding thylakoids where the Calvin cycle occurs. The thylakoid lumen (inside the thylakoid sacs) maintains a proton gradient essential for ATP synthesis.

**Answer 3**

The energy of a photon is given by E = hc/lambda, where h is Planck's constant, c is the speed of light, and lambda is wavelength. Therefore, energy is inversely proportional to wavelength.

Blue light (450 nm) carries MORE energy per photon than red light (680 nm) because it has a shorter wavelength. Higher frequency/shorter wavelength photons have more energy. This is why blue light can excite electrons to higher energy states compared to red light.

**Answer 4**

A photosystem is a protein-pigment complex embedded in the thylakoid membrane that captures light energy for photosynthesis.

Components:

- Reaction-center complex: Contains a pair of chlorophyll a molecules (P680 in PSII, P700 in PSI) that serve as the primary electron donor. This is where photochemistry occurs.

- Light-harvesting complexes (antenna pigments): Contain chlorophyll and accessory pigments that absorb photons and transfer energy to the reaction center via resonance energy transfer.

Working mechanism: Photons excite antenna pigment electrons. Energy is passed from pigment to pigment via resonance energy transfer until it reaches the reaction center, where an electron is transferred to a primary electron acceptor, initiating electron transport.

**Answer 5**

Major pigments:

1. Chlorophyll a: The primary photosynthetic pigment; absorbs red (~680 nm) and blue (~430 nm) light; directly participates in photochemistry at the reaction centers.

2. Chlorophyll b: An accessory pigment; absorbs blue and orange-red light; transfers energy to chlorophyll a; extends the range of wavelengths that can drive photosynthesis.

3. Carotenoids: Accessory pigments that absorb blue and green light; also serve a photoprotective role by dissipating excess energy and scavenging reactive oxygen species that could damage the photosynthetic apparatus.

4. Phycobilins: Found in cyanobacteria and red algae; absorb green and yellow light that chlorophyll cannot capture efficiently.

**Answer 6**

The electron path in linear electron flow:

1. A photon excites P680 in PSII, causing electron ejection

2. The electron is accepted by pheophytin (primary acceptor)

3. Electrons pass through plastoquinone (PQ) -> cytochrome b6f complex -> plastocyanin (PC)

4. A photon excites P700 in PSI, causing electron ejection

5. Electrons pass through ferredoxin (Fd)

6. NADP+ reductase uses electrons from Fd to reduce NADP+ to NADPH

7. Meanwhile, water is split at PSII (photolysis) to replace electrons lost from P680

Overall: H2O -> PSII -> PQ -> Cyt b6f -> PC -> PSI -> Fd -> NADPH

**Answer 7**

Photolysis is the light-driven splitting of water molecules at Photosystem II. The reaction is:

2 H2O -> 4 H+ + 4 e- + O2

Process: Water molecules are split by the oxygen-evolving complex (OEC) associated with PSII. This releases: electrons (which replace those lost from P680), protons (which contribute to the proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane), and O2 (as a byproduct).

Essential role: Without photolysis, PSII would lose electrons and become oxidized, halting electron transport. Water serves as the ultimate electron donor, making sustained photosynthesis possible. The protons released also contribute to the proton-motive force used for ATP synthesis.

**Answer 8**

Similarities:

- Both involve electron transport through the thylakoid membrane

- Both generate a proton gradient used for ATP synthesis

- Both involve Photosystem I

Differences:

Non-cyclic (Linear):

- Involves both PSII and PSI

- Electrons from water replace those lost from PSII

- Products: ATP, NADPH, and O2

- NADP+ is the final electron acceptor

Cyclic:

- Involves only PSI

- No water splitting occurs

- Products: ATP only (no NADPH, no O2)

- Electrons return to the cytochrome b6f complex

- Occurs when the cell needs additional ATP relative to NADPH

- Favored under conditions of high NADPH or when the Calvin cycle demands more ATP

**Answer 9**

ATP synthase (CFoCF1 complex) structure:

- CF1 (head): Projects into the stroma; contains catalytic sites for ATP synthesis

- CFo (base): Embedded in the thylakoid membrane; forms a proton channel

- Stalk: Connects CF1 to CFo; transmits rotational energy

Mechanism (chemiosmosis):

1. Electron transport pumps protons (H+) from the stroma into the thylakoid lumen

2. This creates an electrochemical proton gradient (proton-motive force)

3. Protons flow back to the stroma through CFo, causing rotation

4. Rotation drives conformational changes in CF1 that catalyze ATP synthesis from ADP + Pi

Comparison to respiration: Similar mechanism (chemiosmosis) but opposite orientation. In mitochondria, protons are pumped out of the matrix; in chloroplasts, protons are pumped into the thylakoid lumen. Both use rotary catalysis by ATP synthase.

**Answer 10**

ATP and NADPH roles:

- ATP provides the chemical energy needed for carbon fixation and reduction in the Calvin cycle

- NADPH provides the reducing power (electrons and protons) for reducing 3-phosphoglycerate to G3P

Usage location: Both are produced in the stroma (NADPH) and used in the stroma during the Calvin cycle.

Ratio importance: The Calvin cycle requires 3 ATP and 2 NADPH per CO2 fixed (ratio 1.5:1). Linear electron flow produces approximately equal amounts of ATP and NADPH. Cyclic electron flow can supplement ATP production without additional NADPH generation, helping maintain the proper ATP/NADPH balance needed for the Calvin cycle. An imbalance would either limit carbon fixation or cause accumulation of unused intermediates.

**Answer 11**

Photosystem I was discovered first (in the 1950s) because it was easier to isolate and study. It was named PSI because it was the first photosystem to be characterized biochemically.

Photosystem II was discovered later (in the 1960s) because it is more labile (unstable) and harder to isolate intact. Its more complex water-splitting apparatus makes it more susceptible to damage during purification.

The naming convention reflects the historical order of discovery, NOT the functional order in the electron transport chain. PSII actually functions first in linear electron flow, capturing light energy and initiating electron transport by splitting water.

**Answer 12**

Absorption spectrum: A graph showing how much light a pigment absorbs at different wavelengths. It reveals the absorption peaks of specific pigments.

Action spectrum: A graph showing the rate of photosynthesis at different wavelengths of light. It reveals which wavelengths are most effective at driving the overall process.

Why they differ:

The action spectrum does not perfectly match chlorophyll a's absorption spectrum because accessory pigments (chlorophyll b, carotenoids) also absorb light and transfer energy to chlorophyll a. These accessory pigments absorb wavelengths that chlorophyll a cannot, broadening the range of light that can drive photosynthesis. Carotenoids, for example, strongly absorb blue-green light (~450-550 nm), which chlorophyll a absorbs poorly, yet these wavelengths still drive photosynthesis effectively.

**Answer 13**

Stomata are microscopic pores on leaf surfaces that regulate gas exchange.

Role in photosynthesis:

- CO2 enters through open stomata to reach the mesophyll cells

- O2 (a byproduct of photosynthesis) exits through stomata

- Water vapor also exits through stomata (transpiration)

Trade-offs:

Open stomata: Allow CO2 entry (needed for photosynthesis) but also allow water loss through transpiration. This is risky during hot, dry conditions when water loss could exceed uptake.

Closed stomata: Reduce water loss but limit CO2 availability, reducing photosynthetic rate. In extreme heat or drought, plants may close stomata entirely, even at the cost of stopping photosynthesis.

Plants regulate stomatal aperture to balance carbon gain against water loss.

**Answer 14**

Three possible fates when a photon hits a molecule:

1. Scattered or reflected: The photon bounces off without interaction

2. Transmitted: The photon passes through the molecule

3. Absorbed: The photon's energy is captured by the molecule

Excited state:

When energy is absorbed, an electron in the pigment molecule moves to a higher energy orbital (excited state). This unstable state has several possible outcomes:

- Energy can be released as heat or fluorescence as the electron returns to the ground state

- Energy can be transferred to a neighboring pigment molecule (resonance energy transfer)

- At the reaction center, the high-energy electron can be transferred to an electron acceptor, initiating electron transport

The excited state is short-lived (~10^-9 seconds for fluorescence), so energy must be captured quickly for photochemistry to occur.

**Answer 15**

The atmospheric change was the Great Oxygenation Event (also called the Oxygen Catastrophe) - the accumulation of O2 in Earth's atmosphere from negligible levels to approximately 21%.

Transformative effects:

1. Allowed aerobic respiration to evolve, yielding ~18x more ATP per glucose than anaerobic metabolism

2. Formation of the ozone (O3) layer, which blocked harmful UV radiation and allowed life to colonize land

3. Changed the chemistry of oceans and atmosphere, making them oxidizing environments

4. Drove many anaerobic organisms to extinction while creating new ecological niches

5. The oxygen was produced as a byproduct of photosynthetic water-splitting by cyanobacteria, which gradually overwhelmed the chemical sinks (reduced iron, sulfur, etc.) that had previously kept atmospheric O2 near zero

## Part B: Answers

**Answer 16**

(a) Experimental design:

- Set up 5 test tubes with equal volumes of chloroplast suspension and DCPIP dye (blue)

- Place each tube at equal distance from the light source with different colored filters between the light and the tube

- Use a white light control (no filter)

- Include a dark control (wrapped in foil) to account for non-photochemical reduction

- Illuminate all tubes for a fixed time period (e.g., 10 minutes)

- Measure absorbance of each sample at 600 nm (where oxidized DCPIP absorbs) using a spectrophotometer

- The rate of DCPIP reduction (color change from blue to colorless) indicates the rate of the light reactions

(b) Predicted results:

- Blue filter: High rate of reduction (chlorophyll absorbs strongly at ~430 nm)

- Red filter: High rate of reduction (chlorophyll absorbs strongly at ~680 nm)

- Green filter: Low rate of reduction (chlorophyll reflects/transmits green light)

- Yellow filter: Moderate-low rate (accessory pigments may absorb some light)

- White light: Highest rate (contains all wavelengths)

- Dark control: No reduction

(c) To ensure equal light intensity:

- Use a light meter to measure photon flux density at each position

- Adjust distance from light source or use neutral density filters to equalize intensities

- Alternatively, use a monochromator that selects wavelengths while maintaining constant photon flux

**Answer 17**

(a) Calculations:

Given: 2 H2O produce 1 O2, and the transfer of 2 electrons generates ~1.5 ATP

From 12 mmol O2/hour:

- Water split = 2 x 12 = 24 mmol H2O/hour

- Electrons transferred = 4 x 12 = 48 mmol e-/hour (4 electrons per O2)

- NADPH produced = 2 x 12 = 24 mmol NADPH/hour (2 NADPH per O2)

- ATP produced = (48/2) x 1.5 = 36 mmol ATP/hour

Answer: 24 mmol NADPH and 36 mmol ATP per hour

(b) Limiting analysis:

For 12 mmol CO2 fixed, Calvin cycle needs:

- ATP needed = 12 x 3 = 36 mmol ATP

- NADPH needed = 12 x 2 = 24 mmol NADPH

The ATP/NADPH ratio needed = 36/24 = 1.5

The ATP/NADPH ratio produced = 36/24 = 1.5

They match exactly, so neither is limiting under these conditions. However, if the actual yield is slightly lower (e.g., 1.3 ATP per 2 e-), ATP would become limiting first.

(c) Cyclic electron flow supplements ATP production without generating additional NADPH. If ATP becomes limiting, cyclic flow around PSI increases the ATP/NADPH ratio by generating extra ATP, balancing the supply for the Calvin cycle.

**Answer 18**

(a) Inhibitor sites:

Inhibitor A (DCMU): Blocks at the QB binding site on the acceptor side of PSII, preventing electron transfer from QA to plastoquinone. This stops all downstream electron flow.

Inhibitor B (DBMIB): Blocks at the Qi site of the cytochrome b6f complex, preventing electron transfer from plastoquinol to plastocyanin.

Inhibitor C (Methyl viologen/paraquat): Accepts electrons from PSI (at the ferredoxin site) and transfers them directly to O2, forming superoxide radicals. It bypasses the normal NADP+ reduction pathway.

(b) DCMU causes increased PSII fluorescence because:

When DCMU blocks electron transfer from QA-, QA- accumulates in its reduced state. This prevents re-oxidation of the reaction center. The trapped energy cannot be used for photochemistry, so more energy is lost as fluorescence (chlorophyll fluorescence increases when photochemistry is inhibited).

(c) NADPH predictions:

- Inhibitor A: No NADPH production (electron flow stopped at PSII)

- Inhibitor B: No NADPH production (electron flow stopped at cytochrome b6f)

- Inhibitor C: Reduced NADPH production; methyl viologen competes with NADP+ for electrons from ferredoxin, diverting electrons to O2 instead of NADP+ reduction

**Answer 19**

(a) Energy calculation:

8 photons per O2 evolved

Energy = 8 x 175 kJ/mol = 1,400 kJ/mol of O2

(b) Efficiency calculation:

Efficiency = (Useful energy output / Energy input) x 100

Efficiency = (440 / 1,400) x 100 = 31.4%

The maximum theoretical energy conversion efficiency is approximately 31.4%.

(c) Factors reducing actual efficiency:

1. Fluorescence: Some absorbed energy is re-emitted as light rather than used for photochemistry

2. Heat dissipation: Energy is lost as heat during energy transfer between pigments and during electron transport

3. Photorespiration: Rubisco oxygenase activity wastes energy

4. Imperfect coupling: Not all protons pass through ATP synthase; some leak across the membrane

5. Spectral mismatch: Not all absorbed photons have the optimal energy; excess energy from short-wavelength photons is lost as heat

6. Metabolic overhead: Energy is required for maintenance, protein synthesis, and transport processes

**Answer 20**

(a) Experimental design:

Set up two treatments with isolated chloroplasts:

Treatment 1 (Non-cyclic conditions): Standard assay with intact PSII and PSI function; measure O2 evolution and ATP/NADPH production under white light

Treatment 2 (PSII-blocked conditions): Add DCMU to block PSII, then measure:

- O2 evolution (should be zero since PSII is blocked)

- ATP production (measure using luciferin-luciferase assay or 32P incorporation)

- NADPH production (measure spectrophotometrically at 340 nm)

Expected results for cyclic flow demonstration:

- Treatment 2 shows no O2 evolution (confirms PSII is blocked)

- Treatment 2 shows ATP production continues (demonstrates ATP from cyclic flow)

- Treatment 2 shows little to no NADPH production (confirms it's not non-cyclic)

Controls:

- Dark control (no light) to account for non-photochemical ATP formation

- DCMU-free control to establish baseline

- Heat-inactivated chloroplasts as negative control

(b) Distinguishing features:

- Cyclic flow: ATP produced, no O2 evolved, no NADPH produced

- Non-cyclic flow: ATP and NADPH produced, O2 evolved

By comparing these parameters in the presence and absence of PSII inhibitors, you can differentiate the two pathways.

(c) Conditions favoring cyclic electron flow:

- High NADPH/NADP+ ratio: When NADP+ is scarce, ferredoxin cannot pass electrons to NADP+, so they cycle back through the cytochrome b6f complex

- High ATP demand: When the Calvin cycle needs more ATP (e.g., during active starch synthesis)

- Low CO2 conditions: When carbon fixation is limited, less NADPH is consumed, favoring cyclic flow to maintain ATP supply

- Physiological rationale: Cyclic flow allows the chloroplast to adjust the ATP/NADPH ratio to match metabolic demands without generating excess reducing power
