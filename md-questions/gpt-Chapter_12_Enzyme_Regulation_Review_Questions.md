# Answer Key

## Set 1 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

**1.** Cells regulate enzymes by controlling gene expression (how many enzyme molecules are made) and by regulating enzyme activity directly, for example by inhibitors, activators, or allosteric shape changes.

**2.** Competitive inhibitors bind the active site and compete with substrate; adding more substrate can often reduce inhibition. Noncompetitive inhibitors bind elsewhere, change enzyme shape, and are not normally overcome simply by adding more substrate.

**3.** Irreversible inhibitors form covalent bonds to side chains, often in the active site, permanently inactivating the enzyme. Aspirin acetylates COX; sarin covalently modifies a serine residue in acetylcholinesterase.

**4.** Allosteric regulation occurs when a non-substrate molecule binds at a site other than the active site and changes enzyme shape. Many allosteric enzymes have quaternary structure because catalytic and regulatory subunits can communicate conformational changes.

**5.** Feedback inhibition occurs when a pathway product inhibits an earlier enzyme in its own synthesis pathway. This prevents overproduction and avoids wasting energy and intermediates.

### D. Application / pathway-analysis questions

**1.** Competitive inhibition. The inhibitor competes with substrate for the active site, so high substrate concentration increases the chance that substrate, rather than inhibitor, occupies the active site.

**2.** Noncompetitive inhibition. The inhibitor changes enzyme shape away from the active site, reducing catalytic effectiveness or Vmax; extra substrate cannot fully correct the altered enzyme conformation.

**3.** Feedback inhibition. It prevents excess D production, conserves resources, and helps match pathway flow to cellular demand.

## Set 2 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

**1.** The mutant enzyme likely denatures at 37 °C. Increased temperature disrupts weak interactions stabilising secondary/tertiary structure, altering the active site. Because function does not return at 20 °C, the change is effectively irreversible under the test conditions.

**2.** Their active sites have different shapes and chemical environments. Trypsin recognises peptide bonds next to lysine/arginine, while elastase recognises bonds next to small residues such as alanine.

**3.** Regulating enzyme amount changes how much enzyme is produced through gene expression. Regulating enzyme activity changes how active existing enzyme molecules are, through inhibitors, activators, conformational change, or other modifications.

**4.** Cooperativity occurs when binding of substrate to one subunit stabilises an active conformation in other subunits, increasing overall enzyme activity.

**5.** High product concentration can inhibit enzymes in its own pathway and may activate another pathway, diverting shared substrates away from the overproduced product and toward needed products.

### D. Application / pathway-analysis questions

**1.** If Q and S are high, O -> P and O -> R are inhibited, so the branch from O is blocked. Because O accumulates, O inhibits L -> M. Therefore the L -> N branch is most likely to prevail.

**2.** High ATP and citrate inhibit phosphofructokinase, slowing glycolysis because the cell has enough energy and carbon intermediates. High AMP stimulates phosphofructokinase, increasing glycolysis because AMP signals low energy availability.

**3.** Measure reaction rate across increasing substrate concentrations with and without inhibitor. If high substrate restores the maximum rate, inhibition is likely competitive. If maximum rate remains reduced despite high substrate, inhibition is more likely noncompetitive.
