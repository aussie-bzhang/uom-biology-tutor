## Key Concepts to Remember

**WHY regulate:** Not all genes are expressed in all cells at all times. Cells control the *amount* of each gene product **temporally** (when), **spatially** (where/which cell or tissue), and in **response to environmental signals**. Misregulation causes abnormal phenotypes (e.g. homeotic mutants in Drosophila — legs where antennae should be).

**WHERE regulation happens (eukaryotes):** expression can be controlled at many points — chromatin remodelling, transcription, RNA processing, nuclear export (transport), mRNA stability, translation, post-translational modification, and protein degradation. **Transcriptional control is the most common and most economical point.**

**Epigenetics — DNA methylation:** DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) adds a methyl group to cytosine → 5-methylcytosine, usually at **CpG** sites. Methylation generally **silences** genes; it is heritable through cell division (maintenance methylation) yet reversible — so cells with the *same genome* (e.g. honeybee queen vs worker) can have different phenotypes.

**Positive vs negative control:** Positive control = an **activator** binds and switches transcription ON; negative control = a **repressor** binds and switches transcription OFF. The lac operon uses BOTH (combinatorial control): the LacI repressor (negative, responds to lactose) and CAP (positive, responds to glucose levels).

**Operon logic (lac):** lacZ (β-galactosidase), lacY (permease), lacA (transacetylase) are transcribed as **one polycistronic mRNA**. Lac genes are most useful when **lactose is present AND glucose is absent.**

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Why & Where; DNA Methylation

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Different genes have different roles and are needed at different times, places and amounts. Expressing everything maximally everywhere would waste energy/resources and could be harmful (e.g. producing a developmental protein in an adult tissue). Reasons (any two): (a) economy — make products only when/where required; (b) cell specialisation — different cell types need different proteins; (c) response to a changing environment; (d) correct timing of development.

2. Temporal (e.g. a gene needed only briefly during embryonic development), spatial / cell-type-specific (e.g. haemoglobin in red-blood-cell precursors, not in neurons), and environmental / signal-responsive (e.g. lactose-metabolism genes switched on only when lactose is available).

3. (Any four, in order) Chromatin remodelling → transcriptional control → RNA processing control → nuclear transport/export control → mRNA stability control → translational control → post-translational modification of protein activity → control of protein degradation.

4. DNMT transfers a methyl group onto cytosine, converting it to 5-methylcytosine (usually at CpG sites). The added methyl group does not change base-pairing but generally represses/silences transcription of the associated gene.

5. Prokaryotes: no nucleus, so transcription and translation are coupled (ribosomes translate the mRNA as it is still being made); little/no mRNA processing. Eukaryotes: transcription occurs in the nucleus and translation in the cytoplasm (spatially separated by the nuclear envelope); the pre-mRNA is processed (5′ cap, 3′ poly-A tail, intron removal) and exported before translation.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. Yes — almost all somatic cells carry the full genome; differences arise from which genes are expressed, not from which genes are present. 2. No — methylation generally decreases/silences transcription. 3. Yes — in prokaryotes transcription and translation are coupled. 4. No — they share the same genome; the difference is epigenetic (driven by royal jelly / DNA methylation). 5. Yes — transcriptional control occurs in the nucleus (as does chromatin remodelling, processing and export).

### Part D — Applied

1. (i) LESS methylation (DNMT is inhibited). (ii) The experimental (low-methylation) group is more likely to become QUEENS — royal jelly normally supplies DNMT inhibitors, so reducing methylation mimics the royal-jelly/queen pathway; controls (normal methylation) become workers. (iii) Both groups are genetically identical, so the caste difference cannot be due to DNA-sequence (genetic) differences; changing only the methylation level changes the outcome, which is the definition of an epigenetic effect.

(ii) e.g. DNA methylation of the promoter / repressive chromatin (heterochromatin) / absence of a required tissue-specific activator in neurons and skin. (iii) In situ hybridisation (or a promoter-reporter such as GFP) to localise expression within intact tissue.

## Set 2 — Transcriptional Regulation & the lac Operon

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Positive control: an activator protein binds upstream and switches transcription ON (no activator → little/no transcription). Negative control: a repressor protein binds upstream and switches transcription OFF (it blocks/destabilises RNA polymerase); when the repressor is absent/inactive, transcription proceeds.

2. An operon is a cluster of genes transcribed together from a single promoter into one mRNA, allowing co-regulation. The lac operon produces one polycistronic mRNA carrying lacZ (β-galactosidase), lacY (lactose permease) and lacA (transacetylase); this single mRNA is translated into three separate polypeptides.

3. The lacI gene is expressed constitutively to make the LacI repressor. In the absence of lactose, LacI binds the operator (upstream of/overlapping the promoter) and blocks RNA polymerase, so the Z, Y, A genes are not transcribed.

4. When lactose is present, allolactose (derived from lactose) binds LacI and changes its conformation so it can no longer bind the operator. LacI falls off, RNA polymerase can transcribe lacZYA, and the lactose-utilisation enzymes are made. This is 'induction' because the substrate (lactose) induces the enzymes that metabolise it; lactose is the inducer.

5. CAP (activator, positive control) binds and boosts transcription when glucose is LOW. LacI (repressor, negative control) blocks transcription unless lactose is present to inactivate it. Strong expression therefore requires BOTH: lactose present (repressor off) AND glucose absent (CAP active) — i.e. lactose +, glucose −.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. No — in negative control, binding of the repressor switches transcription OFF. 2. Yes — they are on a single polycistronic mRNA. 3. No — without lactose the repressor is bound and the operon is essentially off. 4. Yes — lactose (allolactose) is the inducer; it binds LacI and releases the DNA. 5. No — with glucose present, CAP is inactive, so expression is low even though the repressor is off.

### Part C — MCQ

1-b 2-b 3-c 4-a 5-b 6-a 7-b 8-b 9-b 10-b

### Part D — Applied

1. (1) Lac−/Glu+: Not expressed (repressor bound; CAP inactive). (2) Lac+/Glu+: Very low / basal (repressor off but CAP inactive because glucose is high). (3) Lac+/Glu−: Expressed strongly (repressor off AND CAP active). (4) Lac−/Glu−: Not expressed (repressor bound; no inducer). STRONGEST = condition 3 (lactose present, glucose absent): the repressor is released and CAP activates the promoter simultaneously.

2. (i) With a LacI that cannot bind DNA, the operon is transcribed even without lactose — it is 'constitutive' (always ON regardless of inducer). (ii) A LacI that binds DNA but not lactose stays locked on the operator; lactose cannot release it, so the operon CANNOT be induced (it is permanently OFF / 'super-repressed'). (iii) The first mutant (constitutive) wastes more energy when only glucose is present, because it keeps making lactose-metabolism enzymes that are not needed.

3. (i) If Y is an activator, deleting Y → transcription of X goes DOWN (loss of activation). (ii) If Y is a repressor, deleting Y → transcription of X goes UP (loss of repression). (iii) The direction of change after removing Y reveals the mode: a decrease indicates positive control (Y was needed to turn X on); an increase indicates negative control (Y was holding X off).

## Set 3 — Detecting & Measuring Gene Expression

### Part A — Short-answer

1. In situ hybridisation uses a labelled nucleic-acid probe complementary to a gene's mRNA; the probe is applied to fixed cells/tissue and binds wherever that mRNA is present. Unlike a present/absent assay, it shows the SPATIAL pattern — exactly which cells or regions express the gene (e.g. segment-specific expression in a fly embryo).

2. The coding sequence of an easily detected reporter (GFP → fluorescence; luciferase → light) is placed under the control of the promoter/regulatory region of interest. Reporter signal (fluorescence or luminescence) then reflects the activity of that promoter — when and where, and how strongly, it drives transcription.

3. Steps: culture the two cell types → isolate RNA from each → reverse-transcribe to cDNA and label with different fluorophores → hybridise onto the microarray of gene probes → scan. Per the lecture scheme: green = expressed in normal cells only, red = expressed in pathological cells only, yellow = expressed in both, dark = expressed in neither.

4. RNA-seq sequences the RNA (via cDNA) in a sample to identify and quantify transcripts at high resolution. Advantages over microarrays: it does not require pre-designed probes/known sequences, has a wider dynamic range, and can detect novel transcripts, splice variants and low-abundance RNAs.

5. Example: homeotic mutants in Drosophila (e.g. Antennapedia — legs growing where antennae should be, or an extra pair of wings). Such phenotypes arise because a regulatory gene is expressed in the wrong place/time, so the wrong developmental programme is switched on in a body segment — illustrating that correct spatial/temporal regulation of expression is essential, not just the gene's sequence.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. Yes — it reveals the spatial location of the mRNA. 2. Yes — GFP signal reports the activity of the driving promoter. 3. Yes — yellow = expressed in both samples. 4. No — RNA-seq does not require pre-known probe sequences (a key advantage over arrays). 5. Yes — reporter assays are commonly used to compare promoter strengths.

### Part C — MCQ

1-b 2-a 3-b 4-b 5-b 6-b 7-b 8-b 9-b 10-b

### Part D — Applied

1. (i) Promoter A is active in liver cells but inactive in skin cells — i.e. it drives liver-specific (cell-type-specific) expression. (ii) Deleting an upstream activator-binding site abolishes expression, so the promoter depends on a positively acting factor — evidence of POSITIVE control (an activator is required to turn it on). (iii) Controls: a 'promoterless GFP' construct (should give no signal) and/or a constitutive promoter-GFP (should glow in all cells), confirming the signal reflects promoter A's activity rather than the GFP gene itself.

2. (i) Both = yellow spots = 400 genes. (ii) Pathological-only = red spots = 300 genes. (iii) These 300 genes are candidate disease-associated (up-regulated/disease-specific) genes; they are clinically interesting as potential biomarkers for diagnosis or as drug targets, since they are switched on specifically in the diseased state.

3. (i) Fold-difference vegetative vs 37°C = 100 ÷ 5 = 20-fold. (ii) hgrA is most active during vegetative growth at 25°C (100 units, far higher than 8 or 5). (iii) Deleting hgrA, which drives hyphal growth, should abolish/reduce filamentous (hyphal) growth — the fungus would fail to grow as hyphae. Confirmation for the single gene: Northern blot or qRT-PCR (or in situ hybridisation) to verify the expression pattern independently.
