# Answer Key

## Set 1 Answers

**A1.** An amino acid has a central alpha carbon bonded to H, an amino group (-NH2, often -NH3+), a carboxyl group (-COOH, often -COO-), and a variable R group.

**A2.** mRNA is read in codons, each codon specifies an amino acid, and amino acids are joined into a chain from the N-terminus toward the C-terminus.

**A3.** Examples: keratin-structural; antibodies-defence; insulin-hormone/signalling; myosin-contractile; RUBISCO-enzyme; transport proteins-transport; gluten-storage.

**A4.** Primary = amino acid sequence, stabilised by peptide bonds. Secondary = alpha helices/beta sheets, stabilised by hydrogen bonding between non-adjacent peptide bonds. Tertiary = 3D folding of one polypeptide, stabilised by R-group interactions such as hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, disulfide links and hydrophobic/dispersion interactions.

**A5.** Denaturation is a change to secondary or tertiary structure. It reduces function because protein activity depends on the correct 3D shape and surface chemistry.

## Set 2 Answers

**B1.** The R group varies among the 20 amino acids and determines polarity, charge, hydrophobicity, and the possible interactions that drive folding and binding.

**B2.** A peptide bond forms between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another through condensation. Repetition forms a backbone with a free amino end (N-terminus) and a free carboxyl end (C-terminus).

**B3.** Peptide groups contain C=O and N-H groups that can hydrogen bond with non-adjacent peptide groups, producing regular alpha helix or beta sheet arrangements.

**B4.** Tertiary structure can be stabilised by hydrogen bonds, electrostatic/ionic interactions, hydrophobic/dispersion-force interactions, disulfide links between cysteines, and interactions with water.

**B5.** Binding can occur through complementary shape and surface chemistry, including ion-ion, ion-dipole, hydrogen bonding, dipole-dipole and dispersion-force interactions.
