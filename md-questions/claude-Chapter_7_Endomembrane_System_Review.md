# Answer Key – Set 1

### Part A – Short-answer

1. A phospholipid has a hydrophilic “head” (a phosphate group plus, e.g., choline, attached via glycerol) and two hydrophobic fatty acid “tails”. In water the hydrophilic heads face outward toward the aqueous environment while the hydrophobic tails turn away from water toward each other, so two layers form with the tails sandwiched in the middle – a bilayer.
2. The fluid mosaic model describes the membrane as a fluid phospholipid bilayer (the phospholipids move laterally all the time) studded with a “mosaic” of embedded proteins. The membrane must stay fluid so that proteins can move and function, vesicles can form and fuse, and transport can occur; a rigid membrane could not carry out these functions.
3. Saturated tails are straight and pack tightly together, making the membrane more viscous/rigid and less permeable. Unsaturated tails have C=C double bonds that introduce kinks, which prevent tight packing, making the membrane more fluid and more permeable.
4. Cholesterol has a rigid four fused-ring structure that is largely non-polar, plus a small hydroxyl (-OH) group. The non-polar rings form London dispersion forces (LDF) with adjacent fatty acid tails, and the -OH group interacts with the polar phosphate head of a phospholipid by an ion-dipole interaction.
5. At high (moderate-to-high) temperatures cholesterol decreases permeability by restraining phospholipid movement and enhancing close packing. At very low temperatures it interferes with the regular close packing of the phospholipids, preventing solidification and so increasing fluidity/permeability.

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. The polar heads face the aqueous interior and exterior.
2. No. Unsaturated tails have the C=C double bonds and kinks; saturated tails are straight and pack tightly.
3. Yes. Kinks reduce packing, leaving gaps and increasing permeability.
4. Yes. The fused-ring portion of cholesterol is largely non-polar.
5. No. Higher temperature generally increases permeability.

### Part D – Applied / Data-interpretation

1. (a) Permeability increases as temperature increases (a positive relationship) for all three membranes.

(b) At any given temperature, a higher proportion of unsaturated tails gives higher permeability. The C=C double bonds in unsaturated tails create kinks that stop the phospholipids packing tightly, leaving more space/gaps through which glycerol can pass.

(c) The fish would have a higher proportion of unsaturated tails. The kinks keep the membrane fluid even at low temperature, preventing it from becoming too rigid/solid and allowing it to keep functioning.

# Answer Key – Set 2

### Part A – Short-answer

1. Passive transport (diffusion) is the movement of a substance down its concentration gradient (from high to low concentration) without the cell using energy. Equilibrium is reached when the substance is evenly distributed, with equal movement in all directions (no net change). Osmosis is the diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane.
2. Small, neutral (non-polar) molecules – such as O2, CO2, N2 and small uncharged molecules like glycerol/ethanol/water – can diffuse directly through the bilayer. Large molecules (e.g. glucose) and ions cannot, because the hydrophobic core repels charged/large polar species.
3. Facilitated diffusion uses transport proteins to move hydrophilic substances down their gradient (still passive, no ATP). Channel proteins form a hydrophilic tunnel that specific ions/molecules pass through; carrier proteins bind the molecule and change shape to shuttle it across.
4. Primary active transport uses ATP directly to pump a substance against its gradient (e.g. the Na+–K+ pump). Secondary active transport uses the electrochemical gradient of another substance (e.g. Na+ or H+), which was set up by primary active transport, to move the target against its gradient (e.g. Na+–glucose symporter). It does not use ATP directly.
5. A uniporter transports one substance in one direction; a symporter transports two substances in the same direction; an antiporter transports two substances in opposite directions.

### Part B – Yes / No

1. No. Passive transport does not require ATP.
2. Yes. Active transport moves substances against the gradient.
3. No. Large polar molecules and ions cannot freely cross the hydrophobic core.
4. Yes. Facilitated diffusion is passive (no ATP).
5. Yes. That is the definition of secondary active transport.

# Answer Key – Set 3

### Part A – Short-answer

1. The endomembrane system is the system of membrane-bound compartments of the cell, including the nuclear envelope, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes/plant vacuoles and associated vesicles. It does NOT include the mitochondria, chloroplasts or microbodies (peroxisomes).
2. Rough ER has ribosomes and produces proteins (and glycoproteins, carbohydrates, some lipids); smooth ER has no ribosomes and produces lipids and carbohydrates. The ER is called the “heart” because it provides the surfaces for synthesising proteins, lipids and carbohydrates that are then distributed throughout the endomembrane system.
3. The Golgi apparatus is made of flattened membrane stacks (cisternae) called Golgi stacks; collectively these are the Golgi apparatus. It collects, packages, modifies and distributes molecules. Vesicles from the ER arrive at the cis face (receiving) and modified products leave at the trans face (shipping).
4. Lysosomes are the “recycle bins” of the animal cell, bounded by a single membrane. They have an acidic interior (internal pH ~4.5) and about 40 hydrolytic enzymes derived from the rough ER and Golgi. They break down material taken in by endocytosis/phagocytosis and recycle old or damaged organelles (autophagy); they can also digest pathogens.
5. Both are bounded by a single membrane, contain hydrolytic enzymes and act as degradative compartments; the plant vacuole is the plant equivalent of the lysosome (its membrane is the tonoplast). In addition, vacuoles store nutrients and pigments and maintain cell turgor pressure.

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. The nuclear envelope is part of the endomembrane system.
2. No. Mitochondria and chloroplasts are excluded.
3. Yes. Rough ER bears ribosomes and makes proteins.
4. No. Vesicles arrive at the cis face and leave at the trans face.
5. Yes. Exocytosis = out; endocytosis = in.

### Part C – Multiple Choice

1.(B) 2.(C) 3.(B) 4.(B) 5.(B) 6.(B) 7.(C) 8.(C) 9.(A) 10.(B)

### Part D – Applied / Data-interpretation

1. (a) Phagocytosis (endocytosis) at the plasma membrane → the bacterium is enclosed in a food vacuole (endosome) → the vacuole fuses with a lysosome → hydrolytic enzymes digest the bacterium → digestion products are released/used by the cell.

(b) Protein synthesised on ribosomes of the rough ER → packaged into a transport vesicle → carried to the Golgi apparatus (enters cis face, modified/packaged, leaves trans face) → secretory vesicle → fuses with the plasma membrane → released by exocytosis.

(c) Mitochondria (like chloroplasts and microbodies) are excluded by definition: they are not part of the vesicle-trafficking synthesis/packaging/secretion network and are thought to have a separate, endosymbiotic origin.
