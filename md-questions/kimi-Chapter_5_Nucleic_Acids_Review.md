# Question 5: Viral Genome Overlapping ORFs (15 marks)

Frame +1 (ORF S): ATG GAG ACA TCA CTC ATG GCA TGG

**Question 1: Transcription and Translation Analysis**

**a) Template strand identification**The template (antisense) strand is the lower strand: 3'-TAC CGA ATG CCT TTC GAA GCT ATT-5'. It serves as the template because RNA polymerase reads this strand in the 3' to 5' direction, synthesizing mRNA in the 5' to 3' direction. The template strand is complementary to the mRNA (with U replacing T). (2 marks)

**b) mRNA sequence**5'-AUG GCU UAC GGA AAG CUU CGA UAA-3' (3 marks)
The mRNA is complementary to the template strand, with uracil (U) replacing thymine (T).

**c) Amino acid sequence**Met-Ala-Tyr-Gly-Lys-Leu-Arg-Stop
Single-letter code: MAYGKLR\* (or just MAYGKLR)
Start codon: AUG (Met) at position 1
Stop codon: UAA at position 8 (4 marks)

**d) Peptide length**The translated peptide contains 7 amino acids (Met through Arg). If the initiating Met is removed post-translationally, the mature peptide would be 6 amino acids: Ala-Tyr-Gly-Lys-Leu-Arg. (2 marks)

**e) Mutation analysis**Original codon at positions 10-12: GGA (Gly)
Mutated codon: GAA (Glu)
This is a MISSENSE mutation. The amino acid changes from Glycine (non-polar) to Glutamic acid (acidic, negatively charged). This could alter protein structure and function, particularly if the glycine was important for flexibility or turn formation. (4 marks)

**b) Valid ORFs**+1: Contains AUG start at position 1 and UAA stop at position 4. Valid ORF: 3 codons (Met-Thr-Gly).
+2: Contains UGA stop early; UGA at position 5 and AUG at position 6 - not a valid ORF (stop before start in same frame).

Amino acids: Met-Thr-Gly (MTG)
Note: This is a short ORF; real bacterial genes would be much longer. (3 marks)

**d) Importance of six-frame translation**Scanning all six reading frames is essential because: (1) we don't know which strand is the coding strand in an unknown sequence, (2) we don't know which of the three possible frames contains the correct ORF, (3) overlapping genes may exist on both strands, and (4) it ensures no coding sequences are missed during gene prediction. Bioinformatics tools like BLAST and gene finders (e.g., Glimmer, GeneMark) use six-frame translation as a fundamental step. (2 marks)

**Question 3: Experimental Design - DNA vs. RNA Detection**

**a) Enzymatic differentiation**Add RNase A to aliquots of both samples and incubate at 37 C for 30 minutes. Then run both treated and untreated samples on an agarose gel.

Expected results:
- The RNA sample will show degradation (smaller bands or no bands) after RNase treatment.
- The DNA sample will remain unchanged (same band intensity).

Controls needed:
- Positive control: Known DNA + DNase (should degrade)
- Positive control: Known RNA + RNase (should degrade)
- Negative control: Known DNA + RNase (should NOT degrade)
- Buffer-only control (5 marks)

**b) Alkaline hydrolysis mechanism**RNA is susceptible to alkaline hydrolysis because the 2'-OH group on ribose acts as a nucleophile. Under alkaline conditions (OH-), the 2'-OH attacks the adjacent phosphorus in the phosphodiester bond, forming a 2',3'-cyclic phosphate intermediate. This cleaves the RNA backbone. DNA lacks the 2'-OH group (has 2'-H instead), so this nucleophilic attack cannot occur, making DNA stable under alkaline conditions. (4 marks)

**c) UV spectrophotometry**Both DNA and RNA absorb at 260 nm. However, upon thermal denaturation, double-stranded DNA shows a greater hyperchromic effect (increase in A260 absorbance) compared to single-stranded RNA, which is already partially unstacked. Additionally, the A260/A280 ratio differs: pure DNA typically has a ratio of ~1.8, while pure RNA has ~2.0. This difference arises from the different sugar compositions affecting secondary structure. (3 marks)

**d) Diphenylamine assay**The diphenylamine (DPA) assay detects deoxyribose specifically. When heated in acidic conditions, deoxyribose in DNA converts to omega-hydroxylevulinyl aldehyde, which reacts with diphenylamine to produce a blue-colored compound (absorbing at 595 nm). RNA contains ribose, not deoxyribose, so it does not produce this reaction. The color intensity is proportional to DNA concentration. (3 marks)

**Question 4: tRNA and the Genetic Code**

**a) Modified bases in tRNA**Three common modified bases:
1. Pseudouridine - provides structural stability through additional hydrogen bonding.
2. Dihydrouridine (D) - introduces conformational flexibility in the D-loop.
3. Inosine (I) - important for wobble base pairing at the anticodon position; can pair with U, C, or A.

Functional significance: These modifications fine-tune tRNA structure, improve translational fidelity, enable wobble pairing, and protect against nuclease degradation. (3 marks)

**b) mRNA codon recognition**Anticodon: 3'-CUA-5'
mRNA codon (read 5' to 3'): 5'-UAG-3'
Wait - this is a stop codon. Let me reconsider: the anticodon is 3'-CUA-5', which pairs with 5'-GAU-3' (Aspartic acid, Asp, D).

Correct answer: The mRNA codon is 5'-GAU-3', which codes for Aspartic acid (Asp, D). (2 marks)

**c) Wobble base pairing**Wobble pairing occurs at the third position (3' end) of the mRNA codon and the first position (5' end) of the tRNA anticodon. The anticodon first base (5' position) can form non-Watson-Crick pairs with the codon third base.

Rules:
- Inosine (I) in anticodon can pair with U, C, or A in codon
- G can pair with U or C
- U can pair with A or G

This allows one tRNA to recognize multiple codons for the same amino acid, reducing the total number of tRNA genes needed. (3 marks)

**d) Experimental verification**(i) Principle: Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases attach specific amino acids to their cognate tRNAs in an ATP-dependent reaction. Each synthetase recognizes both the tRNA and the correct amino acid.

(ii) Isolation: Use biotinylated DNA probes complementary to the tRNA anticodon loop to capture this specific tRNA from a total tRNA extract via streptavidin-coated magnetic beads.

(iii) Assay: After charging the isolated tRNA with aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase and labeled amino acids (e.g., 14C-labeled amino acid mixture), perform acid precipitation. Only charged tRNA retains the radioactive label. Use thin-layer chromatography (TLC) or HPLC to identify which specific amino acid is attached by comparing with standards.
(7 marks)

**Question 5: Viral Genome Overlapping ORFs**

**a) Coding capacity calculation**Single ORF: 180 bp / 3 = 60 codons = 60 amino acids (minus 1 stop codon = 59 aa)

Three overlapping ORFs:
- ORF S: ~60 codons capacity
- ORF P: shifted by +1, also ~59 aa capacity
- ORF C: shifted by +2, also ~59 aa capacity

Combined coding capacity: approximately 3 x 59 = 177 amino acids worth of coding information from the same 180 bp segment.

Note: Actual coding depends on start/stop positions; the theoretical maximum shows nearly triple the information density. (4 marks)

**b) Biological advantage**For viruses: Small genomes need to maximize coding capacity within limited nucleotide space. Overlapping genes allow viruses to encode more proteins without increasing genome size, which is constrained by capsid volume and replication efficiency.

Less common in eukaryotes because: (1) larger genomes reduce selective pressure for genetic economy, (2) mutations in shared regions would affect multiple proteins simultaneously, (3) separate promoters and regulatory elements provide finer transcriptional control, and (4) eukaryotic genes typically contain introns, making frame organization more complex. (3 marks)

**c) Frameshift mutation effects**A single nucleotide insertion in an overlapping region shifts the reading frame for ALL overlapping ORFs simultaneously. This is devastating because:
1. It creates completely different downstream amino acid sequences in all affected proteins.
2. It likely introduces premature stop codons in multiple frames.
3. Unlike in non-overlapping regions where only one protein is affected, here multiple essential viral proteins (e.g., surface antigen from ORF S AND polymerase from ORF P) are compromised.
4. This demonstrates why overlapping genes are evolutionarily advantageous for compact genomes but create mutational vulnerability. (4 marks)

**d) Experimental approach**1. Extract total RNA from HBV-infected liver cells using TRIzol reagent.
2. Perform reverse transcription (RT) with random hexamers to generate cDNA.
3. Design ORF-specific PCR primers for S, P, C, and X regions.
4. Perform semi-quantitative RT-PCR to confirm transcription of all four ORFs.
5. Clone each ORF into expression vectors and perform in vitro translation to verify protein products.
6. Confirm translation using Western blot with antibodies against each viral protein (HBsAg for S, DNA polymerase for P, HBcAg for C, HBx for X).
7. Optionally, use ribosome profiling (Ribo-Seq) to confirm active translation of all ORFs in vivo. (4 marks)
