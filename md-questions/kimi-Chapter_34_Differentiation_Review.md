## Section A: Conceptual Review Questions — Suggested Answers

**Q1: Cell Potency**

Cell potency refers to the developmental potential of a cell — the range of different cell types it can give rise to. The zygote is totipotent, capable of generating all cell types including extraembryonic tissues. During embryogenesis, cells undergo determination, where they become progressively restricted in their fate. This restriction involves changes in gene expression driven by instructive cues such as cytoplasmic determinants and cell signalling molecules. As development proceeds, pluripotent cells become multipotent, then unipotent, ultimately giving rise to terminally differentiated specialised cells.

**Q2: Morphogenesis in Animals vs. Plants**

Animal morphogenesis involves: (1) cell divisions (oriented but also random), (2) cell shape changes, (3) cell migration — active movement of cells to new positions, and (4) cell adhesion/de-adhesion — forming and reorganising tissues. Plants lack cell migration and tissue-level adhesion changes because plant cells are encased in rigid cell walls and cemented together by pectin-rich middle lamella. Instead, plant morphogenesis relies on: (1) oriented cell divisions determining growth direction, (2) cell expansion driven by turgor pressure against loosened cell walls, and (3) continuous organ formation from meristems.

**Q3: House-keeping vs. Cell-specific Genes**

House-keeping genes are expressed in all cell types and encode proteins essential for basic cellular functions. Examples: ACTB (β-actin, cytoskeletal structure) and TUBB (β-tubulin, microtubule formation). Cell-specific genes are expressed only in particular differentiated cell types and confer specialised functions. Examples: LCT (lactase, carbohydrate digestion in enterocytes) and MYH11 (smooth muscle myosin, contraction in muscle cells). Differential expression of cell-specific genes underlies the functional diversity of tissues while house-keeping genes maintain cellular viability.

**Q4: Plant Embryogenesis Steps**

Step 1 — Zygote to two-cell stage: Asymmetric division establishes the apical-basal axis. The apical daughter cell gives rise to the embryo proper; the basal cell forms the suspensor. Step 2 — Octant stage: Oriented cell divisions produce the spherical embryo and linear suspensor. Step 3 — Heart stage: Bilateral symmetry is established through cotyledon primordia outgrowth; radial tissue pattern (protoderm, ground meristem, procambium) and shoot/root meristems form. Step 4 — Torpedo to mature seed: Cotyledons and main axis elongate; tissues differentiate; the embryo prepares for dormancy as a desiccation-tolerant seed.

**Q5: Meristems in Post-embryonic Development**

Apical meristems (shoot and root apical meristems) drive primary growth — longitudinal increases in plant height and root depth. They produce three primary meristems: protoderm (dermal tissue), ground meristem (ground tissue/cortex), and procambium (vascular tissue). Lateral meristems (vascular cambium and cork cambium) drive secondary growth — radial increase in girth/thickness. The vascular cambium produces secondary xylem (inward) and secondary phloem (outward). The cork cambium produces protective periderm (bark). Apical meristems enable indeterminate growth — plants can continuously produce new organs throughout their lifespan.

**Q6: Master Regulator Genes**

Master regulator genes encode transcription factors that control the expression of numerous downstream genes, thereby orchestrating entire developmental programs. In Drosophila, the bithorax complex (Ubx) is a master regulator that specifies the third thoracic segment identity. Loss-of-function mutations cause the haltere to transform into a second pair of wings (bithorax phenotype). In Arabidopsis, AGAMOUS (AG) is a master regulator of stamen and carpel identity. AG mutants show homeotic transformations where stamens become petals and carpels become sepoid organs (agamous phenotype), demonstrating that a single master regulator can redirect an entire organogenesis program.

**Q7: Differential Gene Expression**

All differentiated cells in a multicellular organism contain the same nuclear DNA because DNA is faithfully replicated at every cell division (S phase). Cell type diversity arises through differential gene expression — the selective activation and repression of specific subsets of genes. This regulation occurs at multiple levels: transcription (transcription factor binding, chromatin modification), RNA processing (alternative splicing), translation (miRNA regulation), and protein modification. Each cell type expresses a unique combination of cell-specific genes while maintaining house-keeping gene expression, resulting in distinct proteomes and cellular functions despite identical genomes.

**Q8: Determinate vs. Indeterminate Growth**

Animals exhibit determinate growth: embryogenesis produces all organ and tissue types, and the adult body form is largely predetermined. Growth post-embryonically is mainly an increase in size, not new organ formation. Plants exhibit indeterminate growth: embryogenesis establishes the basic body plan but only produces a limited set of organs. Post-embryonic development continues throughout the plant's life through meristem activity, allowing continuous production of new organs (leaves, stems, roots). This indeterminate growth is an adaptation to their sedentary lifestyle, enabling plants to respond to environmental cues by generating new structures.

**Q9: Plant Cell Expansion Mechanism**

Plant cell expansion is driven by turgor pressure generated when the vacuole accumulates solutes (ions, sugars) via active transport, creating a water potential gradient. Water enters the cell by osmosis through aquaporins, expanding the vacuole and generating hydrostatic pressure (turgor) against the cell wall. For expansion to occur, the cell wall must first be loosened by enzymes: expansins disrupt hydrogen bonds between cellulose and hemicellulose, XTH remodels xyloglucan cross-links, and cellulases degrade specific polysaccharides. This wall loosening reduces wall resistance, allowing turgor pressure to drive irreversible cell elongation. Acid growth theory proposes that H⁺-ATPase pumps protons into the wall space, activating expansins at acidic pH.

**Q10: Plane of Cell Division**

The orientation of the cell division plane (phragmoplast position) determines the direction of tissue growth. Periclinal divisions (parallel to the surface) produce width/radial growth, as seen in lateral root outgrowth and secondary growth from vascular cambium. Anticlinal divisions (perpendicular to the surface) produce longitudinal/lengthwise growth, as in root and shoot elongation from apical meristems. Oblique divisions contribute to branching patterns. The preprophase band (PPB) of microtubules predicts the future division plane, ensuring oriented cell divisions that shape organ morphology.

ΔCt = Ct(target) − Ct(reference) = Ct(LCT) − Ct(ACTB) = 18.5 − 15.2 = 3.3

**b) ΔΔCt and fold-change calculations:**

For LCT (calibrator = zygote):

Zygote: ΔCt = 31.2−15.5 = 15.7; ΔΔCt = 15.7−15.7 = 0; Fold-change = 2^(−0) = 1.0

Stem cell: ΔCt = 24.8−15.8 = 9.0; ΔΔCt = 9.0−15.7 = −6.7; Fold-change = 2^(+6.7) = 103.2

For MYH11 (calibrator = zygote):

**c) Biological interpretation:**

LCT (lactase) is the best molecular marker for enterocyte differentiation. Its expression increases >5000-fold from zygote to differentiated enterocyte, reflecting the enterocytes' specialised role in lactose digestion. LCT is not expressed in muscle (MYH11-positive) cells, demonstrating cell-type-specific gene expression. MYH11 remains silent in enterocytes, confirming reciprocal exclusion of differentiation programs.

**d) House-keeping gene normalisation:**

Normalisation against ACTB corrects for: (1) variations in RNA extraction efficiency, (2) reverse transcription efficiency differences, and (3) sample loading inconsistencies. Raw Ct values are inversely proportional to starting material — without normalisation, technical variations could be mistaken for biological differences. If ACTB varied 2-fold between stages, ΔCt would shift by 1 (since 2-fold = 2^1), causing systematic error in fold-change calculations. This is why geNorm analysis should verify reference gene stability across conditions.

### Problem 2: Meristem Cell Division — Solution

**a) Differentiated cells per day:**

Cell cycle = 18 hours = 0.75 days. Divisions per cell per day = 1 / 0.75 = 1.33.

With 150 dividing cells, each producing 1 differentiated cell per division:

Differentiated cells/day = 150 × 1.33 = 200 cells/day.

**b) Cells in elongation zone:**

Total elongation distance = 2.5 mm = 2500 μm.

A cell entering elongation at 5 μm exits at 75 μm (15×).

Average cell length during transit ≈ 40 μm.

Number of cells = 2500 / 40 ≈ 62 cells (accept 50-75 range depending on assumptions).

Alternative: Cell production rate = 200/day = 8.33/hour. Transit time through elongation zone ≈ 24 hours, so cells = 8.33 × 24 ≈ 200 cells.

**c) Secondary xylem thickness:**

Total radial thickness = 0.3 mm/year × 5 years = 1.5 mm = 1500 μm.

Number of vessel elements (radial) = 1500 μm / 500 μm = 3 vessel elements.

**d) Primary vs. secondary growth division planes:**

Apical meristem cells divide primarily anticlinally (perpendicular to the surface), pushing cells upward/downward and increasing plant height (primary growth). Lateral meristem (vascular cambium) cells divide periclinally (parallel to the surface), adding concentric rings of secondary xylem inward and secondary phloem outward, thereby increasing stem/trunk diameter (secondary growth). The cambium forms a cylindrical sheet, so periclinal divisions expand the cylinder radius.

### Problem 3: Master Regulator Gene Identification — Solution

**a) Forward genetics design:**

(i) Mutagenesis: Treat wild-type Arabidopsis seeds with EMS (ethyl methanesulfonate), which causes G:C→A:T transitions, or use T-DNA insertion mutagenesis via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Aim for ~10,000 M1 plants.

(ii) Screening: Grow M2 plants and screen for floral homeotic phenotypes. Look for plants with petals replacing stamens in whorl 3 (agamous-like phenotype). Backcross mutants to wild-type to remove background mutations.

(iii) Gene mapping: Use map-based cloning. Cross mutant (Col-0 background) to Ler ecotype. Analyse F2 recombinants using molecular markers (SSLPs, SNPs) flanking the mutation. Fine-map to a ~50 kb interval, then sequence candidate genes to identify the EMS mutation or T-DNA insertion site.

**b) Reverse genetics confirmation:**

Design sgRNAs targeting the first 2 exons of AGL using CRISPR-P or similar tools. Clone sgRNA into a plant binary vector with Cas9. Transform wild-type Arabidopsis via floral dip. Select T1 transformants on antibiotic/hygromycin. Genotype T2 plants by PCR across target site followed by sequencing (T7E1 assay or Sanger sequencing) to confirm indels. Controls: (1) empty vector control, (2) sgRNA with mismatches (scrambled), (3) complementation by expressing wild-type AGL cDNA under its native promoter in the mutant background.

**c) ChIP-qPCR enrichment analysis:**

Fold enrichment = 2^(Ct(IgG) − Ct(AGL-IP))

GENE1: 2^(28.5 − 22.3) = 2^6.2 = 73.5-fold enrichment → Strong direct target

GENE2: 2^(29.0 − 27.8) = 2^1.2 = 2.3-fold enrichment → Weak/non-specific binding

GENE3: 2^(28.8 − 24.1) = 2^4.7 = 26.0-fold enrichment → Moderate direct target

GENE1 is the strongest direct target (>70-fold enrichment), followed by GENE3. GENE2 shows borderline enrichment near background levels and is likely an indirect target or non-specific.

**d) Master regulator mechanism:**

A master regulator like AGL encodes a MADS-box transcription factor that binds to CArG-box motifs (CC[A/T]6GG) in target gene promoters. It activates a regulatory cascade: AGL directly activates tier-1 target genes (transcription factors, signalling molecules), which in turn activate tier-2 structural genes (enzymes, cytoskeletal proteins). A single transcription factor can bind hundreds of promoters, and combinatorial interactions with cofactors (e.g., SEPALLATA proteins) create specificity. Feed-forward loops and feedback regulation ensure robust commitment to the developmental program, making the master regulator a switch-like controller of cell identity.

### Problem 4: Cell Wall Biochemistry — Solution

**a) Percentage increase in wall extensibility:**

IAA: (1.85 − 0.42) / 0.42 × 100% = 340% increase

FC: (2.10 − 0.42) / 0.42 × 100% = 400% increase

Cycloheximide: (0.38 − 0.42) / 0.42 × 100% = −9.5% decrease

IAA + CHX: (0.45 − 0.42) / 0.42 × 100% = 7.1% increase

**b) Turgor pressure calculation:**

Π = iCRT = 1 × 0.3 mol/L × 0.0831 L·bar/(mol·K) × 298 K

Π = 0.3 × 0.0831 × 298 = 7.43 bar (or ~0.74 MPa)

This turgor pressure provides the driving force for cell expansion once the wall is loosened.

**c) Volume and surface area calculations:**

(i) Volume increase:

Initial volume = 10 × 10 × 10 = 1000 μm³

Final volume = 10 × 10 × 75 = 7500 μm³

Fold increase = 7500 / 1000 = 7.5-fold

(ii) New cell wall surface area:

Initial surface area = 6 × (10×10) = 600 μm²

Final surface area = 2×(10×10) + 4×(10×75) = 200 + 3000 = 3200 μm²

New wall area synthesised = 3200 − 600 = 2600 μm² (or 4.3× increase)

This requires extensive cellulose, hemicellulose, and pectin biosynthesis via plasma membrane-localised cellulose synthase complexes.

**d) Fusicoccin and the acid growth mechanism:**

Fusicoccin (FC) is a fungal toxin that permanently activates the plasma membrane H⁺-ATPase proton pump. Activated H⁺-ATPase uses ATP hydrolysis (1 ATP → 1 H⁺ exported) to pump protons into the apoplast/cell wall space. This acidifies the wall to pH ~4.5–5.0, which: (1) activates expansins (optimal pH 4.0–5.0) that disrupt cellulose-hemiccellulose hydrogen bonds, (2) activates XTH and other cell wall-modifying enzymes, and (3) increases wall extensibility. With reduced wall resistance, turgor pressure (7.43 bar) drives water influx and cell elongation. Cycloheximide (a protein synthesis inhibitor) blocks the production of new cell wall enzymes and expansins needed for sustained elongation, which is why IAA-induced growth is abolished when protein synthesis is inhibited.

### Problem 5: Western Blot Quantification — Solution

**a) Normalised band intensities (RuBisCO/Tubulin and AGP4/Tubulin):**

Cotyledon: RuBisCO/Tub = 18500/4200 = 4.40; AGP4/Tub = 1200/4200 = 0.29

True leaf: RuBisCO/Tub = 22800/4150 = 5.49; AGP4/Tub = 3800/4150 = 0.92

Root: RuBisCO/Tub = 3200/4100 = 0.78; AGP4/Tub = 15200/4100 = 3.71

Stem: RuBisCO/Tub = 5600/4180 = 1.34; AGP4/Tub = 18900/4180 = 4.52

**b) Tissue differentiation interpretation:**

Strongest photosynthetic differentiation: True leaf (RuBisCO/Tub = 5.49). This is expected because true leaves contain mesophyll cells (palisade and spongy) packed with chloroplasts for photosynthesis. The high RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) reflects abundant Calvin cycle activity. Cotyledons also show high RuBisCO (4.40) but slightly lower, consistent with their role in storage and early photosynthesis.

Strongest vascular differentiation: Stem (AGP4/Tub = 4.52). AGP4 (arabinogalactan protein 4) is a marker for vascular tissue differentiation. Stems contain abundant vascular bundles (xylem and phloem) for long-distance transport. Roots also show high AGP4 (3.71) due to central vascular cylinder development. The reciprocal pattern — high RuBisCO in leaves, high AGP4 in stems/roots — reflects tissue-specific differentiation programs.

**c) Loading control significance:**

Equal Tubulin across lanes confirms equal protein loading (±5% variation), ensuring that differences in RuBisCO and AGP4 band intensities reflect genuine biological variation in marker expression rather than technical loading errors. If Tubulin varied 2-fold (e.g., 4000 vs 8000 AU), normalisation would be required: raw marker intensities would need division by the corresponding Tubulin value. However, large variation in the house-keeping protein itself would indicate that experimental conditions altered basic cellular machinery, violating a key assumption of the house-keeping normalisation strategy and potentially requiring multiple reference proteins or total protein normalisation (Ponceau S staining) instead.

**d) Immunolocalisation experiment design:**

Step 1: Fix leaf tissue in 4% paraformaldehyde to preserve protein localization and tissue architecture.

Step 2: Embed in paraffin or OCT, then section (8-10 μm) using a microtome/cryostat.

Step 3: Deparaffinise and perform antigen retrieval if needed. Block with 5% BSA.

Step 4: Incubate with primary antibodies: anti-RuBisCO (rabbit) and anti-AGP4 (mouse) overnight at 4°C.

Step 5: Wash and incubate with secondary antibodies: Alexa Fluor 488-conjugated anti-rabbit (green) and Alexa Fluor 594-conjugated anti-mouse (red).

Step 6: Counterstain with DAPI (blue, nuclei) and mount with antifade.

Step 7: Image using confocal microscopy.

Expected results: RuBisCO (green) would localise to chloroplasts within mesophyll cells (palisade and spongy layers), showing strong fluorescence in photosynthetic tissue. AGP4 (red) would localise to xylem vessel elements and phloem sieve tubes within vascular bundles, showing fluorescence in vein tissue. Differential fluorescence patterns would confirm cell-type-specific expression of these differentiation markers.
