## Key concepts to remember before you start

Homeostasis = the maintenance of a steady (stable) internal state by means of physiological or behavioural feedback responses. In a multicellular organism every cell depends on its immediate environment, the EXTRACELLULAR FLUID; many factors must be kept within a physiological range: temperature, osmotic potential, pH, ion and nutrient concentrations, and O₂/CO₂ levels.

The stimulus-response (control) model has three parts: a RECEPTOR detects a change away from the desired equilibrium (set point) and signals a CONTROL CENTRE; the control centre processes the information and communicates to an EFFECTOR; the effector produces a RESPONSE that opposes the original change. The signalling between these parts is cell-cell communication (chemical and/or electrical).

NEGATIVE feedback = the response OPPOSES (reverses) the stimulus, returning the variable toward the set point — this maintains homeostasis. POSITIVE feedback = the response AMPLIFIES (increases) the original change, driving the system further from the starting point — this does NOT maintain homeostasis (it is used to drive a process to completion).

Blood glucose example (must be kept ~4–8 mmol/L): too LOW = hypoglycaemia (seizures, loss of consciousness); too HIGH = hyperglycaemia (heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, vision impairment; ketoacidosis, osmolar imbalance, AGEs). Pancreatic β-islet cells release INSULIN when glucose is HIGH → target cells (e.g. liver, muscle) take up glucose; in the liver insulin (1) increases glucose transporters in the membrane and (2) activates glycogen synthase → glucose stored as glycogen → BGL goes DOWN. Pancreatic α-islet cells release GLUCAGON when glucose is LOW → in the liver glucagon (G-protein-coupled receptor) (1) activates glycogen phosphorylase and (2) inactivates glycogen synthase → glycogen broken down to glucose → BGL goes UP. Insulin and glucagon are ANTAGONISTIC; both loops are negative feedback.

Positive feedback example — breastfeeding (milk let-down): suckling stimulates nipple nerves → oxytocin released → smooth muscle in milk ducts contracts → milk let down → baby feeds more strongly → MORE stimulation (amplifies the change). Complex hormonal loops (e.g. hypothalamus–pituitary–gonad axis; insect moulting/ecdysone) coordinate timing across multiple organs with checks and balances.

Plants also use hormonal (chemical) signalling: slower than electrical but longer-lasting. Six classes of plant hormone (auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, ethylene, brassinosteroids, abscisic acid). Hormones can overlap (auxin, brassinosteroids, gibberellins all promote stem growth), act synergistically, or antagonistically (abscisic acid promotes seed dormancy; gibberellins promote germination).

Plant water homeostasis (focus example): water leaves the leaf as vapour through STOMATA, each formed by two GUARD CELLS. When water is sufficient → guard cells are TURGID → stomata OPEN → high transpiration. Under DRY/water-stress conditions → the hormone ABSCISIC ACID (ABA) is produced in roots and leaves → ABA detected by guard-cell receptors (outside and inside the cell) → triggers efflux of anions then K⁺ out of the guard cells → solute (and hence water potential) inside rises (less negative) → water leaves the guard cells by osmosis (via aquaporins) → guard cells become FLACCID → stomata CLOSE → transpiration reduced. Prolonged ABA also switches on drought-response genes (more root growth, less shoot growth, protective proteins). ABA falls again when water is restored = a homeostatic loop.

## Set 1 — Homeostasis & the Stimulus-Response Model

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Homeostasis = the maintenance of a steady (stable) internal state by means of physiological or behavioural feedback responses. Cells depend on the extracellular fluid as their immediate environment, so its conditions must stay within a physiological range or cells malfunction/die. Examples (any three): temperature, osmotic potential, pH, ion/nutrient concentration, O₂/CO₂ levels, blood glucose.

**2.** Receptor = detects a change away from the set point and signals the control centre. Control centre = receives and processes the information and communicates to the effector. Effector = produces a response that opposes the effect of the stimulus, returning the variable toward the set point.

**3.** The set point is the desired equilibrium value the system tries to maintain. In negative feedback the effector’s response OPPOSES the stimulus — if the variable rises above set point the response lowers it, and vice versa — so the variable is pushed back toward the set point.

**4.** Cell-cell communication forms the SIGNALLING links of the model: (i) the receptor SIGNALS to the control centre, and (ii) the control centre COMMUNICATES to the effector. These use chemical and/or electrical signalling (from L32).

**5.** Glucose is the preferred ready energy source and must be available but not in excess. Hypoglycaemia (too low): affects the nervous system — seizures, loss of consciousness. Hyperglycaemia (too high): damages cells — increased risk of heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, vision impairment (also ketoacidosis, osmolar imbalance, AGEs).

**4.** Yes — temperature is a regulated variable.

**5.** Yes — both are listed consequences of hyperglycaemia.

## Set 2 — Negative & Positive Feedback: Blood Glucose & Other Loops

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Negative feedback: the response OPPOSES the original change, returning the variable toward the set point — maintains homeostasis (e.g. blood-glucose control, temperature regulation). Positive feedback: the response AMPLIFIES the original change, driving the system further from the start — does NOT maintain homeostasis; used to drive a process to completion (e.g. milk let-down/oxytocin, blood clotting, childbirth).

**2.** Stimulus = blood glucose rises (e.g. after a meal). Gland/cells = pancreas, β-islet cells. Hormone = insulin. Target organ = liver (and muscle/other target cells). Corrective effect = target cells increase glucose uptake and the liver stores glucose as glycogen → blood glucose drops back to normal.

**3.** Liver cells express the insulin receptor. Insulin binding activates the receptor, which has two effects: (1) increases the number of glucose transporters in the membrane (more glucose enters the cell); (2) activates glycogen synthase, converting glucose into glycogen for storage. Net effect: blood glucose goes DOWN.

**4.** Liver cells express the glucagon G-protein-coupled receptor. Glucagon binding has two effects: (1) activates glycogen phosphorylase (breaks glycogen down into glucose); (2) inactivates glycogen synthase (stops glucose being stored). Net effect: glucose is released into the blood → blood glucose goes UP.

**5.** Breastfeeding: suckling stimulates nipple nerves (stimulus) → oxytocin released (chemical message) → smooth muscle in the milk ducts contracts (effector) → milk is let down and the baby feeds more strongly → this INCREASES the original stimulation (more suckling). It is positive feedback because the response amplifies the stimulus; this is appropriate because the goal is to drive milk ejection strongly to completion, not to hold a variable steady.

**1.** Yes — negative feedback opposes the change.

**2.** No — positive feedback amplifies change; it does NOT maintain homeostasis.

**3.** Yes — β-islet cells release insulin when glucose is high.

**4.** Yes — glucagon drives glycogen breakdown to glucose.

**5.** Yes — they have opposite effects on blood glucose.

## Set 3 — Plant Hormones & Water Homeostasis (Abscisic Acid)

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Six classes: auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, ethylene, brassinosteroids, abscisic acid. General functions (any three): growth & development, seed dormancy, defence against herbivores, stress responses. Overlap example: auxin, brassinosteroids and gibberellins all promote stem growth. Antagonistic example: abscisic acid promotes seed dormancy while gibberellins promote seed germination.

**2.** Stomata (each formed by two guard cells) are pores through which water vapour leaves the leaf. (a) Water-sufficient: guard cells are TURGID → stomata OPEN → high transpiration. (b) Water-stressed: guard cells become FLACCID → stomata CLOSE → transpiration reduced.

**3.** ABA is the drought/water-stress hormone, produced in the roots and leaves when the plant loses water (cells lose turgor). It is detected by receptors on the guard cells (both outside and inside the cell) and triggers a signalling pathway that closes the stomata; prolonged ABA also switches on drought-response genes.

**4.** ABA binds guard-cell receptors → triggers efflux of anions out of the guard cells (depolarisation) → then efflux of K⁺ out of the guard cells (facilitated diffusion through channels) → solute concentration in the guard cells falls so their water potential rises (becomes less negative) → water leaves the guard cells by osmosis (via aquaporins) → guard cells lose turgor (become flaccid) → stomata close.

**5.** Hormonal (chemical) signalling is generally SLOWER than electrical signalling but LONGER-LASTING. Animal hormones travel in the blood and act either on EXTRACELLULAR (cell-surface) receptors if hydrophilic/water-soluble (cannot cross the membrane) or on receptors INSIDE the cell if lipophilic/lipid-soluble (can cross the membrane).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — six classes.

**2.** Yes — turgid guard cells, open stomata.

**3.** Yes — ABA is produced under water stress.

**4.** No — K⁺ moves OUT of the guard cells (efflux), causing closure.

**5.** Yes — slower but longer-lasting than electrical signalling.

### Part D — Applied / Biochemistry

**1.** (a) Plants respond relatively SLOWLY (seconds–minutes for stomatal closure, and slower still for gene-expression/growth changes) — much slower than a nerve impulse (milliseconds) — because they signal with hormones (chemical signalling) rather than fast electrical action potentials. (b) Target cells = the GUARD CELLS of the stomata. (c) ABA is produced in roots and leaves and is distributed through the plant (carried in the vascular tissue / through tissues) to reach the guard cells. (d) Guard cells detect ABA via receptors located both OUTSIDE (on the cell surface) and INSIDE the cell, which trigger the signal-transduction pathway that closes the stoma.

**2.** (a) An open stoma needs HIGH guard-cell turgor: guard cells accumulate solutes (K⁺ and anions), making their water potential more NEGATIVE, so water moves IN by osmosis, the cells swell and bow apart, opening the pore. (b) Under ABA, K⁺ and anions LEAVE the guard cells, so their internal solute concentration drops and their water potential RISES (becomes less negative); water now moves OUT toward the more negative surroundings, the guard cells lose turgor (flaccid), and the stoma closes. (c) Closing stomata REDUCES transpiration (less water vapour escapes), conserving water during drought — the trade-off is that closed stomata also reduce CO₂ uptake, lowering photosynthesis.

**3.** (a) Stimulus = water loss/wilting (drop in turgor/water potential) → receptor = root/shoot cells (and guard-cell ABA receptors) → message = ABA → effector = guard cells → response = stomata close, reducing water loss; when water is restored ABA falls and stomata reopen. This is NEGATIVE feedback because the response (closing stomata, conserving water) OPPOSES the original change (water loss) and returns the plant toward balance. (b) Experiment: grow wild-type plants and ABA-deficient mutant plants; impose drought/water stress (treatment) on both, with well-watered plants of each as controls; measure stomatal aperture (and/or transpiration/water loss) over time. Predicted result if ABA is necessary: wild-type closes its stomata under drought, but the ABA-deficient mutant fails to close (keeps losing water), showing ABA is required. (c) Because guard-cell ABA responses are still being actively researched, it shows scientific knowledge is provisional/contestable — models are refined as new evidence emerges, rather than being fixed final truths.
