# Suggested Answers

These answers are concise model answers. Students should receive credit for equivalent explanations that use correct biological reasoning.

## Set 1

Short-answer questions:

1. Roots obtain water and mineral nutrients; leaves produce sugars by photosynthesis; water, minerals, and sugars must be transported between organs.

2. Vascular tissue allowed long-distance transport of water, nutrients, and sugars, and also provided structural support, enabling larger land plants.

3. Photosynthesis, solute transport, cooling through transpiration, and structural support through turgor pressure.

4. Water potential is the potential energy of water; water moves across a selectively permeable membrane toward lower, more negative water potential.

5. Solutes lower water potential, while pressure potential raises it. Low pressure leads to water entry and flaccidity may occur if water is lost; high turgor balances solute potential and supports rigidity.

Application questions:

1. WP = WPS + WPP = -1.0 + 0 = -1.0 MPa. Water moves from the surrounding solution at 0 MPa into the cell, increasing turgor pressure.

2. WP = -1.0 + 1.0 = 0 MPa. The surrounding pure water is also 0 MPa, so there is no net water movement.

3. Nitrogen and phosphorus are taken up from soil by roots and moved through plant transport tissues. They are needed to synthesize proteins, nucleic acids, membranes, and other growth-related compounds; photosynthesis alone cannot replace mineral nutrients.

## Set 2

Short-answer questions:

1. Apoplast: movement through cell walls and intercellular spaces. Symplast: movement through cytoplasm connected by plasmodesmata after crossing a membrane.

2. The Casparian strip blocks unregulated apoplastic flow at the endodermis, forcing selective membrane crossing into the symplast before entry to vascular tissue.

3. Water and solutes enter root tissues by osmosis/diffusion, move via apoplast and/or symplast, are forced into endodermal cells by the Casparian strip, then solutes are actively transported and water follows into the xylem.

4. Cohesion holds water molecules together by hydrogen bonding; adhesion helps water interact with xylem walls. Together they maintain a continuous water column.

5. Transpiration causes evaporation from leaf surfaces and water vapour loss through stomata. This creates tension in leaf veins, which is transmitted by cohesion through the xylem, pulling water from roots upward.

Application questions:

1. Gravitational cost = 115 x 0.01 MPa = 1.15 MPa. Very tall trees must maintain enough negative pressure/tension to overcome gravity plus resistance; excessive tension increases transport failure risk.

2. Damage would allow more unregulated apoplastic entry into the vascular tissue, reducing selectivity and increasing the risk that harmful ions or pathogens move inward.

3. A stronger water vapour gradient increases transpiration and xylem tension. This can increase water uptake if soil water is available, but under drought it raises the risk of cavitation, low turgor, and wilting.

## Set 3

Short-answer questions:

1. Sieve tube elements are living conducting cells with sieve plates and reduced organelles. Companion cells support them metabolically and provide proteins/metabolites through plasmodesmata.

2. A source produces or releases sugars; a sink consumes or stores sugars.

3. Different organs can act as sources or sinks depending on developmental stage and season, so phloem flow can occur in different directions in different sieve tubes.

4. Loading: sucrose actively enters companion/sieve cells; water enters by osmosis; hydrostatic pressure rises; sap flows away from high pressure; unloading at sinks raises sieve-tube water potential and water leaves.

5. Xylem water transport is largely passive and driven by transpiration-cohesion-tension. Phloem requires active sucrose loading that generates osmotic water entry and pressure-driven mass flow.

Yes/No questions: 1. Yes; 2. Yes; 3. Yes; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

MCQ answers: 1. A; 2. A; 3. A; 4. A; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. A; 10. A

Application questions:

1. Sucrose loading lowers water potential in the sieve tube; water enters from nearby tissue/xylem by osmosis; hydrostatic pressure increases at the leaf source; sap moves toward the lower-pressure root sink where sucrose is unloaded.

2. Nearby mature leaves export sucrose toward the developing fruit because the fruit consumes or stores sugars and therefore acts as a strong sink.

3. ATP inhibition reduces active sucrose loading. Sieve-tube water potential will not decrease as much, less water enters by osmosis, hydrostatic pressure is reduced, and mass flow slows or stops.
