# Reference Answers

## Set 1 Answers

### Short-answer Questions

1. Homeostasis is maintenance of a steady internal state by physiological or behavioural feedback responses; multicellular organisms need it because cells depend on stable extracellular fluid.

2. Temperature, osmotic potential, pH, ion and nutrient concentrations, O2 and CO2 levels.

3. Receptor detects change; control centre processes information and sends signals; effector produces a response opposing the stimulus.

4. High glucose stimulates beta-islet cells in the pancreas to release insulin; target cells increase glucose uptake/storage, lowering blood glucose.

5. Negative feedback opposes change, e.g. insulin lowers high glucose. Positive feedback amplifies change, e.g. oxytocin during breastfeeding increases milk let-down and feeding stimulus.

### Yes/No Questions

1. Yes; 2. No; 3. Yes; 4. No; 5. Yes

### MCQ Answers

1. B; 2. B; 3. C; 4. B; 5. B; 6. B; 7. B; 8. B; 9. A; 10. C

### Application Questions

1. Stimulus: high BGL. Receptor/control: pancreatic beta-islet cells. Message: insulin. Effector: liver and other target cells increase glucose uptake and glycogen synthesis. Correction: blood glucose falls toward 4-8 mmol/L.

2. The statement is incomplete because positive feedback also occurs. In breastfeeding, nipple stimulation leads to oxytocin release, smooth muscle contraction, milk let-down, and stronger feeding, which increases the original stimulus.

3. Blood glucose decreases. Insulin increases glucose transport into cells and activates glycogen synthase, converting glucose to glycogen for storage.

## Set 2 Answers

### Short-answer Questions

1. Hypoglycaemia can impair nervous system function, causing seizures or loss of consciousness; hyperglycaemia can damage cells and increase risk of heart disease, stroke, kidney disease, and vision impairment.

2. Glucagon activates glycogen phosphorylase and inhibits glycogen synthase, promoting glycogen breakdown and glucose release.

3. Insulin lowers blood glucose by uptake/storage; glucagon raises blood glucose by glycogen breakdown and release.

4. They include multiple checks, balances, and organs; they coordinate timing and complex physiological events such as reproductive regulation or insect moulting.

5. Plant hormones often regulate growth, development, tropisms, dormancy, and stress responses across plant tissues; animal hormones often circulate in blood to distant targets.

### Yes/No Questions

### MCQ Answers

1. B; 2. A; 3. B; 4. B; 5. B; 6. A; 7. B; 8. A; 9. B; 10. A

### Application Questions

1. Low glucose during exercise stimulates alpha-islet cells to release glucagon. Glucagon acts on hepatocytes, activates glycogen phosphorylase, breaks glycogen into glucose, and raises blood glucose.

2. Treatment A: insulin increases glycogen synthase, decreases glucose release, increases glycogen storage. Treatment B: glucagon activates glycogen phosphorylase, inhibits glycogen synthase, decreases glycogen stores, increases glucose release.

3. During fasting, the liver would respond poorly to glucagon, so glycogen breakdown and glucose release would be reduced. Blood glucose could fall too low, disrupting homeostasis.

## Set 3 Answers

### Short-answer Questions

1. Plants must balance CO2 uptake for photosynthesis with water loss through transpiration; excessive water loss causes wilting and stress.

2. Guard cells surround stomata. When turgid they open stomata; when flaccid they close stomata.

3. Under water stress, ABA produced in roots/leaves signals guard cells to close stomata and can trigger drought-response gene expression.

4. ABA triggers anion efflux and K+ efflux from guard cells; lower solute concentration causes water to leave by osmosis/aquaporins; cells lose turgor and stomata close.

5. Plant hormone signalling is chemical and often slower but longer lasting; animal electrical signalling via action potentials is rapid and suited for fast coordination.

### Yes/No Questions

1. Yes; 2. No; 3. Yes; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

### MCQ Answers

1. C; 2. B; 3. A; 4. A; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. D; 10. A

### Application Questions

1. Water loss reduces turgor/water potential; root and shoot cells produce ABA; ABA travels to guard cells; receptors detect ABA; ion efflux and water loss make guard cells flaccid; stomata close and transpiration decreases.

2. Water leaves guard cells by osmosis because solute concentration has decreased. Guard cells become flaccid, and the stomatal aperture narrows/closes.

3. The mutant would fail to close stomata efficiently under drought, so transpiration would remain high, water loss would increase, wilting would worsen, and survival risk would increase.
