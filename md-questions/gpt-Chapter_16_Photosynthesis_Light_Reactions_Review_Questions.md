# Answer Key

## Set 1 Short Answer

1. It oxygenated the atmosphere over geological time, enabling aerobic metabolism and changing Earth’s biological possibilities. The lecture links photosynthetic prokaryotes to O2 accumulation about 2.5 billion years ago.
2. In most plants, photosynthesis occurs mainly in leaves, especially mesophyll cells. CO2 enters through stomata, water arrives through veins, and chloroplasts contain stroma and thylakoid membranes where the light reactions occur.
3. Light reactions occur in thylakoid membranes and use light and water to produce ATP, NADPH and O2. The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma and uses CO2, ATP and NADPH to build carbohydrate.
4. Pigments absorb photons at particular wavelengths. Absorbed light excites electrons; energy is transferred through photosystems to drive electron transport and production of ATP/NADPH.
5. PSII absorbs light and loses excited electrons. Water is split to replace these electrons and release O2/H+. Electrons pass through carriers that help build a proton gradient, then reach PSI, are re-excited, and reduce NADP+ to NADPH.

## Set 1 Application/Calculation

1. The O2 coefficient is wrong. Correct: 6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2. The lecture also shows an equivalent full form: 6CO2 + 12H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2 + 6H2O.
2. O2 would not be produced, and PSII would lack replacement electrons. This would impair non-cyclic electron transport, reducing proton-gradient formation, ATP production and NADPH production downstream.
3. Energy is inversely proportional to wavelength; blue/violet light has shorter wavelength and therefore higher photon energy than red light. Pigments absorb specific wavelengths, and absorbed photons excite electrons.

## Set 2 Short Answer

1. They are separate multi-step pathways with different enzymes, cellular locations and energy coupling. Photosynthesis stores light energy in chemical bonds, whereas respiration releases energy from organic molecules to make ATP.
2. In plants and algae, photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts, especially thylakoid membranes and stroma. In cyanobacteria, photosynthetic membranes are present but chloroplasts are absent because they are prokaryotes.
3. Electron transport pumps/accumulates H+ into the thylakoid lumen. H+ then moves back toward the stroma through ATP synthase, and this movement drives ATP formation from ADP and Pi.
4. Non-cyclic flow uses PSII and PSI, splits water, produces O2, ATP and NADPH. Cyclic flow uses PSI and cycles electrons back through carriers, mainly increasing ATP production without producing NADPH or O2.
5. Chloroplasts are needed for photosynthesis and occur mainly in photosynthetic cells, but mitochondria support cellular respiration and ATP production in actively metabolizing cells throughout the plant.

## Set 2 Application/Calculation

1. Light absorption by PSII -> water splitting -> electron transport -> ATP synthase activity occurs as the proton gradient is used -> PSI excitation -> NADP+ reduction. Some steps overlap, but this is the conceptual order.
2. NADPH production would fall or stop because NADP+ could not be reduced efficiently. Electrons would accumulate upstream, disrupting normal non-cyclic electron flow and limiting Calvin-cycle reducing power.
3. Chloroplasts: thylakoid membrane; high H+ in thylakoid lumen; ATP synthase makes ATP as H+ moves to stroma. Mitochondria: inner mitochondrial membrane; high H+ in intermembrane space; ATP synthase makes ATP as H+ moves to matrix.
