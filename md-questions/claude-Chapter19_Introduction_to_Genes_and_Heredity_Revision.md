*Subtitle: What is genetics and how is it studied?*

**Key idea to remember: Genetics studies genes at MANY interconnected levels — molecular (DNA sequence), cytogenetic (chromosomes), organismal/transmission (crosses and inheritance), population, and evolutionary. A gene is a DNA sequence expressed as RNA and protein; gene variants (alleles) are copied faithfully by replication, distributed by mitosis within an organism, and segregated by meiosis to the next generation.**

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** (1) Molecular genetics — studies the DNA sequence of genes (and their products). (2) Cytogenetics — studies chromosomes (number, structure, behaviour) under the microscope. (3) Organismal & transmission genetics — studies how genes/traits are passed between generations via crosses. (4) Population genetics — studies allele frequencies and variation across populations. (5) Evolutionary genetics — studies genetic relationships and change among species over time (phylogenies).

**2.** A gene is a sequence of DNA that is expressed to produce functional products. The two main product types are RNA (e.g. mRNA) and polypeptides/proteins. Central pathway: DNA (gene) is transcribed to RNA, and the (m)RNA is translated to a polypeptide/protein, which carries out a function.

**3.** In prokaryotes there is no nucleus, so transcription and translation are coupled and occur simultaneously in the cytoplasm. In eukaryotes they are separated in space and time — transcription (and RNA processing) in the nucleus, translation in the cytoplasm. Eukaryotic pre-mRNA contains introns, which are spliced out (removed) before the mature mRNA is translated.

**4.** A gene is the DNA sequence; an allele is a particular variant (version) of that gene; the genotype is the set of alleles an individual carries for the gene(s); the phenotype (biological characteristic/trait) is the observable feature produced when the gene products function. Genotype + environment give rise to phenotype.

**5.** A DNA sequence change can alter the gene product, changing how it functions. Gene products act as enzymes (catalyse reactions), structural molecules (build cell/body components) and regulatory molecules (control other genes/processes). If a variant changes one of these functions, the downstream trait/phenotype can change (e.g. different body colour in the fly example).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — genes sit on chromosomes and are transmitted between generations.

**2.** Yes — genetic information is stored predominantly in DNA.

**3.** Yes — introns are spliced out of eukaryotic pre-mRNA before translation.

**4.** Yes — different allele combinations produce trait differences between individuals.

**5.** Yes — eukaryotic transcription (nucleus) and translation (cytoplasm) are spatially separated.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (i) MOLECULAR genetics. (ii) CYTOGENETICS. (iii) ORGANISMAL/TRANSMISSION genetics. (iv) POPULATION genetics. (v) EVOLUTIONARY genetics.

**2.** (i) DNA gene sequence → transcription → pre-mRNA → (eukaryote) splicing removes introns → mature mRNA → translation → protein → altered protein function → altered phenotype/trait. (ii) In a eukaryote the transcript is a pre-mRNA that must have introns spliced out in the nucleus before being exported for translation in the cytoplasm (spatially/temporally separated). (iii) Example: one fly homozygous for the normal body-colour allele is grey, while another carrying a variant allele is black — a visible trait difference arising from different alleles.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Within an organism, DNA replication copies the genetic information (semi-conservatively) and mitosis divides it equally into two daughter cells. Because replication is faithful and mitosis distributes a complete copy to each daughter, the daughter cells are genetically identical to each other and to the parent cell (e.g. both keep the Aa combination).

**2.** Variants are transmitted to the next generation through meiosis, which segregates the genetic information into gametes so each gamete carries only ONE copy of each gene (e.g. an Aa parent makes A gametes and a gametes). Unlike mitosis (which keeps the full diploid set and produces identical cells), meiosis halves the chromosome number and shuffles/segregates alleles, generating genetically varied gametes.

**3.** P = the original true-breeding parents; F1 = their first-generation offspring (often uniform hybrids, e.g. Aa); F2 = the offspring of an F1 x F1 cross. A hybrid (Aa) carries two different alleles, letting geneticists see which allele is expressed (dominance) and how the alleles interact.

**4.** F1 are all Aa (heterozygous). If A is completely dominant, every Aa individual expresses the A (dominant) phenotype, so all F1 look alike (uniform) and resemble the dominant parent.

**5.** A pedigree is a family diagram showing individuals (squares = male, circles = female, filled = affected) and how a trait is transmitted across generations. By fitting a genetic model (e.g. dominant/recessive) to the observed pattern, geneticists can predict the probability that a future offspring will have the trait.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — mitosis gives genetically identical daughter cells with the complete set.

**2.** Yes — meiosis produces gametes carrying one copy of each gene.

**3.** Yes — AA x aa gives all Aa (heterozygous) F1.

**4.** Yes — Aa x Aa gives a 3:1 dominant:recessive phenotype ratio with complete dominance.

**5.** Yes — the F2 genotype ratio is 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (i) F1 = all Aa, grey/normal body (dominant phenotype). (ii) Punnett square for Aa x Aa: gametes A and a from each parent give AA, Aa, Aa, aa. (iii) F2 GENOTYPE ratio = 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa; F2 PHENOTYPE ratio = 3 dominant (grey) : 1 recessive. (iv) Of 120 F2, 1/4 are recessive = 30 flies show the recessive phenotype.

**2.** (i) After mitosis, each daughter cell is still Aa — identical to the parent, because replication copies both alleles and mitosis gives each daughter a full set. (ii) After meiosis, gametes carry a SINGLE allele: half carry A and half carry a (1 A : 1 a). (iii) Mitosis copies the genome faithfully so all body cells are identical (Aa); meiosis segregates the alleles into different gametes (A or a), and fertilisation combines gametes from two parents, so offspring can inherit new allele combinations and be genetically different.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** A dihybrid cross follows TWO genes (two traits) simultaneously. For AaBb x AaBb with complete dominance and independent assortment, the F2 phenotype ratio is 9 : 3 : 3 : 1 = 9 showing both dominant traits : 3 dominant for the first/recessive for the second : 3 recessive for the first/dominant for the second : 1 recessive for both.

**2.** If two genes are on DIFFERENT chromosomes, they assort independently, so all four gamete combinations (AB, Ab, aB, ab) are produced in EQUAL frequency. If they are on the SAME chromosome (linked), they tend to stay together, so the parental combinations are MORE frequent and recombinant combinations are rarer. Independent assortment = random, independent distribution of alleles of different genes; linkage = genes inherited together because they are physically close on one chromosome.

**3.** Different genes/products can act in the same pathway to build one trait, interacting through: (1) biochemical pathways (enzymes acting in sequence), (2) developmental pathways, and (3) signalling/regulatory pathways (one gene's product regulates another gene). The combined action of products A and B yields the biological characteristic.

**4.** A gene interaction (epistasis) is when one gene's effect depends on another gene. This can modify the simple 9:3:3:1 dihybrid ratio because certain genotype combinations produce the same or a new phenotype (e.g. if both a functional A product AND a functional B product are needed to build a normal wing, genotypes lacking one can give 'vestigial' or 'wings absent', changing the observed ratio).

**5.** Gene expression can be regulated temporally (when), spatially (where/which cells), in response to external cues, and at multiple stages (transcription, splicing, translation, etc.). Regulation in response to external cues often uses cell-signalling pathways (Lecture 18) that switch genes on/off; because different cells express different gene sets, regulated expression underlies cellular differentiation into specialised cell types.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — independently assorting AaBb x AaBb gives 9:3:3:1.

**2.** Yes — genes on different chromosomes assort independently.

**3.** Yes — linked genes are inherited together, making parental combinations more frequent.

**4.** Yes — gene interactions can change how often traits appear.

**5.** Yes — differentiation depends on regulated, cell-type-specific gene expression.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (i) F1 = all AaBb, grey body + normal wings (both dominant). (ii) F2 (genes on different chromosomes) = 9:3:3:1 — 9 grey/normal (A\_B\_) : 3 grey/vestigial (A\_bb) : 3 black/normal (aaB\_) : 1 black/vestigial (aabb). (iii) aabb = 1/16 of offspring; 1/16 of 160 = 10 flies black-bodied with vestigial wings.

**2.** (i) Different chromosomes: four gamete types AB, Ab, aB, ab each at 25% (1/4). (ii) Linked on the same chromosome with parental arrangement AB/ab: the gametes are mostly parental (AB and ab), so AB and ab are common while recombinant Ab and aB are rare — recombinants only form when crossing-over occurs between the two loci, which is infrequent for close genes. (iii) By measuring the proportion of recombinant offspring: a low recombinant frequency (well below the 50% expected for independent assortment) indicates the genes are linked; the recombination frequency also estimates the genetic distance between them.

**3.** (i) Building a normal wing may need gene product A to act first and then gene product B (a two-step pathway); only flies with at least one functional allele of BOTH genes (A\_B\_) complete the pathway and make a normal wing. (ii) If either step is missing (e.g. aaB\_ lacks product A, A\_bb lacks product B, aabb lacks both), the pathway is blocked at some point, giving altered phenotypes such as vestigial or 'wings absent'. So both functional gene products are required for the normal trait. (iii) This shows gene interactions (epistasis) can mask or modify the standard Mendelian 9:3:3:1 ratio, producing modified ratios because phenotype depends on the combination of alleles across more than one gene.
