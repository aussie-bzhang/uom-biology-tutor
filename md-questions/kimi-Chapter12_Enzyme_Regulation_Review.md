## Question 1: Competitive Inhibition Kinetics (10 marks)

### Part (a) - Lineweaver-Burk Analysis (4 marks)

First, calculate 1/[S] and 1/v for both conditions:

| **1/[S] (mM⁻¹)** | **1/v₀ (no inh.)** | **1/v₀ (+ 2 mM X)** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2.00 | 1.22 | 2.08 |
| 1.00 | 0.75 | 1.25 |
| 0.50 | 0.53 | 0.82 |
| 0.25 | 0.41 | 0.60 |
| 0.125 | 0.35 | 0.48 |
| 0.0625 | 0.325 | 0.41 |

**No inhibitor:**

From the Lineweaver-Burk plot: y-intercept = 1/Vmax = 0.30 µmol⁻¹ min

∴ Vmax = 1/0.30 = 3.33 µmol/min (1 mark)

x-intercept = -1/Km = -0.50 mM⁻¹

∴ Km = 2.0 mM (1 mark)

**With 2.0 mM inhibitor X:**

y-intercept = 1/Vmax = 0.30 µmol⁻¹ min → Vmax = 3.33 µmol/min (same as above)

x-intercept = -1/Km(apparent) = -0.167 mM⁻¹

∴ Km(apparent) = 6.0 mM (1 mark for both calculations)

### Part (b) - Inhibition Type (3 marks)

Compound X is a COMPETITIVE inhibitor. (1 mark)

Evidence:

* Vmax remains unchanged (3.33 µmol/min) - the same y-intercept on the LB plot
* Km increases from 2.0 mM to 6.0 mM in the presence of inhibitor - the x-intercept moves closer to the origin
* The lines intersect on the y-axis, which is the diagnostic feature of competitive inhibition (1 mark for each piece of evidence)

This is consistent with the inhibitor binding to the active site, competing with substrate for access. Increasing substrate concentration can overcome the inhibition.

### Part (c) - Ki Calculation (3 marks)

For competitive inhibition: Km(apparent) = Km(1 + [I]/Ki)

6.0 = 2.0(1 + 2.0/Ki) (1 mark for correct substitution)

3.0 = 1 + 2.0/Ki

2.0 = 2.0/Ki

Ki = 1.0 mM (2 marks for correct answer and working)

The relatively high Ki indicates that compound X has moderate affinity for the enzyme compared to the substrate.

## Question 2: Irreversible Inhibition & Enzyme Recovery (10 marks)

### Part (a) - Initial Enzyme Activity (2 marks)

Specific activity = 500 units/mg

Enzyme concentration = 0.05 mg/mL

Volume = 1.0 mL

Total enzyme mass = 0.05 mg/mL × 1.0 mL = 0.05 mg (1 mark)

Total activity = 500 units/mg × 0.05 mg = 25 units (1 mark)

### Part (b) - Rate Constant Determination (4 marks)

For first-order inactivation: ln(A/A₀) = -kt, or ln(% residual activity) = -kt + ln(100)

| **Time (min)** | **ln(% residual activity)** |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | 4.605 |
| 2 | 4.277 |
| 4 | 3.951 |
| 6 | 3.611 |
| 8 | 3.296 |
| 10 | 2.944 |

Plotting ln(% activity) vs time gives a straight line. (1 mark for correct plot/data)

Slope = -k = (2.944 - 4.605) / (10 - 0) = -1.661 / 10 = -0.166 min⁻¹ (2 marks)

∴ k = 0.166 min⁻¹ (1 mark)

The half-life of the enzyme under these conditions: t₁/₂ = ln(2)/k = 0.693/0.166 = 4.17 min.

### Part (c) - Experimental Design for 2-PAM Recovery (4 marks)

Experimental Design:

**1.** Set up multiple identical AChE + OP-1 incubation mixtures (e.g., 1.0 mL each with 0.05 mg/mL AChE and a fixed [OP-1]).

**2.** At time t = 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 15, 20 min after adding OP-1, add 2-PAM (final concentration 1 mM) to one tube each. (1 mark for time course design)

**3.** Incubate all tubes with 2-PAM for 30 min at 25°C to allow reactivation.

**4.** Assay residual enzyme activity for each tube. (1 mark for activity measurement)

**Controls required:**

* Positive control: AChE without OP-1 (100% activity reference)
* Negative control 1: AChE + OP-1 without 2-PAM treatment (shows complete inactivation)
* Negative control 2: AChE + 2-PAM without OP-1 (ensures 2-PAM doesn't inhibit)

## Question 3: Allosteric Regulation of Phosphofructokinase (10 marks)

### Part (a) - Sigmoidal Kinetics (3 marks)

The curve without effector shows sigmoidal (S-shaped) kinetics because PFK is an allosteric enzyme with multiple subunits and multiple binding sites. (1 mark)

Key features of the sigmoidal curve:

* At low [F6P], the enzyme is in the low-activity T (tense) state - activity is very low
* As [F6P] increases, substrate binding to one subunit enhances binding to other subunits (positive cooperativity)
* The enzyme undergoes a conformational shift to the high-activity R (relaxed) state, causing a steep rise in velocity
* At high [F6P], the curve plateaus as all enzyme molecules are in the R state (1 mark for explanation)

ATP (inhibitor) shifts the curve right (increases [S]0.5), making the sigmoid more pronounced. AMP (activator) shifts the curve left (decreases [S]0.5). (1 mark for effector descriptions)

### Part (b) - [S]0.5 Determination (3 marks)

Vmax = 15.0 µmol/min, so 0.5 Vmax = 7.5 µmol/min

From the data table, interpolating for v = 7.5 µmol/min:

* No effector: [S]0.5 ≈ 1.5 mM (between 1.0 and 2.0 mM)
* + 1 mM ATP: [S]0.5 ≈ 5.0 mM (between 2.0 and 5.0 mM, closer to 5.0)
* + 0.5 mM AMP: [S]0.5 ≈ 0.7 mM (between 0.5 and 1.0 mM) (1 mark for each, accept reasonable estimates)

ATP decreases PFK's apparent affinity for F6P (increases [S]0.5) by stabilizing the T state. AMP increases the apparent affinity (decreases [S]0.5) by stabilizing the R state. (1 mark for explanation if not already awarded)

### Part (c) - Metabolic Calculation (4 marks)

Resting state: ATP = 1 mM, AMP = 0.1 mM

At [F6P] = 1.0 mM:

From the data table, at rest (approximated by +1 mM ATP condition as the dominant inhibitor): v₀ ≈ 1.2 µmol/min (using no effector row at 1.0 mM F6P as baseline: v = 5.0)

Better approach: During exercise, [ATP] drops to 0.5 mM and [AMP] rises to 0.5 mM. The activation by AMP dominates.

At [F6P] = 1.0 mM:

* Resting (approx. no effector, but with moderate ATP): v₀ ≈ 2.0-3.0 µmol/min (use no effector as best reference)
* Exercise (+ 0.5 mM AMP condition): v₀ = 7.0 µmol/min

Fold-change = 7.0 / 2.5 ≈ 2.8-fold increase (accept 2-4 fold depending on assumptions) (2 marks for reasonable calculation)

Metabolic significance: This allosteric regulation allows glycolytic flux to increase dramatically when energy is needed (high AMP signals low energy state), without requiring changes in gene expression. (2 marks for explanation)

## Question 4: Feedback Inhibition in Branched Pathway (10 marks)

### Part (a) - Feedback Network Diagram (4 marks)

The diagram should show:

* Aspartate → [Aspartokinase - inhibited by Lys + Thr together] → Aspartyl-phosphate (1 mark)
* Common pathway → multiple branches
* Branch 1: Enzyme 1a → Met (inhibited by Met)
* Branch 2: Enzyme 1b → Lys (inhibited by Lys)
* Branch 3: Enzyme 1c → ... → Thr (inhibited by Thr)
* Thr → [Enzyme 2] → ... → Ile (inhibited by Ile) (2 marks for all four branch-specific inhibitions)
* Lys + Thr →⊖ Aspartokinase (cooperative inhibition) (1 mark for showing cooperative inhibition of first enzyme)

### Part (b)(i) - Lysine-insensitive Aspartokinase Mutation (2 marks)

If aspartokinase is insensitive to lysine but still sensitive to threonine:

* Lysine synthesis will continue unchecked - lysine will overaccumulate
* The common pathway intermediates (aspartyl-phosphate, etc.) will be depleted because flux is diverted excessively toward lysine
* Less carbon will be available for methionine, threonine, and isoleucine synthesis
* Threonine levels may drop, which could relieve some inhibition of aspartokinase, further increasing flux (1 mark for lysine accumulation + 1 mark for other amino acid depletion)

### Part (b)(ii) - Excess Isoleucine, No Threonine (2 marks)

In medium with excess isoleucine but no threonine:

* Isoleucine inhibits its own branch (Thr → next step), but since there's no threonine entering from outside, this is irrelevant
* The cell cannot make isoleucine without threonine (threonine is the precursor)
* Aspartokinase is not inhibited (no threonine = no cooperative inhibition with lysine)
* The pathway will continue producing aspartyl-phosphate and other intermediates, but flux toward threonine/isoleucine will be limited by the lack of substrate
* Lysine and methionine synthesis will proceed normally (1 mark for recognition that Ile synthesis requires Thr + 1 mark for no growth due to Ile auxotrophy)

### Part (c) - Metabolic Advantage (2 marks)

It is advantageous because:

* If isoleucine inhibited aspartokinase directly, it would also shut down synthesis of methionine, lysine, and threonine - all of which the cell still needs
* By inhibiting only the enzyme that converts threonine to the next intermediate, isoleucine specifically reduces its own production without affecting other essential amino acids
* This allows the cell to maintain appropriate levels of all amino acids while specifically controlling isoleucine concentration
* Threonine can still accumulate and be used for protein synthesis (2 marks for this key insight about specificity vs. global shutdown)

## Question 5: Mixed Inhibition and Drug Design (10 marks)

### Part (a) - Lineweaver-Burk Analysis (5 marks)

Calculated 1/[S] and 1/v values:

| **1/[S] (µM⁻¹)** | **1/v₀ (no drug)** | **1/v₀ (+ 5 µM NSAID-Z)** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 2.00 | 2.22 | 4.55 |
| 1.00 | 1.33 | 2.63 |
| 0.50 | 0.87 | 1.67 |
| 0.20 | 0.59 | 1.05 |
| 0.10 | 0.48 | 0.80 |
| 0.05 | 0.41 | 0.66 |

From the Lineweaver-Burk plot analysis: (1 mark for correct table)

**No drug:**

y-intercept = 1/Vmax = 0.35 → Vmax = 2.86 nmol/min

x-intercept = -1/Km = -0.56 → Km = 1.79 ≈ 1.8 µM

**+ 5 µM NSAID-Z:**

y-intercept = 1/Vmax(apparent) = 0.55 → Vmax(apparent) = 1.82 nmol/min

x-intercept = -1/Km(apparent) = -0.28 → Km(apparent) = 3.57 ≈ 3.6 µM (2 marks for all parameters)

Type of inhibition: NON-COMPETITIVE (or mixed) inhibition. (1 mark)

Justification:

* Vmax decreases (from 2.86 to 1.82 nmol/min) - characteristic of non-competitive inhibition
* Km(apparent) increases (from 1.8 to 3.6 µM) - suggests the inhibitor also affects substrate binding
* The lines on the LB plot intersect to the left of the y-axis, not on it (true mixed inhibition)
* If it were pure non-competitive, Km would be unchanged; the change in Km indicates mixed inhibition (1 mark for correct justification)

### Part (b) - Ibuprofen Concentration Calculation (3 marks)

For competitive inhibition: v = Vmax[S] / (Km(1 + [I]/Ki) + [S])

For 75% inhibition, v = 0.25 Vmax (since 75% inhibition means only 25% activity remains)

0.25 = [S] / (Km(1 + [I]/Ki) + [S])

Given: [S] = 5 µM, Km = 2.0 µM, Ki = 2.5 µM (1 mark for correct setup)

0.25 = 5 / (2.0(1 + [I]/2.5) + 5)

0.25 = 5 / (2.0 + 0.8[I] + 5)

0.25 = 5 / (7.0 + 0.8[I])

7.0 + 0.8[I] = 5 / 0.25 = 20

0.8[I] = 13

[I] = 16.25 µM (2 marks for correct calculation, accept 16-17 µM)

### Part (c) - Aspirin vs Ibuprofen Duration (2 marks)

Aspirin causes irreversible inhibition by covalently acetylating a serine in the COX active site. (1 mark for mechanism)

Key differences:

* Aspirin: Once a COX molecule is acetylated, it is permanently inactivated. The enzyme must be replaced by new protein synthesis. Since platelets (which produce prostaglandins involved in inflammation) cannot synthesize new proteins, the effect lasts for the lifetime of the platelet (7-10 days). This is why aspirin has long-lasting effects despite its short plasma half-life.
* Ibuprofen: Causes reversible competitive inhibition. It binds non-covalently and dissociates from the enzyme. As ibuprofen is cleared from the plasma (half-life 2-4 hours), the inhibition is gradually reversed. New COX molecules that were never bound to drug become active, restoring prostaglandin synthesis. (1 mark for the key distinction: irreversible vs reversible)
