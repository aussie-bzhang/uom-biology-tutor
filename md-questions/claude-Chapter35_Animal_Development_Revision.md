## Core Concepts at a Glance (read before you start)

**Cell potency (recap):** totipotent = can make ALL cell types of the organism INCLUDING extraembryonic tissue like placenta (e.g. zygote); pluripotent = all body cell types but NOT extraembryonic tissue (e.g. embryonic stem cells, iPSCs, inner cell mass); multipotent = several cell types (e.g. adult/tissue-specific stem cells); unipotent = only one cell type (e.g. skin cells making more skin cells).

**Potency vs differentiation:** they run in OPPOSITE directions: as a cell becomes more differentiated (specialised), its potency falls. The zygote is most potent + least differentiated; a mature cell is least potent + most differentiated.

**Key stages of animal development:** fertilisation -> cleavage -> blastula -> gastrulation -> gastrula -> organogenesis -> (in a frog) tadpole -> metamorphosis -> adult. Studied here mainly in Xenopus laevis (frog) and humans.

**Cleavage:** rapid divisions of the zygote into smaller cells with NO net growth (cells skip the G1/G2 growth phases), so the embryo is divided into many small cells without getting bigger. Produces a blastula: a simple ball of cells surrounding a fluid-filled cavity (blastocoel).

**Gastrulation:** massive coordinated cell MOVEMENT transforms the blastula into a gastrula. It (1) builds a multi-layered embryo, (2) sets up the main body axes, and (3) creates the three germ layers. Mechanics: cells at the base flatten/change shape, some detach and migrate inward, an invagination extends into a long thin tube (the gut/endoderm), and future mouth and anus sites are set. Looks different between species but the principles are the same.

**The three germ layers + derivatives:** ectoderm (outer) -> skin (epidermis) + nervous system; mesoderm (middle) -> muscles, bones, heart, blood vessels, connective tissue; endoderm (inner) -> epithelial lining of the gut and lungs (digestive/respiratory tracts).

**Diploblastic vs triploblastic:** diploblastic = 2 germ layers (no mesoderm), e.g. cnidarians, usually radial symmetry; triploblastic = 3 germ layers, more complex, e.g. frogs/humans/sea-urchins, usually bilateral symmetry. Radial symmetry (Radiata): ANY plane through the central axis gives similar halves. Bilateral symmetry (Bilateria): a SINGLE plane through the anterior-posterior midline gives mirror-image halves.

**Four major events of development:** Determination -> Differentiation -> Morphogenesis -> Growth. (Determination = commitment; differentiation = visible specialisation; morphogenesis = shaping; growth = increase in size.)

**Mitosis vs meiosis (recap):** mitosis (somatic cells) -> 2 identical diploid (2n) daughter cells; meiosis (germ cells) -> 4 non-identical haploid (n) daughter cells. Fertilisation restores the diploid number.

**Gametogenesis:** spermatogenesis: 1 primary spermatocyte (2n) -> meiosis -> 4 functional sperm (n); sperm = structure suits function (acrosome, nucleus, mitochondria-rich midpiece, tail for movement). Oogenesis: 1 primary oocyte (2n) -> meiosis with unequal cytoplasmic division -> 1 large egg (n) + polar bodies (which degrade); the egg is loaded with resources for the embryo.

**Fertilisation:** oocyte released ~day 14, viable 12-24 h; sperm survive ~4-5 days. Fertilisation activates development and includes checks/barriers: the zona pellucida (protective layer), sperm-egg fusion, and a block to polyspermy (only one sperm). Site = oviduct.

**Mammalian early development:** cleavage is slow in mammals (8-cell, 16-cell, compaction). Blastocyst = mammalian blastula: inner cell mass (ICM) = pluripotent (the embryo); trophoblast = outer cells that become the placenta; blastocoel = cavity. Implantation ~6-7 days post-fertilisation. Trimesters: 1st = primitive organs form (organogenesis, nervous system, vertebrae/segments); 2nd = rapid growth; 3rd = internal organs mature.

**Regulation by Hox genes:** Hox (homeotic) genes provide the positional blueprint: genes nearest the 3' end are expressed most anteriorly, those nearest 5' more posteriorly (e.g. Hoxb1 in hindbrain, Hoxb9 in spinal cord), creating an anterior-to-posterior expression gradient.

**Post-embryonic development & stem cells:** development does not stop at birth. Adult STEM cells are undifferentiated and divide indefinitely; each division gives 1 new stem cell + 1 daughter cell that differentiates (self-renewal). They drive maintenance/replacement (e.g. gut lining replaced every 5-7 days from crypt stem cells -> goblet, enterocyte, enteroendocrine), healing/regeneration, and defence.

**Unidirectional specialisation can be reversed (reprogramming):** normally specialisation is one-way (potency only decreases). But Gurdon (1962, nuclear transfer cloning a frog) and Yamanaka (2006, induced pluripotent stem cells / iPSCs from skin cells using OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC) showed a mature cell's program CAN be reset to recover potency — jointly awarded the 2012 Nobel Prize. iPSCs are used to model disease, trial drugs, and in regenerative medicine.

**Development reused elsewhere:** the same developmental mechanisms operate in tissue maintenance, tissue regeneration (e.g. zebrafish fin / axolotl limb via a blastema; cells reactivate developmental genetic programs), tissue engineering (organoids self-organise in vitro), and CANCER (mis-regulated proliferation/migration pathways).

**5.** Gastrulation is the process in which massive, coordinated cell movement transforms a blastula into a gastrula. It (1) establishes a complex multi-layered embryo, (2) sets up the main body axes, and (3) creates the three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm).

**3.** An adult stem cell is an undifferentiated cell that can divide indefinitely. Self-renewal means each division produces 1 new stem cell (which maintains the pool) + 1 daughter cell that goes on to differentiate. Example: stem cells in the intestinal crypt continuously replace the gut lining (every ~5-7 days), producing goblet cells, enterocytes and enteroendocrine cells.

**4.** Hox genes are arranged in a cluster whose order matches the body axis: genes nearest the 3' end are switched on in the most ANTERIOR regions, and genes nearest the 5' end in more POSTERIOR regions, producing an anterior-to-posterior expression gradient. For example Hoxb1 is expressed in the hindbrain (anterior) and Hoxb9 in the spinal cord (more posterior). They act as a positional blueprint telling cells where they are along the body.

**5.** 'Unidirectional' means specialisation normally goes one way only - potency decreases and a differentiated cell does not spontaneously revert. Gurdon (1962) transplanted the nucleus of a differentiated frog cell into an enucleated egg and obtained a whole cloned frog, showing the nucleus retained all the genes and its program could be reset. Yamanaka (2006) reprogrammed differentiated cells into induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) by adding defined transcription factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC). Both showed potency can be experimentally restored (2012 Nobel Prize).

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (1) Gut turnover. (a) At steady state, the whole lining (~3 x 10^10 cells) is replaced every ~6 days, so cells produced per day is about 3 x 10^10 / 6 = 5 x 10^9 per day. (b) Per hour: 5 x 10^9 / 24 is about 2 x 10^8 (around 200 million per hour). (c) The asymmetric outcome (1 stem cell + 1 differentiating daughter) means every division REPLACES the stem cell that was 'used', so the stem-cell pool is never depleted while a steady stream of differentiated cells is still produced - this is what allows indefinite maintenance. (d) Rapidly dividing tissues need tight control of proliferation; if that control is lost, the high division rate makes uncontrolled growth (cancer) more likely - the same proliferation/migration pathways that build tissue can drive tumour growth.

**2.** (2) Reprogramming experiment. (a) Introduce the defined reprogramming transcription factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC) into the fibroblast. (b) Hypothesis: these factors will reset the skin cell to a pluripotent (iPSC) state; negative control: identical fibroblasts WITHOUT the factors (should remain skin cells). (c) Confirm pluripotency by showing the cells can differentiate into derivatives of all three germ layers (e.g. neuron-like = ectoderm, muscle/bone-like = mesoderm, gut-like = endoderm) and/or express pluripotency markers. (d) It is surprising because differentiation is normally one-way (potency only decreases); restoring potency reverses that. Recognised: Gurdon and Yamanaka, 2012 Nobel Prize.

**3.** (3) Regeneration. (a) Factors include the animal's developmental complexity and the availability/retention of potent (stem-like) cells able to re-enter developmental programs (also body size, immune/scarring responses) - any reasonable factor. (b) A blastema is a mass of relatively undifferentiated, proliferating cells that forms at the wound/amputation site and re-activates developmental programs to rebuild the missing structure. (c) Repair restores barrier function but often with scar tissue and loss of original structure/function; regeneration rebuilds the original tissue/organ faithfully. (d) iPSCs or directed stem cells could supply the potent cells humans lack - e.g. in type 1 diabetes, stem-cell-derived insulin-producing beta cells were transplanted and the patient began producing insulin, effectively regenerating a lost cell function.

**Common traps to avoid (Chapter 35):**

* Totipotent (can make placenta too) vs pluripotent (all body cells but NOT placenta). The zygote is totipotent; ICM / ES cells / iPSCs are pluripotent.
* Potency and differentiation are INVERSE - as differentiation goes up, potency goes down.
* Cleavage = rapid division with NO growth (skips G1/G2); the embryo gets MORE cells, not bigger.
* Gastrulation does THREE things: multi-layered embryo + body axes + three germ layers, driven by cell MOVEMENT.
* Germ-layer derivatives: ectoderm = skin + nervous system; mesoderm = muscle/bone/heart/blood; endoderm = gut + lung lining.
* Diploblastic = 2 layers, NO mesoderm, radial (cnidarians); triploblastic = 3 layers, bilateral (most animals).
* Mitosis = 2 identical diploid; meiosis = 4 non-identical haploid. Spermatogenesis = 4 sperm; oogenesis = 1 egg + degrading polar bodies.
* Blastocyst: ICM = pluripotent embryo; trophoblast = placenta. Implantation ~6-7 days.
* Stem-cell division = 1 stem cell + 1 differentiating daughter (self-renewal) - this is why the pool is not used up.
* Specialisation is normally one-way, BUT Gurdon (nuclear transfer) & Yamanaka (iPSCs: OCT4/SOX2/KLF4/c-MYC) reversed it - 2012 Nobel.
