## Question 1

The fluid mosaic model describes biological membranes as a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins that float within the lipid matrix. Key components include:

• Phospholipid bilayer: Hydrophilic phosphate heads face the aqueous environments (cytoplasm and extracellular fluid), while hydrophobic fatty acid tails form the membrane interior.

• Membrane proteins: Integral proteins span the membrane; peripheral proteins associate with membrane surfaces.

• Cholesterol: Modulates fluidity by interacting with phospholipid tails.

The membrane is "fluid" because phospholipids and proteins can move laterally within the plane of the membrane. It is a "mosaic" because of the diverse mixture of proteins embedded in or attached to the lipid bilayer, creating a varied pattern rather than a uniform structure.

## Question 2

Saturated fatty acid tails have no carbon-carbon double bonds, allowing them to pack tightly together via London dispersion forces (LDF). This tight packing reduces membrane fluidity and permeability.

Unsaturated fatty acid tails contain one or more carbon-carbon double bonds (typically in cis configuration), which introduce "kinks" or bends in the hydrocarbon chains. These kinks prevent close packing of the phospholipids, creating more space between molecules.

Effects on membrane properties:
• At low temperatures: membranes with more unsaturated tails remain more fluid because the kinks prevent freezing.
• Permeability: increased unsaturation leads to higher permeability because the gaps between phospholipids allow molecules to pass through more easily.
• The graph in the lecture (Figure 1) demonstrates that liposomes with 50% unsaturated tails show significantly higher glycerol permeability compared to those with 0% or 20% unsaturated tails across all temperatures.

## Question 3

Cholesterol has a four-fused-ring steroid structure with a hydroxyl (-OH) group. This amphipathic structure allows cholesterol to interact with phospholipids via ion-dipole interactions between its -OH group and the phosphate heads, and via LDF with fatty acid tails.

At high temperatures:
Cholesterol restricts the movement of phospholipids, reducing membrane fluidity and decreasing permeability. It inserts between phospholipids and enhances close packing, making the membrane less leaky.

At low temperatures:
Cholesterol prevents tight packing of phospholipids by disrupting the orderly arrangement of fatty acid tails. This maintains membrane fluidity and actually increases permeability compared to cholesterol-free membranes at the same low temperature.

Overall, cholesterol acts as a "fluidity buffer," keeping membranes functional across a range of temperatures.

## Question 4

**(a) Passive transport (simple diffusion): Movement of small, nonpolar molecules (e.g., O2, CO2) directly through the lipid bilayer DOWN their concentration gradient. No energy required, no membrane proteins needed.**

**(b) Facilitated diffusion: Transport of polar or charged molecules (e.g., glucose, ions) across the membrane via channel proteins or carrier proteins, still DOWN the concentration gradient. No energy required, but specific transport proteins are needed. Channel proteins form hydrophilic tunnels; carrier proteins bind the molecule and undergo conformational change.**

**(c) Active transport: Movement of molecules AGAINST their concentration gradient. REQUIRES energy (ATP or electrochemical gradient).**
• Primary active transport: Directly uses ATP (e.g., Na+/K+ pump) to move molecules against their own gradient.
• Secondary active transport: Uses the electrochemical gradient of one substance (e.g., H+ or Na+) established by primary active transport to drive the transport of a different substance against its gradient (e.g., symporters and antiporters).

## Question 5

Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER):
• Structure: Network of membrane cisternae studded with ribosomes on the cytosolic surface.
• Function: Synthesis of proteins (secretory, membrane, and lysosomal proteins), glycoproteins, and some carbohydrates.
• The ribosomes synthesize polypeptides that enter the ER lumen for folding and modification.

Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum (SER):
• Structure: Tubular network of membrane cisternae without ribosomes.
• Function: Synthesis of lipids (including phospholipids and steroids), carbohydrate metabolism, and detoxification reactions (in liver cells).
• Produces lipids and carbohydrates but NOT proteins.

Both RER and SER are dynamic, interconnected structures that ramify through the cytoplasm.

## Question 6

The Golgi apparatus consists of flattened stacks of membrane cisternae called Golgi stacks. It functions as the cell's processing, packaging, and distribution center within the endomembrane system.

Cis face (receiving face):
• Faces the ER and receives transport vesicles containing newly synthesized proteins and lipids.
• Initial processing (e.g., phosphorylation) occurs here.

Trans face (shipping face):
• Faces the plasma membrane and packages modified molecules into vesicles for delivery.
• Final modifications (e.g., glycosylation) and sorting occur here.

Between the cis and trans faces are medial cisternae where progressive modifications occur. The Golgi acts as a functional extension of the ER, constantly receiving vesicles and dispatching them to their final destinations (plasma membrane, lysosomes, or secretion).

## Question 7

Lysosomes are membrane-bound organelles containing approximately 40 different hydrolytic enzymes that function as the "recycling bins" of animal cells. They break down materials ingested by endocytosis, recycle old organelles (autophagy), and digest pathogens.

Synthesis and delivery pathway:
**1. Hydrolytic enzymes are synthesized on ribosomes attached to the RER.**
**2. They enter the ER lumen where they are folded and initial modifications occur.**
**3. Transport vesicles carry them to the cis face of the Golgi apparatus.**
**4. In the Golgi, the enzymes are tagged with mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) markers.**
**5. M6P receptors in the trans-Golgi network recognize these tags and package the enzymes into vesicles.**
**6. These vesicles fuse with late endosomes, forming lysosomes.**

The acidic interior (pH ~4.5) is essential because:
• The hydrolytic enzymes are acid hydrolases that function optimally at low pH.
• The low pH is maintained by proton pumps (H+-ATPases) in the lysosomal membrane.
• If the enzymes were released into the cytoplasm (pH ~7.2), they would be inactive and could not damage cellular components.

## Question 8

Similarities:
• Both are surrounded by a single membrane
• Both contain hydrolytic enzymes for degradation
• Both function as degradative compartments

Differences:
• Lysosomes (animals): Smaller, numerous; primary function is intracellular digestion of macromolecules, pathogens, and organelle recycling.
• Vacuoles (plants): Much larger, typically one central vacuole occupying up to 90% of cell volume; surrounded by a tonoplast membrane.

Additional roles of plant vacuoles:
• Storage of nutrients, ions, and metabolic products
• Storage of pigments (e.g., anthocyanins in flower petals)
• Maintenance of cell turgor pressure against the cell wall, providing structural support
• Sequestration of toxic compounds
• Regulation of cytoplasmic pH and ion homeostasis

## Question 9

Experimental Design to Distinguish Channel vs. Carrier Proteins:

**Method 1 — Transport Kinetics:**
• Measure the rate of radioactive tracer transport at increasing substrate concentrations.
• Channel proteins show linear kinetics (rate proportional to concentration) at low concentrations.
• Carrier proteins show saturation kinetics (reach Vmax at high concentrations, following Michaelis-Menten kinetics) due to limited binding sites.

**Method 2 — Inhibitor Sensitivity:**
• Add channel blockers (e.g., tetraethylammonium for K+ channels): channel-mediated transport will be inhibited; carrier-mediated transport will be unaffected.
• Add competitive inhibitors (structural analogs of the substrate): carrier-mediated transport will be competitively inhibited; channel-mediated transport will be unaffected.

**Controls:**
• Negative control: Cells without the transport protein (knockdown or knockout) — should show no specific transport.
• Positive control: Known channel protein or carrier protein under identical conditions.

**Expected results:**
• If transport is blocked by channel blockers and shows linear kinetics → channel protein.
• If transport shows competitive inhibition and saturation kinetics → carrier protein.

## Question 10

**(a) At 25 deg C ranking (most to least permeable): Membrane A > Membrane B > Membrane C.**

**Reasoning:**
• Membrane A (50% unsaturated, 0% cholesterol): The high proportion of unsaturated tails creates many kinks, preventing tight packing and creating gaps that allow glycerol to pass easily. No cholesterol means no restriction of phospholipid movement.
• Membrane B (20% unsaturated, 20% cholesterol): Moderate unsaturation provides some gaps, but cholesterol at this temperature restricts phospholipid movement and reduces permeability.
• Membrane C (0% unsaturated, 50% cholesterol): Saturated tails pack tightly, and high cholesterol further enhances this packing, making the membrane the least permeable.

**(b) At 35 deg C, increasing cholesterol in Membrane B from 20% to 50% would DECREASE permeability. At this relatively high temperature, cholesterol restricts phospholipid movement and promotes closer packing, reducing the gaps through which glycerol can diffuse.**

**(c) At 5 deg C, a cell needing high permeability should adopt a composition with HIGH unsaturated fatty acid tails and MODERATE cholesterol. The kinks from unsaturated tails prevent freezing and maintain gaps even at low temperatures, while moderate cholesterol would help maintain fluidity without overly restricting permeability (since at low temperatures, cholesterol actually disrupts tight packing of saturated tails). Based on the data, a composition similar to Membrane A (high unsaturation, low cholesterol) would be optimal.**

## Question 11

**(i) Direction of net water movement:**
• Solution X (150 mOsm/L): Water moves INTO the cell (hypotonic solution; lower solute concentration outside means higher water potential outside).
• Solution Y (300 mOsm/L): No net water movement (isotonic; equal solute concentrations on both sides, equilibrium).
• Solution Z (450 mOsm/L): Water moves OUT of the cell (hypertonic solution; higher solute concentration outside draws water out).

**(ii) Cell shape predictions:**
• Solution X: HEMOLYSIS — Water influx causes the cell to swell and burst (lyse) due to lack of cell wall.
• Solution Y: NORMAL — Balanced water movement maintains normal biconcave disc shape.
• Solution Z: CRENAATION — Water efflux causes the cell to shrink and develop a spiky, scalloped surface.

**(iii) If membrane becomes permeable to solutes:**
• In Solution X: Solutes would move into the cell down their concentration gradient, and water would follow until both solute and water concentrations equalize. No hemolysis would occur because solute entry would reduce the osmotic gradient.
• In Solution Z: Solutes would move into the cell, water would follow, and the cell would gradually return toward normal volume as equilibrium is reached.
• At equilibrium, the cell would have the same internal solute concentration as the external solution, and no further net movement of water or solutes would occur.

## Question 12

**(a) Plot description: RER radioactivity peaks at 5 min (850 cpm/mg) and declines steadily. Golgi radioactivity starts low (120), peaks at 15 min (680), then declines. Secretory vesicle radioactivity starts very low (30), rises progressively, and peaks at 30 min (720).**

**(b) The pattern reflects the directional flow of secretory proteins through the endomembrane system:**
• RER peaks first because it is the site of protein synthesis; radioactive proteins are made here immediately during the pulse.
• As proteins exit the RER in transport vesicles, RER radioactivity decreases while Golgi radioactivity increases (peak at 15 min).
• After processing in the Golgi, proteins are packaged into secretory vesicles, causing the Golgi signal to decline and the secretory vesicle signal to rise (peak at 30 min).
• This sequential peaking pattern is classic evidence for the RER --> Golgi --> secretory vesicle --> exocytosis pathway.

**(c) At 60 minutes, the remaining radioactivity would be found:**
• In secretory vesicles (380 cpm/mg — vesicles awaiting exocytosis)
• Outside the cell in the extracellular medium (secreted proteins)
• Possibly at the plasma membrane (if the proteins are membrane-bound)

**(d) Brefeldin A blocks transport from ER to Golgi. With this treatment:**
• RER radioactivity would remain high and not decline (proteins cannot leave).
• Golgi and secretory vesicle fractions would show very low radioactivity throughout.
• Radioactive proteins would accumulate in the RER, confirming that the ER-to-Golgi transport step is essential for the secretory pathway.

## Question 13

**(a) HEXA synthesis and delivery pathway:**
**1. Ribosomes on the RER synthesize the HEXA polypeptide chain.**
**2. The protein enters the ER lumen for folding and N-linked glycosylation (addition of oligosaccharide chains).**
**3. Transport vesicles carry HEXA to the cis-Golgi.**
**4. In the cis/medial-Golgi, a specific enzyme phosphorylates mannose residues on the oligosaccharide chain, creating mannose-6-phosphate (M6P) tags.**
**5. M6P receptors in the trans-Golgi network bind to the tagged enzyme and package it into clathrin-coated vesicles.**
**6. These vesicles deliver HEXA to late endosomes, which mature into lysosomes.**
**7. The acidic pH (~4.5) of the lysosome causes the M6P receptor to release HEXA, and the receptor is recycled back to the Golgi.**

**(b) GM2 ganglioside accumulates because without functional HEXA, the lysosome cannot break down this substrate. Normal autophagy and endocytosis continue to deliver GM2 ganglioside into lysosomes, but it cannot be degraded. This leads to progressive lysosomal storage, swollen lysosomes, and eventual cellular dysfunction — particularly in neurons, which are especially sensitive to lysosomal accumulation.**

**(c) Enzyme replacement therapy strategy:**
• Purify functional recombinant HEXA enzyme.
• Tag the enzyme with M6P residues to ensure uptake by cell surface M6P receptors (which are present on most cell types).
• Administer intravenously.

**Challenges and solutions:**
• Challenge 1: The blood-brain barrier prevents enzyme entry into the brain. Solution: Intrathecal (direct CSF) delivery.
• Challenge 2: Immune response against the foreign enzyme. Solution: Use human recombinant enzyme or immune tolerance induction.
• Challenge 3: Insufficient uptake by target cells. Solution: Optimize M6P tagging density or use nanoparticle delivery vehicles.
• Challenge 4: Short enzyme half-life. Solution: Develop modified enzymes with extended circulating lifetimes or gene therapy for sustained production.
