## Core Concepts at a Glance (read before you start)

**Embryogenesis:** formation of a multicellular organism from a single zygote. The zygote undergoes many rounds of cell division producing specific cell types in precise, NON-random patterns along major spatial axes (a body plan).

**Cell-fate establishment (determination):** happens early in embryogenesis. Cells become progressively more RESTRICTED in what they can become — their potency decreases. Fate is set by instructive cues (cytoplasmic factors + cell-signalling molecules) that depend on a cell's POSITION in the embryo.

**Morphogenesis:** the process that sculpts the final body form. In animals it involves cell division, cell-shape change, cell movement, cell adhesion/de-adhesion, and programmed cell death (apoptosis). In plants, cell MOVEMENT and ADHESION do NOT occur (walls hold cells in place); morphogenesis relies on oriented divisions + expansion.

**Differentiation:** cells become specialised. ONE genome → MANY cell types because cells express DIFFERENT subsets of genes (differential gene expression). House-keeping genes (e.g. actin, tubulin, histone) are ON in all cells; cell-specific genes (e.g. synapsin in neurons, lactase in enterocytes, myosin H11 in muscle) are ON in only some. So cell fate = gene regulation.

**Animal vs plant body plan:** animals establish an anterior/posterior axis + segments; plants establish an apical/basal axis (shoot-root) plus a radial axis (concentric dermal/ground/vascular tissues).

**Determinate vs indeterminate growth:** animals = determinate (predetermined body form, all organs made during embryogenesis, just increase in size). Plants = indeterminate (flexible form, few organs made in embryo, then CONTINUOUS organ/tissue formation through meristems).

**Master regulator (homeotic) genes:** control whole genetic programs, so a single mutation can transform one structure into another — e.g. Drosophila bithorax (extra wings) and Arabidopsis AGAMOUS / AG (floral organ identity). Big phenotypic jumps via small regulatory changes.

**Key steps of plant embryogenesis:** (1) asymmetric division of zygote sets the apical-basal axis → apical cell = embryo, basal cell = suspensor; (2) oriented divisions → spherical (octant) embryo + linear suspensor; (3) heart stage = bilateral symmetry, cotyledon outgrowth, radial tissue pattern, shoot + root meristems form; (4) torpedo stage = elongation + differentiation → mature seed (dormancy).

**Plant cell expansion:** walls are semi-rigid. Cell takes up water → vacuole expands → turgor pressure rises → wall resists; high turgor triggers release of wall-loosening enzymes → wall softens → cell expands (direction set by wall/microfibril arrangement).

**Meristems:** regions of undifferentiated cells that divide indefinitely (self-renewal). One division gives 1 new meristematic cell + 1 cell that differentiates. Apical meristems (shoot/root) drive PRIMARY growth (length); their primary meristems = protoderm→dermal, ground meristem→ground, procambium→vascular tissue. Lateral meristems (vascular cambium + cork cambium) drive SECONDARY growth (girth) and are unique to seed plants.

**Root primary growth — 3 overlapping zones:** zone of cell division (root apical meristem) → zone of elongation (cells lengthen) → zone of differentiation/maturation (cells specialise).

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Embryogenesis is the formation of a complete multicellular organism from a single fertilised egg (zygote). The zygote divides repeatedly, producing specific cell types in precise patterns along the major spatial axes. “Not random” is central because the cell types are arranged according to a defined body plan — a reproducible spatial map — not scattered arbitrarily; this is what allows a functional, correctly proportioned organism to form.

**2.** Determination is the (often invisible) COMMITMENT of a cell to a particular fate; it happens early. Differentiation is the later, visible acquisition of specialised structure/function. Becoming “more restricted” means a cell's potency falls — early cells can give rise to many types (high potency), but as development proceeds each lineage can form fewer and fewer types, until a fully differentiated cell makes essentially one type.

**3.** Animal morphogenesis involves: (1) cell division, (2) cell-shape change (expansion), (3) cell movement, (4) cell adhesion/de-adhesion, and (5) programmed cell death (apoptosis). The two that do NOT occur in plants are cell MOVEMENT and cell ADHESION/de-adhesion — because plant cells are encased in rigid cell walls and cemented in place, so they cannot migrate or rearrange; plants instead rely on oriented division + expansion.

**4.** All cells share the same genome (DNA is faithfully copied at every division), so they differ not in which genes they HAVE but in which genes they EXPRESS. By switching different subsets of genes on/off (differential gene expression), a neuron makes neuronal proteins and an enterocyte makes gut-specific proteins, even though both carry the full gene set.

**5.** House-keeping genes are expressed in essentially ALL cells because they perform basic functions (examples: actin, tubulin, histone). Cell-specific genes are expressed in only some cell types (examples: synapsin in neurons, lactase in enterocytes, myosin H11 in muscle). The fact that different cells express different cell-specific genes IS differential gene expression, and it is why specification of cell fate is fundamentally a matter of gene regulation.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes. Cells become progressively more restricted (potency decreases) as embryogenesis proceeds.

**2.** No. Cell movement does not occur in plant morphogenesis (cells are held by walls); plants use oriented division and expansion.

**3.** Yes. DNA is replicated at every division, so differentiated cells carry essentially the same genome — they differ in gene expression, not gene content.

**4.** Yes. Fate depends on position; position-dependent instructive cues (cytoplasmic factors and signalling molecules) determine fate.

**5.** No. House-keeping genes are expressed in (nearly) all cells; it is cell-specific genes that are restricted to a few types.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** In animals (fly), early morphogenesis features cell movement, cell adhesion/de-adhesion (to build tissues), cell-shape change, and divisions that are relatively more random (though can be oriented). In plants (Arabidopsis) there is essentially NO cell movement and NO adhesion/de-adhesion (walls fix cells in place); morphogenesis depends on tightly ORIENTED cell divisions plus directional expansion. Both still divide and change shape.

**2.** Animals establish an anterior/posterior axis and form segments along the body. Plants establish an apical/basal axis (the shoot-root axis) and, in addition, a radial axis giving concentric tissue layers (dermal, ground, vascular).

**3.** Determinate growth: a predetermined body form in which all organ/tissue types are formed during embryogenesis and the organism then mainly increases in SIZE — typical of animals. Indeterminate growth: a flexible body form in which only a few organs form in the embryo and new organs/tissues are produced CONTINUOUSLY throughout life — typical of plants, driven by the ongoing activity of meristems.

**4.** A master regulator (homeotic) gene controls the expression of many downstream genes, so it can switch on an entire genetic program in a field of cells. Animal example: Drosophila bithorax mutation transforms one segment so an extra pair of wings forms. Plant example: Arabidopsis AGAMOUS (AG) mutation changes floral organ identity. In each, altering one regulatory gene changes a whole structure.

**5.** Because a master regulator sits at the top of a genetic program, a SMALL change in that one gene can switch an entire downstream program on or off, producing a LARGE, discrete change in form (a “hopeful monster”). This shows that big phenotypic jumps need not require many simultaneous mutations — modest regulatory changes feed into ordinary gradual evolution, with natural selection then retaining the rare variants that produce viable, successful bodies.

**1.** Yes. Both build specific cell types in ordered, non-random patterns from a zygote — they have much in common.

**2.** Yes. Indeterminate growth means meristems keep producing new organs and tissues throughout the plant's life.

**3.** No. Animals show determinate growth: organ types are set in the embryo, and the animal mainly increases in size rather than adding new limbs.

**4.** Yes. A single master regulator mutation (e.g. bithorax) can transform a structure, producing an extra pair of wings.

**5.** No. Secondary (radial) growth is unique to SEED plants; mosses and liverworts do not have it.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Step 1 (zygote → two-cell): an asymmetric division sets the apical-basal axis — apical daughter → embryo, basal daughter → suspensor. Step 2 (octant stage): oriented divisions form a spherical embryo on top of a linear suspensor. Step 3 (heart stage): the embryo becomes bilaterally symmetrical (cotyledon outgrowth), the radial pattern of tissue types is set, and the shoot + root meristems form (this is the morphogenesis-rich step). Step 4 (torpedo stage → mature seed): cotyledons and main axis elongate, tissues differentiate, then extensive division/expansion/differentiation prepare the seed for dormancy.

**2.** The zygote divides ASYMMETRICALLY, producing a small apical daughter cell and a larger basal daughter cell. This asymmetry defines the apical-basal axis of the future plant. The apical daughter cell gives rise to the EMBRYO proper (shoot end); the basal daughter cell gives rise mainly to the SUSPENSOR (which anchors the embryo and channels nutrients).

**3.** Water enters the cell (down its water-potential gradient) and the vacuole expands; this raises turgor pressure inside the cell. The semi-rigid wall initially RESISTS the increase. The high turgor then triggers the release of wall-loosening enzymes that soften the wall, allowing it to yield; the cell then expands (its direction governed by wall/microfibril orientation). Turgor provides the force; wall-loosening permits the expansion.

**4.** Primary growth increases the HEIGHT/length of the plant and is produced by the apical meristems (shoot and root). Secondary growth increases the GIRTH/thickness and is produced by the lateral meristems (vascular cambium and cork cambium). Apical = elongation; lateral = thickening.

**5.** Each apical meristem produces three primary meristems that mature into three tissue systems: protoderm → dermal tissue (outer protective layer); ground meristem → ground tissue; procambium → vascular tissue (xylem and phloem). So the apical meristem is the source, the primary meristems are partially-committed intermediates, and the tissue systems are the differentiated end products.

### Part D — Applied

**1.** (1) Turgor / water potential. (a) Ψ = Ψs + Ψp = −0.9 + 0 = −0.9 MPa. Soil water is −0.2 MPa, which is HIGHER (less negative) than the cell, so water enters the cell (water moves toward more negative Ψ). (b) New Ψ = −0.9 + 0.5 = −0.4 MPa. Soil (−0.2) is still higher than the cell (−0.4), so there is STILL a net driving force for water entry, though it is now smaller. (c) As water enters, turgor (Ψp) rises and pushes outward on the wall; the high turgor triggers wall-loosening enzymes that soften the wall so it can yield, and the turgor force then drives irreversible ELONGATION. Without wall loosening, turgor would simply build up and the cell could not grow.

**2.** (2) Zone of elongation. (a) The gaps that widen MOST lie a short distance behind the tip — the zone of elongation (cells lengthening). (b) Gaps right at the tip (near the root cap), in the zone of cell division, widen little because here cells are dividing and staying small rather than elongating. (c) Independent variable = position/distance from the root tip (or time); dependent variable = the width of each gap (amount of elongation). (d) Because only the marks in one specific region spread apart, the experiment shows root lengthening comes from cell ELONGATION concentrated in a defined zone, not from uniform expansion along the whole root.

**3.** (3) Secondary growth / rings. (a) The vascular cambium produces the wood (secondary xylem) on its inner side and secondary phloem on its OUTER side. (b) ≈ 38 years old; the estimate assumes exactly one ring is formed per year (one growing season), which holds for typical temperate trees but can fail where growth is not strictly annual. (c) Spring wood has larger, wider-lumen xylem cells (fast growth, high water demand) while summer wood has smaller, denser cells (slower growth) — the contrast makes each annual ring visible. (d) Secondary growth continually adds new layers of strong, lignified secondary xylem around the trunk, thickening it so it can mechanically support great height and weight; primary growth alone only elongates and could not provide the structural girth needed for tall trees.

**Common traps to avoid (Chapter 34):**

* Determination (commitment, often invisible) comes BEFORE differentiation (visible specialisation) — don't treat them as the same thing.
* All body cells share the SAME genome; differences come from differential gene EXPRESSION, not from losing genes.
* Cell MOVEMENT and cell ADHESION do NOT occur in plant morphogenesis (walls fix cells in place); plants use oriented division + expansion.
* Animals = determinate growth (organs made in embryo, then grow in size). Plants = indeterminate (continuous new organs via meristems).
* PRIMARY growth = length (apical meristems). SECONDARY growth = girth (lateral meristems: vascular + cork cambium); secondary growth is unique to SEED plants.
* Vascular cambium: secondary XYLEM on the inside, secondary PHLOEM on the outside. Cork cambium makes the protective “bark”.
* Apical daughter cell → embryo; basal daughter cell → suspensor.
* Water moves toward MORE NEGATIVE water potential; Ψ = Ψs + Ψp, and turgor (Ψp) plus wall-loosening together drive cell expansion.
