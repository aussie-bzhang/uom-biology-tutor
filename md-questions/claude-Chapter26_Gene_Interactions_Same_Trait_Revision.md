**Biology Revision — Chapter 26**

**Genetic Analysis Involving Interactions Between Loci Affecting the Same Trait**

*Topic 3: Genes and Heredity · Week 9, Lecture 26 (A/Prof Hayley Bugeja)*

This revision pack contains three independent question sets. Each set has 5 short-answer questions, 5 Yes/No questions, 10 multiple-choice questions (MCQ), and 3 applied questions (experiment / calculation / data analysis). A full answer key for all three sets is provided at the end of the document.

**Key idea for this chapter:** A single trait can be controlled by more than one gene. When two genes interact, the standard dihybrid F2 ratio of **9:3:3:1** is modified. If the two genes make **independent / additive** contributions, four distinct (sometimes novel) phenotypes appear and the ratio stays **9:3:3:1** (e.g. chicken combs walnut/rose/pea/single; dog black/brown/blue/silver). If one locus **masks** the other — **epistasis** — classes collapse, e.g. recessive epistasis gives **9:3:4**. The locus that masks is epistatic; in a linear pathway the gene acting upstream is usually epistatic.

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** A polygenic trait is influenced by two or more gene loci. Two different genes can affect the same characteristic because their products act in the same developmental process or biochemical pathway (e.g. one makes a pigment, another distributes it; or one signals while another responds).

**2.** R and P each contribute to comb shape. When both dominant alleles are present (R– P–) their combined action produces a NEW phenotype, walnut; R alone (R– pp) gives rose; P alone (rr P–) gives pea; neither (rr pp) gives single. The interaction therefore generates two phenotypes (walnut, single) not seen in the pure-breeding parents.

**3.** Additive interaction: the independent contributions of two genes combine to produce the phenotype. R present, no P → rose; P present, no R → pea; both R and P → walnut; neither → single comb.

**4.** A modifier gene alters the phenotypic effect of another gene. In dogs the B/b locus determines the TYPE of pigment (black dominant, brown recessive); the D/d locus is a modifier that determines the DISTRIBUTION/density of pigment (dense D– vs dilute dd).

**5.** Two genes are segregating. They assort independently — shown by the 9:3:3:1 F2 ratio (four distinct phenotypes in a 9:3:3:1 proportion), which is the signature of two independently assorting loci.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** Epistasis is when the phenotype produced by the genotype at one locus depends on the genotype at a different locus — one gene masks the effect of another. The masking locus is epistatic; the masked locus is hypostatic.

**2.** In recessive epistasis, the homozygous-recessive genotype at the epistatic locus produces the same phenotype regardless of the second locus. So two of the standard four classes (e.g. the '3' and the '1' that share the epistatic recessive genotype) become phenotypically identical and merge, turning 9:3:3:1 into 9:3:4 (or 9:4:3).

**3.** Wng protein (from wg+) is an upstream signalling glycoprotein needed to activate the Vg master regulator. If wa/wa (no functional Wng), the pathway fails and wings cannot develop — wings are absent whatever the vg genotype. So wa/wa is epistatic: both wa/wa vg+/– and wa/wa vg/vg are wingless, merging the two classes to give 9 wild-type : 4 wingless : 3 vestigial.

**4.** LFY confers flower identity, which must occur BEFORE AG can differentiate flower parts. Without LFY (lfy/lfy), no flower identity is established, so no flower forms regardless of AG — lfy is therefore epistatic to AG.

**5.** The E/e locus controls whether ANY colour is expressed. ee = no colour expressed = cream, regardless of whether the B/b locus codes for black or brown pigment. Because ee overrides/masks the pigment-colour locus, ee is epistatic to B/b.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short-answer

**1.** (i) Express the F2 ratio out of 16: a ratio summing to 16 (e.g. 9:3:3:1, 9:3:4, 9:7) signals two genes. (ii) Compare with the standard 9:3:3:1: an exact 9:3:3:1 with four distinct phenotypes = independent/additive interaction; a collapsed ratio (9:3:4, 9:7, 12:3:1, 15:1) = the genes interact (epistasis).

**2.** 9:3:3:1 = two genes assorting independently with each genotype class giving its own phenotype (additive/modifier interaction). 9:3:4 = two genes assorting independently but with recessive epistasis, where one recessive genotype masks the other locus so two classes merge.

**3.** All the examples are dihybrid (two heterozygous loci) self-crosses. The 4×4 Punnett square gives 16 equally likely genotype boxes that always group as 9 (both dominant) : 3 : 3 : 1 (both recessive); gene interaction then determines how many of these groups are visibly distinct.

**4.** (1) Additive/modifier interaction keeps a 9:3:3:1 phenotype ratio — e.g. chicken combs (walnut/rose/pea/single) or dog coat (black/blue/brown/silver). (2) Epistasis modifies the ratio (e.g. to 9:3:4) — e.g. Drosophila wings (wingless epistatic) or dog black/brown/cream (ee epistatic).

**5.** When two genes interact to affect one trait, the expected dihybrid ratio is altered: additive interactions preserve 9:3:3:1, whereas epistatic interactions merge classes and produce modified ratios such as 9:3:4.

**1.** Yes.

**2.** Yes.

**3.** Yes — the two classes sharing the epistatic recessive genotype merge.

**4.** No — a single gene gives 3:1 (or 1:2:1), never 9:3:3:1.

**5.** Yes.
