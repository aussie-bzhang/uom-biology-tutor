# Answer Key – Set 1

### Part A – Short-answer

1. The nucleus is bounded by a double membrane (the nuclear envelope), which is perforated by nuclear (annular) pores (~50 nm) that control the movement of molecules in and out. Inside is the nucleolus, a sub-region where ribosomal genes are transcribed (ribosomal biogenesis). The nuclear envelope is continuous with the endoplasmic reticulum. Functions (any two): stores most of the cell's DNA, is the site of DNA replication, and is where gene transcription is switched on or off; mRNA made here leaves via the pores to be translated in the cytoplasm.
2. A chromosome is a long DNA molecule packaged with proteins. Chromatin is the complex of DNA and proteins; the proteins that the DNA wraps around are histones. Different species have different, characteristic numbers of chromosomes.
3. A mitochondrion has two membranes: a smooth outer membrane and a highly convoluted inner membrane whose inward folds are called cristae. The fluid interior is the matrix, which contains DNA and ribosomes. Its main function is aerobic (cellular) respiration, transforming the energy in fuels such as glucose into ATP. Cells that need a lot of energy have many mitochondria because more cristae/membrane surface and more organelles allow more ATP to be produced.
4. A chloroplast has a double membrane. The inner membrane forms flattened sacs called thylakoids, which contain chlorophyll and other light-harvesting pigments; stacks of thylakoids are grana, and the surrounding fluid is the stroma. Its main function is photosynthesis – converting light energy into chemical energy.
5. The first prokaryotes appeared about 3.7 billion years ago and the first eukaryotes about 2.1 billion years ago (a gap of roughly 1.5 billion years). The Grypania spiralis fossil (~2.1 billion years old), a distinctive spirally-coiled photosynthetic “alga”, provides evidence that eukaryotes existed by about 2.1 billion years ago.

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. The nuclear envelope is a double membrane.
2. Yes. The nucleolus carries out ribosomal biogenesis.
3. No. Mitochondria have two (double) membranes.
4. No. They are called cristae; thylakoids are found in chloroplasts.
5. Yes. Prokaryotes ~3.7 bya, eukaryotes ~2.1 bya.

### Part D – Applied / Reasoning

1. (a) The nucleus is thought to have formed from invaginations of the plasma membrane around the nucleoid (DNA) of an ancient prokaryote. This is supported by the nuclear envelope being continuous with the ER – i.e. it is part of a membrane system derived from the cell's own membrane.

(b) Mitochondria and chloroplasts are thought to have arisen by (primary) endosymbiosis: the engulfing of a purple bacterium (mitochondria) and a cyanobacterium (chloroplasts).

(c) Mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria, so they kept their own DNA and ribosomes. The nucleus, by contrast, formed from the host cell's own membrane folding around DNA that was already there – no separate organism was engulfed – so there is no extra bacterial genome.

# Answer Key – Set 2

### Part A – Short-answer

1. Endosymbiosis is when one organism (the endosymbiont) lives inside another (the host). In primary endosymbiosis: a free-living bacterium is taken into a host eukaryote by endocytosis and surrounded by a food (phagocytic) vacuole; it is not digested but persists inside the cell; over time the outer (vacuole) membrane breaks down, leaving an endosymbiont with its original two membranes; and much of its genetic information is transferred to the host nucleus.
2. Mitochondria arose from a purple bacterium; chloroplasts arose from a photosynthetic cyanobacterium. “A single event” means each happened once in evolutionary history – the one mitochondrial event gave rise to the mitochondria of all eukaryotes, and the one chloroplast (primary) event gave rise to the chloroplasts of all algae and land plants.
3. Any four: (1) they look morphologically similar to bacteria; (2) they have a double membrane (outer like a cell membrane, inner folded into cristae/lamellae); (3) they are semi-autonomous and retain their own genome (DNA/RNA); (4) they have their own protein-synthesis machinery, including ribosomes; (5) their metabolism resembles living prokaryotes (cyanobacteria / purple bacteria); (6) some plastids (e.g. Cyanophora) still have a bacterial peptidoglycan wall between the membranes.
4. They are “semi-autonomous” because they retain their own DNA and ribosomes and can replicate/divide independently of the nucleus, making some of their own proteins – but they are not fully independent, since much of their genetic information has been transferred to the nucleus and they rely on the cell for many components.
5. Peptidoglycan is a component of bacterial cell walls (not found in eukaryotic organelles that arose from the host). Its retention between the plastid's two membranes shows that the plastid descended from a (cyano)bacterium – a direct relic of its bacterial ancestry.

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. Mitochondria arose from a purple bacterium.
2. Yes. Chloroplasts arose from a cyanobacterium.
3. No. They retain their own genome (semi-autonomous).
4. No. The bacterium is not eaten; it persists as an endosymbiont.
5. Yes. Both are recognised pieces of evidence.

### Part D – Applied / Reasoning

1. (a) They were once free-living bacteria, so they kept their own (bacteria-like) genome and protein-making machinery – strong evidence for an endosymbiotic origin.

(b) The inner membrane is the original bacterial plasma membrane, while the outer membrane derives from the host's vacuole/cell membrane during engulfment – consistent with the bacterium having been taken inside.

(c) Their close metabolic resemblance to specific living prokaryotes (cyanobacteria; purple bacteria) points to those bacteria as their ancestors.

(d) Peptidoglycan is a bacterial cell-wall material; its retention shows the plastid is descended from a bacterium (cyanobacterium).

# Answer Key – Set 3

### Part A – Short-answer

1. In primary endosymbiosis a eukaryote engulfs a prokaryote (a cyanobacterium or purple bacterium). In secondary endosymbiosis a eukaryote engulfs another eukaryotic cell that already contains a chloroplast (i.e. an alga). So the engulfed cell in secondary endosymbiosis is a eukaryote, not a prokaryote.
2. In primary endosymbiosis the engulfed bacterium keeps its original two membranes (inner = bacterial membrane; outer = from the host vacuole), so the chloroplast has two membranes. In secondary endosymbiosis a whole eukaryotic alga (which already has a two-membrane chloroplast) is engulfed, adding the alga's plasma membrane and the host's food-vacuole membrane. If the food-vacuole membrane breaks down the plastid ends up with three membranes; if it does not, it has four.
3. Examples: euglenoids, dinoflagellates and haptophytes. They are called “protistan pirates” because they did not evolve chloroplasts themselves but acquired (“stole”) them by engulfing other chloroplast-containing algae.
4. The host nucleus can contain genetic material from three sources: nuclear (the host's own), mitochondrial, and chloroplastic. These arise because, over evolutionary time, genes from the mitochondrion and the chloroplast (and from the engulfed alga) are transferred into the host nucleus.
5. A nucleomorph is the small, remnant nucleus of the engulfed eukaryotic alga that has not been completely lost. It is found in cryptomonads (and is a tell-tale sign of secondary endosymbiosis).

### Part B – Yes / No

1. Yes. A eukaryote engulfs another chloroplast-containing eukaryote.
2. No. A primary-endosymbiotic chloroplast has two membranes.
3. Yes. Secondary endosymbiosis gives 3 or 4 membranes.
4. Yes. The nucleomorph is the relic nucleus, seen in cryptomonads.
5. Yes. Gene transfer to the host nucleus occurs over time.

### Part D – Applied / Reasoning

1. (a) Two membranes. The inner membrane is the original bacterial (cyanobacterial) plasma membrane; the outer membrane comes from the host's vacuole/plasma membrane during engulfment.

(b) (i) If the food-vacuole membrane breaks down: three membranes. (ii) If it does not break down: four membranes.

(c) Expected genome sources: the host (pirate) nuclear genome, the host mitochondrial genome, and the plastid (chloroplastic) genome – plus, possibly, a nucleomorph (the remnant nucleus of the engulfed alga). A nucleomorph is present if the engulfed eukaryote's nucleus has not been fully lost or transferred, as in cryptomonads.

(d) Each genome resembles that of its ancestral organism (e.g. the chloroplast genome resembles cyanobacteria). By comparing the sequences, scientists can identify which organisms the organelles descended from and reconstruct the history of the engulfment events.
