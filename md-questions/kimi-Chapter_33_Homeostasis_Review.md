## Problem 1 Answers

**(a)(i)** Rate = (9.5 − 5.0) / 30 = 4.5 / 30 = 0.15 mmol·L⁻¹·min⁻¹ (BGL rising).
**(a)(ii)** Rate = (5.2 − 9.5) / 60 = (−4.3) / 60 ≈ −0.072 mmol·L⁻¹·min⁻¹ (BGL falling). The negative sign indicates restoration toward the set point.

**(b)** Negative feedback loop:
**• Receptor:** β-islet cells (glucose-sensing ion channels/Ca²⁺ signalling) detect BGL > 6.5 mmol/L.
**• Control centre:** The β-islet cell itself (or the pancreatic islet as an integrated unit) processes the signal and triggers insulin vesicle exocytosis.
**• Effector:** Liver cells (hepatocytes), muscle cells, and adipocytes respond to insulin by increasing GLUT4 translocation and glycogen synthesis, thereby removing glucose from blood.
**• Response:** BGL decreases back toward the 4–8 mmol/L set point; the stimulus is thereby diminished, halting further insulin secretion.

**(c)** Experimental design:
**• Null hypothesis (H₀):** Insulin treatment does not change glycogen synthase activity in cultured hepatocytes compared to untreated controls.
**• Groups:** (1) Control — hepatocytes in glucose-free DMEM + vehicle (buffer only); (2) Experimental — same medium + 100 nM insulin for 15 min.
**• Assay:** Radioactive incorporation assay: measure [¹⁴C]-UDP-glucose incorporation into glycogen over 30 min, or use a phospho-specific antibody against the active (dephosphorylated) form of glycogen synthase in Western blot.
**• Confounding variable:** Baseline glucose concentration in the medium must be identical in both groups (e.g., 5.5 mM), because high glucose alone can allosterically activate glycogen synthase independent of insulin.

## Problem 2 Answers

**(a)** Percentage reductions (relative to control):
• Stomatal aperture: (8.2 − 2.1) / 8.2 × 100 = 74.4 % reduction.
• Transpiration rate: (12.5 − 3.8) / 12.5 × 100 = 69.6 % reduction.

**(b)** Necessity & sufficiency argument:
**• Necessity:** The ABA inhibitor partially rescues stomatal aperture (6.5 μm vs. 2.1 μm) and transpiration (9.1 vs. 3.8), showing that without ABA biosynthesis, closure is greatly attenuated. This demonstrates ABA is necessary.
**• Sufficiency:** The data do not directly test sufficiency (exogenous ABA application alone is not shown). However, the near-control ABA level (52 ng·g⁻¹) in the inhibitor-treated group correlates with near-control stomatal behaviour, suggesting ABA levels are strongly predictive. To prove sufficiency, one would need to show that spraying well-watered plants with exogenous ABA induces stomatal closure.

**(c)** Osmolarity calculation:
• K⁺ remaining: 150 × 0.60 = 90 mM.
• Cl⁻ remaining: 120 × 0.60 = 72 mM.
• Total osmolarity from K⁺ + Cl⁻ = 90 + 72 = 162 mOsm·L⁻¹ (initial was 270 mOsm·L⁻¹).
**Mechanism:** The drop in intracellular solute concentration lowers the osmotic potential (raises water potential) inside the guard cells. Water moves passively by osmosis (and aquaporins) out of the guard cells into the adjacent epidermal cells. Loss of turgor pressure causes the guard cells to become flaccid, and the stomatal pore closes.

## Problem 3 Answers

**(a)** Feedback loops:

**Scenario A (Positive feedback):**• Stimulus: Baby suckling → mechanical stretch of nipple.
• Receptor: Sensory nerve endings in the nipple/areola.
• Control centre: Hypothalamus (oxytocinergic neurons in the paraventricular and supraoptic nuclei).
• Effector: Myoepithelial cells (smooth muscle) surrounding milk ducts.
• Response: Milk ejection (let-down); baby feeds more strongly, amplifying the initial stimulus.

**Scenario B (Negative feedback):**• Stimulus: Core temperature rises to 39.2 °C (deviation above 37 °C set point).
• Receptor: Warm-sensitive thermoreceptors in the preoptic anterior hypothalamus.
• Control centre: Hypothalamic thermoregulatory centre.
• Effector: Blood vessels (vasodilation) and sweat glands (sweating).
• Response: Heat loss increases; temperature falls back toward 37 °C, thereby reducing the stimulus.

**(b)** Classification rationale:
• Scenario A is positive feedback because the response (milk ejection) intensifies the original stimulus (suckling). The loop is self-reinforcing, driving the system away from the initial state until an external limit (empty breast or satiated baby) terminates it. It does not maintain a set point; rather, it produces a rapid, transient output.
• Scenario B is negative feedback because the response (heat loss) opposes the initial stimulus (temperature rise). The hypothalamus compares actual temperature to the 37 °C set point; the greater the deviation, the stronger the corrective response. Once the set point is re-approached, effector activity is dampened, maintaining homeostasis.

**(c)** Evolutionary rationale: Positive feedback generates an all-or-none, rapid output that is energetically efficient for brief, critical events. In labour, a transient oxytocin burst ensures complete uterine contraction and delivery within hours; a slow negative-feedback system would risk prolonged, incomplete contractions, endangering mother and foetus. Similarly, lactation requires a pulsed ejection reflex to match infant demand dynamically.

## Problem 4 Answers

**(a)** Predictions for non-phosphorylating insulin analogue:
**• (i) Glucose uptake:** Greatly reduced (basal level only). Autophosphorylation of the RTK intracellular domain is the first step in the signalling cascade. Without it, IRS-1/IRS-2 cannot be recruited, PI3K is not activated, and Akt-mediated translocation of GLUT4 vesicles to the membrane fails. The signal is blocked at the very first step, so no amplification occurs.
**• (ii) Glycogen synthesis:** Greatly reduced. Glycogen synthase kinase-3 (GSK3) remains active (not phosphorylated/inhibited by Akt), keeping glycogen synthase in its inactive, phosphorylated state. The enzymatic amplification cascade (one activated receptor → many PI3K molecules → many Akt → many GSK3 targets) is absent.

**(b)** Expected changes with exogenous testosterone misuse:
• GnRH pulse frequency: ↘ (negative feedback on hypothalamus).
• LH secretion: ↘ (less GnRH stimulation + direct inhibition of gonadotropes).
• Leydig cell activity: ↘ (deprived of LH stimulation).
• Endogenous testosterone production: ↘ (Leydig cells atrophy from disuse).
• Spermatogenesis: ↘ (reduced FSH and intratesticular testosterone; Sertoli cells lose support).

**(c)** Molecular weight calculation:
• 29 amino-acid residues: 29 × 110 Da = 3,190 Da.
• However, a peptide of n residues has (n − 1) peptide bonds, each losing one water molecule (18 Da). Water lost = 28 × 18 = 504 Da.
• Net MW ≈ 3,190 − 504 = 2,686 Da (literature value for glucagon ≈ 3,483 Da including full side-chain masses; the 110 Da/residue approximation gives a rough estimate). Accept any answer showing the (n − 1) × 18 correction.

## Problem 5 Answers

**(a)** Cross-kingdom comparison:

**Feature Animal (Insulin/Glucagon) Plant (ABA)**Transport Endocrine (blood/capillary) Paracrine/diffusion in apoplast; also vascular (xylem)
Receptor location Extracellular (RTK for insulin); GPCR for glucagon Both extra- and intracellular (ABA receptors on plasma membrane and cytosolic PYR/PYL/RCAR family)
Speed Minutes (fast, e.g., GLUT4 translocation within 10–30 min) Minutes to hours (stomatal closure within 10–30 min; gene expression over hours)
Effectors Ion channels (Ca²⁺), kinases (Akt, PKA), metabolic enzymes Ion channels (SLAC1 anion channel, K⁺ channels), SnRK2 kinases, transcription factors (AREB/ABF)

Key similarity: Both use phosphorylation cascades and second messengers (Ca²⁺, cAMP in animals; Ca²⁺, ROS, NO in plants). Key difference: Animal hormones travel systemically via dedicated circulatory system; plant hormones rely more on cell-to-cell diffusion and bulk flow in xylem.

**(b)** Genetic engineering predictions:
**• Overexpression of SnRK2:** INCREASED drought tolerance. SnRK2 is a positive regulator of ABA signalling. Constitutively active SnRK2 would phosphorylate and activate SLAC1 anion channels and downstream transcription factors even at low ABA levels, causing rapid stomatal closure and expression of drought-response genes.
**• Overexpression of PP2C:** DECREASED drought tolerance. PP2C is a negative regulator that dephosphorylates and inactivates SnRK2. More PP2C would suppress the ABA signal, keeping stomata open during water stress and preventing drought-adaptive gene expression.

**(c)** Type 1 diabetes consequences:
**• BGL at 09:30:** Likely > 15–20 mmol/L (severe hyperglycaemia). Without insulin, GLUT4 remains intracellular; glucose cannot enter muscle/adipose cells. Hepatocytes continue glucose output (glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis) unopposed. The negative feedback loop is broken because the effector (insulin) is absent.
**• Urine osmolarity:** INCREASED. When blood glucose exceeds the renal transport maximum (~11 mmol/L), glucose appears in the filtrate. It acts as an osmotic diuretic, preventing water reabsorption in the proximal tubule and collecting duct. The urine becomes hyperosmotic and voluminous (osmotic diuresis/polyuria).
**• Long-term retinal risk:** Chronic hyperglycaemia leads to non-enzymatic glycation of proteins and lipids, forming advanced glycation end-products (AGEs). AGEs bind to RAGE receptors on retinal pericytes and endothelial cells, triggering oxidative stress, inflammation, and vascular leakage (diabetic retinopathy). This is a direct consequence of failed glucose homeostasis.
