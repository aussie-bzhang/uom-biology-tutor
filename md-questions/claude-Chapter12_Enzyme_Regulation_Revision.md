# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Enzyme Inhibitors

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** (i) Regulation of gene expression — controlling how many enzyme molecules are made; (ii) Regulation of enzyme activity — the existing enzyme changes shape or is blocked by regulators (inhibitors/activators).

**2.** A competitive inhibitor binds at the active site, competing with the substrate. While the inhibitor occupies the active site, the substrate cannot bind, so fewer enzyme-substrate complexes form and the rate falls.

**3.** A noncompetitive inhibitor binds at a different site (away from the active site). This changes the shape of the enzyme so that, even if substrate can still bind, the active site functions less effectively, lowering the rate.

**4.** Competitive: inhibitor and substrate compete for the same site, so flooding the enzyme with substrate out-competes the inhibitor and restores activity. Noncompetitive: the inhibitor binds a separate site and distorts the active site; extra substrate cannot displace it, so adding substrate does not reverse the inhibition (it is reversed only by chemically removing the inhibitor).

**5.** Irreversible inhibition: the inhibitor forms a covalent bond with a side chain (often in the active site) and permanently inactivates the enzyme. Examples from the lecture: Sarin bonding to serine in acetylcholinesterase; aspirin acetylating cyclooxygenase (COX).

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — they resemble the substrate, which is why they fit the active site.

**2.** No — it binds at a separate (non-active) site.

**3.** Yes — adding more substrate out-competes a competitive inhibitor.

**4.** Yes — irreversible inhibitors form covalent bonds with the enzyme.

**5.** No — a noncompetitive inhibitor decreases Vmax (it does not increase it).

### Part C. Multiple-choice

**1.** B

**2.** B

**3.** A — competitive: Km increased, Vmax unchanged.

**4.** B — noncompetitive: Km unchanged, Vmax decreased.

**5.** B

**6.** C

**7.** C — covalent bond = irreversible inhibition.

**8.** B

**9.** C

**10.** B — adding a competitive inhibitor lowers (not raises) the rate; the other three all increase it.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) Competitive — oxaloacetate closely resembles the substrate succinate (both four-carbon dicarboxylic acids), so it competes for the same site. (b) It binds at the active site of succinate dehydrogenase. (c) Reverse it by adding more substrate (succinate), which out-competes the inhibitor.

**2.** (a) Competitive inhibitor: apparent Km increases, Vmax unchanged. (b) Noncompetitive inhibitor: Km unchanged, Vmax decreases. (c) Excess substrate restores the original Vmax for the competitive inhibitor (it is overcome by substrate) but NOT for the noncompetitive inhibitor (Vmax stays lowered).

**3.** (i) Competitive — substrate-shaped molecule occupying the active site. (ii) Noncompetitive — binds away from the active site and distorts the protein/active site (reversible, non-covalent). (iii) Irreversible — covalent, permanent blockage of a catalytic residue.

## Set 2 — Allosteric Regulation and Cooperativity

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** Allosteric regulation: a non-substrate molecule binds the enzyme at a site different from the active site (an “other site”), which changes the enzyme’s shape and therefore its activity.

**2.** Active form — has the proper shape to bind substrate (catalysis can occur). Inactive form — the shape is altered so the active site cannot bind substrate (no catalysis).

**3.** The active site lies on the catalytic subunit. Inhibitors and activators bind to separate polypeptides called regulatory subunits at regulatory (allosteric) sites. Most allosteric enzymes have quaternary structure (multiple subunits).

**4.** An activator binds a regulatory site and stabilises the active conformation, promoting substrate binding; an inhibitor binds a regulatory site and stabilises the inactive conformation, preventing effective substrate binding. They push the enzyme toward opposite shapes.

**5.** Cooperativity: binding of one substrate molecule to the active site of one subunit locks (or favours) the active conformation in all subunits, so substrate binding by one subunit increases the affinity/activity of the others. It is another form of allosteric activation.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — the regulator binds away from the active site.

**2.** Yes (True) — they have at least an active site plus one or more regulatory sites.

**3.** No — an activator stabilises the ACTIVE form (an inhibitor stabilises the inactive form).

**4.** Yes — that is cooperativity.

**5.** Yes — at low concentrations regulators dissociate and the enzyme can oscillate again.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** True. Allosteric enzymes have at least two kinds of binding site: the active site (on the catalytic subunit, where substrate binds) and one or more regulatory/allosteric sites (where activators or inhibitors bind). Having both sites is what allows regulation separate from catalysis.

**2.** (a) Activator binding stabilises the active form: the subunits adopt the shape with functional active sites, so substrate binds and catalysis proceeds (rate increases). (b) Inhibitor binding stabilises the inactive form: the active sites are distorted/non-functional, so substrate cannot bind effectively and the rate falls.

## Set 3 — Metabolic Regulation and Feedback Inhibition

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** Feedback inhibition: the end product of a pathway inhibits the first enzyme specific to its own synthesis. As the product accumulates it shuts down its own production, limiting its concentration and preventing wasteful overproduction. It is a form of negative regulation.

**2.** The end product isoleucine inhibits threonine deaminase (enzyme 1, the first enzyme specific to the pathway). Isoleucine binds at an allosteric site, not the active site, switching the enzyme to its inactive form so threonine is no longer converted to intermediate A.

**3.** PFK is stimulated (activated) by AMP/ADP and inhibited by ATP and by citrate. High ATP/citrate signals that energy and biosynthetic precursors are plentiful, so glycolysis slows; high AMP/ADP signals low energy, so glycolysis speeds up. This adjusts the rate of respiration to the cell’s needs.

**4.** Inhibiting the first committed (specific) enzyme stops the whole branch early, so no intermediates are wasted by being made and then left unused. Inhibiting a later step would still allow accumulation of useless intermediates upstream.

**5.** Negative feedback (feedback inhibition): an end product slows an earlier enzyme, e.g. isoleucine inhibiting threonine deaminase, or ATP/citrate inhibiting PFK. Positive regulation (activation): a molecule speeds an enzyme, e.g. AMP/ADP activating PFK; an excess product of one pathway can also activate an enzyme in another pathway to divert raw materials.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — it is negative regulation.

**2.** Yes — the end product inhibits an early (first committed) enzyme.

**3.** Yes — AMP stimulates PFK.

**4.** Yes — ATP inhibits PFK.

**5.** Yes — an excess product can allosterically activate an enzyme in another pathway and divert raw materials.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** Diagram (described): L → M → O → P → Q, with L also → N, and O also → R → S. Feedback inhibition (red, minus): O ⊣ (L→M); Q ⊣ (O→P); S ⊣ (O→R). Layout: L branches to M and N; M→O; O branches to P and R; P→Q; R→S.

L ──► M ──► O ──► P ──► Q

│ │ └──► R ──► S

└──► N

Inhibition (red, −): O ⊣ L→M ; Q ⊣ O→P ; S ⊣ O→R

**.** Answer to the question: If both Q and S are present in HIGH concentrations, they inhibit BOTH branches out of O (O→P is blocked by Q; O→R is blocked by S). With O unable to be used to make P or R, O accumulates. High O then inhibits the L→M reaction (O ⊣ L→M). Because the L→M branch is shut down, the only remaining route for L is L → N. Therefore the reaction L → N would prevail.

**2.** Glycolysis will SLOW DOWN at the phosphofructokinase step. High ATP signals abundant energy and high citrate signals the citric acid cycle is well supplied; both allosterically inhibit PFK. This is feedback regulation: when energy is plentiful, the cell turns down the rate of glucose breakdown to avoid wasteful over-production of ATP and intermediates.
