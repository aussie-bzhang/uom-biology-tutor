# **Question 1 Answers: DNA Sequence Analysis & Mutation Identification**

**a) Mutation identification:**

* • Mutant A: C→T transition at the last position of the stop codon (TAA → TGA). Both TAA and TGA are STOP codons.
* • Mutant B: C→A transversion at the second position of the last codon (GAC → GAA). This changes Asp (GAC) → Glu (GAA).
* • Mutant C: A→T transversion at the first position of the stop codon (TAA → TTA). This changes STOP → Leu, resulting in read-through.

**b) Polypeptide sequences:**

* • Mutant A: Met-Ala-Tyr-Gly-Pro-Asn-Cys-Leu-Asp-STOP (identical to wild-type)
* • Mutant B: Met-Ala-Tyr-Gly-Pro-Asn-Cys-Leu-Glu-STOP (Asp→Glu at position 9)
* • Mutant C: Met-Ala-Tyr-Gly-Pro-Asn-Cys-Leu-Asp-Leu-... (read-through; polypeptide continues until next in-frame STOP)

**c) Classification:**

* • Mutant A: Silent mutation (DNA change but no amino acid change; both TAA and TGA are stop codons).
* • Mutant B: Missense mutation (single amino acid substitution: Asp→Glu). Both are acidic amino acids, so this is a conservative change.
* • Mutant C: Nonsense mutation (if considering loss of stop codon as "non-stop" or read-through). More precisely, this is a stop-codon mutation leading to C-terminal extension.

**d) Functional consequences:**

* • Mutant A: No effect on enzyme activity. The polypeptide is identical; the stop codon change is neutral.
* • Mutant B: Likely no effect or minimal effect. Asp→Glu is a conservative substitution (both acidic, similar size and charge). The enzyme active site may tolerate this change if position 9 is not critical.
* • Mutant C: Loss-of-function. The C-terminal extension alters protein folding, stability, and subcellular localization. The aberrant tail may expose hydrophobic regions or remove targeting signals, leading to degradation or mislocalization.

# **Question 2 Answers: Mutation Rate Calculation & Experimental Design**

**a) Method of the median calculation:**

* P₀ = number of cultures with zero mutants / total cultures = 17/20 = 0.85
* Using P₀ = e⁻ᵐ: ln(0.85) = -m → m = -ln(0.85) = 0.1625 mutations per culture
* (Acceptable range: 0.16–0.17)

**b) Mutation rate per nucleotide per generation:**

* Total cell divisions per culture ≈ N\_final - N\_initial ≈ 2×10⁹ (since N\_initial = 1 is negligible)
* Mutation rate = m / (N\_final × gene length) = 0.1625 / (2×10⁹ × 1,080)
* = 0.1625 / (2.16×10¹²) = 7.52 × 10⁻¹⁴ mutations per nucleotide per cell division
* Alternatively, using total generations: rate per generation per nucleotide = m / (generations × gene length) = 0.1625 / (30 × 1,080) = 5.02 × 10⁻⁶ per generation per gene, or per nucleotide: divide by 1,080 = 4.65 × 10⁻⁹ per nucleotide per generation.
* Note: Standard mutation rate in E. coli is ~10⁻¹⁰ per base per replication; the fluctuation test estimates mutation rate per cell division, which approximates the replication rate.

**c) Ames test design for Compound X:**

* i) Controls: Negative control = solvent only (e.g., DMSO) with no Compound X; Positive control = known mutagen (e.g., 2-aminoanthracene or sodium azide).
* ii) S9 mix: A rat liver microsomal enzyme fraction that mimics mammalian metabolism. Many compounds are promutagens (require metabolic activation to become mutagens). The S9 mix contains cytochrome P450 enzymes that can convert inactive precursors into DNA-reactive metabolites.
* iii) Expected outcome for mutagenicity: A dose-dependent increase in revertant colonies compared to the negative control. The number of revertants should be at least 2× the spontaneous revertant frequency, and the increase should be reproducible across replicate plates.
* iv) Quantification: Calculate revertants per plate for each dose (including solvent control). Plot dose-response curve. Use statistical test (e.g., t-test or ANOVA) to compare treated vs. control. A compound is considered mutagenic if it produces a statistically significant, dose-dependent increase in revertants in at least one tester strain (e.g., TA98 for frameshift mutagens, TA100 for base-pair substitution mutagens).

# **Question 3 Answers: Reading Frame Analysis & Frameshift Mutations**

**a) Mutant sequence after deletion of 7th nucleotide (first T in codon 3):**

* Original: ATG GGG TTT CCT GAA GAC TAT TAT AAA GAT GAC TGC AGG AAA TAA
* After deletion of T (position 7): ATG GGG TTC CTG AAG ACT ATT ATA AAG ATG ACT GCA GGA AAT AA...
* New codons: ATG | GGG | TTC | CTG | AAG | ACT | ATT | ATA | AAG | ATG | ACT | GCA | GGA | AAT | AA...

**b) Mutant polypeptide:**

* Met-Gly-Phe-Leu-Lys-Thr-Ile-Ile-Lys-Met-Thr-Ala-Gly-Asn...
* The sequence diverges at position 3 (Phe is retained, but then Leu instead of Pro).
* The next in-frame STOP would require scanning downstream. In this segment, no STOP appears within the shown sequence; the original STOP (TAA) is now out of frame (shifted to AAT). The polypeptide would be significantly extended or terminate at a downstream out-of-frame STOP.

**c) Why frameshifts are more severe than point mutations:**

* A point mutation (SNP) affects only a single codon, altering at most one amino acid (missense) or creating a premature STOP (nonsense). The remainder of the polypeptide (downstream) is unchanged.
* A frameshift mutation alters the reading frame from the point of insertion/deletion onward. Every downstream codon is changed, typically resulting in a completely different amino acid sequence and often an early STOP codon (nonsense-mediated decay or truncated protein). Because proteins are densely packed with functional domains, altering 50–100+ amino acids almost always destroys tertiary structure, active sites, and binding interfaces.
* Additionally, frameshifts frequently introduce premature STOP codons in the new reading frame, leading to nonsense-mediated mRNA decay (NMD) and no protein production.

**d) Frameshift suppression by second-site mutation:**

* Yes, a +1 insertion followed by a −1 deletion (e.g., +1 at position 6, −1 at position 9) would restore the original reading frame after the deletion point. This is called "frameshift suppression" or "second-site reversion."
* The resulting protein would have a short stretch of altered amino acids (between the insertion and deletion points), but the downstream sequence would be identical to wild-type.
* Partial function might be retained if: (i) the altered segment is in a non-critical region (e.g., a flexible loop rather than the active site), (ii) the length of the altered segment is short (minimizing structural disruption), and (iii) the introduced amino acids have similar biochemical properties to the wild-type residues.

# **Question 4 Answers: Chromosomal Mutation Analysis & Karyotyping**

**a) Chromosomal mutation types:**

* • Patient 1: Reciprocal translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22. This is the Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)(q34;q11)), associated with chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) when acquired, but can also be inherited as a balanced translocation carrier.
* • Patient 2: Aneuploidy — Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome). Three copies of chromosome 21 instead of the normal two.
* • Patient 3: Deletion — Cri-du-chat syndrome (5p deletion, deletion of the short arm of chromosome 5).

**b) Inheritance vs. de novo and mechanisms:**

* • Patient 1 (translocation): Can be inherited from a balanced carrier parent (who has normal phenotype because no genetic material is lost/gained). During meiosis, the translocation chromosomes segregate via "alternate" or "adjacent-1/2" segregation, producing unbalanced gametes. It can also arise de novo. Mechanism: aberrant repair of double-strand breaks during meiosis I (pachytene).
* • Patient 2 (trisomy 21): Usually de novo (90% of cases). Mechanism: nondisjunction during maternal meiosis I (most common), where homologous chromosomes fail to separate. Less commonly, mitotic nondisjunction in early embryogenesis produces mosaic Down syndrome.
* • Patient 3 (deletion): Usually de novo. Mechanism: chromosomal breakage and incorrect rejoining during meiosis or early mitosis, or unequal crossing-over between repetitive sequences (non-allelic homologous recombination, NAHR).

**c) Paracentric inversion and crossing-over:**

* During prophase I, the inverted chromosome pairs with its normal homolog, forming an inversion loop to align homologous regions.
* If a single crossover occurs within the inverted segment (between two non-sister chromatids):
* – One recombinant chromatid receives a dicentric chromosome (two centromeres) and an acentric fragment (no centromere).
* – The other recombinant chromatid receives a duplication of the terminal segment on one end and a deletion on the other.
* – The dicentric chromatid is pulled to opposite poles during anaphase I, forming a "dicentric bridge" that breaks, and the acentric fragment is lost.
* – Gametes receiving these recombinant chromatids are unbalanced (partial duplication + deletion) and typically nonviable.
* – Gametes with the parental (non-recombinant) chromatids are balanced and viable.
* This explains why paracentric inversion carriers often have reduced fertility — many conceptions are unbalanced and result in spontaneous abortion.

**d) Large-scale vs. small-scale mutation effects:**

* Large-scale mutations (deletions, translocations) almost always remove or disrupt entire genes, regulatory regions, or topological associating domains (TADs). They cause haploinsufficiency (one functional copy insufficient) or gene fusion (if breakpoints join two genes). Because they affect large genomic segments, the probability of removing essential sequences is high, leading to loss-of-function.
* Small-scale point mutations can occasionally create gain-of-function because they precisely alter a single amino acid or regulatory base. For example, a missense mutation in a receptor tyrosine kinase can constitutively activate signaling (e.g., EGFR mutations in cancer), or a promoter mutation can increase transcription. The precision of SNPs allows "tuning" of protein function, whereas large rearrangements are too blunt to create such specific activating changes.

# **Question 5 Answers: Clinical Case Study — Sickle Cell Anemia**

**a) DNA and mRNA analysis:**

* • Wild-type (HbA): DNA = GAG; mRNA = GAG; Amino acid = Glutamic acid (Glu)
* • Mutant (HbS): DNA = GTG; mRNA = GUG; Amino acid = Valine (Val)
* • This is a transversion: A→T substitution at the second position of the codon (purine→pyrimidine change: A→T is transversion; note: GAG→GTG is a T replacing A in the coding strand, which corresponds to A→U in mRNA, so the DNA template change is T→A, making the coding strand change A→T — this is a transversion).

**b) Classification and fitness effects:**

* • Effect on polypeptide: Missense mutation (single amino acid change: Glu→Val).
* • (i) HbS homozygote in malaria-free region: Deleterious. Chronic hemolytic anemia, vaso-occlusive crises, organ damage, reduced life expectancy without modern medical care.
* • (ii) HbA/HbS heterozygote in malaria-endemic region: Beneficial (or at least neutral-to-beneficial). Heterozygote advantage — the "sickle cell trait."
* • Molecular basis of heterozygote advantage: Heterozygotes have ~50% normal hemoglobin and ~50% HbS. Under low oxygen tension (as occurs in parasitized red blood cells), HbS polymerizes slightly, causing infected RBCs to sickle. These sickled cells are preferentially cleared by the spleen, reducing parasite burden. The parasite Plasmodium falciparum also grows poorly in sickled cells due to altered pH and membrane properties. Thus, heterozygotes have reduced malaria severity while maintaining sufficient oxygen-carrying capacity for normal function.

**c) HbC vs. HbS severity:**

* HbC (Glu→Lys) is generally LESS severe than HbS (Glu→Val).
* Reasoning: Glutamic acid is acidic and highly polar; it sits on the exterior surface of the hemoglobin tetramer. Valine is nonpolar and hydrophobic. When Val replaces Glu at position 6, the hydrophobic Val residue on the surface creates a "sticky patch" that interacts with a complementary hydrophobic pocket on another hemoglobin molecule (on the β chain), promoting polymerization of deoxy-HbS into long fibers that distort the RBC into a sickle shape.
* Lysine, by contrast, is basic and polar (like Glu, it is charged, though positively rather than negatively). It does NOT create a hydrophobic sticky patch. Therefore, HbC does not polymerize under low oxygen. HbC homozygotes have mild hemolytic anemia (HbC disease), and HbC/HbS compound heterozygotes have sickle-HbC disease of intermediate severity. The key difference is that Val introduces hydrophobicity-driven polymerization, whereas Lys retains charge and solubility.

**d) PCR-based diagnostic assay design:**

* i) Primer design: Design a forward primer ~150 bp upstream of codon 6 and a reverse primer ~150 bp downstream, yielding a ~300 bp amplicon encompassing the mutation site. The HbS mutation (A→T at the second position of codon 6 in the coding strand) abolishes a DdeI restriction site (5'-GAG↓CTC-3' in the wild-type; note: the actual DdeI site is 5'-G↓AGCT-3' — the HbS mutation changes the central A to T, disrupting the site).
* ii) Expected patterns after DdeI digest (assuming the amplicon contains one DdeI site in HbA):
* – HbA/HbA (wild-type): Two bands (e.g., 200 bp + 100 bp) after complete digest.
* – HbS/HbS (homozygous mutant): One undigested band (300 bp) because the DdeI site is abolished.
* – HbA/HbS (heterozygote): Three bands (300 bp + 200 bp + 100 bp) — the 300 bp band from the mutant allele and the two smaller bands from the wild-type allele.
* – HbC (AAG): HbC creates a new restriction site or can be detected by allele-specific PCR. Alternatively, use a second restriction enzyme (e.g., MstII) that cuts HbA but not HbS, and design allele-specific primers for HbC.
* iii) Controls: Include known HbA, HbS, and HbC DNA samples as positive controls; a no-template control (NTC) to detect contamination; and a heterozygous control (HbA/HbS) to confirm heterozygote detection. Validate by Sanger sequencing a subset of samples to confirm restriction patterns match actual genotypes.
