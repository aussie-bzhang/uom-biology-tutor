# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Photosynthesis: Overview, Site and Equation

### A. Short-answer

**1.** 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O →(light) C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂. It is endergonic (requires energy input from light) and anabolic (it builds a large molecule, glucose, from small ones, storing energy).

**2.** Plants, algae (e.g. green algae), and cyanobacteria. In plants and algae it occurs in the chloroplast (in the thylakoid membranes and stroma); cyanobacteria have thylakoid membranes but no chloroplast (they are prokaryotes).

**3.** CO₂ enters and O₂ exits through the stomata (microscopic pores, mostly on the leaf). Water absorbed by the roots travels up to the leaf in the veins (xylem). The gas exchange and most photosynthesis happen in the mesophyll, the interior leaf tissue rich in chloroplasts.

**4.** From outside in: an outer membrane, an inner membrane, then the stroma (dense aqueous fluid). Suspended in the stroma is a third membrane system of flattened sacs called thylakoids; a stack of thylakoids is a granum, and the space inside a thylakoid is the thylakoid lumen/space. Chlorophyll is located in the thylakoid membranes.

**5.** Any three: (i) Photosynthesis is anabolic, respiration is catabolic; (ii) Photosynthesis occurs in chloroplasts (chlorophyll-containing cells), respiration in cytosol + mitochondria of essentially all metabolising cells; (iii) Photosynthesis reduces NADP⁺ → NADPH, respiration reduces NAD⁺ → NADH (and FAD → FADH₂); (iv) Photosynthesis uses CO₂ + H₂O → sugar + O₂, respiration uses sugar + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O; (v) ETC location: thylakoid membrane vs inner mitochondrial membrane.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. Light energy must be supplied; photosynthesis is endergonic.

**2.** Yes. Leaves are the major sites (though all green parts have some chloroplasts).

**3.** No. Chlorophyll is in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast, not in mitochondria.

**4.** Yes. Photosynthesis reduces NADP⁺ to NADPH (an anabolic carrier); respiration uses NAD⁺/NADH.

**5.** No. They are separate pathways occurring in different organelles with different enzymes and electron carriers — not two directions of a single reversible reaction (see Set 1 applied Q3).

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-C, 2-B, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B, 6-B, 7-B, 8-B, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Option (c): 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂. Atom check — Left: C = 6 (from 6 CO₂); H = 12 (from 6 H₂O); O = 12 (CO₂) + 6 (H₂O) = 18. Right: C = 6 (glucose); H = 12 (glucose); O = 6 (glucose) + 12 (6 O₂) = 18. All balanced. (Option (a) is unbalanced — only 1 O₂; (d) is just (c) doubled; (b) is the expanded version.) (b) Option (b) writes 12 H₂O in and 6 H₂O out because isotope-labelling (using ¹⁸O) showed that ALL the O₂ released comes from WATER, not from CO₂. Twelve waters are split to supply the electrons/O₂, and six new waters are re-formed during the reactions — so 6 H₂O appears on the product side. The net is the same as (c).

**2.** (a) Magnification = image size ÷ actual size = 6 cm ÷ 6 µm = 60 000 µm ÷ 6 µm = 10 000×. (b) 20 µm ÷ 2 µm = about 10 times longer. (c) Many small chloroplasts give a far larger total surface area of thylakoid membrane (and can be moved/positioned within the cell toward the light), maximising light capture and CO₂/gas exchange per cell — far more effective than one large organelle.

**3.** (a) No — they are not literally one reversible reaction. (b) Differences: location/organelle (chloroplast thylakoids/stroma vs mitochondria + cytosol) and the enzymes & carriers used (e.g. RuBisCO and NADP⁺/NADPH in photosynthesis vs the respiratory dehydrogenases and NAD⁺/NADH, FAD/FADH₂ in respiration); the two pathways run through different intermediates, not the same steps backwards. (c) At the biosphere scale they ARE complementary: photosynthesis takes CO₂ + H₂O + light → sugar + O₂, and respiration takes sugar + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + usable energy. Together they recycle carbon and oxygen and channel the Sun's energy through living systems — a cycle, not a single reversible reaction.

## Set 2 — Light, Pigments and Photosystems

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Light is electromagnetic radiation propagated as waves; the energy per photon is INVERSELY proportional to wavelength — shorter wavelengths (e.g. violet/UV) carry more energy, longer wavelengths (e.g. red/IR) carry less. A photon is a discrete particle/packet of light energy.

**2.** A photon can (i) bounce off (be scattered/reflected), (ii) pass through (be transmitted), or (iii) be absorbed, adding its energy to the molecule and raising it to an excited state. Only ABSORPTION is useful for photosynthesis.

**3.** Major pigment: chlorophyll a. Accessory pigments: chlorophyll b, carotenoids, phycobilins. Benefit: they absorb additional wavelengths that chlorophyll a misses, broadening the range of light captured (and some carotenoids also protect against light damage), then pass the energy to the reaction centre.

**4.** The porphyrin ring (the light-absorbing 'head') contains a central magnesium (Mg) atom and is responsible for absorbing photons. The long hydrocarbon 'tail' is hydrophobic and anchors the molecule into the proteins of the thylakoid membrane (a photosystem), positioning the pigment correctly.

**5.** A photosystem is a light-capturing unit in the thylakoid membrane made of a reaction-centre complex (a special pair of chlorophyll a plus the primary electron acceptor) surrounded by several light-harvesting (antenna) complexes of pigments that funnel absorbed light energy to the reaction centre.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. Energy ∝ 1/wavelength — shorter wavelength means higher energy.

**2.** Yes. Absorption raises the molecule from the ground state to an excited (higher-energy) state.

**3.** Yes. Chlorophyll a is the major/primary pigment.

**4.** Yes. The porphyrin ring holds a central Mg atom.

**5.** Yes. They differ by a single functional group (CH₃ in chlorophyll a vs CHO in chlorophyll b).

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-B, 2-C, 3-C, 4-B, 5-B, 6-B, 7-B, 8-A, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Most → least energetic: blue (450 nm) > green (530 nm) > red (700 nm) — shorter wavelength carries more energy. (b) UV photons (~300 nm) are short-wavelength/high-energy and can break bonds or damage molecules (e.g. DNA), while IR photons (~1000 nm) are long-wavelength/low-energy — too little energy to excite photosynthetic pigments to the required level. (c) Since E ∝ 1/λ, a 350 nm photon has 700/350 = 2× the energy of a 700 nm photon.

**2.** (a) Leaves look green because the pigments absorb strongly in the blue and red but absorb POORLY in the green (~500–550 nm); that green light is mostly reflected/transmitted to our eyes. (b) Fastest photosynthesis at wavelengths the pigments absorb best — in the blue (~430–470 nm) and red (~660–680 nm); slowest in the green (~520–550 nm), where absorption is lowest. (c) Experiment: place equal sprigs of Elodea/Anacharis in sealed tubes of CO₂-rich water under lamps of equal intensity but different coloured filters (blue, green, red, white), and count O₂ bubbles released per minute (or measure O₂/CO₂ change). Bubble rate = rate of photosynthesis. Expect highest rates under red and blue, lowest under green.

**3.** (a) The single functional-group difference (CH₃ vs CHO) shifts the wavelengths each pigment absorbs slightly, so chlorophyll a and b together cover a wider span of the spectrum — capturing more of the available light. (b) The central Mg is essential for the porphyrin ring's light absorption; removing/replacing it would change or abolish the molecule's ability to absorb light and pass on the excitation energy, crippling photosynthesis. (c) The hydrocarbon tail is non-polar/hydrophobic, so it is insoluble in the watery stroma but very soluble in the lipid interior of the thylakoid membrane — 'like dissolves like' — which keeps chlorophyll anchored in the membrane where the photosystems operate.

## Set 3 — The Light-Dependent Reactions

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Inputs: light energy, water (H₂O), NADP⁺, ADP + Pᵢ. Outputs: ATP, NADPH, and O₂ (released from the splitting of water). The ATP and NADPH are then used by the Calvin cycle.

**2.** Photolysis is the light-driven splitting of WATER (H₂O), using energy captured by chlorophyll at Photosystem II. It releases electrons (to replace those lost by P680), protons (H⁺, which add to the gradient) and O₂ (as a by-product: 2 H₂O → 4 H⁺ + 4 e⁻ + O₂).

**3.** Water → PSII (P680) → primary electron acceptor → plastoquinone (Pq) → cytochrome complex → plastocyanin (Pc) → PSI (P700) → primary acceptor → ferredoxin (Fd) → NADP⁺ reductase → NADPH.

**4.** As electrons move through the chain, protons are pumped INTO the thylakoid lumen (plus more H⁺ from photolysis), making the lumen high in H⁺ and the stroma low. These protons diffuse back DOWN their gradient from the lumen into the stroma through ATP synthase, and that flow powers ATP synthesis (ADP + Pᵢ → ATP); the ATP forms on the stroma side.

**5.** Non-cyclic (linear) flow uses BOTH PSII and PSI, splits water, and produces ATP, NADPH and O₂. Cyclic flow uses ONLY PSI (electrons cycle PSI → Fd → cytochrome → Pc → back to PSI), producing ONLY ATP — no NADPH and no O₂. Cyclic flow tops up ATP when the cell needs more ATP than NADPH.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. Water is split (photolysis) to supply electrons and is the source of the released O₂.

**2.** Yes. PSII was discovered second but acts first in the light reactions.

**3.** Yes. H⁺ accumulates to high concentration inside the thylakoid lumen.

**4.** No. Cyclic flow (PSI only) makes only ATP — no NADPH and no O₂.

**5.** Yes. Both use an electron transport chain to pump protons and an ATP synthase that makes ATP from the proton gradient (chemiosmosis).

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-B, 2-C, 3-B, 4-B, 5-B, 6-B, 7-B, 8-A, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Photolysis splits WATER (H₂O); the products are electrons (e⁻), protons (H⁺) and oxygen (O₂). (b) The photochemical step — the excitation of reaction-centre electrons in the photosystems — is most directly driven by light. The Calvin cycle does NOT use light directly; it runs on the ATP and NADPH made by the light reactions. (c) Water → PSII (P680) → primary acceptor → Pq → cytochrome complex → Pc → PSI (P700) → Fd → NADP⁺ reductase → NADPH. O₂ is released at PSII (from splitting water); ATP is generated by chemiosmosis as protons (pumped during transport through the cytochrome complex) flow back through ATP synthase.

**2.** (a) NADPH production would STOP — PSI (with Fd and NADP⁺ reductase) is what passes the re-energised electrons to NADP⁺; without PSI there is no pathway to reduce NADP⁺ to NADPH. (b) Some ATP could still form from electrons leaving PSII and passing down to the cytochrome complex (proton pumping → gradient → ATP synthase), but the chain would back up once electrons could go no further past the cytochrome complex/Pc, so ATP output would also fall sharply. (c) With little/no NADPH (and reduced ATP), the Calvin cycle cannot fix CO₂ into sugar, so sugar production collapses and the plant would starve/die.

**3.** (a) Chloroplast: HIGH H⁺ inside the thylakoid lumen, LOW H⁺ in the stroma; protons flow from the lumen → stroma through ATP synthase. (b) Mitochondrion: HIGH H⁺ in the intermembrane space, LOW H⁺ in the matrix; protons flow from the intermembrane space → matrix through ATP synthase. (c) Energy source: in the chloroplast the protons are pumped using energy from LIGHT-driven electron transport (electrons from water/photosystems); in the mitochondrion the energy comes from electrons delivered by NADH/FADH₂ (from food oxidation). Structural difference: the chloroplast builds its gradient across the THYLAKOID membrane (into the lumen), whereas the mitochondrion builds it across the INNER membrane (into the intermembrane space). (d) All actively metabolising plant cells need mitochondria because every living cell must respire to make ATP (day and night, including non-green tissues like roots); only green, light-exposed cells (mainly mesophyll) contain chloroplasts, because only they perform photosynthesis.
