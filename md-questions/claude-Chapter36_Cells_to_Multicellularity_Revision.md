## Key concepts to revise before you start

**The hierarchy of life:** cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism. A tissue is a group of structurally similar cells with a common embryonic origin that work as a functional unit. An organ is built from several tissue types; an organ system is built from several organs.

**Germ layers (animals):** the three embryonic germ layers give rise to distinct tissues — ectoderm → skin + nervous system; mesoderm → muscle, bone, blood, blood vessels, connective tissue; endoderm → epithelial lining of the gut, lungs, etc.

**Four animal tissue types:** (1) Epithelial — covers/lines surfaces and secretes; (2) Connective — supports, binds, transports, stores; (3) Muscle — contracts to generate movement; (4) Nervous — detects, processes and transmits information.

**Epithelial shapes & layers:** squamous = flat; cuboidal = cube-like; columnar = tall and narrow. Simple = single layer; stratified = multiple layers; pseudostratified = looks layered but is a single layer (nuclei at different heights). Thin/flat (squamous) suits diffusion; tall (columnar) suits absorption/secretion; many layers (stratified) suit protection.

**Connective examples:** blood (transport), adipose (cushioning, energy storage), bone (structural support/strength), ligaments & tendons (mechanical strength + flexibility). All share an extracellular matrix between sparse cells.

**Muscle:** properties = excitability, contractility, extensibility, elasticity. Three types: skeletal (striated, voluntary, multinucleate), cardiac (striated, involuntary, heart), smooth (non-striated, involuntary, gut/vessels). Skeletal muscle = bundles of multinucleate fibres → myofibrils → ordered myosin (thick) + actin (thin) = striations. Contraction = ATP-dependent myosin motor pulling on actin (sliding-filament), triggered by electrical signals.

**Nervous:** neurons transmit electrical signals (action potentials); glial/support cells (e.g. Schwann cells, astrocytes) provide mechanical and nutritional support and insulation but do NOT generate action potentials; sensory receptor cells detect stimuli.

**Organs combine tissues:** e.g. the small intestine uses epithelial (absorb/secrete), connective (mucosa support), smooth muscle (move food) and nervous tissue (coordinate contractions + sensation). Organ systems (e.g. the gastrointestinal system) combine organs, and organ systems work together via the circulatory system.

**Three plant tissue systems:** dermal (protective outer covering + gas exchange; epidermis, cuticle), vascular (transport — xylem moves water/minerals, phloem moves sugar), ground (most metabolism incl. photosynthesis, storage, support). They arise from primary meristems: protoderm → dermal, ground meristem → ground, procambium → vascular.

**Plant hierarchy & the leaf:** organs = roots, stems, leaves; organ systems = root system (anchoring + nutrition) and shoot system (metabolic). A leaf integrates all three tissue systems: epidermis + cuticle (dermal), palisade + spongy mesophyll (ground/photosynthesis), xylem + phloem in veins (vascular), with guard cells/stomata controlling gas exchange.

**Reproductive organs:** flowers (sepals, petals, stamens, carpels), fruit (derived from the ovary wall; aids seed dispersal), and cones (mega-/microstrobili in conifers). A botanical fruit develops from the ovary and contains seeds — so tomato, pea pod and corn kernel are fruits.

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Animal Tissues: The Four Tissue Types

### Part A — Short-answer

1. A tissue is a group of cells that work together as a functional unit. All cells of a tissue share a common embryonic origin and are structurally similar (built of the same/related cell types arranged the same way).
2. Epithelial — covers and lines surfaces, secretes/absorbs. Connective — supports, binds, transports and stores. Muscle — contracts to produce movement. Nervous — detects, processes and transmits information.
3. Pseudostratified epithelium looks like it has several layers because the nuclei sit at different heights, but every cell actually reaches and rests on the basement membrane, so it is really a single layer. Truly stratified epithelium has genuine multiple cell layers stacked on top of one another, and only the deepest layer touches the basement membrane.
4. Whole muscle is made of bundles of muscle fibres (cells). Each fibre is a multinucleate cell packed with many myofibrils. Each myofibril is a highly ordered, repeating arrangement of thick myosin filaments and thin actin filaments. The regular overlap of these filaments produces the alternating dark/light bands, giving the striated appearance.
5. Neurons transmit electrical signals (action potentials) to gather, process and relay information. Glial/support cells (e.g. Schwann cells, astrocytes) provide mechanical and nutritional support and insulation but do NOT generate action potentials. Only neurons generate action potentials.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. No — the four tissue types arise from different germ layers (e.g. nervous + skin from ectoderm; muscle, bone, blood from mesoderm; gut lining from endoderm).
2. Yes — a thin, flat (squamous) shape gives a short diffusion distance, ideal for rapid exchange.
3. Yes — contraction is driven by an ATP-dependent myosin motor.
4. No — glial cells (e.g. Schwann cells) support and insulate neurons but do not generate action potentials.
5. Yes — multiple layers (stratified) provide a thick, hard-wearing protective barrier, which suits protection rather than diffusion/absorption.

### Part C — MCQ answers

**1-C 2-B 3-C 4-B 5-B 6-C 7-B 8-C 9-B 10-B**

### Part D — Applied / experimental

1. **Sliding-filament.** (a) Shortening = (2.5 − 2.0)/2.5 = 0.5/2.5 = 20%. (b) Relaxed length = 8,000 × 2.5 µm = 20,000 µm = 20 mm; contracted = 8,000 × 2.0 µm = 16,000 µm = 16 mm; absolute shortening = 4 mm (also 20%). (c) Myosin heads bind actin, perform a power stroke that pulls actin inward, then detach and re-cock — each detach/re-cock cycle uses one ATP. Sustained sliding therefore needs a continuous ATP supply; without ATP the heads cannot detach (cross-bridges lock) and shortening stops.
2. **Distinguishing epithelia.** (a) Section A = simple columnar (single row of nuclei near the base = one true layer). Section B = pseudostratified columnar (nuclei at staggered heights but all cells touch the basement membrane). (b) Look at the base of every cell: if each cell, traced down, reaches the basement membrane, it is pseudostratified (single layer); if upper cells do not reach it, it is truly stratified. (c) Neither A nor B is ideal for a thick protective barrier — that role calls for stratified (multi-layered) epithelium; of the two, B's staggered/pseudostratified arrangement is sturdier than the single neat row of A, but real protection needs many genuine layers (stratified squamous).
3. **Structure–function matching.** (a) Alveoli → simple squamous: a single, very thin flat layer gives the shortest diffusion path for gases. (b) Small-intestine lining → simple columnar: tall cells provide volume for absorptive/secretory machinery and a large apical surface. (c) Skin surface → stratified squamous: many flat layers resist abrasion and protect. Experiment: independent variable = epithelial thickness (or number of layers); dependent variable = rate of diffusion (e.g. time for a dye/gas to cross); control = same membrane material, area, temperature and concentration gradient, varying only thickness. Predict faster diffusion across thinner epithelium.

## Set 2 — Animal Organs & Organ Systems

### Part A — Short-answer

1. An organ is a structure built from several tissue types that work together to perform a specific function (e.g. the small intestine, heart). An organ system is a group of organs that together carry out a major body function (e.g. the gastrointestinal/digestive system, the circulatory system).
2. Epithelial — secretes digestive juices/hormones and absorbs nutrients. Connective (mucosa) — supports the lining and houses secretory cells. Smooth muscle — moves food along the gut. Nervous tissue — controls/coordinates smooth-muscle contractions and provides sensation.
3. Examples: stomach (storage + digestion); liver (metabolism + detoxification); gallbladder (stores/secretes bile to digest fats); pancreas (digestive enzymes into the gut + hormones into the blood); large intestine (absorbs water + vitamins); salivary glands (begin digestion). (Any four.)
4. All four systems exchange materials with the blood: the respiratory system loads O₂ and unloads CO₂; the digestive system passes nutrients/water into the blood; the endocrine system secretes hormones into the blood; the excretory system removes nitrogenous waste from the blood. The circulatory system is the common transport network linking them all.
5. The alveolar lining (endoderm-derived epithelium) provides a thin gas-exchange surface, but gases must also be carried to and from the body in blood travelling through capillaries (mesoderm-derived). A functional gas-exchange organ therefore needs the epithelial surface AND the blood vessels together — contributions from more than one germ layer.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. No — organs are built from several tissue types working together.
2. Yes — smooth-muscle contractions push food along the gut.
3. Yes — the circulatory system is the shared transport network connecting them.
4. Yes — the pancreas secretes digestive enzymes into the gut and hormones into the blood.
5. No — alveolar epithelium comes from endoderm while the capillaries come from mesoderm.

### Part C — MCQ answers

**1-C 2-A 3-B 4-B 5-B 6-B 7-C 8-B 9-C 10-C**

### Part D — Applied / experimental

1. **Germ-layer fate-mapping.** (a) Label endoderm to mark the alveolar epithelium; label mesoderm to mark the capillaries (run as two separate labelling treatments). Independent variable = which germ layer is labelled; dependent variable = which mature lung structures fluoresce; control = an unlabelled embryo (no fluorescence) to show the signal is from the label. (b) Endoderm label → fluorescence in the alveolar lining; mesoderm label → fluorescence in the capillary walls. (c) Because two different germ-layer labels end up in two different parts of the same organ, the functional lung must be assembled from more than one germ layer.
2. **Build-an-organ (small intestine).** (a) Inner → outer: epithelium (secretes digestive juices/hormones, absorbs nutrients) → mucosa/connective tissue (supports, holds secretory cells, blood supply) → smooth muscle (moves food along) → outer epithelial + connective covering (covers the organ, lines the abdominal cavity). (b) (i) If smooth muscle failed, food would not be propelled — no peristalsis, so digestion/absorption stall and contents back up. (ii) If the nervous network failed, contractions would be uncoordinated and sensory feedback lost, so mixing and movement become disorganised. (c) Folds + villi massively increase the internal surface area; since absorption rate scales with surface area, more area means far more nutrient uptake per unit length of gut.
3. **Oxygen-delivery chain.** (a) 5 L/min = 5,000 mL/min; O₂ delivered = 5,000 mL × (20 mL O₂ / 100 mL blood) = 5,000 × 0.20 = 1,000 mL O₂ per minute. (b) Fraction consumed = 250 / 1,000 = 0.25, i.e. about 25% of delivered O₂ is used at rest (the rest returns in venous blood as a reserve). (c) The respiratory system loads O₂ onto blood, the circulatory system delivers it, and tissues/organs consume it — they must be coupled. During exercise demand rises to ~1,000 mL/min, so the body increases breathing rate/depth (more O₂ loaded), raises cardiac output (more blood pumped) and tissues extract a larger fraction of the delivered O₂.

## Set 3 — Plant Tissues, Organs & the Leaf

### Part A — Short-answer

1. Dermal tissue — protective outer covering and gas exchange (1st line of defence). Vascular tissue — transport: xylem carries water + minerals, phloem carries sugars. Ground tissue — most metabolic functions (incl. photosynthesis), storage and structural support.
2. Protoderm → dermal tissue; ground meristem → ground tissue; procambium → vascular tissue. (All derive from apical meristems via the primary meristems.)
3. In a non-woody plant the dermal tissue is the epidermis: a tightly packed single layer of cells, with the upper/exposed parts covered by a waxy cuticle. The tight packing forms a continuous protective barrier (first line of defence) while the cuticle limits water loss; stomata in the epidermis still allow controlled gas exchange.
4. Dermal — epidermis/cuticle (protection + limits water loss) or guard cells (control stomatal gas exchange). Ground — palisade/spongy mesophyll (photosynthesis). Vascular — xylem (water in) and phloem (sugar out) within the veins.
5. A botanical fruit is a structure that develops from the ovary wall of a flower after fertilisation and typically encloses the seed(s). Its role is to protect the developing seeds and aid their dispersal.

### Part B — Yes/No

1. Yes — dermal, ground and vascular tissue systems are all integrated within a leaf.
2. No — xylem transports water and minerals (upward); phloem transports sugars.
3. Yes — ground tissue carries out most metabolism, including photosynthesis.
4. Yes — guard cells are specialised epidermal (dermal-system) cells.
5. Yes — a tomato develops from the ovary and contains seeds, so it is botanically a fruit.

### Part C — MCQ answers

**1-C 2-B 3-A 4-C 5-C 6-B 7-B 8-B 9-C 10-D**

### Part D — Applied / experimental

1. **Leaf cross-section mapping.** (a) Dermal: cuticle, upper epidermis, lower epidermis, guard cells, stoma. Ground: palisade mesophyll, spongy mesophyll. Vascular: xylem, phloem. (b) Palisade cells (packed with chloroplasts) sit near the top to catch the most light; the air spaces of the spongy mesophyll let CO₂ diffuse to those cells; veins (xylem + phloem) deliver water and carry away sugars throughout; stomata on the shaded lower surface admit CO₂ while limiting water loss; the cuticle/epidermis protects the whole leaf — so all three systems cooperate to maximise photosynthesis. (c) Removing the cuticle would let water evaporate freely from the leaf surface, causing rapid water loss and wilting, and would weaken the protective barrier against pathogens.
2. **Stomatal density / gas exchange.** (a) Density = 15 stomata / 0.20 mm² = 75 stomata per mm². (b) Total ≈ 75 × 3,000 = 225,000 stomata. (c) Dermal tissue must balance taking in CO₂ against losing water through open stomata. In a drier, sunnier site, water loss is the bigger threat, so fewer stomata reduce transpiration (at some cost to CO₂ uptake). Fair test: compare the same species, mature leaves at the same position, same leaf area and counting method, with the only difference being the growth environment (wet/shaded vs dry/sunny); take several leaves and average.
3. **Fruit-or-vegetable classification.** (a) Carrot = root (swollen taproot, storage); potato = stem (an underground stem/tuber with 'eyes' = buds); lettuce = leaf; broccoli = flower (unopened flower buds + stalk); cucumber = fruit (develops from the ovary, contains seeds). (b) Test: does it develop from the ovary of a flower and contain seeds? If yes, it is a botanical fruit. (c) A pea pod, tomato and corn kernel all develop from the ovary (ovary wall) of a flower and enclose seeds, so all three are botanically fruits regardless of how we use them in cooking.

## Common traps to watch (Wentao’s checklist)

* Tissue = common embryonic origin + structurally similar cells; the FOUR animal types are epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous.
* Germ-layer shortcut: ectoderm = skin + nervous system; mesoderm = muscle/bone/blood/blood vessels/connective; endoderm = lining of gut & lungs.
* Epithelial shape vs layer are separate descriptors: squamous/cuboidal/columnar (shape) and simple/stratified/pseudostratified (layering). Pseudostratified is really ONE layer.
* Thin/flat squamous → diffusion; tall columnar → absorption/secretion; many layers (stratified) → protection.
* Muscle contraction is the sliding-filament mechanism: myosin (thick) is the ATP-using motor that pulls on actin (thin); striations come from their ordered overlap; triggered by electrical signals.
* Only NEURONS fire action potentials; glial cells (Schwann, astrocytes) only support/insulate — they do NOT.
* An ORGAN is built from several tissue types; an ORGAN SYSTEM from several organs; the circulatory system links the other systems.
* A functional gas-exchange surface needs more than one germ layer: alveolar epithelium (endoderm) + capillaries (mesoderm).
* Plant tissue systems: dermal (cover/protect/gas exchange), vascular (xylem = water up, phloem = sugar), ground (metabolism/photosynthesis/support). Meristem origins: protoderm→dermal, ground meristem→ground, procambium→vascular.
* Guard cells are dermal (epidermal) cells; palisade & spongy mesophyll are ground tissue; xylem & phloem are vascular.
* A botanical FRUIT develops from the OVARY and contains seeds — so tomato, pea pod, cucumber and corn kernel are fruits; potato is a stem and carrot is a root.
