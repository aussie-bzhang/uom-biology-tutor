## Question 1: Water Potential & Osmosis Calculation

*A plant cell has a solute potential (Ψs) of −0.8 MPa and a pressure potential (Ψp) of 0.3 MPa. It is placed in a beaker containing an external solution with Ψ = −0.2 MPa.*

1. **a)** Calculate the total water potential (Ψ) of the plant cell. Show your working. [2 marks]
2. **b)** Predict the direction of net water movement between the cell and the external solution. Explain what will happen to the cell's turgor pressure over time. [3 marks]
3. **c)** Calculate the pressure potential (Ψp) that would need to develop inside the cell to prevent any further net water influx from the external solution. [2 marks]
4. **d)** If the cell were placed in pure water (Ψ = 0 MPa) and became fully turgid with Ψp = 0.8 MPa, calculate the final solute potential (Ψs) of the cell. Explain why this represents the equilibrium state. [3 marks]

## Question 2: Transpiration-Cohesion-Tension Mechanism

*A coastal redwood (Sequoia sempervirens) reaches a height of 110 m. The water potential at the soil–root interface is −0.2 MPa, and the water potential in the leaf air spaces is −2.2 MPa. The gravitational component of water potential increases by −0.01 MPa per metre of height above ground.*

1. **a)** Calculate the total hydrostatic (gravitational) component of water potential at the top of the tree. [1 mark]
2. **b)** Calculate the total water potential gradient (ΔΨ) that must be overcome to move water from the soil to the leaf air spaces at the top of the tree. [3 marks]
3. **c)** The tensile strength of water under pure conditions is approximately −30 MPa. However, in xylem vessels, cavitation typically occurs at around −3 to −8 MPa. Using these values, calculate the theoretical maximum height a tree could achieve under ideal pure-water conditions versus realistic xylem conditions. Discuss what other factors limit actual tree height. [4 marks]
4. **d)** Explain how the properties of cohesion and adhesion contribute to the ascent of water in the xylem. Why are these properties essential for the transpiration-cohesion-tension mechanism to function? [2 marks]

## Question 3: Experimental Design: Apoplast vs Symplast Pathways

1. **a)** Describe your experimental setup, including: (i) how you would apply the ³²P tracer to the root system, (ii) the time course of sampling, and (iii) how you would distinguish radioactivity in apoplastic versus symplastic compartments. [4 marks]
2. **b)** Explain how the Casparian strip in the endodermis affects your expected results. Predict the pattern of ³²P distribution you would observe in the root cross-sections at the endodermis if: (i) the symplast pathway dominates, versus (ii) the apoplast pathway dominates. [3 marks]
3. **c)** In a separate treatment, you apply a metabolic inhibitor (e.g., DNP, which blocks ATP production) to the roots. Predict how this would affect ³²P accumulation in the xylem sap compared to the control. Explain your reasoning with reference to active and passive transport processes. [3 marks]

## Question 4: Phloem Loading & Mass Flow Calculation

*In a mature sunflower (Helianthus annuus) leaf acting as a source, the sucrose concentration in sieve tube elements at the loading site is 0.50 M. In the adjacent mesophyll cells (where sucrose is produced), the concentration is 0.18 M. The surrounding xylem sap has a water potential of −0.5 MPa, and the temperature is 25°C (298 K).*

1. **a)** Using the van’t Hoff equation (Π = iCRT, where i = 1 for sucrose, R = 0.00831 L·MPa·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹), calculate the osmotic pressure difference (ΔΠ) between the sieve tube elements and the surrounding xylem. [3 marks]
2. **b)** Given that the pressure potential inside the sieve tube at the source is 1.2 MPa, calculate the total water potential of the sieve tube sap. Predict the direction of water movement between the xylem and the sieve tube, and explain how this drives phloem loading. [3 marks]
3. **c)** At the sink (a developing seed 0.3 m below the leaf), the sucrose concentration in the sieve tube is 0.12 M and the pressure potential is 0.4 MPa. Calculate the total turgor pressure gradient (ΔΨp) driving mass flow between the source and the sink. Assuming Poiseuille’s law applies, explain how sieve tube radius affects the volume flow rate, and discuss why sieve plates would reduce flow compared to open tubes. [4 marks]

## Question 5: Integrated Analysis: Drought Stress & Transport Coordination

3. **c)** The rate of phloem transport often declines during severe drought even though photosynthate (sucrose) is still being produced. # Answer Key

## Question 1: Water Potential & Osmosis

**a)** Using the water potential equation: Ψ = Ψs + Ψp

Χ\_cell = (−0.8 MPa) + 0.3 MPa = −0.5 MPa [2 marks]

**b)** Water moves from higher (less negative) to lower (more negative) water potential.

• External solution: Ψ = −0.2 MPa; Cell: Ψ = −0.5 MPa.

• Since −0.2 MPa > −0.5 MPa, net water movement is INTO the cell (endosmosis). [1 mark]

• As water enters, turgor pressure increases (Ψp becomes more positive), causing the cell to swell and become turgid. [1 mark]

• Water influx continues until Χ\_cell = Χ\_external, i.e., Ψp rises to balance the Ψs difference. [1 mark]

**c)** For no net water movement: Χ\_cell = Χ\_external = −0.2 MPa

−0.2 MPa = −0.8 MPa + Ψp → Ψp = 0.6 MPa [2 marks]

**d)** At equilibrium with pure water (Χ = 0 MPa):

0 MPa = Ψs + 0.8 MPa → Ψs = −0.8 MPa [1 mark]

This is equilibrium because Χ\_inside = Χ\_outside = 0 MPa, so there is no net water movement. The positive pressure potential exactly balances the negative solute potential, maintaining the cell in a fully turgid state. [2 marks]

## Question 2: Transpiration-Cohesion-Tension

**a)** Gravitational component at 110 m:

ΔΨ\_gravity = −0.01 MPa/m × 110 m = −1.1 MPa [1 mark]

**b)** Total gradient from soil to leaf:

• Χ\_soil = −0.2 MPa

• Χ\_leaf = Χ\_air spaces = −2.2 MPa (pressure in air spaces = 0 MPa, so Χ\_total = −2.2 MPa)

• Total ΔΨ = Χ\_leaf − Χ\_soil = (−2.2) − (−0.2) = −2.0 MPa [1 mark]

• This gradient overcomes both gravity (−1.1 MPa) and maintains the driving force for ascent. [1 mark]

• The negative pressure (tension) at the top = −2.0 MPa − (−1.1) = −0.9 MPa relative to local gravity, or −2.2 MPa absolute. [1 mark]

**c)** Theoretical maximum heights:

• Under ideal pure-water conditions (tensile strength −30 MPa):

Max height = 30 MPa / 0.01 MPa/m = 3,000 m (purely physical limit) [1 mark]

• Under realistic xylem conditions (cavitation at −3 to −8 MPa):

Conservative: 3 MPa / 0.01 = 300 m; Optimistic: 8 MPa / 0.01 = 800 m [1 mark]

• Other limiting factors: mechanical stability of trunk (buckling risk), carbon cost of building/maintaining tall trunks, decreasing photosynthetic efficiency at height due to hydraulic constraints, wind damage risk, and the need for sufficient root biomass to match canopy size. [2 marks]

**d)** Cohesion and adhesion:

• Cohesion — hydrogen bonds between water molecules create a continuous, unbroken column of water in the xylem that can transmit tension from leaf to root. Without cohesion, the water column would break (cavitate) under tension. [1 mark]

• Adhesion — hydrogen bonds between water molecules and the hydrophilic lignin/cellulose walls of xylem vessels counteract gravity and help support the water column against downward pull, especially in narrow vessels (capillary action). [1 mark]

## Question 3: Experimental Design

**a)** Experimental setup:

• (i) Grow hydroponically cultured seedlings in sterile nutrient solution. Apply ³²P-labelled phosphate (KH₂³²PO₄) to the root bathing solution at a known specific activity (e.g., 100 kBq/mL). [1 mark]

• (ii) Harvest roots at multiple time points (e.g., 15 min, 30 min, 1 h, 2 h, 4 h). At each time point, wash roots briefly in ice-cold non-radioactive phosphate solution to remove surface-bound tracer. [1 mark]

• (iii) For compartmental analysis: Freeze roots, section them transversely, and use either:

– Autoradiography (exposure to X-ray film) to visualise ³²P distribution across tissues; or

– Sequential elution: first wash in cold water (removes apoplastic ³²P), then extract remaining tissue (symplastic ³²P) and count radioactivity separately in a scintillation counter. [2 marks]

**b)** Casparian strip and expected results:

• The Casparian strip blocks the apoplastic pathway at the endodermis, forcing all water and solutes to cross a cell membrane (enter the symplast) before reaching the xylem. This enables selective uptake. [1 mark]

• If symplast dominates: Strong ³²P signal in cortical cell cytoplasm and plasmodesmata; intense labelling in endodermal cells and pericycle; relatively uniform distribution across the stele. The radioactive front moves as a regulated wave. [1 mark]

• If apoplast dominates: Strong ³²P signal in cell walls and intercellular spaces throughout the cortex, but a sharp drop at the Casparian strip (blocked); only limited symplastic ³²P appears in the stele after the endodermis. [1 mark]

**c)** Effect of metabolic inhibitor (DNP):

• DNP blocks ATP production by dissipating the proton gradient across the mitochondrial membrane. [1 mark]

• Phosphate uptake into roots primarily occurs via H⁺/phosphate cotransporters, which are secondary active transporters driven by the H⁺-ATPase. Without ATP, the proton pump fails, the H⁺ gradient collapses, and phosphate uptake is severely reduced. [1 mark]

• Therefore, ³²P accumulation in the xylem sap would be substantially decreased (typically by 60–80%) compared to the control, confirming that phosphate loading into the xylem is an active, energy-dependent process. [1 mark]

## Question 4: Phloem Loading & Mass Flow

**a)** Osmotic pressure calculation:

• Π\_sieve tube = iCRT = 1 × 0.50 mol/L × 0.00831 L·MPa·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹ × 298 K = 1.238 MPa [1 mark]

• Π\_xylem: Assuming dilute xylem sap with negligible solutes, Π\_xylem ≈ 0 MPa (relative to −0.5 MPa total, mostly pressure potential).

• ΔΠ = 1.238 − 0 = 1.24 MPa (approx. 1.2 MPa) [2 marks]

**b)** Water potential of sieve tube and direction of water movement:

• Ψ\_sieve tube = Ψp + Ψs = 1.2 MPa + (−1.238 MPa) = −0.038 MPa ≈ −0.04 MPa [1 mark]

• Ψ\_xylem = −0.5 MPa

• Since −0.04 MPa > −0.5 MPa, water moves FROM the xylem INTO the sieve tube elements (osmosis). [1 mark]

• Water influx increases the hydrostatic pressure (turgor) inside the sieve tube, creating the positive pressure gradient that drives mass flow toward sinks. This is the fundamental mechanism of phloem loading. [1 mark]

**c)** Turgor pressure gradient and flow:

• Ψp\_source = 1.2 MPa; Ψp\_sink = 0.4 MPa

• ΔΨp = 1.2 − 0.4 = 0.8 MPa (this is the driving force for mass flow) [1 mark]

• By Poiseuille’s law (Q = πr⁴ΔP / 8ηL), volume flow rate Q is proportional to r⁴. Doubling the sieve tube radius increases flow 16-fold. [1 mark]

• Sieve plates contain pores but also deposit callose, which narrows the effective diameter. According to Poiseuille’s law, even small constrictions dramatically reduce flow (r⁴ dependence). Additionally, sieve plates increase viscous drag at the pore entrances. This explains why phloem sap moves relatively slowly (≈100–400 cm/h) despite the substantial pressure gradient. [2 marks]

## Question 5: Drought Stress & Transport Coordination

**a)** Minimum sustainable leaf water potential:

• Gravitational component at 0.6 m: ΔΨ\_g = −0.01 MPa/m × 0.6 m = −0.006 MPa (negligible but included). [1 mark]

• For water to move: Ψ\_leaf must be more negative than Ψ\_soil + gravity

Ψ\_leaf < (−2.0 MPa) + (−0.006 MPa) = −2.006 MPa

Therefore, minimum sustainable Ψ\_leaf ≈ −2.0 MPa [2 marks]

**b)** Stomatal closure effects:

• Stomatal closure reduces the water vapour concentration gradient between leaf air spaces and the external atmosphere, thereby reducing transpiration rate. [1 mark]

• Reduced transpiration means less water is pulled from the xylem into the leaf mesophyll, which decreases the tension (negative pressure) in the xylem sap. The pressure potential becomes less negative. [1 mark]

• This is beneficial because: (i) it reduces the risk of xylem cavitation (embolism), which would permanently block water transport in that vessel; (ii) it conserves soil water reserves; and (iii) it maintains a minimal water potential gradient sufficient for slow but continuous water uptake. [1 mark]

**c)** Phloem transport decline and consequences:

• According to the mass flow hypothesis, phloem transport depends on water entering sieve tubes at the source (creating high pressure) and leaving at the sink (maintaining the pressure gradient). This water comes primarily from the xylem. [1 mark]

• Under drought, reduced xylem water potential means less water is available to move osmotically into the sieve tubes. Consequently, the pressure potential at the source does not build up as effectively, and the ΔΨp driving mass flow decreases. [1 mark]

• Additionally, reduced transpiration decreases the overall ‘bulk flow’ capacity of the plant vascular system. [1 mark]

• Consequences for source-sink: Root growth (a major sink for sugars) is preferentially maintained over shoot growth because roots are essential for water foraging. However, if drought is severe, overall growth slows, fruit/seed filling is reduced, and the plant enters survival mode. Carbon allocation shifts toward osmolyte synthesis (e.g., proline) for cellular protection rather than biomass accumulation. [1 mark]
