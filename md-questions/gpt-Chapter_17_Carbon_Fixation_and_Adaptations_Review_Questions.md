# Answer Key

## Review Set 1 Answers

A1. Although called light-independent, the Calvin cycle requires ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions. It does not directly use photons, but it depends on the chemical energy and reducing power generated in the thylakoid membranes.

A2. RuBP is the 5-carbon CO2 acceptor. Rubisco catalyses the addition of CO2 to RuBP, producing an unstable 6-carbon intermediate that splits into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3PG).

A3. Carbon fixation: CO2 is attached to RuBP by Rubisco. Reduction: 3PG is reduced using ATP and NADPH to form G3P. Regeneration: most G3P is used, with ATP, to regenerate RuBP so the cycle can continue.

A4. Photorespiration occurs when Rubisco adds O2 to RuBP instead of CO2. It produces phosphoglycolate and 3PG, wastes energy, releases previously fixed carbon, and reduces net sugar production.

A5. C4 plants separate steps spatially: initial CO2 fixation occurs in mesophyll cells and the Calvin cycle occurs mainly in bundle sheath cells. CAM plants separate steps temporally: CO2 is fixed at night and used in the Calvin cycle during the day.

B. Yes/No: 1 No; 2 Yes; 3 Yes; 4 Yes; 5 No.

C. MCQ: 1 B; 2 A; 3 C; 4 A; 5 A; 6 B; 7 C; 8 A; 9 B; 10 B.

D1. Two net G3P molecules require twice the resources for one net G3P: 6 CO2, 18 ATP and 12 NADPH.

D2. Closure of stomata reduces CO2 entry while O2 can accumulate relative to CO2. Rubisco oxygenase activity is therefore favoured, photorespiration increases, and net carbon fixation decreases.

D3. This is CAM photosynthesis. Opening stomata at night reduces water loss; CO2 is stored as organic acids and released during the day for the Calvin cycle while stomata remain closed.

## Review Set 2 Answers

A1. Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy stored in organic molecules. These molecules support photoautotrophs directly and provide energy and carbon skeletons for most food webs; photosynthesis also replenishes atmospheric oxygen.

A2. Inputs include CO2, ATP and NADPH, with RuBP and enzymes such as Rubisco required for the cycle. Outputs include carbohydrates or sugar precursors such as G3P, ADP + Pi, and NADP+.

A3. As a carboxylase, Rubisco adds CO2 to RuBP, supporting the Calvin cycle and carbon gain. As an oxygenase, it adds O2 to RuBP, producing phosphoglycolate and 3PG, leading to photorespiration and carbon loss.

A4. C4 plants initially fix CO2 in mesophyll cells using PEP carboxylase to form a 4-carbon compound such as oxaloacetate/malate. The 4-carbon compound moves to bundle sheath cells, releases CO2, and concentrates it around Rubisco for the Calvin cycle.

A5. CAM plants open stomata at night and fix CO2 using PEP carboxylase, storing it as organic acids such as malate in vacuoles. During the day, stomata close, malate is decarboxylated, and CO2 enters the Calvin cycle.

B. Yes/No: 1 Yes; 2 Yes; 3 Yes; 4 No; 5 Yes.

C. MCQ: 1 D; 2 A; 3 A; 4 C; 5 B; 6 B; 7 A; 8 C; 9 B; 10 C.

D1. Four net G3P molecules require 4 x 3 CO2 = 12 CO2, 4 x 9 ATP = 36 ATP, and 4 x 6 NADPH = 24 NADPH.

D2. C3: RuBP. C4: PEP. CAM: night. C4: bundle sheath cells.

D3. This is most likely a C4 plant. High performance in hot dry conditions plus chloroplast-rich bundle sheath cells suggests spatial separation of CO2 fixation and the Calvin cycle, which limits photorespiration.
