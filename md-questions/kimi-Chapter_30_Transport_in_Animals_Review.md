# Question 1 – Answers

**(a)** For a sphere: SA = 4πr² = 4π(2 mm)² = 16π ≈ 50.27 mm². Volume = (4/3)πr³ = (4/3)π(8) ≈ 33.51 mm³. SA:V = 50.27 / 33.51 ≈ **1.50 mm⁻¹**.

**(b)** Total O₂ delivery by diffusion = rate × SA = 0.03 mmol cm⁻² h⁻¹ × 50.27 mm². Convert mm² to cm²: 50.27 mm² = 0.5027 cm². Delivery = 0.03 × 0.5027 ≈ 0.015 mmol h⁻¹. Demand = 0.5 mmol h⁻¹. Delivery is only ~3 % of demand. **Diffusion alone is insufficient.** Minimum fold-increase in SA required = 0.5 / 0.015 ≈ **33.3-fold**. This is why gills, lungs, or circulatory systems evolved—to massively increase effective exchange surface without increasing body size.

**(c)** Experimental design (agar-block diffusion model):
**•** Prepare agar blocks of identical composition but different sizes (e.g., 1 cm³, 2 cm³, 3 cm³ cubes) soaked in phenolphthalein indicator.
**•** Immerse in NaOH solution; time how long for the colour change to reach the centre of each block.
**•** Control: identical agar blocks with a central cavity (simulating a “circulatory vessel”) to show that diffusion distance, not just SA:V, is the critical variable.
**•** Measurable outcome: time to full colour change (t) vs. half-thickness (x); expect t ∝ x² (Fick’s second law). The block with the cavity will equilibrate fastest, demonstrating that reducing diffusion distance is more effective than merely increasing surface area.

# Question 2 – Answers

**(a)** In a closed system (squid), blood is confined to vessels, allowing high hydrostatic pressure generated by a powerful muscular heart. High pressure drives rapid flow (Q) and precise distribution to active tissues (e.g., mantle muscles). The closed network also maintains separation between transport fluid (blood) and interstitial fluid, preserving high colloid osmotic pressure and efficient exchange. In an open system (crab), hemolymph empties into the hemocoel; pressure drops rapidly as fluid bathes tissues directly. Flow is slower and less directed, limiting the rate of O₂ delivery to metabolically active tissues during sustained burst activity.

**(b)** Insects rely on a highly branched tracheal system that delivers air directly to tissues, bypassing the circulatory system for gas exchange. The open circulatory system is then used mainly for nutrient and hormone transport, not O₂ delivery. **Trade-off:** the tracheal system is diffusion-limited in very large or active insects; hence, some large insects augment tracheal ventilation with abdominal pumping (active ventilation), but this still cannot support the sustained aerobic scope of endothermic mammals.

**(c)** Experimental design:
**•** Capture matched-size individuals; acclimate in flow-through seawater tanks (25 °C) for 48 h.
**•** Protocol: 2-min standardized chase in a circular arena; immediately sample hemolymph (crab) or blood (squid) from the pericardial sinus / branchial heart at 0, 2, 5, 10, and 20 min post-exercise.
**•** Measurements: (i) heart rate via non-invasive Doppler flow probe (squid) or electrocardiogram implant (crab); (ii) lactate concentration via enzymatic assay (lactate oxidase).
**•** Predicted outcome: squid will show rapid heart-rate recovery (<5 min) and low lactate accumulation because the closed system efficiently clears metabolites; crab will exhibit slower recovery and higher lactate, indicating greater reliance on anaerobic metabolism due to circulatory limitation.

# Question 3 – Answers

**(a)** By continuity equation: Q = A\_aorta × v\_aorta = A\_cap × v\_cap.
v\_cap = (A\_aorta × v\_aorta) / A\_cap = (4.5 cm² × 40 cm s⁻¹) / 4,500 cm² = 180 / 4,500 = **0.04 cm s⁻¹ (or 0.4 mm s⁻¹)**.

**(b)** The ~1,000-fold reduction in velocity maximizes the residence time of red blood cells within the capillary bed. Slow transit allows sufficient time for O₂ and CO₂ diffusion down their partial-pressure gradients, and for nutrient/waste exchange across the thin (0.5 µm) capillary endothelium. If velocity remained high, equilibration would be incomplete (perfusion-limited exchange).

**(c)** Pressure drop = 95 – 35 = 60 mmHg. Percentage drop = (60 / 95) × 100 ≈ **63.2 %**. This steep gradient is essential because it provides the hydrostatic driving force (CHP) that pushes fluid and small solutes out of the capillary into the interstitial space (filtration). Without this pressure head, the balance of Starling forces would favour reabsorption everywhere, preventing adequate delivery of nutrients and removal of metabolic waste from tissues.

# Question 4 – Answers

**(a)** NFP = (CHP + IFOP) – (BCOP + IFHP) = (35 + 4) – (26 + (–2)) = 39 – 24 = **+15 mmHg**. Because NFP > 0, **net filtration** occurs—fluid and small solutes leave the capillary.

**(b)** NFP = (15 + 4) – (26 + (–2)) = 19 – 24 = **–5 mmHg**. Because NFP < 0, **net reabsorption** occurs—fluid is drawn back into the capillary. This ensures that ~85–90 % of filtered fluid is reclaimed, while the remaining 10–15 % is drained by lymphatic vessels, maintaining interstitial fluid homeostasis.

**(c)** New BCOP = 26 × 0.70 = 18.2 mmHg. New NFP = (35 + 4) – (18.2 + (–2)) = 39 – 16.2 = **+22.8 mmHg** (increase of +7.8 mmHg, ~52 % higher than baseline). The markedly elevated filtration pressure drives excessive fluid into the interstitium. Because reabsorption at the venular end is also reduced (new NFP there = 19 – 16.2 = +2.8 mmHg, still favouring filtration rather than reabsorption), net fluid accumulation exceeds lymphatic drainage capacity, resulting in **clinical oedema**. This is observed in hypoproteinaemia (e.g., liver cirrhosis or nephrotic syndrome).

# Question 5 – Answers

**(a)** In the fish heart, the single ventricle pumps blood sequentially through the gills (high resistance, R\_gills) and then the systemic tissues (R\_systemic). Total resistance R\_total = R\_gills + R\_systemic. Because ΔP = Q × R, the pressure generated by the ventricle must overcome the sum of both resistances. Consequently, the pressure drop across the gills leaves only modest pressure for the systemic circuit. This limits systemic perfusion and metabolic rate. In contrast, a double circuit (birds/mammals) has two ventricles: the right ventricle overcomes R\_gills/lungs, while the independently powerful left ventricle generates high systemic pressure, enabling much greater tissue perfusion and aerobic capacity.

**(b)** Hypothesis: During diving, the Foramen of Panizza allows right-to-left shunting, bypassing the pulmonary circuit. This reduces blood flow to non-functional lungs (air-breathing is suspended), conserving cardiac energy and preventing hypoxia-induced vasoconstriction in the pulmonary bed. The shunted blood maintains systemic circulation using residual O₂ stored in blood and tissues.
Experimental validation:
**•** Surgical implantation of ultrasonic flow probes around the pulmonary artery and the left aorta in captive crocodiles.
**•** Blood samples (0.5 mL) from the right atrium, pulmonary artery, and systemic aorta at rest, during forced submergence (5 min), and during recovery.
**•** Measure: (i) blood flow rate (Q) via flow probes; (ii) blood gases (pO₂, pCO₂, pH) via blood-gas analyser; (iii) lactate as anaerobic marker.
**•** Predicted outcome: during submergence, pulmonary flow (Q\_pulm) drops >80 %, while systemic flow is maintained; pO₂ in systemic blood remains higher than in pulmonary blood, confirming right-to-left shunt; pCO₂ rises modestly, indicating CO₂ storage rather than ventilatory elimination.

**(c)** Expected curve: sigmoidal (S-shaped) relationship.
**•** X-axis: degree of ventricular separation (0 → 1).
**•** Y-axis: maximum metabolic rate (mL O₂ kg⁻¹ h⁻¹).
**•** Shape: shallow rise from fish (single ventricle, ~100–200 mL O₂ kg⁻¹ h⁻¹) to amphibians/reptiles (partial separation, ~200–400); steep rise from crocodilians (complete anatomical separation but shunt, ~400–600) to birds/mammals (complete functional separation, ~800–2,000+).
**Physiological mechanisms:**1. Pressure separation: complete septation allows the left ventricle to generate high systemic pressure (120 mmHg in mammals vs. ~40 mmHg in fish), driving greater tissue perfusion.
2. Oxygen transport capacity: double circulation supports higher haematocrit and haemoglobin concentrations without the “sludging” risk of low-pressure systems, increasing arterial O₂ content (CaO₂) and maximal O₂ delivery (DO₂ = Q × CaO₂).
