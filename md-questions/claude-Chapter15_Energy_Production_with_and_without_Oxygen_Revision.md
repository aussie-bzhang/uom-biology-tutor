# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Oxidative Phosphorylation and the Electron Transport Chain

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Oxidative phosphorylation is the synthesis of ATP that is driven by the re-oxidation of the reduced electron carriers (NADH and FADH₂) in the presence of O₂. Its two components are (i) electron transport (the respiratory chain) and (ii) chemiosmosis (ATP synthesis powered by the proton gradient).

**2.** From one glucose: 10 NADH and 2 FADH₂ (plus 4 ATP already made by substrate-level phosphorylation). Breakdown: glycolysis = 2 NADH (+2 ATP); oxidation of 2 pyruvate = 2 NADH; citric acid cycle (2 acetyl-CoA) = 6 NADH + 2 FADH₂ (+2 ATP). Totals delivered to oxidative phosphorylation: 10 NADH + 2 FADH₂.

**3.** NADH and FADH₂ hand their high-energy electrons (and H⁺) to the carriers of the chain; the electrons are passed from one carrier to the next, losing energy at each step. At the end they combine with O₂ and H⁺ to form H₂O. O₂ is the final (terminal) electron acceptor.

**4.** Protons are pumped from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space, building a higher H⁺ concentration (electrochemical gradient) there. The potential energy stored in this gradient drives protons back through ATP synthase, powering ATP synthesis (chemiosmosis). Without the gradient there is no power source for ATP synthase.

**5.** Four complexes: I, II, III, IV. Two mobile carriers: ubiquinone (coenzyme Q), which shuttles between I/II and III, and cytochrome c, which shuttles between III and IV.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. The complexes and ATP synthase are embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane (cristae).

**2.** No. NADH enters at Complex I; FADH₂ enters at Complex II (succinate dehydrogenase).

**3.** Yes. O₂ is reduced and combines with H⁺ and electrons to form water (2 H⁺ + ½ O₂ → H₂O).

**4.** Yes. Electron flow drives proton pumping from the matrix into the intermembrane space.

**5.** No. The chain creates the proton gradient; the actual ADP + Pᵢ → ATP reaction is carried out by ATP synthase during chemiosmosis.

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-C, 2-B, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B, 6-A, 7-B, 8-B, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Electrons flow spontaneously from carriers of higher free energy (NADH) toward those of lower free energy, ending at O₂ — i.e. downhill / energetically favourable, from a strong reducing agent to a strong electron acceptor. (b) Releasing the energy in many small steps lets the cell capture it gradually (as a proton gradient → ATP) instead of losing it all as a single damaging burst of heat; it makes energy capture efficient and controllable. (c) FADH₂ enters at Complex II, which is 'lower' on the energy diagram and bypasses Complex I; its electrons therefore pass through fewer proton-pumping complexes, pump fewer protons, and so generate less ATP than NADH (≈1.5 vs 2.5 ATP).

**2.** (a) NADH → Complex I → ubiquinone (Q) → Complex III → cytochrome c → Complex IV → O₂. (b) The two mobile carriers are ubiquinone (Q) and cytochrome c. (c) At Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase), where electrons combine with O₂ and H⁺ to form water.

**3.** (a) Electron flow stops — with Complex IV blocked, electrons cannot reach O₂, so the whole chain backs up and halts. (b) NADH cannot be re-oxidised to NAD⁺ (the chain is jammed), so NADH accumulates. (c) ATP synthesis falls sharply: no electron flow → no proton pumping → the gradient collapses → ATP synthase slows/stops. (d) Oxygen consumption stops (O₂ is no longer being reduced to water). (e) Confirming observation: oxygen uptake ceases even though glucose/NADH is still present — glycolysis (cytosolic, O₂-independent) would continue to make a little ATP and could still produce pyruvate/lactate, so a halt in O₂ consumption pinpoints the block to the respiratory chain rather than glycolysis.

## Set 2 — Chemiosmosis, ATP Synthase and Overall ATP Yield

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Chemiosmosis: the proton gradient built by the chain stores potential energy. Protons flow back down their electrochemical gradient (from the intermembrane space into the matrix) through the only available channel — ATP synthase. This proton flow drives the enzyme, coupling the energy of the gradient to the phosphorylation of ADP: ADP + Pᵢ → ATP.

**2.** H⁺ ions flow down their gradient into a channel in the stator and then into binding sites on the rotor, changing the shape of its subunits so the rotor spins within the membrane. Each H⁺ makes one turn, then exits through a second stator channel into the matrix. The spinning rotor turns an internal rod (a stalk) extending into the stationary catalytic knob; this turning activates catalytic sites in the knob that produce ATP from ADP + Pᵢ.

**3.** C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂ → 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O + energy (ATP), with ΔG ≈ –686 kcal/mol.

**4.** Substrate-level phosphorylation gives a net 2 ATP in glycolysis and 2 ATP in the citric acid cycle (4 ATP). Oxidative phosphorylation gives the rest: using simplified values 10 NADH × 3 = 30 and 2 FADH₂ × 2 = 4 (≈ 34), minus ~2 ATP used to shuttle cytosolic NADH electrons in → about 32–36 ATP total.

**5.** Any two of: (i) NADH/FADH₂ yield non-integer amounts (≈2.5 and ≈1.5 ATP), so totals are not whole numbers; (ii) cytosolic NADH costs energy to shuttle into the mitochondrion, lowering its yield (≈1.5); (iii) some of the gradient is used for other transport work, and the gradient is not perfectly coupled to ATP synthase — 'biology is a science of exceptions and variations'. (Realistic net ≈ 30 ATP.)

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. Protons flow down their gradient from the intermembrane space back into the matrix, and that flow powers ATP synthase.

**2.** Yes. ATP synthase catalyses ADP + Pᵢ → ATP.

**3.** No. Most ATP comes from oxidative phosphorylation; only 4 of ~30–32 come from substrate-level phosphorylation.

**4.** No. 57 is the thermodynamic maximum (686 ÷ 12) assuming 100% efficiency; real cells reach only ~30 because energy is lost as heat.

**5.** Yes. With every energy transformation some usable (free) energy becomes unusable energy/heat, increasing entropy.

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-A, 2-B, 3-A, 4-A, 5-B, 6-B, 7-B, 8-C, 9-C, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Maximum ≈ 57 ATP. (b) 686 kcal ÷ 12 kcal per ATP = 57.2, i.e. about 57 ATP. (c) This assumes 100% of the free energy is captured in ATP bonds. By the second law of thermodynamics, every energy transformation loses some usable energy as heat, so no real process can be 100% efficient; the chain and shuttles are also imperfectly coupled, so cells reach only ~30 ATP.

**2.** (a) Efficiency ≈ 30 ÷ 57 ≈ 0.53 = ~53%. (b) Equivalently (30 × 12) ÷ 686 = 360 ÷ 686 ≈ 0.525 = ~52–53%. (c) The other ~47% is released as heat (unusable energy), which raises the entropy of the surroundings. This obeys the second law (free energy decreases, entropy increases) but does NOT violate the first law — the total energy is conserved; it is simply redistributed between ATP (usable) and heat (unusable).

**3.** (a) No — 57 ATP would require ~100% efficiency, which is forbidden by the second law (some energy must be lost as heat). 57 is the theoretical ceiling, not an achievable yield. (b) The realistic biological yield is the ~30–32 range (the lecture's accepted figures are ~30 net, with ~32 as the simplified whole-number value); the slogan reminds us that exact numbers vary with cell type, shuttle used and conditions, so we quote a range rather than one fixed integer. (c) Balance sheet (realistic): glycolysis 2 ATP (substrate-level) + citric acid cycle 2 ATP (substrate-level) = 4; oxidative phosphorylation = 10 NADH × 2.5 = 25, plus 2 FADH₂ × 1.5 = 3, = 28. Total ≈ 4 + 28 = 32, and after subtracting the cost of shuttling cytosolic NADH the net lands at about 30 ATP.

## Set 3 — Energy without Oxygen: Anaerobic Respiration and Fermentation

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Anaerobic respiration DOES use an electron transport chain but with a final electron acceptor OTHER than O₂ (e.g. SO₄²⁻, Fe³⁺, CO₂); it occurs in certain prokaryotes. Fermentation uses NO electron transport chain and NO inorganic terminal acceptor — electrons are passed to an organic molecule (pyruvate or acetaldehyde) derived from the nutrient itself.

**2.** Glycolysis needs NAD⁺ as the oxidising agent in its oxidation step; without recycling, the small pool of NAD⁺ is quickly used up (all converted to NADH) and glycolysis — and therefore ATP production — stops. Fermentation regenerates NAD⁺ by transferring the hydrogen/electrons from NADH onto pyruvate (→ lactate) or onto acetaldehyde (→ ethanol), turning NADH back into NAD⁺.

**3.** (a) Lactic acid fermentation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 2 ADP + 2 Pᵢ → 2 lactate + 2 ATP; enzyme = lactate dehydrogenase. (b) Alcoholic fermentation: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 2 ADP + 2 Pᵢ → 2 ethanol + 2 CO₂ + 2 ATP; enzymes = pyruvate decarboxylase (removes CO₂ → acetaldehyde) and alcohol dehydrogenase (acetaldehyde → ethanol).

**4.** Glycolysis + fermentation = 2 ATP (net); glycolysis + aerobic respiration = about 32 ATP. The difference arises because in fermentation glucose is only PARTIALLY oxidised — a great deal of chemical energy remains locked in lactate or ethanol — whereas aerobic respiration FULLY oxidises glucose to CO₂, releasing far more energy for ATP synthesis.

**5.** Examples: sulfate (SO₄²⁻), ferric iron (Fe³⁺) and carbon dioxide (CO₂). The organisms (many bacteria and archaea) live in environments where O₂ is scarce or absent — e.g. waterlogged sediments, deep soils, the gut, sulfur-rich waters (the lecture's H₂S warning sign).

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. In fermentation the electron acceptor is an organic molecule (pyruvate → lactate, or acetaldehyde → ethanol).

**2.** Yes. The only ATP in fermentation is the net 2 ATP made by substrate-level phosphorylation during glycolysis.

**3.** Yes. Net 2 ATP per glucose.

**4.** Yes. Alcoholic fermentation releases CO₂ (via pyruvate decarboxylase); lactic acid fermentation does not.

**5.** Yes. Anaerobic respirers keep an electron transport chain — they simply use a non-oxygen terminal acceptor. (Fermenters do not.)

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-B, 2-C, 3-B, 4-B, 5-A, 6-B, 7-B, 8-C, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Glycolysis oxidises glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate using NAD⁺; if NAD⁺ is not regenerated, the cell runs out of this oxidising agent (it is all trapped as NADH), the oxidation step cannot proceed, and glycolysis halts — stopping ATP production. (b) Fermentation hands the electrons/H from NADH to pyruvate (forming lactate) or to acetaldehyde (forming ethanol), converting NADH back to NAD⁺. (c) ATP production would stop within seconds: with no O₂ (no oxidative phosphorylation) and no NAD⁺ regeneration, glycolysis cannot turn over, so even the small 2-ATP substrate-level supply ceases and the cell rapidly runs out of ATP.

**2.** (a) Lactic acid fermentation → 2 lactate (3-carbon, no gas); alcoholic fermentation → 2 ethanol + 2 CO₂. (b) Alcoholic fermentation releases a gas — carbon dioxide (CO₂). (c) The 150/210 kJ figures are the total free energy released by the overall reaction, but most of that is lost as heat; only enough to make a net 2 ATP per glucose is actually captured — energy released is not the same as energy captured as ATP. (d) Experiment: set up two flasks of glucose solution, one inoculated with yeast and one with a lactic-acid bacterium (or muscle extract), both sealed and kept anaerobic, each connected to a tube leading into limewater. Expected observation: the yeast flask produces gas bubbles that turn limewater milky (CO₂) and smells of alcohol; the lactic-acid flask produces little or no gas and the medium becomes acidic (turns an indicator). Gas production → alcoholic fermentation; no gas + acidification → lactic acid fermentation.

**3.** (a) 32 ÷ 2 = 16-fold more ATP per glucose from aerobic respiration. (b) Trade-off: fermentation regenerates NAD⁺ and supplies ATP very fast and without O₂, letting the muscle keep contracting during intense exertion — but it is highly inefficient (only 2 ATP) and produces lactate that lowers pH/causes fatigue; it sacrifices yield for speed and O₂-independence. (c) Because glucose is only partially oxidised, much of its chemical energy is still stored in the C–H/C–C bonds of lactate. When O₂ returns, lactate can be oxidised back to pyruvate (by lactate dehydrogenase) and fed into aerobic respiration, recovering that remaining energy as additional ATP.
