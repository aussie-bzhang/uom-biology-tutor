# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Glycolysis

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm (cytosol). Starting molecule: glucose (6-carbon). End products: 2 pyruvate (3-carbon each), plus 2 ATP and 2 NADH. It occurs in 10 steps.

**2.** Energy-investment phase = steps 1–5 (2 ATP are used/invested to phosphorylate and split the sugar). Energy-payoff phase = steps 6–10 (4 ATP and 2 NADH are produced).

**3.** Per glucose: 2 ATP used, 4 ATP formed → net 2 ATP; 2 NADH formed; glucose → 2 pyruvate (+ 2 H2O).

**4.** Phosphofructokinase (PFK). Activated by ADP/AMP (low energy signal); inhibited by ATP and by citrate (high energy / well-supplied signal).

**5.** Aerobic (cellular respiration): pyruvate is fully oxidised; waste products H2O and CO2; net ~32 ATP per glucose. Anaerobic (fermentation): incomplete oxidation; pyruvate → lactic acid (lactate) or ethanol + CO2; net only 2 ATP per glucose.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm.

**2.** No — glycolysis can proceed without oxygen (it can be anaerobic).

**3.** Yes — PFK is inhibited by ATP and citrate.

**4.** No — the NET yield is 2 ATP (4 formed minus 2 used); the gross is 4.

**5.** Yes — the ATP-forming steps (by phosphoglycerokinase and pyruvate kinase) are substrate-level phosphorylation.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) Gross ATP = 4 (formed in the payoff phase). (b) Net ATP = 4 − 2 = 2. (c) 2 NADH and 2 pyruvate per glucose.

**2.** Glycolysis is considered ancient because: (i) it does not require oxygen (early Earth had little/no free O2); (ii) it occurs in the cytosol and needs no membrane-bound organelles (which early prokaryotes lacked); (iii) it is essentially universal — found in almost all organisms — suggesting it arose very early and was conserved. It uses only soluble enzymes and simple substrates.

**3.** Initial steps (energy investment, steps 1–5): mainly phosphorylation reactions that CONSUME ATP (2 ATP invested) to phosphorylate glucose and split the 6-carbon sugar into two 3-carbon sugars (G3P). Later steps (energy payoff, steps 6–10): oxidation and substrate-level phosphorylation reactions that RELEASE energy, producing NADH (by oxidising G3P) and ATP (4 ATP formed). So the early steps invest energy to prime the molecule; the later steps harvest energy.

## Set 2 — The Citric Acid (TCA) Cycle

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** It occurs in the mitochondria (mitochondrial matrix). Starting input: the acetyl group of acetyl CoA (2 carbons). It has 8 reactions/steps.

**2.** Per ONE acetyl CoA: 2 CO2, 1 GTP, 3 NADH and 1 FADH2 (with regeneration of oxaloacetate).

**3.** In step 1 the acetyl group combines with oxaloacetate (catalysed by citrate synthase) to form citrate. Oxaloacetate is regenerated in the last step. Regeneration is essential because the pathway is cyclic — oxaloacetate must be available again to accept the next acetyl group, or the cycle would stop.

**4.** Any three of: glucose (via pyruvate), fatty acids (via beta-oxidation), some amino acids, glycerol, glycogen. All can supply acetyl CoA or carbon to the cycle.

**5.** GTP can transfer its high-energy phosphate group directly to ADP, forming ATP (a direct phosphate transfer / substrate-level phosphorylation).

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — in the mitochondrial matrix.

**2.** Yes — the acetyl group is fully oxidised to 2 CO2.

**3.** Yes — oxaloacetate is regenerated in the last step.

**4.** Yes — some amino acids enter the cycle directly as intermediates.

**5.** No — it is a cyclic pathway, not linear.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** For the FULL oxidation of one glucose (lecture figures): (a) 6 CO2; (b) 10 NADH; (c) 2 FADH2; (d) 4 ATP (substrate-level — 2 from glycolysis + 2 (as GTP→ATP) from the two turns of the cycle). [The bulk of the energy is in the 10 NADH and 2 FADH2, which the electron transport chain later uses.]

**2.** The cycle does not halt because intermediates that are withdrawn are continually REPLENISHED by anaplerotic (“filling-up”) reactions — e.g. pyruvate can be converted to oxaloacetate (by pyruvate carboxylase), and various amino acids feed carbon back into cycle intermediates. These top-up reactions keep oxaloacetate and the other intermediates available so the cycle keeps turning.

## Set 3 — Cellular Respiration Overview and Metabolic Integration

### Part A. Short-answer

**1.** (1) Glycolysis — cytosol; (2) Formation of acetyl CoA (pyruvate oxidation) — mitochondria; (3) Citric acid cycle — mitochondria (matrix); (4) Electron transport and chemiosmosis — mitochondria (inner membrane).

**2.** Both ultimately feed in as acetyl CoA (carbohydrate → glycolysis → pyruvate → acetyl CoA; fat → beta-oxidation → acetyl CoA).

**3.** Most of the free energy is captured in reduced electron carriers — NADH and FADH2 — which the electron transport chain later uses to make most of the ATP (only ~1 ATP/GTP is made directly per acetyl CoA).

**4.** Catabolic role: they break down fuels and funnel electrons toward oxygen to release energy (ATP). Anabolic role: their intermediates are withdrawn as precursors to build the cell’s own molecules (amino acids, nucleotides, lipids, sugars). So the same pathways both supply energy and supply building blocks.

**5.** Amino acids must first be deaminated (the amino group removed, e.g. as NH3/NH4+) before their carbon skeletons can be oxidised in the cycle. Fatty acids undergo beta-oxidation into 2-carbon fragments and enter the cycle as acetyl CoA.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — glycolysis in cytosol, citric acid cycle in mitochondria.

**2.** Yes — both ultimately produce acetyl CoA.

**3.** Yes — amino acids must be deaminated first.

**4.** Yes — allosteric enzymes control key points (e.g. phosphofructokinase).

**5.** No — they also supply precursors for biosynthesis (anabolism).
