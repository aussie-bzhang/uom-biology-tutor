# Chapter 23 — Genetic Analysis of Single-Gene Traits

*Topic 3: Genes and Heredity | Week 8, Lecture 23 | A/Prof Hayley Bugeja | University of Melbourne, School of BioSciences*

Three sets of review questions (English). Each set: 5 Short-Answer + 5 Yes/No + 10 Multiple-Choice + 3 Applied (genetics-problem / pedigree / probability) questions. Answer key for all three sets is at the end of the document.

*Key idea: Different crosses answer different questions. A TEST CROSS mates an individual of unknown genotype (B/?) with a homozygous-recessive tester (b/b): the offspring directly reveal the unknown allele (all dominant → B/B; 1:1 → B/b). A BACK CROSS mates an F1 hybrid back to a parental genotype. A RECIPROCAL CROSS swaps which sex carries which trait (♀ trait × ♂ normal, then ♀ normal × ♂ trait): if the two reciprocal crosses give DIFFERENT results in sons vs daughters, the gene is SEX-LINKED (X-linked); if they give the same result regardless of parent sex, it is AUTOSOMAL. X-linked recessive traits appear more often in males (XY are hemizygous). PEDIGREES use squares (♂), circles (♀) and filled symbols (affected); to find the mode of inheritance, look for the ONE individual that rules out the alternatives (e.g. an affected child from two unaffected parents → recessive; an affected daughter with an unaffected father rules OUT X-linked recessive). Sex-determination systems vary: XX/XY (mammals, Drosophila), XX/X0 (grasshoppers), ZW/ZZ (birds), haplodiploidy (bees).*

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** A test cross mates an individual of unknown genotype (e.g. B/\_) with a tester that is homozygous recessive (b/b). The tester must be b/b because it contributes only recessive (b) alleles, so the offspring phenotypes are determined entirely by the allele the unknown parent contributes — letting you ‘read’ that allele directly. If all offspring show the dominant phenotype, the unknown parent is homozygous (B/B); if offspring are 1 dominant : 1 recessive, the unknown parent is heterozygous (B/b).

**2.** A back cross mates an F1 individual with one of the PARENTAL genotypes (either parent). A test cross specifically uses the homozygous-recessive parent/genotype. So a back cross to a homozygous-recessive parent IS a test cross; a back cross to the homozygous-dominant parent is not (it cannot reveal a hidden recessive allele in the same way).

**3.** A reciprocal cross repeats a cross with the sexes of the phenotypes swapped (♀ trait × ♂ normal, then ♀ normal × ♂ trait). If the two crosses give the SAME outcome regardless of which parent carried the trait → the gene is autosomal. If the outcomes DIFFER between the two crosses (e.g. different ratios in sons vs daughters) → the gene is sex-linked (X-linked).

**4.** Outcome 1: all offspring dominant phenotype → unknown parent is B/B (homozygous dominant). Outcome 2: 1:1 dominant : recessive offspring → unknown parent is B/b (heterozygous), because half its gametes carry b which, with the tester’s b, gives recessive (b/b) offspring.

**5.** Phenotype alone cannot distinguish B/B from B/b (both look dominant). A test cross exposes the hidden allele because the b/b tester contributes only b, so any b in the unknown parent shows up as recessive offspring. (Selfing also works in plants but is impossible in many animals and gives a 3:1 that is harder to read in small samples; the test cross gives a cleaner 1:1 vs all-dominant readout.)

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — the tester is homozygous recessive (b/b).

**2.** Yes — all-dominant offspring indicate the unknown parent is B/B (with the caveat that a small sample could mislead).

**3.** Yes — a 1:1 ratio indicates the unknown parent is B/b.

**4.** No — a back cross can be to EITHER parental genotype; only the cross to the homozygous-recessive parent is a test cross.

**5.** No — for an autosomal gene, reciprocal crosses give the same result regardless of which parent carries the trait.

**2.** B — reveals the unknown genotype.

**4.** B — 1:1 → B/b.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) 4 black : 4 brown = 1:1 → black parent is B/b. Punnett B/b × b/b: B×b → B/b (black); b×b → b/b (brown) → ½ black : ½ brown. (b) All-black offspring → most likely B/B; but you cannot be 100% certain — a B/b parent could, by chance, produce 8 black puppies (probability (½)⁸ ≈ 0.004 that all 8 are black even though it is B/b... actually for B/b×b/b each offspring is ½ black, so 8 black in a row has probability (½)⁸ ≈ 0.4% — unlikely but possible). (c) A larger litter lowers the chance that a heterozygote ‘accidentally’ produces only dominant offspring, increasing confidence that all-dominant truly means B/B.

**2.** (a) 94 crosses: F1 all black and F2 3:1 → the black parent was homozygous BB (BB × bb → all Bb black F1; Bb × Bb → 3 black : 1 white F2). This also confirms black is DOMINANT to white. (b) 6 crosses: F1 ½ black : ½ white → those black parents were heterozygous Bb (Bb × bb → ½ Bb black : ½ bb white) — i.e. those 6 ‘black’ animals were Bb, not BB. (c) In the 6 exceptional cases the F2 blacks are Bb and the F2 whites are bb. Crossing black F2 (Bb) × white F2 (bb) → ½ Bb (black) : ½ bb (white) = 1:1.

**3.** (a) Cross 1: white-eyed ♀ × red-eyed ♂. Cross 2 (reciprocal): red-eyed ♀ × white-eyed ♂. (b) If AUTOSOMAL (red R dominant): both reciprocal crosses give all-red F1 in both sons and daughters (RR × rr or rr × RR → all Rr red). (c) If X-LINKED recessive: Cross 1 (XʳXʳ white ♀ × XᴿY red ♂) → daughters XᴿXʳ red (carriers), sons XʳY white — sons resemble their mother. Cross 2 (XᴿXᴿ red ♀ × XʳY white ♂) → all F1 red (daughters XᴿXʳ, sons XᴿY). Diagnostic: under X-linkage the two reciprocal crosses give DIFFERENT F1 (Cross 1 produces white-eyed SONS), whereas autosomal inheritance gives all-red F1 in both crosses. So look at whether the white-♀ × red-♂ cross produces white-eyed sons.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** XX/XY: females are XX (homogametic — all eggs carry an X); males are XY (heterogametic — sperm carry either X or Y). ‘Heterogametic’ means producing two different kinds of gamete with respect to the sex chromosome, so the heterogametic sex (the male here) determines offspring sex.

**2.** XX/XY: ♀ XX, ♂ XY — e.g. humans, Drosophila (♂ heterogametic). XX/X0: ♀ XX, ♂ X0 (one X, no second sex chromosome) — e.g. grasshoppers (♂ heterogametic). ZW/ZZ: ♀ ZW, ♂ ZZ — e.g. birds, some moths (♀ heterogametic). Haplodiploidy: ♀ diploid (from fertilised eggs), ♂ haploid (from unfertilised eggs) — e.g. honeybees/ants.

**3.** Males are hemizygous for X-linked genes (only one X, no homologous allele on the Y). A single recessive allele on that one X is therefore expressed, since there is no second X carrying a dominant allele to mask it. Females (XX) need two copies of the recessive allele to be affected, which is rarer; hence the trait is seen more often in males.

**4.** Autosomal recessive: an affected mother (aa) passes one a to ALL children; whether they are affected depends on the father’s allele, and sons and daughters are affected equally (no sex difference). X-linked recessive: an affected mother (XᵃXᵃ) passes an Xᵃ to all children — ALL her sons (XᵃY) are affected, while daughters are affected only if the father is also affected (otherwise they are carriers). So the X-linked pattern is sex-dependent; the autosomal pattern is not.

**5.** X-linked alleles are written ON the chromosome symbol: Xᴬ (dominant), Xᵃ (recessive), with Y carrying no copy (e.g. male XᵃY). Autosomal alleles are written without a chromosome symbol (A, a). Writing the X/Y makes hemizygosity explicit and shows why males and females differ — essential for tracking sex-linked inheritance.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — XY males are heterogametic.

**2.** Yes — in ZW/ZZ birds the female (ZW) is heterogametic.

**3.** Yes — drones develop from unfertilised eggs and are haploid.

**4.** Yes — a hemizygous male expresses a single X-linked recessive allele.

**5.** Yes — an XᵃXᵃ mother gives an Xᵃ to every son, so all sons are affected (XᵃY).

### Part C. Multiple-Choice

**1.** B — XY.

**2.** B — produces two kinds of sex-chromosome gamete.

**6.** B — all sons affected (XᵃY).

**7.** B — all daughters carriers (XᴬXᵃ), unaffected.

**8.** B — all Aa carriers, regardless of sex (autosomal).

**9.** B — XᵃY.

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) Carrier mother XᶜXᶜ × colour-blind father XᶜY. Mother gametes Xᶜ, Xᶜ; father gametes Xᶜ, Y. Punnett: XᶜXᶜ, XᶜY / XᶜXᶜ, XᶜY. (b) Daughters: ½ XᶜXᶜ (carrier, normal vision) : ½ XᶜXᶜ (colour-blind). Sons: ½ XᶜY (normal) : ½ XᶜY (colour-blind). (c) Affected children = ¼ XᶜXᶜ + ¼ XᶜY = ½ of ALL children are colour-blind; among daughters, ½ are colour-blind.

**2.** (a) Autosomal recessive: Cross A (aa ♀ × AA ♂) and Cross B (AA ♀ × aa ♂) both give all-Aa, all-NORMAL F1 in both sons and daughters — identical results. (b) X-linked recessive: Cross A (XᵃXᵃ ♀ × XᴬY ♂) → daughters XᴬXᵃ (normal carriers), sons XᵃY (AFFECTED). Cross B (XᴬXᴬ ♀ × XᵃY ♂) → daughters XᴬXᵃ (carriers, normal), sons XᴬY (normal) → all F1 normal. (c) Diagnostic: under X-linkage, the affected-♀ × normal-♂ cross (Cross A) produces AFFECTED SONS while daughters are normal; under autosomal inheritance all F1 are normal in both crosses. So look for affected sons in the affected-mother cross.

**3.** (a) Human/Drosophila: ♀ XX, ♂ XY; heterogametic = male. (b) Grasshopper: ♀ XX, ♂ X0; heterogametic = male. (c) Bird/moth: ♀ ZW, ♂ ZZ; heterogametic = female. (d) Honeybee: ♀ diploid, ♂ haploid; the ♂ has no ‘partner’ chromosome set (functionally hemizygous for everything). In ZW/ZZ, the male is ZZ (two Z’s) and the female is ZW (one Z). A recessive allele on the Z is masked in females… wait — reasoning: the female is hemizygous for Z-linked genes (only one Z), so a single recessive Z-linked allele is expressed in FEMALES, making the trait more common in the heterogametic FEMALE — the mirror image of XX/XY where the heterogametic MALE shows X-linked recessives more often. (The key point: the heterogametic sex — male in XY, female in ZW — is the one that expresses sex-linked recessives more readily.)

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** Squares = males, circles = females, diamonds = sex unspecified; a FILLED (shaded) symbol = affected, an open symbol = unaffected. Generations are labelled with Roman numerals (I, II, III…) from top to bottom, and individuals within a generation are numbered left to right (1, 2, 3…). A horizontal line joins mates; vertical/branching lines connect parents to offspring.

**2.** Autosomal recessive rule: if two UNAFFECTED parents produce an AFFECTED child, the trait must be recessive (the parents are both heterozygous carriers, Aa × Aa, and the child is aa). It is autosomal (not X-linked) if affected individuals of both sexes appear and there is no sex bias. The unaffected-parents-to-affected-child pattern reveals recessivity because a dominant trait cannot ‘skip’ — an affected child would need an affected parent.

**3.** X-linked recessive can be ruled out if an AFFECTED FEMALE has an UNAFFECTED FATHER: an affected female must be XᵃXᵃ, so she must have received an Xᵃ from her father, who would therefore be XᵃY and affected himself. If the father is unaffected, the trait cannot be X-linked recessive (it is more likely autosomal). Similarly, an affected mother whose sons are all unaffected argues against X-linked recessive (all her sons should be affected).

**4.** Pedigrees are limited by small family sizes and chance, so a given pattern may be consistent with more than one mode of inheritance. The strategy is to find the ONE individual whose phenotype is incompatible with all modes except one — e.g. an affected child of unaffected parents excludes dominant; an affected daughter of an unaffected father excludes X-linked recessive — and use that to narrow to the single most likely mode.

**5.** Multiplication (‘and’): the probability that several independent events ALL happen is the product of their individual probabilities (e.g. P(parent1 passes a) × P(parent2 passes a)). Addition (‘or’): the probability of EITHER of two mutually exclusive outcomes is the sum of their probabilities. To find the chance a child shows a recessive trait you multiply the chance each parent transmits the recessive allele, summing over the alternative parental genotype scenarios where needed.
