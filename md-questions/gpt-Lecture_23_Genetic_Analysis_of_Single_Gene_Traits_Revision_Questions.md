**Genetic Analysis Involving Single-Gene Traits**

*Three practice sets: short-answer, Yes/No, MCQ, and biochemical/application questions*

# Core ideas to revise before attempting the questions

* A hybrid cross, test cross, back cross and reciprocal cross answer different genetic questions.
* A test cross uses a homozygous recessive tester to reveal the genotype of an individual with a dominant phenotype.
* A back cross crosses a hybrid offspring back to one of its parents or to a genotype similar to a parent.
* A reciprocal cross swaps which parent carries the dominant or recessive phenotype and is useful for detecting sex-linked inheritance.
* Autosomal and X-linked traits can give different offspring patterns, especially when comparing sons and daughters.
* Pedigrees assemble family relationships and affected status to infer likely modes of inheritance and estimate probabilities.

## Practice Set 2 Answers

### A. Short-answer questions

1. Autosomal inheritance involves genes on non-sex chromosomes and usually affects males and females similarly. X-linked inheritance involves genes on the X chromosome and can produce sex-specific transmission patterns.

2. Males in an XY system have one X chromosome, so for most X-linked loci they carry only one allele rather than two.

3. If reciprocal crosses produce different offspring patterns depending on which sex carries the allele, this suggests sex-linked inheritance. Autosomal crosses often give the same outcome in reciprocal crosses, assuming no other effects.

4. A carrier mother can pass the recessive X-linked allele to a son. Because the son has only one X chromosome, the recessive allele is expressed if inherited.

5. Fathers pass the Y chromosome to sons and the X chromosome to daughters, so an X-linked allele from the father goes to daughters, not sons.
