# Suggested Answers

These answers are concise model answers. Students should receive credit for equivalent explanations that use correct biological reasoning.

## Set 1

Short-answer questions:

1. Large animals have low SA:V ratios and long internal diffusion distances; diffusion alone is too slow to supply all cells and remove wastes.

2. Bulk flow moves fluid over long distances, continually replacing fluid near exchange surfaces and cells, thereby maintaining steep concentration gradients.

3. Gas exchange, nutrient delivery, waste removal, and cell/tissue communication or coordination.

4. Pump: drives flow; fluid: carries substances; vessels/spaces: route flow; exchange surfaces: allow diffusion; return mechanisms/valves: return fluid and direct flow.

5. Their body plan keeps most cells close to external water or internal cavities, so diffusion distance is short enough for their metabolic needs.

Yes/No questions: 1. Yes; 2. No; 3. Yes; 4. No; 5. Yes

MCQ answers: 1. B; 2. A; 3. D; 4. B; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. B; 9. A; 10. A

Application questions:

1. 1 cm cube: SA = 6 cm2, V = 1 cm3, SA:V = 6. 5 cm cube: SA = 150 cm2, V = 125 cm3, SA:V = 1.2. The larger model has less exchange surface per unit volume and longer internal distances.

2. It is less likely to need a complex circulatory system because all cells are close to the external environment; diffusion can meet needs if metabolic demand is modest.

3. Bulk flow refreshes oxygen-rich fluid and removes CO2-rich fluid, preserving a gradient. If the pump stops, gradients collapse and diffusion slows, reducing oxygen delivery.

## Set 2

Short-answer questions:

1. Open system: fluid leaves vessels or spaces and bathes tissues directly; hemolymph functions as both transport and extracellular fluid.

2. Closed system: blood remains in vessels, while interstitial fluid surrounds cells; exchange occurs across capillary beds.

3. Open systems are generally lower pressure, lower flow, less precise, and often more energy efficient; closed systems have higher pressure, faster flow, and more directed delivery.

4. They deliver oxygen/nutrients rapidly, allow flow redistribution, and retain cells/large molecules that increase transport capacity.

5. Vertebrates show a trend from single-circuit fish circulation to more separated double circuits in terrestrial vertebrates, culminating in complete separation in birds and mammals.

Yes/No questions: 1. Yes; 2. Yes; 3. No; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

MCQ answers: 1. A; 2. A; 3. A; 4. A; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. A; 10. A

Application questions:

1. Open: hemolymph, open body cavity, lower pressure, lower energy, less precise flow, arthropods/non-cephalopod molluscs. Closed: blood plus interstitial fluid, vessels/capillaries, higher pressure, greater energy, directed flow, annelids/vertebrates.

2. The tracheal system delivers gases directly to tissues, so hemolymph does not need to carry most oxygen; open circulation can still serve nutrient/waste transport.

3. Fish < amphibian/lungfish-like partial separation < crocodilian < mammal/bird. Greater separation improves oxygen delivery and supports higher metabolic rates.

## Set 3

Short-answer questions:

1. Hydrostatic pressure is fluid pressure in vessels produced by the pump; it is highest near the arterial side leaving the heart.

2. Plasma proteins remain in blood and draw water into capillaries by osmosis, creating blood osmotic pressure.

3. Filtration is net movement of fluid out of capillaries; reabsorption is net movement back into capillaries.

4. It is closer to the heart, so capillary hydrostatic pressure is higher and can exceed inward osmotic forces.

5. If outward filtration exceeds inward reabsorption and lymphatic return, excess fluid accumulates in tissues, causing oedema.

Yes/No questions: 1. Yes; 2. No; 3. Yes; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

MCQ answers: 1. A; 2. A; 3. A; 4. A; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. A; 10. A

Application questions:

1. Net = 40 - 25 = 15 mmHg outward; filtration occurs.

2. Net = 16 - 25 = -9 mmHg, so 9 mmHg inward; reabsorption occurs.

3. Low plasma proteins reduce blood osmotic pressure, weakening reabsorption. More fluid remains in interstitial spaces, increasing oedema risk.
