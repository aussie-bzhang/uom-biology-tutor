## Core concepts to remember before you start

* **What plants must transport:** water + dissolved minerals taken up by roots and moved UP to the shoot (xylem); sugars (sucrose) made in leaves moved to non-photosynthetic tissues (phloem). Water is needed for photosynthesis, transporting solutes, cooling (transpiration/latent heat) and structural support (turgor pressure).
* **Vascular tissue** (xylem + phloem) evolved ~400 mya in vascular plants (lycophytes, ferns, gymnosperms, angiosperms); it allows long-distance transport AND rigid structural support (lignified tracheids), which is why trees can grow very tall (e.g. the redwood Hyperion ~115.9 m).
* **Water potential (WP) Golden Rule:** water always moves across a selectively permeable membrane towards LOWER (more negative) water potential. WP = WPS (solute) + WPP (pressure). ↑ solute → ↓ (more negative) WP; ↑ internal/turgor pressure → ↑ WP. In a plant cell, turgor pressure = pressure potential (WPP).
* **Two root pathways:** apoplast (cell walls + intercellular spaces, no membranes crossed — fast, unregulated) and symplast (interconnected cytoplasm via plasmodesmata, must cross a membrane to enter — slow, regulated). At the endodermis the **Casparian strip** blocks the apoplast, forcing water/solutes through a membrane (symplast) → selective uptake.
* **Xylem (water) is PASSIVE:** dead, lignified tubular cells (tracheids/vessel elements). The transpiration–cohesion–tension mechanism: evaporation from leaves → tension → cohesion (water–water H-bonds) + adhesion (water–wall) keep a continuous column and pull water UP. Pressure-potential gradient: soil > root > stem > leaf > atmosphere (most negative). Gravity costs −0.01 MPa per metre of height.
* **Phloem (sugar) is ACTIVE at loading:** living sieve-tube elements (sieve plates, plasmodesmata; no nucleus/ribosomes/Golgi/cytoskeleton) supported by companion cells. Mass-flow hypothesis: sucrose loaded at source (needs ATP) → WP more negative → water enters by osmosis → high WPP → bulk flow to sink → unloaded at sink → water leaves. Flow can be bidirectional depending on source/sink positions.

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A — Short answer

**1.** Plants must obtain water and dissolved minerals from the soil via the roots and move them up to the shoot, and must move sugars from the leaves to non-photosynthetic tissues. Water is required for (1) photosynthesis in the leaves, (2) transporting solutes between plant organs, (3) cooling the plant (transpiration), and (4) structural support (turgor pressure). General flow: water and mineral nutrients move up (roots → shoot, in the xylem); sugars move from source to sink (generally leaves → roots, in the phloem, but bidirectional).

**2.** Vascular tissue provides long-distance transport of water and nutrients AND rigid structural support. The water-conducting cells (tracheids/vessel elements) have lignified secondary walls; lignin is strong, so the same tissue that conducts water also holds the plant upright. This support lets plants grow tall (e.g. redwoods over 100 m), raising leaves into the light. A consequence is that water must be lifted great heights against gravity, which the transpiration-cohesion-tension mechanism achieves.

**3.** Macronutrients are needed in larger amounts — the main ones named are nitrogen and phosphorus; the main micronutrient (needed in tiny amounts) is iron. All are needed to synthesise organic compounds for growth. Nitrogen deficiency: stunted, yellowed (chlorotic), spindly seedlings. Iron deficiency: yellowing (chlorosis), typically of younger/newer leaves, because iron is needed for chlorophyll-related processes.

**4.** A source is a tissue that produces or releases more sugar than it uses (net exporter), e.g. a photosynthesising leaf. A sink is a tissue that consumes or stores sugar (net importer), e.g. roots, fruits, growing tissues. A leaf is usually a source because it makes sugars by photosynthesis; a root is usually a sink because it cannot photosynthesise and needs imported sugar. Flow can be bidirectional because any sieve tube carries sugar from whichever source is loading to whichever sink is unloading, and these can change (e.g. a storage organ can switch from sink to source).

**5.** Turgor pressure (the outward pressure of water-filled cells pushing on the cell wall) keeps cells rigid (turgid) and provides non-woody structural support. When water is lost and turgor pressure falls, cells become flaccid, lose rigidity, and the whole plant wilts (loses cellular support).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1. No** — most large plants cannot rely on direct diffusion (small SA:V, large distances); they need vascular tissue for transport.

**2. No** — mosses and liverworts (non-vascular) diverged earlier; vascular plants appeared later (~400 mya).

**3. Yes** — evaporation of water during transpiration removes latent heat, cooling the plant.

**4. Yes** — nitrogen and phosphorus are the main macronutrients; iron is the main micronutrient.

**5. Yes** — lost turgor makes cells flaccid and causes wilting.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A — Short answer

**1.** Golden Rule: water always moves across a selectively permeable membrane towards regions of lower (more negative) water potential. Equation: WP = WPS + WPP (solute potential + pressure potential). Raising solute concentration makes WPS more negative, lowering WP (drives water in). Raising internal/turgor pressure makes WPP more positive, raising WP (opposes further water entry).

**2.** Apoplast: water/solutes travel through the interconnected cell walls and intercellular spaces without crossing any membrane; movement is rapid but unregulated. Symplast: water/solutes travel through the interconnected cytoplasm of cells via plasmodesmata; to enter the symplast a substance must first cross a cell membrane, so movement is slower but regulated (selective).

**3.** The Casparian strip is a waterproof (suberised) band in the walls of the endodermis (inner layer of the root cortex). It blocks the apoplast pathway, so water and solutes that reached the endodermis via the apoplast are forced to cross a selectively permeable membrane to enter the symplast. Because membranes are selective, the plant can control which solutes are taken up into the vasculature — protecting it from harmful or unwanted ions.

**4.** Xylem is composed of long tubular vessels of dead cells (vessel elements and tracheids) with secondary cell walls of lignin, forming hollow conduits. Cohesion is the attraction between water molecules (hydrogen bonds), which holds the water column together as a continuous thread. Adhesion is the attraction between water molecules and the xylem wall (capillary action), which helps water cling to and climb the vessel walls. Together they maintain an unbroken continuous column.

**5.** (1) Transpiration: water vapour diffuses out of the stomata down a concentration gradient. (2) Water evaporates from the surface of mesophyll cells, creating surface tension. (3) Increased surface tension pulls water out of the leaf veins into the apoplast of the mesophyll. (4) Tension in the leaf veins pulls water up from the stem; cohesion transmits this tension down the continuous column so water is drawn up from the roots (water enters root/xylem by osmosis). The whole mechanism is passive (no metabolic pump).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1. No** — water moves towards LOWER (more negative) water potential, not higher.

**2. Yes** — entry to the symplast requires first crossing a cell membrane (hence it is regulated).

**3. No** — mature conducting xylem cells are dead, hollow, lignified tubes.

**4. Yes** — transpiration-cohesion-tension is passive; it is driven by evaporation, not by an ATP-using pump.

**5. Yes** — the gradient runs soil > root > stem > leaf > atmosphere, with the atmosphere most negative, so water moves up that gradient.

### Part D — Applied

**1. Water-potential calculation.** (a) Cell X: WP = WPS + WPP = −1.0 + 0 = −1.0 MPa. Cell Y: WP = −1.0 + 1.0 = 0 MPa. (b) Water moves from higher (less negative) WP to lower (more negative) WP, i.e. from Cell Y (0 MPa) to Cell X (−1.0 MPa). (c) Cell Y is at full turgor: its WP (0 MPa) equals that of pure water, and its high pressure potential (+1.0) exactly balances its solute potential (−1.0), so there is no driving force for further net water entry into Y — matching the lecture’s ‘high turgor pressure → no net flow’ case.

**2. Potometer experiment.** (a) IV = air movement (fan on vs off); DV = rate of water uptake (distance the bubble/meniscus moves per minute, or volume/time); controlled variables = light, temperature, humidity (other than the fan effect), same shoot/leaf area. (b) Turning the fan on increases transpiration rate: moving air sweeps away the humid layer at the stomata, keeping the water-vapour concentration gradient (inside airspaces vs outside air) steep, so more water vapour diffuses out. (c) Faster evaporation increases tension in the leaf; cohesion transmits this tension down the continuous water column through the xylem all the way to the roots, increasing water uptake from the soil.

**3. Pressure-potential gradient.** (a) Ordering the values: soil −0.3 > root −0.6 > trunk −0.8 > leaf cell walls −1.0 > leaf air spaces −7.0 > outside air −10 to −100 MPa — i.e. it does run from least to most negative soil → root → stem → leaf → atmosphere. (b) Water moves from higher (less negative) to lower (more negative) WP, so it flows up this gradient from soil through the plant and out to the atmosphere. (c) If stomata close on a hot, dry day, transpiration (and the very negative leaf/air WP that pulls water) is reduced, so the gradient weakens and water movement up the xylem slows — conserving water but also reducing CO₂ uptake and cooling.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A — Short answer

**1.** A sieve-tube element is a living cell that retains its end walls (modified into perforated sieve plates) and is connected to neighbours by plasmodesmata, forming a continuous tubular network; it lacks a nucleus, ribosomes, Golgi apparatus and cytoskeleton. Because it has lost much of its machinery, it relies on an adjacent companion cell for metabolites and proteins (‘life-support’), connected by large plasmodesmata allowing free diffusion. It matters that phloem cells are alive (unlike dead xylem cells) because sugar transport depends on living membranes and active loading.

**2.** (1) Loading: sucrose and other solutes are actively transported into companion cells and flow into sieve tubes via plasmodesmata (requires ATP). (2) Sucrose accumulation makes the water potential of the sieve-tube element more negative, so water enters by osmosis from adjacent tissue. (3) The resulting rise in hydrostatic pressure (WPP) pushes the phloem contents (‘mass’) away from the high-pressure region. (4) At the sink, sucrose is unloaded into sink cells, raising the water potential of the sieve-tube element there. (5) Water then leaves the phloem by osmosis, reinforcing the pressure gradient that drives bulk flow from source to sink.

**3.** ATP is required at the loading step (active transport of sucrose into companion cells/sieve tubes at the source). The osmotic steps (water entering at the source, water leaving at the sink) and the resulting bulk flow are passive. Sugar transport is called active overall because it depends on energy-requiring loading to establish the pressure gradient; water transport in the xylem is passive because it is driven by evaporation (transpiration) and cohesion, needing no metabolic pump.

**4.** Loading sucrose raises the solute concentration inside the sieve-tube element, which makes WPS (and therefore the overall water potential) more negative. Water then enters by osmosis from adjacent tissue (including the xylem). Because the sieve tube is enclosed, the incoming water raises the hydrostatic/pressure potential (WPP) at the source; this high pressure pushes the phloem contents towards lower-pressure sinks — driving mass flow.

**5.** Xylem: dead, lignified, hollow cells; carries water and dissolved minerals; energy source is passive (solar-driven evaporation/transpiration); driving force is transpiration-generated tension plus cohesion/adhesion (water pulled UP). Phloem: living sieve-tube elements + companion cells; carries sucrose (and organic solutes) as phloem sap; requires ATP for loading; driving force is a source-to-sink pressure (WPP) gradient set up osmotically (bulk/mass flow, bidirectional).

### Part B — Yes/No

**1. No** — sieve-tube elements are living but lack a nucleus, ribosomes and Golgi (they depend on companion cells).

**2. Yes** — loading sucrose is active and requires ATP.

**3. Yes** — loaded sucrose lowers (makes more negative) the WP, drawing water in by osmosis.

**4. No** — phloem flow is bidirectional, from any source to any sink depending on their locations.

**5. Yes** — unloading at the sink raises the sieve-tube WP, so water leaves by osmosis.

### Part D — Applied

**1. Practice exam figure.** (a) Source-end xylem A: WP = WPS + WPP = −0.1 + (−0.7) = −0.8 MPa. Source-end sieve tube B: WP = −1.7 + 0.6 = −1.1 MPa. Sink-end sieve tube B: WP = −0.7 + 0.3 = −0.4 MPa. (b) At the source, water moves from the xylem (−0.8 MPa) into the sieve tube (−1.1 MPa) because water flows towards the lower (more negative) WP — −1.1 is more negative than −0.8, so yes, water enters the sieve tube. (c) Pressure potential is higher at the source (WPP = 0.6) than at the sink (WPP = 0.3); this pressure difference pushes the phloem contents from the high-pressure source towards the lower-pressure sink — the direction of mass flow.

**2. Girdling experiment.** (a) Sugars accumulate ABOVE the ring (on the leaf/source side), because removing the bark removes the phloem and breaks the source→sink transport route; sugars made in the leaves cannot pass the gap to reach the roots. (b) Water transport to the leaves is NOT immediately stopped, because water travels in the xylem (the inner wood), which is left intact — so the upper part can still receive water for a while. (c) It demonstrates that phloem (outer, in the bark) carries sugars downward to sinks, while xylem (inner wood) carries water upward — two separate transport tissues with separate roles.

**3. Aphid stylet / tracer.** (a) Phloem sap flows out of a cut stylet on its own because the phloem is under positive hydrostatic pressure (high WPP at the source) — the same pressure that drives mass flow forces sap out of the opening. (b) Radiolabelled sucrose (made from ¹⁴CO₂ in the source leaf) will appear progressively in sinks — roots, growing tips, fruits — following the source→sink direction of phloem flow. (c) This supports mass flow because it shows phloem moves under pressure from sugar-loaded sources to sinks, and that sap is pushed out by internal pressure rather than pulled or actively secreted at the stylet.
