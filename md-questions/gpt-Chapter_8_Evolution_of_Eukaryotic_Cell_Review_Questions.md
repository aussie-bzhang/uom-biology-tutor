**Chapter 8 Review Questions
The Evolution of the Eukaryotic Cell**

*Based on University of Melbourne BIOL10008 Lecture 8: The Evolution of the Eukaryotic Cell*

Suggested use: Attempt each set without notes first. Then check the answer key at the end. For MCQs, choose the single best answer unless the question explicitly says otherwise.

* Core themes: eukaryotic organelles; nucleus, mitochondria and chloroplast structure; membrane invagination; primary and secondary endosymbiosis.
* Key structural idea: the nucleus is continuous with the endoplasmic reticulum and may have evolved through invagination of the plasma membrane around an ancestral nucleoid.
* Key evolutionary idea: mitochondria and chloroplasts are double-membrane organelles with bacterial-like features, their own DNA/RNA/ribosomes, and semi-autonomous division.
* Key comparison: primary endosymbiosis involves a prokaryotic endosymbiont; secondary endosymbiosis involves one eukaryote engulfing another eukaryote that already contains a chloroplast.

# Answer Key

## Set 1 Short-answer

1. The nucleus is surrounded by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope. Nuclear pores regulate molecular movement. It contains most cellular DNA, is the site of DNA replication, regulates gene transcription, contains the nucleolus for ribosomal gene transcription/ribosome biogenesis, and is continuous with the ER.

2. An ancestral plasma membrane could have folded inward around the nucleoid. These invaginations could surround the DNA and form a nuclear envelope, while connected membranes could develop into the endoplasmic reticulum and broader endomembrane system.

3. Mitochondria have an outer membrane and a highly folded inner membrane. The inward folds are cristae, which increase surface area for respiration-related proteins. The matrix contains mitochondrial DNA and ribosomes.

4. Mitochondria transform energy in fuel molecules such as glucose into ATP during aerobic respiration. Their inner membrane/cristae contain protein complexes involved in electron transport and ATP production.

5. Evidence includes: bacterial-like morphology; double membranes; semi-autonomy and independent division; their own DNA/RNA and ribosomes; bacterial-like metabolism; and, for some plastids such as Cyanophora, retention of a peptidoglycan wall between membranes.

## Set 1 Application / interpretation

1. It may contain more mitochondria and/or mitochondria with extensive cristae. More mitochondria and greater inner-membrane surface area support more respiration-related protein complexes and ATP production.

2. An endosymbiotic origin is likely, because two membranes, bacterial-like ribosomes, own DNA and semi-autonomous division resemble mitochondria/chloroplasts and their bacterial ancestors.

3. Ancestral prokaryote with nucleoid -> plasma membrane invagination -> membrane surrounds nucleoid -> nuclear envelope/endomembrane system forms.

## Set 2 Short-answer

1. Chloroplasts are double-membrane organelles that carry out photosynthesis. Their inner membrane forms thylakoids, which contain chlorophyll and other pigments that harvest light energy and help convert it into chemical energy.

2. Both are double-membrane organelles that can divide independently and retain some DNA/ribosomes. Mitochondria are associated with aerobic respiration and ATP production; chloroplasts are associated with photosynthesis and conversion of light energy to chemical energy.

3. A photosynthetic cyanobacterium was engulfed by a host cell through endocytosis/phagocytosis but was not digested. It persisted as an endosymbiont, retained its two original membranes after the food vacuole broke down, and transferred some genetic information to the nucleus over time.

4. Primary endosymbiosis involves a prokaryote entering a host cell and becoming an organelle. Secondary endosymbiosis involves a eukaryote engulfing another eukaryote that already contains a chloroplast, producing plastids with extra membranes.

5. Four membranes suggest that the plastid came from a eukaryotic cell that already had a chloroplast and was engulfed by another eukaryote. Extra membranes can represent the engulfed eukaryote and/or phagocytic vacuole history.

## Set 2 Application / interpretation

1. Secondary endosymbiosis is more likely. Primary plastids typically have two membranes, while secondary endosymbiosis can leave plastids with three or four membranes.

2. Mitochondria are linked to respiration because their inner membranes contain the machinery for electron transport and ATP production. Chloroplasts are linked to photosynthesis because their thylakoids contain chlorophyll and pigments that harvest light energy.

3. It supports secondary endosymbiosis because the engulfed eukaryote contributed nuclear, mitochondrial and chloroplastic genetic material, some of which could be transferred to the host nucleus over evolutionary time.
