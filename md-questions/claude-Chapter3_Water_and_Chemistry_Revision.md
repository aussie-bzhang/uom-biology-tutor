## Revision Set 1 — Answers

### A. Short answer

**1.** Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P) and Sulfur (S).

**2.** Proton: relative mass ≈ 1, positive (+) charge. Neutron: relative mass ≈ 1, no charge. Electron: negligible mass, negative (−) charge.

**3.** Electronegativity is the attraction of an atomic nucleus for outer/bonding electrons. (a) It increases across a period because nuclear charge (number of protons) increases while the number of shells stays the same. (b) It decreases down a group because more electron shells are added, so bonding electrons are further from, and more shielded from, the nucleus. The most electronegative elements have the fewest shells and the highest nuclear charge.

**4.** Ionic: ΔEN > 1.9. Polar covalent: ΔEN 0.4–1.9. Non-polar covalent: ΔEN < 0.4.

**5.** In an ionic bond the more electronegative atom effectively “steals” the electron(s), forming permanently charged ions (a cation and an anion). In a polar covalent bond the electrons are still shared (not transferred), but unequally — they sit closer to the more electronegative atom, giving partial charges (δ+ / δ−).

### B. Yes / No

**1.** No — electrons have negligible mass.

**2.** Yes.

**3.** No — one atom effectively takes the electrons; they are not shared.

**4.** No — electronegativity decreases down a group (more shells).

**5.** Yes.

### C. MCQ

**.** 1-B, 2-A, 3-C, 4-B, 5-B, 6-C, 7-B, 8-B, 9-C, 10-C

### D. Calculation

**1.** (a) N–H: 3.0 − 2.1 = 0.9 → polar covalent. (b) K–Cl: 3.1 − 0.8 = 2.3 → ionic. (c) C–C: 2.5 − 2.5 = 0.0 → non-polar covalent. (d) C–O: 3.5 − 2.5 = 1.0 → polar covalent.

**2.** (a) ΔEN = 1.0, which is in the 0.4–1.9 range → polar covalent. (b) Chlorine carries the δ− charge because it is more electronegative (3.1 vs 2.1), so it pulls the shared electrons closer to itself; hydrogen is left δ+.

## Revision Set 2 — Answers

### A. Short answer

**1.** Carbon can form up to 4 covalent bonds. The four arrangements are: (i) 4 single bonds; (ii) 2 single bonds + 1 double bond; (iii) 2 double bonds; (iv) 1 single bond + 1 triple bond.

**2.** A macromolecule is a very large molecule (a polymer) containing thousands or more atoms. Polymers are built by joining many small repeating subunits called monomers. All macromolecules except lipids are polymers of monomers (large lipids are still treated as macromolecules but are not true polymers).

**3.** (i) Dipole-dipole — between polar molecules. (ii) Hydrogen bonding — between molecules where H is bonded to O, F or N and a lone pair on a nearby electronegative atom. (iii) London dispersion (van der Waals) — can occur between any molecules but is the only force between non-polar molecules.

**4.** When hydrogen is covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (O, F or N) the bond is extremely polar and the hydrogen nucleus becomes an exposed (“naked”) positive charge. This exposed proton is then attracted to an unshared (lone) pair of electrons on a different electronegative atom, forming a hydrogen bond.

**5.** To be polar a molecule must (1) contain one or more polar bonds, AND (2) have its charge distributed asymmetrically across the molecule.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** No — lipids are the exception; they are not polymers of monomers.

**3.** Yes — it is the only intermolecular force between non-polar molecules.

**4.** No — the four polar bonds are arranged symmetrically, so the charge is symmetrical and the molecule is non-polar.

**5.** Yes — larger molecules have more electrons, increasing the likelihood/strength of dispersion forces.

**.** 1-C, 2-B, 3-D, 4-B, 5-B, 6-C, 7-B, 8-A, 9-B, 10-C

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) CH₄ — non-polar: no polar bonds and symmetrical charge. (b) CCl₄ — non-polar: four polar bonds but arranged symmetrically (charge cancels). (c) CH₃Cl — polar: one polar bond and an asymmetric charge distribution. (d) HCl — polar: one polar bond with asymmetric charge (Cl is δ−, H is δ+).

**2.** (a) London dispersion force (a van der Waals force / hydrophobic interaction). (b) Larger molecules contain more electrons; the bigger, shifting electron cloud is more easily and more strongly polarised at any instant, producing stronger temporary dipoles and therefore stronger dispersion forces.

## Revision Set 3 — Answers

### A. Short answer

**1.** Hydrogen bonding. Four properties (any four): high specific heat (4.186 J g⁻¹ °C⁻¹); high melting point (0 °C) and boiling point (100 °C) relative to other small molecules; high heat of vaporization; cohesion; adhesion (and surface tension).

**2.** Cohesion = the attraction of water molecules to each other (via hydrogen bonding). Adhesion = the attraction of water to other (different) molecules. Biological example of cohesion: cohesion helps water flow/move up from the roots to the leaves of a tree (and contributes to surface tension that supports a water-walking spider).

**3.** Water can both accept a proton (acting as a base) and donate a proton (acting as an acid), and it weakly ionises by reacting with itself to form H⁺ and OH⁻. Reactions: H₂O + H⁺ → H₃O⁺ (acting as a base, receiving H⁺); H₂O + OH⁻ → 2OH⁻ (acting as an acid, donating an H⁺).

**4.** pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]. Pure water at 25 °C has pH 7 and [H⁺] = 10⁻⁷ mol/L.

**5.** Because pH is a negative log₁₀ scale, each whole-number change in pH corresponds to a ×10 change in [H⁺]. pH affects biological molecules by influencing the rates of biological reactions and by changing the 3-D structure of molecules (e.g. proteins), which alters their function; organisms therefore maintain an optimal internal pH.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** Yes — water is common in the universe but mostly as ice.

**3.** No — non-polar molecules are hydrophobic and do not dissolve readily in water.

**4.** Yes — lower pH = more acidic (pH 4 has 100× more H⁺ than pH 6).

**5.** No — higher pH means LOWER H⁺ concentration.

### C. MCQ

**.** 1-B, 2-C, 3-A, 4-B, 5-B, 6-B, 7-B, 8-B, 9-B, 10-B

### D. Calculation / applied

**1.** (a) pH = −log₁₀(10⁻⁵) = 5; since pH < 7 it is acidic. (b) pH 9 → [H⁺] = 10⁻⁹ mol/L. (c) pH 4 vs pH 6 differ by 2 units → 10² = 100 times more H⁺ ions in the pH 4 solution.

**2.** High specific heat and high heat of vaporization. High specific heat means water absorbs a large amount of heat for only a small rise in temperature, so the shallow pool warms slowly and stays cooler/more stable through hot daytime temperatures. High heat of vaporization means that as some water evaporates it carries away a large amount of heat (evaporative cooling), further limiting the temperature rise — together these buffer the temperature and improve tadpole survival.
