# Chapter 22 — Meiosis and Segregation of Alleles

*Topic 3: Genes and Heredity | Week 8, Lecture 22 | A/Prof Hayley Bugeja | University of Melbourne, School of BioSciences*

Three sets of review questions (English). Each set: 5 Short-Answer + 5 Yes/No + 10 Multiple-Choice + 3 Applied (genetics-problem / calculation / reasoning) questions. Answer key for all three sets is at the end of the document.

*Key idea: Meiosis is ONE round of DNA replication followed by TWO divisions (meiosis I + meiosis II), producing four haploid (n) cells from one diploid (2n) cell. The reductive step is MEIOSIS I, where HOMOLOGOUS chromosomes pair (synapse) and then separate — this halves the chromosome number. In MEIOSIS II, SISTER CHROMATIDS separate (like mitosis). Because the two alleles of a gene sit on homologous chromosomes that separate in meiosis I, a heterozygote (e.g. Yy) makes ½ Y and ½ y gametes — this is Mendel’s First Law (the Law of Segregation). A monohybrid F1 × F1 cross (Yy × Yy) gives a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (and a 1:2:1 genotypic ratio) in F2. Mitosis gives 2 identical diploid cells; meiosis gives 4 genetically varied haploid cells.*

## Set 1 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** Meiosis is ‘reductive’ because it halves the chromosome number (2n → n). There is ONE round of DNA replication followed by TWO divisions (meiosis I and II). The starting cell is diploid (2n); the four products are haploid (n).

**2.** Order: (Interphase – DNA replication) → Prophase I → Metaphase I → Anaphase I → Telophase I → Prophase II → Metaphase II → Anaphase II → Telophase II. The distinguishing event is PAIRING (synapsis) of homologous chromosomes in prophase I / metaphase I, which does not happen in mitosis, followed by separation of homologues in anaphase I.

**3.** End of meiosis I: HOMOLOGOUS CHROMOSOMES separate (one homologue of each pair to each cell). End of meiosis II: SISTER CHROMATIDS separate. The chromosome number is reduced to haploid at the end of MEIOSIS I (each cell then has n chromosomes, each still with two chromatids).

**4.** Mitosis: 2 daughter cells, diploid (2n), genetically identical to the parent → somatic cells (growth/repair). Meiosis: 4 daughter cells, haploid (n), genetically varied → germ/reproductive cells (gametes).

**5.** Meiosis halves the chromosome number to make haploid gametes; fertilisation fuses two gametes to restore the diploid number. Cycling between reduction (meiosis) and fusion (fertilisation) keeps chromosome number constant generation-to-generation while generating new genetic combinations (variation) through independent assortment and the random union of gametes.

### Part B. Yes/No

**1.** Yes — meiosis I and meiosis II.

**2.** Yes — homologues synapse during prophase I/metaphase I.

**3.** No — homologous chromosomes separate at the end of meiosis I; sister chromatids separate at the end of meiosis II.

**4.** No — mitosis produces 2 genetically identical diploid cells; meiosis produces the 4 varied haploid cells.

**5.** Yes — reduction to haploid occurs at the end of meiosis I (meiosis II then separates the chromatids).

**5.** B — meiosis II and mitosis.

**6.** A — meiosis I is the reductive division.

**10.** B — two sister chromatids (after replication).

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) Metaphase I: 46 chromosomes, each with 2 chromatids = 92 chromatids (arranged as 23 homologous pairs). (b) End of meiosis I: each cell has 23 chromosomes (each still with 2 chromatids) — this is the reduction to haploid. (c) End of meiosis II: each of the 4 cells has 23 chromosomes, each now a single chromatid. Reduction happens at meiosis I (46 → 23); meiosis II separates chromatids without further reducing chromosome number.

**2.** (a) MEIOSIS I — because homologous chromosomes separate then, so each daughter cell receives only one homologue of each pair (n). (b) The misconception arises because meiosis II also produces a division; but meiosis II separates sister chromatids of an already-haploid cell, so it does NOT change the chromosome number — it is equational, like mitosis. (c) Not correct: after DNA replication the cell is still diploid (2n) — it has simply doubled its DNA into sister chromatids. It only becomes haploid after homologues separate at the end of meiosis I.

**3.** (a) Metaphase I: chromosomes line up as PAIRED homologues (bivalents/tetrads) on the metaphase plate — in mitotic metaphase they line up singly. (b) Anaphase I: whole homologous chromosomes (each still with two chromatids) move to opposite poles — in mitotic anaphase sister chromatids separate. (c) This is METAPHASE II (haploid number, single chromosomes on a new plane perpendicular to the meiosis I plane); next, the sister chromatids will separate at anaphase II.

## Set 2 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** Law of Segregation: the two alleles of a gene separate (segregate) from each other during gamete formation, so each gamete carries only one allele of each gene. Physical basis: the two alleles sit at the same locus on a pair of homologous chromosomes; these homologues separate in anaphase I of meiosis, sending one allele to each daughter cell.

**2.** In Yy, Y is on one homologue and y is on the other. Meiosis I separates these homologues, so half the resulting cells carry Y and half carry y → ½ Y : ½ y gametes.

**3.** P = parental generation (pure-breeding parents). F1 = first filial generation (offspring of P). F2 = offspring of F1 × F1. In Mendel’s cross, F1 are all yellow (dominant); F2 segregate ~3 yellow : 1 green.

**4.** Real ratios approximate 3:1 because segregation and fertilisation are random (chance) events; with large sample sizes the observed ratio approaches the theoretical 3:1, but small chance deviations always occur. This shows segregation produces equal-probability gametes whose union at fertilisation is governed by probability/sampling, not exact counting.

**5.** Phenotypic ratio (Yy × Yy) = 3 dominant : 1 recessive. Genotypic ratio = 1 YY : 2 Yy : 1 yy. They differ because under complete dominance YY and Yy look the same (both dominant phenotype), so the three genotypes collapse into two phenotype classes (3:1).

**1.** Yes — that is the Law of Segregation.

**2.** Yes — ½ Y : ½ y.

**3.** Yes — 3:1 phenotypic ratio.

**4.** Yes — 1 YY : 2 Yy : 1 yy.

**5.** No — each gamete carries only ONE allele of each gene (alleles segregate).

(b) Punnett square Pp × pp: P×p → Pp; p×p → pp; gives ½ Pp (purple) : ½ pp (white). (c) P(yy) = ¼; P(dominant phenotype) = ¾.

## Set 3 — Answers

### Part A. Short-Answer

**1.** A properly diagrammed cross includes: (1) a key/description of the genetic symbols used; (2) parent (P) phenotypes and genotypes; (3) the gametes each parent produces (with ratios); (4) a Punnett square showing how gametes combine (optional for simple crosses); (5) the offspring (F1/F2) genotypes and phenotypes with their ratios.

**2.** A Punnett square systematically combines all possible gamete genotypes from each parent to predict offspring genotypes and their proportions. For simple crosses the outcome can be worked out by inspection, so it is optional; but for crosses with many gamete types (e.g. dihybrid, 4×4 = 16 boxes) it prevents errors and keeps the bookkeeping organised.

**3.** B/B makes only B gametes; b/b makes only b gametes. F1 = all B/b. Phenotype = all black, because black (B) is dominant to brown (b), so the heterozygote shows the black phenotype.

**4.** Bb × Bb: each makes ½ B : ½ b. Punnett square gives 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb (genotypic) = 3 black : 1 brown (phenotypic). The square combines the two gamete types from each parent into the four equally likely offspring combinations, three of which carry at least one B (black) and one of which is bb (brown).

**5.** Convention: the dominant allele is a capital letter and the recessive the matching lower-case (e.g. B = black, b = brown), and the key states what each symbol means. Defining symbols first removes ambiguity, lets the reader follow the genotypes, and makes the cross reproducible and checkable.

**2.** Yes — pure-breeding parents give a uniform (all-identical) F1.

**3.** Yes — 3 black : 1 brown.

**4.** No — it is optional for simple crosses (helpful but not required).

**5.** Yes — more gamete types → larger Punnett square (e.g. 2×2 vs 4×4).

### Part D. Applied

**1.** (a) P: B/B (black) × b/b (brown); gametes B and b. (b) F1 = all B/b, black. (c) F1 × F1 (Bb × Bb) Punnett square: BB, Bb / Bb, bb → genotypic 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb; phenotypic 3 black : 1 brown. (d) Brown = bb = ¼; ¼ × 24 = 6 brown puppies.

**2.** (a) Ccᵃ makes gametes C and cᵃ; cᵈcᵃ makes gametes cᵈ and cᵃ. (b) Punnett square (rows C, cᵃ × columns cᵈ, cᵃ): Ccᵈ, Ccᵃ / cᵃcᵈ, cᵃcᵃ. (c) Offspring: ¼ Ccᵈ = black, ¼ Ccᵃ = black, ¼ cᵃcᵈ (= cᵈcᵃ) = cream, ¼ cᵃcᵃ = albino. **3.** (a) Cross the unknown black dog with a brown (b/b) dog — the homozygous-recessive tester, because b/b contributes only b, so the offspring phenotypes directly reveal the black dog’s alleles. (b) If black dog is B/B: B/B × b/b → all B/b = all black. If black dog is B/b: B/b × b/b → ½ B/b (black) : ½ b/b (brown) = 1:1. (c) The appearance of ANY brown (b/b) puppy proves the black parent carried a b allele, so it must be heterozygous (B/b) — one brown offspring is enough to demonstrate this.
