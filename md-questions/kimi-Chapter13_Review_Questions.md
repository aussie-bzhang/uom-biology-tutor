## Question 1 — Enzyme Regulation & Feedback Inhibition

**a)** *(4 marks)*

The allosteric inhibitor in Tube A binds to a regulatory (allosteric) site on Enzyme A, distinct from the active site. This binding induces a conformational change in the enzyme that reduces its catalytic activity. The inhibitor stabilizes the T (tense) state of the enzyme, decreasing its affinity for the substrate. As a result, the reaction rate is slower and it takes twice as long to reach completion compared to Tube B (no inhibitor).

This is an example of feedback inhibition — a common regulatory mechanism in which the end product of a biosynthetic pathway inhibits the first committed step, preventing overproduction. The inhibition is reversible and non-covalent.

**b)** *(3 marks)*

In Tube C, the addition of excess substrate (at 5× the concentration of Tube A) partially overcomes the inhibition, resulting in 6 μmol of product — higher than Tube A but still lower than Tube B. This demonstrates that the inhibitor and substrate compete for binding to the enzyme, or that high substrate concentrations can shift the equilibrium toward the active R (relaxed) state.

The observation that excess substrate can partially restore activity suggests the inhibitor binds to a separate allosteric site (not the active site), confirming this is allosteric inhibition rather than competitive inhibition at the catalytic site.

**c)** *(3 marks)*

Adding excess substrate to Tube C increases the reaction rate to match Tube B because the high substrate concentration drives the enzyme population toward the active R-state conformation. According to the Monod-Wyman-Changeux (MWC) model of allosteric regulation, substrate binding to one subunit increases the affinity of other subunits for substrate (cooperativity).

In metabolic terms, this means that when substrate concentration is high (e.g., after a meal), the pathway can operate despite the presence of some end-product inhibitor. This allows the cell to respond dynamically to changing metabolic demands — producing more metabolite when substrate is abundant while still preventing overaccumulation of the end product.

## Question 2 — β-Oxidation and ATP Yield Calculation

**a)** *(6 marks)*

Palmitic acid (C₁₆:0) activation and β-oxidation:

• Number of β-oxidation cycles: (16 ÷ 2) − 1 = 7 cycles

• Acetyl-CoA produced: 16 ÷ 2 = 8 molecules

• NADH produced: 7 cycles × 1 = 7 molecules

• FADH₂ produced: 7 cycles × 1 = 7 molecules

ATP yield calculation:

• From NADH: 7 × 2.5 = 17.5 ATP

• From FADH₂: 7 × 1.5 = 10.5 ATP

• From acetyl-CoA (citric acid cycle): 8 × 10 = 80 ATP

• Gross ATP yield: 17.5 + 10.5 + 80 = 108 ATP

• Subtract activation cost (2 ATP equivalents): 108 − 2 = 106 ATP

**Total net ATP yield from one molecule of palmitic acid = 106 ATP**

**b)** *(4 marks)*

Energy needed = 10% of 100,000 kJ = 10,000 kJ

Moles of ATP required = 10,000 kJ ÷ 50 kJ/mol = 200 mol ATP

Number of ATP molecules = 200 mol × 6.022 × 10²³ molecules/mol = 1.204 × 10²⁶ molecules

Molecules of palmitic acid = 1.204 × 10²⁶ ÷ 106 = 1.14 × 10²⁴ molecules

**Approximately 1.1 × 10²⁴ molecules of palmitic acid (or about 0.19 mol ≈ 48 g)**

## Question 3 — Glycolysis vs. Gluconeogenesis Regulation

**a)** *(4 marks)*

In Condition 1 (high glucose, high insulin), glycolysis is favoured because:

• Insulin activates phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1), the key regulatory enzyme of glycolysis, by promoting its dephosphorylation and increasing its affinity for fructose-6-phosphate

• High fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F2,6BP) levels, stimulated by insulin, allosterically activate PFK-1

• Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase (FBPase-1), the gluconeogenic counterpart, is inhibited by F2,6BP

In Condition 2 (low glucose, high glucagon), gluconeogenesis is favoured because glucagon raises intracellular cAMP, which activates protein kinase A (PKA). PKA phosphorylates the bifunctional enzyme PFK-2/FBPase-2, converting it to a phosphatase that lowers F2,6BP levels. This simultaneously removes activation of PFK-1 and removes inhibition of FBPase-1, favouring glucose synthesis.

**b)** *(3 marks)*

A futile cycle occurs when glycolysis and gluconeogenesis operate simultaneously, resulting in net ATP hydrolysis without any productive metabolic output. For example, if PFK-1 converts F6P to F1,6BP while FBPase-1 converts F1,6BP back to F6P, the net result is ATP → ADP + Pi with no net flux.

In vivo, this is prevented by reciprocal allosteric regulation: F2,6BP activates PFK-1 and inhibits FBPase-1. When one pathway is stimulated, the other is automatically suppressed. Additionally, hormonal regulation (insulin vs. glucagon) ensures that both pathways are never fully active at the same time.

**c)** *(3 marks)*

The pyruvate kinase reaction is highly exergonic (ΔG°′ = −31.4 kJ/mol) and essentially irreversible under cellular conditions. A simple reversal would require enormous energy input and is thermodynamically unfavourable.

Instead, gluconeogenesis uses a two-step bypass: (1) pyruvate carboxylase converts pyruvate to oxaloacetate in the mitochondria, consuming ATP and CO₂; (2) PEPCK converts oxaloacetate to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) in the cytoplasm (or mitochondria), consuming GTP. This bypass circumvents the thermodynamic barrier and allows the cell to control gluconeogenesis independently through regulation of these two enzymes.

## Question 4 — Protein Metabolism & the Urea Cycle

**a)** *(2 marks)*

Nitrogen mass = 150 g protein × 0.16 = 24 g of nitrogen per day.

**Scott must eliminate 24 g of nitrogen daily.**

**b)** *(3 marks)*

Overall urea cycle equation:

**2 NH₄⁺ + CO₂ + 3 ATP + H₂O → H₂NCONH₂ + 2 ADP + 4 Pi + AMP + PPi + fumarate**

Or in simplified form: 2 NH₃ + CO₂ + 3 ATP → urea + 2 ADP + AMP + 4 Pi

**c)** *(3 marks)*

Moles of nitrogen = 24 g ÷ 14 g/mol = 1.714 mol N

Each urea molecule contains 2 N atoms, so moles of urea = 1.714 ÷ 2 = 0.857 mol urea

Each urea cycle turn costs 3 ATP equivalents (2 ATP → 2 ADP, and 1 ATP → AMP + PPi, where PPi hydrolysis is equivalent to ~2 Pi).

Total ATP cost = 0.857 mol × 3 = 2.57 mol ATP × 6.022 × 10²³

**Total ATP cost = 1.55 × 10²⁴ ATP equivalents per day (≈ 2.57 mol ATP)**

**d)** *(2 marks)*

Scott’s belief is incorrect because there is no storage form of protein in the body. Unlike carbohydrates (stored as glycogen) and lipids (stored as triglycerides), excess amino acids cannot be stockpiled. All protein consumed beyond what is needed for protein synthesis, enzyme production, and tissue maintenance is catabolized.

The carbon skeletons of deaminated amino acids are converted to pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, or citric acid cycle intermediates and used for energy (ATP production) or converted to glucose (gluconeogenesis) or fatty acids (lipogenesis). The nitrogen is excreted as urea. Therefore, excessive protein intake does not lead to increased muscle mass unless accompanied by resistance training that stimulates protein synthesis.

## Question 5 — Integrated Metabolism: Carbon Flow Between Pathways

**a)** *(4 marks)*

Pathway: Glucose → Fatty acids/triglycerides (lipogenesis in the fed state):

**1.**Glucose enters glycolysis in the cytoplasm, producing pyruvate

**2.**Pyruvate enters mitochondria and is converted to acetyl-CoA by pyruvate dehydrogenase

**3.**Acetyl-CoA enters the citric acid cycle, producing citrate

**4.**When ATP and citrate levels are high (fed state), citrate is transported to the cytoplasm

**5.**ATP-citrate lyase cleaves citrate to acetyl-CoA and oxaloacetate in the cytoplasm

**6.**Acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC) converts acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA (rate-limiting step, activated by citrate and insulin)

**7.**Fatty acid synthase (FAS) uses malonyl-CoA, acetyl-CoA, NADPH, and ATP to synthesize palmitate

**8.**In the cytoplasm of adipocytes, fatty acids are esterified with glycerol-3-phosphate (from glycolysis) to form triglycerides

**b)** *(3 marks)*

Amino acids from dietary protein undergo transamination, where their α-amino group is transferred to α-ketoglutarate (forming glutamate), leaving behind a carbon skeleton (α-keto acid). The carbon skeletons enter metabolism at different points:

• Glucogenic amino acids (e.g., alanine) → pyruvate → acetyl-CoA or oxaloacetate

• Ketogenic amino acids (e.g., leucine) → acetyl-CoA or acetoacetyl-CoA

The deaminated carbon skeletons can be converted to acetyl-CoA and then to fatty acids via the same lipogenic pathway described in part (a). Citric acid cycle intermediates (oxaloacetate, α-ketoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, fumarate) serve as the entry points, depending on the specific amino acid.

**c)** *(3 marks)*

Glycerol (a 3-carbon molecule from triglyceride lipolysis) enters gluconeogenesis because it can be phosphorylated to glycerol-3-phosphate and then oxidized to dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP), which is a glycolytic/gluconeogenic intermediate. DHAP can be isomerized to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate and converted to glucose via the gluconeogenic pathway.

In contrast, fatty acids are broken down by β-oxidation to acetyl-CoA (a 2-carbon unit). In animals, the pyruvate dehydrogenase reaction converting pyruvate to acetyl-CoA is irreversible, and there is no pathway to convert acetyl-CoA back to pyruvate. Although acetyl-CoA enters the citric acid cycle (as citrate), the two carbons are lost as CO₂ during the cycle, so there is no net conversion of acetyl-CoA carbons to oxaloacetate or glucose. This is why fatty acids cannot be used for net glucose synthesis in animals. (Note: Plants and some bacteria have the glyoxylate cycle, which bypasses the CO₂-releasing steps.)
