# Answer Key

## Practice Set 1

### A. Short-answer questions

1. **1.** A gene is a DNA sequence that can be expressed as a functional RNA or as a polypeptide/protein product. A trait is the observable biological characteristic that may result from one or more gene products and their interactions with environment and other genes.
2. **2.** DNA is the nucleic-acid molecule carrying genetic information. In eukaryotes, DNA is packaged with proteins as chromatin, which is organised into chromosomes. Genes are specific DNA sequences located on chromosomes.
3. **3.** The gene is transcribed into pre-mRNA containing exons and intron. The intron is removed by RNA splicing to form mature mRNA. The mature mRNA is translated into a polypeptide.
4. **4.** Cross the two strains, record F1 phenotype, then cross F1 individuals to obtain F2. A consistent segregation pattern such as all F1 showing one phenotype and F2 near 3:1 would support a single-gene model with dominance, although further tests would be needed.
5. **5.** Mitosis should produce two daughter cells. Each daughter cell should retain the Aa combination because mitosis copies and separates chromosomes to preserve the same genetic information in body cells.

### B. Yes/No questions

1. Yes, 2. No, 3. Yes, 4. No, 5. Yes

### C. Multiple-choice questions

1. B, 2. B, 3. A, 4. B, 5. B, 6. A, 7. A, 8. A, 9. D, 10. A

## Practice Set 2

### A. Short-answer questions

1. **1.** Crosses allow geneticists to observe how variants are transmitted and how genotype combinations map to phenotype. They help test models such as dominance, segregation and single-gene inheritance.
2. **2.** Within an organism, variants are copied and transmitted through DNA replication and mitosis. To the next generation, variants are segregated into gametes through meiosis and then combined at fertilisation.
3. **3.** All F1 offspring are expected to be Aa. If A is dominant, all F1 offspring show the A-associated dominant phenotype.
4. **4.** The Punnett square gives AA, Aa, Aa and aa. The expected genotype ratio is 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa. With complete dominance, the expected phenotype ratio is 3 dominant : 1 recessive.
5. **5.** Cross the dominant-phenotype fly with aa. If the unknown fly is AA, all offspring are dominant phenotype and Aa. If it is Aa, about half offspring are Aa dominant and half are aa recessive.

### B. Yes/No questions

1. Yes, 2. Yes, 3. Yes, 4. No, 5. Yes

### C. Multiple-choice questions

1. A, 2. B, 3. A, 4. B, 5. C, 6. A, 7. A, 8. B, 9. A, 10. A

## Practice Set 3

### A. Short-answer questions

1. **1.** Two traits may depend on alleles at two loci, so offspring phenotypes depend on combinations such as A\_B\_, A\_bb, aaB\_ and aabb rather than one gene alone.
2. **2.** Genes on different chromosomes tend to assort independently, producing combinations at roughly equal expected frequencies. Genes on the same chromosome, especially close together, may be inherited together more often, so parental combinations can be more frequent than recombinant combinations.
3. **3.** A\_B\_ means at least one dominant A allele and one dominant B allele. The expected fraction is 9/16.
4. **4.** If both gene products are needed in the same pathway, loss of either product can reduce or block the pathway output. Therefore mutation in either gene can produce an abnormal wing phenotype.
5. **5.** Cross double heterozygotes or appropriate parental strains, count offspring phenotypes, compare the observed ratio with an expected 9:3:3:1 model using a chi-square test. A significant deviation, especially with biologically interpretable phenotype classes, would support interaction or linkage requiring further testing.

### B. Yes/No questions

1. Yes, 2. Yes, 3. Yes, 4. No, 5. Yes

### C. Multiple-choice questions

1. A, 2. C, 3. C, 4. A, 5. A, 6. A, 7. B, 8. A, 9. A, 10. A
