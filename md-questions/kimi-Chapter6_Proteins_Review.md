# Question 1: Experimental Design — Protein Denaturation by pH

**Background:** A food science laboratory is investigating why whey protein loses its functional properties (solubility, emulsification) when heated in acidic conditions (e.g., during yogurt production at pH 4.5). You are asked to design an experiment to determine the mechanism of acid-induced denaturation.

**Task:** Design a controlled experiment to test how pH affects the denaturation of bovine serum albumin (BSA). Your experimental design must include:

1. a clear hypothesis relating pH to specific amino acid R-group changes;
2. a description of at least 5 pH conditions to test (including a control) and the rationale for your choices;
3. the method you would use to detect and quantify protein denaturation (choose from: circular dichroism spectroscopy, UV absorbance at 280 nm, or enzyme activity assay — justify your choice);
4. the specific intermolecular forces or bonds you predict will be disrupted at each pH condition, citing which R-groups are involved;
5. expected results presented as a sketch or table, with molecular-level explanations.

**Hints:** Consider the pKa values of ionizable groups: carboxyl groups (Asp/Glu side chains pKa ~4.0), amino groups (Lys side chain pKa ~10.5), and the N-terminal amino/C-terminal carboxyl groups. Remember that disruption of ionic interactions can lead to loss of tertiary structure.

## Question 1 — Model Answer

**Hypothesis:** As pH decreases from neutral to acidic conditions, protonation of Asp and Glu side chains (pKa ~4.0) will convert carboxylate groups (−COO⁻) to neutral carboxylic acids (−COOH). This eliminates negative charges that participate in stabilizing ionic interactions in the native protein structure, leading to disruption of tertiary structure and loss of function.

**pH conditions and rationale:**

1. pH 2.0 — far below all pKa values; maximum protonation of all acidic groups;
2. pH 4.0 — near the pKa of Asp/Glu side chains (~50% protonation);
3. pH 4.5 — the target condition (yogurt production);
4. pH 7.0 — physiological pH control; native charge state;
5. pH 10.0 — deprotonates Lys side chains, disrupts positive charges.

**Detection method:** Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy is the best choice. CD measures the differential absorption of left- and right-circularly polarized light by chiral structures. The α-helix and β-sheet content of a protein give characteristic CD spectra. Denaturation causes loss of secondary structure, producing a dramatic change in the CD signal. This method directly reports on structural changes, unlike UV absorbance (which only monitors aromatic residue environment) or activity assays (which may miss subtle structural changes).

**Expected disrupted interactions:**

1. At pH 2–4: Ionic interactions involving Asp/Glu (now protonated) and Lys/Arg (still positive) are disrupted due to loss of negative charges.
2. Hydrogen bonding networks involving carboxylate side chains are altered.
3. Electrostatic repulsion between newly protonated groups may further destabilize structure.
4. Disulfide bonds remain intact (covalent, not pH-sensitive).

## Question 2 — Model Answer

**Part A:** N-terminus — Met-Glu-Trp-Val-Thr-Phe-Ile-Ser-Leu — C-terminus (9 residues). One-letter code: M-E-W-V-T-F-I-S-L.

**Part B:**

1. Peptide bonds: 9 − 1 = 8 peptide bonds.
2. Water lost: 8 × 18.02 g/mol = 144.16 g/mol.
3. Molecular mass: (9 × 110 Da) − 144.16 Da = 990 − 144.16 = 845.84 Da. (Alternative: sum individual amino acid masses and subtract 8 water molecules gives a more precise value of ~1046 Da, but 110 Da/residue is the standard approximation taught in this course.)

**Part C:**

1. Helix length: 9 residues ÷ 3.6 residues/turn × 0.54 nm/turn = 1.35 nm.
2. Hydrogen bonds: In an α-helix, each residue (except the first 4 and last 4) forms 1 H-bond with a residue 4 positions away. For a 9-residue helix: approximately 5 H-bonds (n−4 = 9−4 = 5).

**Part D:** (1) Post-translational modifications (e.g., phosphorylation, glycosylation) add mass. (2) The 110 Da/residue is an approximation; actual residue masses vary (Trp is much heavier than Gly). (3) Disulfide bond formation removes 2 H atoms. (4) Bound ions or cofactors contribute additional mass.

## Question 3 — Model Answer

**Part A — Charge state table:**

| **R-group** | **pH 2.0** | **pH 7.0** | **pH 12.0** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Asp/Glu side chain | 0 (protonated) | − (deprotonated) | − |
| Lys side chain | + (protonated) | + (protonated) | 0 (deprotonated) |
| Arg side chain | + | + | + (or weakly +) |
| Cys side chain | 0 | 0 (or partial −) | − |
| N-terminus | + | + | 0 |
| C-terminus | 0 | − | − |

**Part B:**

1. Highest solubility: pH 7.0. At this pH, there are maximal charged groups (both positive and negative), enabling favorable interactions with water molecules. The protein is on average charged, preventing aggregation.
2. Most denaturation: pH 12. At high pH, all acidic groups are deprotonated (negative), and Lys side chains become neutral (NH₂). This eliminates most ionic interactions that stabilize tertiary structure. Additionally, the disulfide bond may be susceptible to base-catalyzed β-elimination at very high pH.
3. Highest affinity for cation-exchange: pH 2.0. At this pH, all basic groups (Lys, Arg, N-terminus) are protonated and carry positive charges, while acidic groups are neutral. The protein has maximum net positive charge, binding strongly to negatively charged cation-exchange resins.

**Part C:** Reduction of the disulfide bond by β-mercaptoethanol converts the covalent S–S bond to two free thiol (–SH) groups. This disrupts the tertiary structure by removing a covalent cross-link that constrains the polypeptide chain in its native conformation. The protein becomes more flexible and its melting temperature decreases significantly. Secondary structure (α-helix, β-sheet) may be partially retained, but the overall 3D fold is compromised.

**Part D:** Total basic residues: 4 Lys + 2 Arg + 1 N-terminus = 7 positive charges at low pH. Total acidic residues: 2 Asp + 3 Glu + 1 C-terminus = 6 negative charges at high pH. The pI is approximately the average of the pKa values of the groups that change charge around the neutral point. With more basic than acidic groups, the pI is expected to be in the basic range, approximately pH 8–9. A more precise estimate: average of the pKa of the last acidic group to protonate and the first basic group to deprotonate.

## Question 4 — Model Answer

**Part A:** The optimum temperature is approximately 37°C (body temperature). The curve rises from 0°C to 37°C as increasing thermal energy increases molecular collision frequency and the fraction of molecules with sufficient activation energy. Above 37°C, activity decreases rapidly because thermal energy disrupts the weak non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, dispersion forces) that maintain the enzyme’s active 3D structure. At 70°C, the enzyme is completely denatured.

**Part B:** Pre-incubation at 70°C causes irreversible denaturation. The enzyme’s tertiary structure (and potentially secondary structure) is disrupted as thermal energy breaks hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, and dispersion forces between R-groups. When the enzyme is returned to 37°C, it cannot refold into its native active conformation because: (1) the kinetic barrier to correct refolding is high in the presence of competing incorrect interactions; (2) the active site geometry required for catalysis is destroyed. The primary structure (peptide bonds) remains intact.

**Part C:** The student’s hypothesis is incorrect. Urea at 2 M concentration is a denaturant. It disrupts hydrogen bonding networks by competing for hydrogen bond donors/acceptors. It also destabilizes hydrophobic interactions by increasing the solubility of non-polar R-groups in aqueous solution. The result would be loss of both secondary and tertiary structure, leading to decreased (not increased) enzyme activity. The enzyme requires a precise 3D structure for catalysis; “flexibility” in the wrong regions destroys the active site.

**Part D:** The thermophilic α-amylase would show: (1) a higher optimum temperature (~70–80°C); (2) broader temperature stability; (3) residual activity at temperatures where the mesophilic enzyme is denatured. Structural basis for thermostability includes: increased number of disulfide bonds, more ionic interactions (salt bridges) between oppositely charged R-groups, increased proline content (reduces conformational flexibility), more compact hydrophobic core, and shorter surface loops.

## Question 5 — Model Answer

**Part A — Three-step purification:**

**Step 1: Ammonium sulfate precipitation (40–60% saturation).** Rationale: differential solubility. Most E. coli proteins precipitate at lower ammonium sulfate concentrations, while lysozyme remains soluble due to its high basic residue content and compact structure. Centrifuge to remove precipitated contaminants. Alternatively, lower concentrations (20–30%) can be used to selectively precipitate lysozyme if it is less soluble than contaminants at those conditions.

**Step 2: Cation-exchange chromatography (CM-Sepharose or SP-Sepharose) at pH 7.0–8.0.** Rationale: charge. Lysozyme has a very high pI (11.0), so at pH 7–8 it carries a strong net positive charge, while most E. coli proteins (pI 6–7) are neutral or negatively charged. Lysozyme binds to the negatively charged resin; contaminants flow through. Elute with a salt gradient (e.g., 0–1 M NaCl).

**Step 3: Size-exclusion chromatography (Sephadex G-50 or G-75).** Rationale: size/shape. Lysozyme (14.7 kDa) separates from any remaining contaminants of different size. This step also removes salts from the previous ion-exchange step and provides a buffer exchange.

**Part B:** Explanation 1: Dialysis at pH 7.0 removed a stabilizing cofactor or metal ion (e.g., Ca²⁺) that was essential for maintaining the native structure. Without the cofactor, the protein partially unfolds. Explanation 2: The protein may have been denatured during a previous step (e.g., exposure to organic solvent or mechanical shear), and the denatured form aggregated during dialysis, causing apparent loss of activity. To distinguish: test for aggregation by dynamic light scattering (DLS) or turbidity; test for cofactor requirement by adding back candidate ions and measuring activity recovery.

**Part C:**

1. SDS-PAGE (sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis): separates proteins by molecular mass. A single band at ~14.7 kDa indicates high purity.
2. Mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF or ESI-MS): provides precise molecular mass. For lysozyme, expect ~14,700 Da. Deviations indicate modifications or impurities.
3. Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy: reports on secondary structure content. Native lysozyme is rich in α-helix and β-sheet; the CD spectrum confirms structural integrity.

**Part D:** Lysozyme’s active site is a cleft in the protein surface with a shape complementary to the bacterial cell wall polysaccharide substrate. Specific R-groups line this cleft: Glu35 and Asp52 serve as catalytic residues (acid-base catalysis and nucleophilic attack, respectively). Non-covalent interactions between the substrate and enzyme include: hydrogen bonds between sugar hydroxyl groups and polar R-groups (Asn, Arg) in the cleft; dispersion forces between hydrophobic patches of the substrate and non-polar R-groups; and dipole-dipole interactions. These interactions precisely position the substrate for catalysis and stabilize the transition state. Denaturation destroys the cleft geometry by disrupting the tertiary structure, eliminating the precise arrangement of catalytic residues and binding sites, thereby abolishing activity.
