**Chapter 7 Review Questions
The Endomembrane System**

*Based on University of Melbourne BIOL10008 Lecture 7: The endomembrane system*

Suggested use: Attempt each set without notes first. Then check the answer key at the end. For MCQs, choose the single best answer unless the question explicitly says otherwise.

* Core themes: membrane structure and fluidity; passive, facilitated and active transport; vesicle traffic; ER, Golgi, lysosomes and plant vacuoles.
* Key contrast: passive transport moves down a concentration gradient; active transport requires energy and moves against an electrochemical gradient.
* Key pathway: rough ER/smooth ER synthesis -> transport vesicles -> Golgi cis face -> Golgi processing and sorting -> trans face -> secretion, lysosome/vacuole, or membrane delivery.

# Answer Key

## Set 1 Short-answer

1. Phospholipids are amphipathic. Their hydrophilic phosphate heads interact with water, while hydrophobic fatty acid tails avoid water and associate with other tails through London dispersion forces. This arrangement favours bilayers and closed vesicles.

2. Double bonds create kinks in fatty acid tails. The kinks prevent tight packing, increase membrane fluidity, and generally increase permeability.

3. At high temperatures cholesterol reduces permeability by helping fatty acid tails pack more closely. At very low temperatures it disrupts close packing and can increase permeability/fluidity.

4. Simple diffusion moves small neutral molecules down their gradient without ATP or transport proteins. Facilitated diffusion moves substances down their gradient using channel or carrier proteins, without ATP. Primary active transport directly uses ATP to move substances against their gradient.

5. A secreted protein is synthesized on ribosomes associated with rough ER, enters/associates with the ER, is packaged into transport vesicles, moves to the Golgi cis face, is processed and sorted through the Golgi, exits the trans face in vesicles, and is released by exocytosis at the plasma membrane.

## Set 1 Application / calculation

1. More rigid/less fluid. Saturated tails are straight and pack closely; little cholesterol means less buffering of membrane packing and fluidity.

2. Facilitated diffusion via a channel protein or carrier protein, because the solute is moving down its concentration gradient but needs a protein pathway through the hydrophobic bilayer.

3. pH 4.5 is acidic. Lysosomal enzymes are hydrolytic enzymes adapted to work in acidic conditions, helping them digest food particles, pathogens and damaged organelles.

## Set 2 Short-answer

1. Membrane fluidity allows phospholipids and membrane proteins to move laterally, helps transport proteins function, supports vesicle formation/fusion, and allows the membrane to maintain selective transport rather than becoming rigid and nonfunctional.

2. A uniporter moves one substance in one direction. A symporter moves two substances in the same direction. An antiporter moves two substances in opposite directions.

3. Primary active transport directly uses ATP to pump a substance against its gradient, such as the Na+/K+ pump. Secondary active transport uses an electrochemical gradient created by another process to drive target transport, such as Na+ moving down its gradient to help glucose move against its gradient.

4. Endocytosis packs material into vesicles for transport into a cell. Exocytosis releases material or waste from a cell when vesicles fuse with the plasma membrane.

5. Lysosomes are single-membrane animal-cell compartments with acidic interiors and hydrolytic enzymes for digestion and recycling. Plant vacuoles are single-membrane compartments bounded by the tonoplast; they also contain hydrolytic enzymes but additionally store nutrients and pigments and help maintain turgor pressure.

## Set 2 Application / calculation

1. Increase unsaturated phospholipid tails, because double-bond kinks prevent close packing and increase fluidity/permeability. Cholesterol can also help at very low temperature by disrupting excessive packing.

2. The Na+/K+ pump creates a Na+ gradient. If ATP hydrolysis is blocked, the Na+ gradient weakens, so secondary active transport that depends on Na+ moving down its gradient to drive glucose uptake is reduced or stopped.

3. Rough ER -> transport vesicle -> Golgi cis face -> Golgi trans face -> exocytosis/plasma membrane.
