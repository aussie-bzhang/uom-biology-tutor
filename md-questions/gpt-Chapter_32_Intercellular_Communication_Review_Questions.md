These answers are concise model answers. Students should receive credit for equivalent explanations that use correct biological reasoning.

## Set 1

Short-answer questions:

1. As cells specialise, no single cell can perform all functions independently. Cells must coordinate metabolism, movement, stress responses, development, blood flow, and other activities across tissues.

2. Autocrine signals act on the secreting cell; juxtacrine signals act on adjacent cells; paracrine signals act on nearby cells; endocrine signals are hormones transported to distant targets, usually through the circulation.

3. A hormone is a chemical signal produced in small amounts at one site and transported to another site where it acts on cells with the correct receptor.

4. Water-soluble hormones travel easily in blood and bind cell-surface receptors, triggering transduction pathways. Lipid-soluble hormones pass through membranes, often require carrier molecules in blood, and commonly bind intracellular receptors that regulate gene expression.

5. Epinephrine binds a receptor on a target cell (reception), activates relay molecules and enzymes (transduction), and produces a response such as glycogen breakdown to glucose in muscle.

Yes/No questions: 1. Yes; 2. No; 3. Yes; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

MCQ answers: 1. A; 2. A; 3. A; 4. A; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. A; 10. A

Application questions:

1. The claim is false. Nervous systems relay fast electrical signals, but endocrine systems use hormones transported in blood to coordinate distant tissues over broader time scales.

2. Without the receptor, the hormone cannot be detected by that target cell. Signal transduction would not begin, so the usual cellular response would be absent or greatly reduced.

3. Only cells with the correct receptor and downstream transduction machinery respond strongly. Other tissues may be exposed to the hormone but lack the ability to detect or process that signal.

## Set 2

Short-answer questions:

1. Dendrites receive input; the soma contains the cell body and integrates functions; the axon conducts action potentials; axon terminals form synapses with target cells.

2. Sensory neurons detect stimuli and send signals toward the CNS; interneurons process and relay signals within the nervous system; motor neurons send output to effectors such as muscles.

3. The Na+/K+ pump establishes ion gradients, and K+ channels allow K+ movement that helps make the inside of the neuron relatively negative at rest.

4. Depolarisation: voltage-gated Na+ channels open and Na+ enters. Repolarisation: Na+ channels close and voltage-gated K+ channels open, allowing K+ to leave. Hyperpolarisation: extra K+ loss makes the membrane briefly more negative than resting potential.

5. After firing, voltage-gated channels remain inactive briefly. This refractory period stops the recently activated membrane region from immediately firing again, so the signal propagates forward.

Yes/No questions: 1. Yes; 2. Yes; 3. No; 4. Yes; 5. Yes

MCQ answers: 1. A; 2. A; 3. A; 4. A; 5. A; 6. A; 7. A; 8. A; 9. A; 10. A

Application questions:

1. Blocking voltage-gated Na+ channels prevents sufficient Na+ influx, so depolarisation is weak or absent. Threshold may not be reached, and sensory information may fail to propagate.

2. Keeping K+ channels open increases K+ efflux, prolonging or exaggerating hyperpolarisation. The neuron is less likely to reach threshold again quickly, reducing firing frequency.

3. The myelinated axon is faster because action potentials are regenerated mainly at Nodes of Ranvier, allowing saltatory conduction from node to node rather than continuous propagation along the entire membrane.

## Set 3

Short-answer questions:

1. An action potential travels electrically along a neuron. At the synapse it triggers neurotransmitter release, converting the signal to a chemical message that can trigger another electrical response or muscle contraction.

2. Neurotransmitters are stored in vesicles; an action potential arrives; voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open; Ca2+ influx triggers vesicle exocytosis; neurotransmitter binds postsynaptic receptors; ion channels change activity; the postsynaptic cell responds; neurotransmitter is removed and vesicle membrane is recycled.

3. Excitatory neurotransmitters such as glutamate increase the likelihood of postsynaptic firing. Inhibitory neurotransmitters such as GABA reduce the likelihood of firing. Acetylcholine signals muscle contraction; serotonin is associated with mood.

4. Removal stops or limits the signal, prevents continuous receptor activation, and resets the synapse for future signalling.

5. Hormones often travel through blood to distant targets and may act more slowly or broadly. Synaptic neurotransmitters act across a short synaptic cleft and are usually fast and specific.
