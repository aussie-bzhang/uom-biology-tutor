## Key concepts to remember before you start

Multicellular organisms must coordinate all their cells. This requires fast and slow long-distance communication, using two complementary systems: chemical signals and electrical signals.

Four ranges of chemical signalling (by how far the signal travels): AUTOCRINE — acts on the same cell that made it; JUXTACRINE — acts only on directly adjacent (touching) cells; PARACRINE — acts on nearby cells by local diffusion; ENDOCRINE (hormones) — travels long distance, usually via the bloodstream, to act on distant target cells. A cell only responds if it has the correct RECEPTOR.

Hormone definition: “a chemical signal produced in minute amounts at one site in a multicellular organism and transported to another site where it acts on target cells.” Three chemical classes: PROTEIN/peptide (water-soluble, easily carried in blood, released by exocytosis); STEROID (lipid-soluble, derived from cholesterol, pass through membranes, carried by carrier molecules in blood); AMINE (derived from tyrosine, can be water-soluble e.g. epinephrine OR lipid-soluble e.g. thyroxine).

Solubility decides receptor location & mechanism. WATER-SOLUBLE hormones cannot cross the membrane → bind CELL-SURFACE receptors → trigger second messengers (e.g. G-protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → protein kinase cascade) → fast response. LIPID-SOLUBLE hormones cross the membrane → bind INTRACELLULAR receptors → hormone-receptor complex enters nucleus → regulates gene transcription → new protein → slower but longer-lasting response.

Key endocrine organs: neuroendocrine (hypothalamus/pituitary), thyroid, pancreas, adrenal, pineal/photoreceptive cells, gonads. The same hormone can have different roles in different species (e.g. prolactin).

Electrical signalling = neurons. Resting potential (~‒60 mV, negative inside) is set up by (1) the Na⁺/K⁺ pump (ATP-driven, 3 Na⁺ out : 2 K⁺ in) and (2) K⁺ leak channels; voltage-gated channels are closed at rest. Action potential (AP) sequence: a stimulus depolarises the membrane to THRESHOLD (~‒50 mV) → voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ influx → positive feedback drives rapid DEPOLARISATION (up to ~+50 mV) → Na⁺ channels close & voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → K⁺ efflux causes REPOLARISATION → brief HYPERPOLARISATION/undershoot → pump + leak restore resting potential.

The REFRACTORY PERIOD (voltage-gated channels temporarily inactivated) ensures the AP travels one way only — it cannot go backwards up the axon. APs are all-or-nothing. MYELINATION (glial cells, e.g. Schwann cells, wrapping the axon) with gaps called NODES OF RANVIER lets the AP “jump” node to node (saltatory conduction) — much faster.

NEUROTRANSMISSION links electrical → chemical → electrical (or muscle contraction). Along the cell = action potential; between cells = neurotransmission across the synapse. Neurotransmitters (100+, e.g. ACh = muscle contraction, glutamate = excitatory, GABA = inhibitory, serotonin = mood) are stored in vesicles and can be excitatory or inhibitory. Synapse steps: AP arrives → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ influx → vesicle exocytosis → neurotransmitter binds postsynaptic receptor → ion channels open → change in membrane potential → response → neurotransmitter removed (glial uptake / enzymatic degradation) → vesicle membrane retrieved. SSRIs treat low mood by blocking serotonin re-uptake, prolonging the signal.

# Answer Key

## Set 1 — Chemical Signalling: Types, Hormones & Endocrine System

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Autocrine = signal acts on the same cell that made it; juxtacrine = acts only on directly adjacent/touching cells; paracrine = acts on nearby cells by local diffusion through the extracellular fluid; endocrine (hormonal) = travels long distance, usually via the blood, to act on distant target cells. The defining difference is the distance/route the signal travels (same cell → adjacent → nearby → distant).

**2.** Hormone = a chemical signal produced in minute amounts at one site in a multicellular organism and transported to another site where it acts on target cells. A cell shows no response if it lacks the specific RECEPTOR for that signal — only cells with the matching receptor are “target” cells.

**3.** PROTEIN/peptide — water-soluble; easily transported dissolved in blood; secreted by exocytosis. STEROID — lipid-soluble (made from cholesterol); transported in blood bound to carrier molecules; can pass through membranes. AMINE (from tyrosine) — can be water-soluble (e.g. epinephrine) OR lipid-soluble (e.g. thyroxine); transport/route depends on which.

**4.** Water-soluble: cannot cross the membrane → binds a CELL-SURFACE receptor → activates second-messenger cascade (e.g. G-protein → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → protein kinase) → rapid response, often short-lived. Lipid-soluble: crosses the membrane → binds an INTRACELLULAR receptor → hormone-receptor complex enters the nucleus → regulates gene transcription → new protein made → slower onset but longer-lasting response.

**5.** Any four of: hypothalamus/pituitary (neuroendocrine), thyroid, pancreas, adrenal gland, pineal gland, gonads. “Variable roles”: the same hormone exists in many species but controls different processes in each (e.g. prolactin is involved in milk production in mammals but has other roles, such as water/salt balance or parental/reproductive behaviour, in fish, amphibians and birds).

**1.** Yes — that is the definition of autocrine signalling.

**2.** No — hormones USUALLY travel via the blood, but “usually” is not “always”; other chemical signals (autocrine/paracrine/juxtacrine) act locally without the bloodstream.

**3.** No — steroid hormones are lipid-soluble.

**4.** No — lipid-soluble hormones cross the membrane and bind intracellular receptors.

**5.** Yes — the pancreas is a key endocrine organ (e.g. insulin/glucagon).

### Part D — Applied / Biochemistry

**1.** Epinephrine is WATER-SOLUBLE. Evidence: (a) its receptor is on the OUTER cell surface — a lipid-soluble hormone would not need a surface receptor because it could cross the membrane; (b) it works through a relay / second-messenger cascade (receptor → relay molecules → enzyme), which is the hallmark of water-soluble signalling — the signal must be transduced across the membrane rather than the hormone itself entering. If the surface receptor were blocked, epinephrine could no longer trigger the cascade, the enzyme would not be activated, glycogen would not be broken down, and the cell would not release glucose (no response).

**2.** X = PROTEIN/peptide hormone (a 51-amino-acid water-soluble molecule like insulin) → surface receptor (water-soluble, cannot cross membrane). Y = STEROID hormone (lipid-soluble, made from cholesterol, carried on a carrier protein) → intracellular receptor (lipid-soluble, crosses membrane). Z = AMINE hormone (made from tyrosine) that is lipid-soluble and enters cells to switch on genes (like thyroxine) → intracellular receptor. Reasoning rule: water-soluble → surface receptor + second messenger; lipid-soluble → enters cell → intracellular receptor + gene regulation.

**3.** Set-up: place the secreting cells and a population of responding (target) cells in culture and vary the DISTANCE / route between them. Manipulated variable: physical separation — e.g. compare (i) responders placed close to the secreting cells vs (ii) responders only reachable via a circulating medium/long channel that mimics the bloodstream. Expected results: if PARACRINE, only nearby responders react (response falls off sharply with distance and is lost once cells are separated); if ENDOCRINE, responders react even when distant, provided the signal can be carried in the medium to them. Control: include responder cells with the receptor BLOCKED (or lacking the receptor) to confirm any response is receptor-mediated and specific; also a no-signal (medium-only) control to confirm responders do not react on their own.

## Set 2 — Electrical Signalling: Neurons & the Action Potential

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Dendrites = receive input (signals) from other cells. Soma (cell body) = contains the nucleus; integrates incoming signals. Axon = conducts the electrical signal (action potentials) away from the soma toward the terminals. Axon terminals = form synapses with target cells (other neurons or muscle), where signal is passed on.

**2.** The resting potential (~‒60 mV, inside more negative than outside) is set up by: (1) the Na⁺/K⁺ pump, which uses ATP to move 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle (net positive charge moved out); and (2) K⁺ leak channels, allowing K⁺ to diffuse out down its gradient, leaving the inside negative. Voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels are CLOSED at rest. An electrochemical equilibrium is reached at the resting value.

**3.** Resting (channels closed, ~‒60 mV) → DEPOLARISATION: stimulus depolarises membrane to threshold (~‒50 mV) → voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ influx drives potential up toward +50 mV → REPOLARISATION: Na⁺ channels close, voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → K⁺ efflux brings potential back down → HYPERPOLARISATION/undershoot: excess K⁺ loss drops potential below rest → pump + K⁺ leak restore resting potential. Na⁺ channels = depolarisation; K⁺ channels = repolarisation/hyperpolarisation.

**4.** Positive feedback: depolarisation opens some voltage-gated Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ enters → further depolarisation → opens MORE Na⁺ channels → still more Na⁺ enters, and so on. Because crossing threshold triggers this self-amplifying loop, the AP always reaches the same full size regardless of how much the threshold was exceeded — hence “all-or-nothing” (it either fires fully or not at all).

**5.** Refractory period = a time after an AP when voltage-gated channels (especially Na⁺ channels) are inactivated/closed and cannot reopen immediately. It prevents a new AP forming in the region just fired, so the AP can only propagate FORWARD (it cannot go backwards up the axon). Myelination: glial cells (e.g. Schwann cells) wrap the axon in insulating myelin, leaving gaps (Nodes of Ranvier) where voltage-gated channels are concentrated; the AP “jumps” from node to node (saltatory conduction), greatly increasing conduction speed.

### Part B — Yes/No

**1.** Yes — the inside is more negative at rest.

**2.** Yes — it is an active (ATP-driven) pump.

**3.** Yes — voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open during the rising phase.

**4.** Yes — K⁺ efflux repolarises the membrane.

**5.** No — the refractory period PREVENTS backward travel; the AP goes one way only.

### Part C — Multiple Choice

**1.** C — Axon.

**2.** B — 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle.

**3.** B — about ‒60 mV.

**4.** B — Threshold (~‒50 mV).

**5.** B — Na⁺ influx through voltage-gated Na⁺ channels.

**6.** B — Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open, K⁺ leaves.

**7.** C — Hyperpolarisation / undershoot.

**8.** B — Ensures one-way (forward) propagation.

**9.** B — Glial cells (Schwann cells).

**10.** C — The AP jumps node to node, speeding conduction.

### Part D — Applied / Biochemistry

**1.** (a) From rest (‒60 mV) to peak (+50 mV) = a depolarisation of 110 mV. (b) Threshold → peak: Na⁺ entering through voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. (c) Falling phase (repolarisation): K⁺ leaving through voltage-gated K⁺ channels. (d) During the absolute refractory period the voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are INACTIVATED and cannot reopen, so no new positive-feedback Na⁺ influx can occur — therefore a second full-sized AP cannot be generated until the channels reset.

**2.** (a) Each cycle moves 3 positive charges out and 2 positive charges in → NET of 1 positive charge OUT of the cell per cycle. (b) Continually exporting net positive charge makes the inside of the cell slightly more negative, contributing (together with K⁺ leak) to the negative resting potential. (c) If the pump is blocked (e.g. ouabain), the Na⁺ and K⁺ gradients gradually run down: the resting potential becomes less negative (the cell slowly depolarises toward 0), the driving gradients for the action potential collapse, and the neuron progressively loses the ability to fire repeated APs (and to restore rest after each one).

**3.** (a) Axon A (myelinated) conducts FASTER. Voltage-gated channels are concentrated at the Nodes of Ranvier; the insulating myelin lets the depolarisation jump from node to node (saltatory conduction) instead of regenerating at every point along the membrane. (b) Demyelination slows conduction and makes signalling unreliable (signals may be delayed, distorted or fail), because the AP can no longer jump efficiently between nodes. (c) Measure conduction velocity (e.g. distance between two electrodes ÷ the time delay for the AP to travel between them), or simply the time taken for the AP to travel a fixed length of axon.

## Set 3 — Neurotransmission, Neurotransmitters & Integrating Signals

### Part A — Short Answer

**1.** Neurotransmission converts an electrical signal into a chemical one and back again. Within a single neuron the signal travels as an electrical action potential ALONG the cell; to cross from one cell to the next (at the synapse) the electrical signal triggers release of a chemical neurotransmitter (neurotransmission BETWEEN cells), which then produces a new electrical change (or a muscle contraction) in the receiving cell.

**2.** Order: AP arrives at presynaptic terminal → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ influx → exocytosis of synaptic vesicles (neurotransmitter released) → neurotransmitter binds receptor on postsynaptic cell → ion channels open (or close) → e.g. Na⁺ flows into the postsynaptic cell, changing membrane potential → excitatory/inhibitory response (firing or muscle contraction) → neurotransmitter removed by glial uptake / enzymatic degradation → vesicle membrane retrieved/recycled. (Neurotransmitters are synthesised and stored in vesicles beforehand.)

**3.** When the AP reaches the terminal it depolarises the membrane, opening voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels. The resulting Ca²⁺ influx is the trigger for exocytosis: rising intracellular Ca²⁺ causes synaptic vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitter into the cleft.

**4.** Acetylcholine (ACh) — signals muscle contraction; Glutamate — excitatory (integration of information); GABA — inhibitory (integration of information); Serotonin — associated with mood. “Excitatory” neurotransmission makes the postsynaptic cell MORE likely to fire (depolarises it toward threshold); “inhibitory” makes it LESS likely to fire (hyperpolarises / stabilises it).

**5.** Chemical (hormones): slower onset, long range (can reach the whole body via blood), targets selected by which cells carry the receptor, and effects are relatively long-lasting. Electrical (neurons): very fast, point-to-point range (signal directed precisely along specific neurons to specific targets), highly specific by wiring, and effects are brief. The two systems are complementary: nerves for rapid precise control, hormones for slower widespread/sustained coordination.

### Part D — Applied / Biochemistry

**1.** (a) Correct order: (ii) AP arrives at the presynaptic terminal → (iv) Ca²⁺ enters through voltage-gated channels → (v) vesicles undergo exocytosis → (i) neurotransmitter binds the postsynaptic receptor → (iii) Na⁺ flows into the postsynaptic cell, changing its membrane potential → (vi) neurotransmitter is removed by glial uptake/enzymes. (b) Blocking presynaptic voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels stops step (iv); without Ca²⁺ influx there is no exocytosis (v), so no neurotransmitter is released. Consequence: the postsynaptic cell receives no signal — it does not change its membrane potential and does not fire (or the muscle does not contract).

**2.** (a) Normally, released serotonin (5-HT) acts briefly in the cleft before being pumped back into the presynaptic terminal by the 5-HT re-uptake transporter. An SSRI blocks this transporter, so serotonin stays in the cleft LONGER and at HIGHER concentration, binding postsynaptic 5-HT receptors more and for longer → increased/prolonged postsynaptic signalling. (b) A drug that blocks the POSTSYNAPTIC 5-HT receptor would have the OPPOSITE effect: even if serotonin is present, it cannot bind/activate the receptor, so signalling DECREASES. (c) “Chemical imbalance” is over-simplified because mood/depression involves more than just the level of one neurotransmitter — it involves receptor numbers and sensitivity, multiple neurotransmitter systems, neural circuits and feedback, and longer-term adaptive (e.g. plasticity) changes, which is why SSRIs often take weeks to act rather than working instantly.

**3.** (a) ELECTRICAL event: light/sight of the cheetah detected by sensory neurons → action potentials carried to the CNS and out to motor neurons driving leg-muscle contraction (point-to-point, fast). CHEMICAL (endocrine) event: the brain signals the adrenal glands to release epinephrine into the blood, which reaches muscle cells throughout the body. (b) Epinephrine is water-soluble, so its receptor is on the cell SURFACE; it cannot cross the membrane, so it works via a second-messenger cascade that activates the enzyme breaking glycogen down to glucose to fuel the muscles. (c) Comparison table:

• Electrical (nerve impulse): speed = very fast (ms); range = point-to-point (specific muscles); duration = brief.

• Chemical (epinephrine): speed = slower (seconds–minutes); range = whole-body (via blood); duration = longer-lasting.

**.** A fast-escape response benefits from BOTH: the nervous system gives the immediate, precisely targeted command to start running, while the hormonal system provides a slower, sustained, body-wide mobilisation of energy (glucose release, increased blood flow) that keeps the muscles fuelled throughout the sprint.
