**Question 1 — Answer**

**a) Experimental Design for PFK-1 Assay:**

(i) Reaction mixture: F6P (substrate), ATP (phosphoryl donor and allosteric regulator), Mg²⁺ cofactor, buffer at pH 7.4, and the enzyme extract containing PFK-1.

(ii) Detection: Couple the reaction to aldolase, triose phosphate isomerase, and glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase. Monitor NADH formation (or oxidation) spectrophotometrically at 340 nm. Alternatively, measure F1,6BP production using a colorimetric assay.

(iii) Essential control: A negative control without F6P (to measure background ATPase activity) and/or a boiled enzyme control to account for non-enzymatic reactions.

**b) Allosteric Regulation:**

(i) Increased ATP: At moderate concentrations, ATP is a substrate promoting activity. At high concentrations, ATP binds to the allosteric inhibitory site of PFK-1, decreasing its affinity for F6P and reducing glycolytic flux. This prevents excessive ATP production when energy is abundant.

(ii) Increased AMP: AMP is a potent allosteric ACTIVATOR of PFK-1. High AMP signals low energy status (ATP depleted). AMP binding increases PFK-1 affinity for F6P, stimulating glycolysis to regenerate ATP.

(iii) Increased citrate: Citrate inhibits PFK-1. High citrate indicates that the TCA cycle has ample substrate (acetyl-CoA) and the cell has sufficient biosynthetic precursors. This diverts glucose-6-phosphate toward glycogen synthesis or the pentose phosphate pathway instead of glycolysis.

**c) Beneficial PFK-1 Inhibition Scenario:**

When the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) is needed to generate NADPH and ribose-5-phosphate (e.g., during rapid cell division or antioxidant defense), inhibiting PFK-1 causes glucose-6-phosphate to accumulate. This substrate is then diverted into the PPP, which is critical for nucleotide synthesis and maintaining reduced glutathione. Thus, PFK-1 inhibition enables the cell to prioritize biosynthetic needs over ATP production.

**Question 2 — Answer**

**a) ATP Yield Calculation (per glucose):**

Glycolysis (cytoplasm):

• 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation, net)

• 2 NADH (from glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase)

Pyruvate oxidation (mitochondrial matrix):

• 2 NADH (one per pyruvate, × 2 pyruvate)

• 2 CO₂ released

TCA cycle (2 turns per glucose):

• 6 NADH (3 per turn × 2)

• 2 FADH₂ (1 per turn × 2)

• 2 GTP ≡ 2 ATP (substrate-level, 1 per turn × 2)

• 4 CO₂ released (2 per turn × 2)

TOTAL direct carriers: 10 NADH + 2 FADH₂ + 4 ATP (net substrate-level)

Oxidative phosphorylation (given P/O ratios):

• 10 NADH × 2.5 ATP/NADH = 25 ATP

• 2 FADH₂ × 1.5 ATP/FADH₂ = 3 ATP

TOTAL ATP = 25 + 3 + 4 = 32 ATP per glucose

Note: Some textbooks report 30-32 ATP due to variations in shuttle systems (glycerol-3-phosphate vs. malate-aspartate) used to transport cytoplasmic NADH into mitochondria.

**b) ¹⁴C Labeling Pattern:**

In glucose, C-1 and C-6 become the methyl carbon (C-3) of pyruvate, which is lost as CO₂ during pyruvate dehydrogenase reaction. The ¹⁴C from [1-¹⁴C]-glucose therefore enters the TCA cycle as ¹⁴C-labeled acetyl-CoA (both carbons of the acetyl group are labeled equally due to symmetry of the succinate molecule after the first turn).

The two acetyl carbons are released as CO₂ in the isocitrate dehydrogenase (α-ketoglutarate → succinyl-CoA, releasing one CO₂) and α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase (succinyl-CoA formation, releasing another CO₂) steps. Due to the cycle's symmetry, the label appears in CO₂ in the FIRST and SECOND turns of the TCA cycle, but the exact distribution requires detailed carbon tracing through the symmetric intermediates.

**c) Reasons for Lower Experimental Yield:**

• Proton leak across the inner mitochondrial membrane (uncoupling): some protons return to the matrix without passing through ATP synthase, dissipating energy as heat rather than ATP.

• The malate-aspartate shuttle vs. glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle: cytoplasmic NADH from glycolysis yields only 1.5 ATP per NADH if using the glycerol shuttle ( FAD-dependent), reducing the total by 2 ATP.

**Question 3 — Answer**

**a) Why the TCA Cycle Would Halt:**

The TCA cycle is a closed loop: oxaloacetate (4C) + acetyl-CoA (2C) → citrate (6C) → ... → oxaloacetate (4C) + 2 CO₂. For every acetyl-CoA that enters, one oxaloacetate must be regenerated. If intermediates (such as α-ketoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, oxaloacetate) are continuously removed for biosynthesis (amino acid synthesis, heme synthesis, gluconeogenesis), the pool of oxaloacetate would be depleted. Without oxaloacetate, the condensation reaction with acetyl-CoA (catalyzed by citrate synthase) cannot occur, and the cycle stops even if acetyl-CoA and NAD⁺ are abundant.

**b) Pyruvate Carboxylase as Anaplerotic Enzyme:**

When oxaloacetate is diverted to gluconeogenesis (via PEP carboxykinase), the TCA cycle intermediate pool decreases. Pyruvate carboxylase converts pyruvate to oxaloacetate, directly replenishing the cycle intermediate. This is an ATP-dependent reaction, reflecting the energy investment needed to fix CO₂ and maintain cycle flux. By producing oxaloacetate, the reaction ensures that: (i) the TCA cycle can continue oxidizing acetyl-CoA, and (ii) gluconeogenesis has sufficient oxaloacetate precursor for glucose synthesis.

**c) Pyruvate Carboxylase Deficiency — Metabolic Consequences:**

(i) Fasting hypoglycemia: Without pyruvate carboxylase, oxaloacetate cannot be synthesized from pyruvate. Since oxaloacetate is required for the first step of gluconeogenesis (OAA → PEP), the liver cannot synthesize glucose from pyruvate, lactate, or alanine. During fasting, when glycogen stores are depleted, this leads to severe hypoglycemia because gluconeogenesis is impaired.

(ii) Impaired acetyl-CoA oxidation: Acetyl-CoA from β-oxidation enters the TCA cycle by condensing with oxaloacetate. Without pyruvate carboxylase activity, oxaloacetate levels fall, and acetyl-CoA cannot enter the TCA cycle. Acetyl-CoA accumulates and is diverted to ketogenesis, potentially causing ketoacidosis. The inability to oxidize fatty acids completely means the cell loses a major energy source during fasting.

**Question 4 — Answer**

**a) Redox Reactions and ΔG°′ Calculation:**

Half-reactions:

• Oxidation: Succinate → Fumarate + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ (E°′ = +0.031 V)

• Reduction: ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O (E°′ = +0.815 V)

Overall: Succinate + ½O₂ → Fumarate + H₂O

ΔE°′ = E°′(acceptor) – E°′(donor) = 0.815 – 0.031 = +0.784 V

ΔG°′ = –nFΔE°′ = –2 × 96.5 kJ/V·mol × 0.784 V = –151.3 kJ/mol

The large negative ΔG°′ confirms the reaction is highly exergonic and can drive ATP synthesis.

**b) Effect of Rotenone:**

Rotenone inhibits Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase/ubiquinone oxidoreductase), blocking electron transfer from NADH to ubiquinone.

• (i) NADH oxidation rate: DECREASED to near zero. NADH cannot donate electrons to the chain.

• (ii) FADH₂ oxidation rate (from succinate): UNCHANGED or INCREASED. Succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II) transfers electrons directly to ubiquinone, bypassing Complex I. With the ubiquinone pool more oxidized (less competition from NADH), succinate oxidation may actually increase.

• (iii) Overall ATP synthesis: DECREASED. Although FADH₂ from succinate can still generate ~1.5 ATP, the loss of all NADH-derived ATP (from TCA cycle and glycolysis) causes a major reduction in total ATP yield.

**c) Cyanide Poisoning:**

Cyanide (CN⁻) binds to the ferric iron (Fe³⁺) in the heme a3-CuB binuclear center of cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV), blocking the final electron transfer to molecular oxygen.

• Electron transport chain backs up: Electrons cannot be passed to O₂, so all upstream carriers (cytochrome c, cytochrome b-c1 complex, ubiquinone, Complex I/II) become fully reduced.

• Proton pumping ceases: No electron flow means no proton gradient across the inner mitochondrial membrane. ATP synthase cannot synthesize ATP.

• NADH/NAD⁺ ratio: The NADH/NAD⁺ ratio INCREASES dramatically because NADH cannot be reoxidized by Complex I. The entire system becomes reduced, forcing cells to rely on anaerobic glycolysis (lactate fermentation) for ATP, which is far less efficient.

**Question 5 — Answer**

**a) Allosteric Coordination of Metabolism:**

High ATP, citrate, and NADH signal that the cell has ample energy and biosynthetic precursors. Their regulatory effects include:

• ATP: Inhibits PFK-1 (glycolysis) and pyruvate kinase; activates pyruvate carboxylase and gluconeogenic enzymes. High ATP shifts metabolism away from glucose degradation.

• Citrate: Inhibits PFK-1 (reducing glycolytic flux); activates acetyl-CoA carboxylase (stimulating fatty acid synthesis). Citrate also indicates that the TCA cycle is well-supplied with substrates.

• NADH: High NADH inhibits pyruvate dehydrogenase (via activation of PDH kinase) and stimulates the malate valve (transporting reducing equivalents out of mitochondria). The high NADH/NAD⁺ ratio also inhibits glycolysis at the glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase step.

Together, these signals coordinate a metabolic shift: glucose is spared (stored as glycogen or used for the pentose phosphate pathway), while fatty acids are synthesized and stored as triacylglycerols.

**b) Anaerobic vs. Aerobic ATP Yield:**

Anaerobic glycolysis + lactate fermentation:

• Glycolysis: 2 ATP invested, 4 ATP produced = 2 ATP net

• NAD⁺ regeneration: Pyruvate → lactate (no additional ATP)

• TOTAL anaerobic yield: 2 ATP per glucose

Comparison: Aerobic oxidation yields ~32 ATP; anaerobic glycolysis yields only 2 ATP (16-fold less).

Advantage of anaerobic glycolysis: Despite low yield, the rate of ATP production is much FASTER. Glycolysis generates 2 ATP in ~0.5-1 minute, while complete aerobic oxidation takes significantly longer. During intense exercise, the muscle's ATP demand exceeds the rate at which oxidative phosphorylation can supply it. Anaerobic glycolysis allows rapid, sustained power output (though limited by lactate accumulation and NAD⁺ regeneration). The trade-off is efficiency for speed.

**c) Metabolic Fate of Alanine Carbons:**

Alanine structure: CH₃-CH(NH₂)-COOH (3 carbons: C1 = carboxyl, C2 = α-carbon, C3 = methyl). Transamination yields pyruvate (CH₃-CO-COOH).

(i) Gluconeogenesis pathway: Pyruvate → (pyruvate carboxylase) → oxaloacetate → (PEP carboxykinase) → phosphoenolpyruvate → → glucose.

• All 3 carbons from alanine can be recovered as glucose.

• No carbon is lost as CO₂ in this direction (the CO₂ fixed by pyruvate carboxylase is the same CO₂ released by PEP carboxykinase, net zero).

• Energy cost: 4 ATP + 2 GTP per glucose (2 alanine → 1 glucose).

(ii) Fatty acid synthesis pathway: Pyruvate → (pyruvate dehydrogenase) → acetyl-CoA → (fatty acid synthase) → fatty acids.

• C1 of pyruvate (from alanine carboxyl C) is lost as CO₂ during pyruvate dehydrogenase reaction.

• C2 and C3 of pyruvate become the 2-carbon acetyl group of acetyl-CoA and can be incorporated into fatty acids.

• Thus, 2 of 3 alanine carbons (67%) are retained in fatty acids; 1 carbon (33%) is lost as CO₂.

This difference explains why conversion to fat is energetically favorable (excess ATP promotes acetyl-CoA carboxylase and fatty acid synthesis) but results in carbon loss, whereas gluconeogenesis preserves all carbons but requires energy input.
