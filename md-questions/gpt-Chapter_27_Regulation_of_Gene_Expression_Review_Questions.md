# Reference Answers

## Practice Set 1 - Core Concepts: Why and Where Gene Expression Is Regulated

### 1. Short-Answer Questions

**1.** Genes are regulated because cells need different gene products depending on function, environment, developmental stage, and cell type. Constant expression of all genes would waste resources and may be harmful.

**2.** Gene product amount may change when a product is in high demand, in low demand, needed only in a specific environment, needed briefly during development, or needed only in a particular cell or tissue type.

**3.** In prokaryotes, transcription and translation can be closely coupled in the cytoplasm. In eukaryotes, transcription occurs in the nucleus, the pre-mRNA is processed and exported, and translation occurs in the cytoplasm.

**4.** Examples include chromatin remodelling, transcriptional control, RNA processing control, mRNA transport control, mRNA stability control, translational control, post-translational control of protein activity, and protein degradation control.

**5.** DNA methylation adds methyl groups, commonly to cytosine in CpG contexts. Methylation can alter chromatin state or regulator binding and often reduces transcription from affected regions.

### 2. Yes/No Questions

**1.** Yes. Demand for gene products changes with cell function and environment.

**2.** No. In eukaryotes, transcription occurs in the nucleus and translation occurs in the cytoplasm.

**3.** Yes. Cytosine methylation at CpG sites is a central example.

**4.** Yes. Proteins can be activated, inhibited, modified, or degraded after translation.

**5.** No. Regulation is important throughout life, including development, tissue function, environmental responses, and disease.

### 3. Multiple-Choice Questions

**1.** B. Different genes are needed at different times, places, and environmental conditions.

**2.** A. A gene expressed only in a specific tissue type.

**3.** C. Chromatin remodelling.

**4.** A. DNA methyltransferase.

**5.** B. DNA methyltransferases.

**6.** A. Gene expression may be reduced or silenced.

**7.** A. Processing control.

**8.** A. mRNA stability control.

**9.** A. Expression only at certain times or developmental stages.

**10.** A. Abnormal development or disease-related phenotypes may occur.

### 4. Biochemical / Genetic Application Questions

**1.** Liver cells and neurons have the same genome but different expression programs. Regulatory differences determine which genes are transcribed, which RNAs are processed and exported, how stable the RNAs are, which proteins are translated, and how proteins are modified or degraded. Any combination of chromatin, transcriptional, processing, transport, stability, translational, and post-translational regulation can produce different protein profiles.

**2.** If promoter methylation normally represses Gene X, inhibiting DNA methyltransferase may reduce methylation and increase Gene X expression. The reasoning is that reduced methylation can make the DNA/chromatin environment more permissive for transcription or reduce recruitment of silencing factors.

**3.** Collect embryos at multiple time points across development and perform in situ hybridisation using a labelled probe against the gene transcript. The staining pattern reveals location, and comparing time points reveals the two-hour expression window. A reporter gene under the gene promoter could also be used to monitor temporal and spatial expression.

## Practice Set 2 - Transcriptional Regulation and the Lac Operon

### 1. Short-Answer Questions

**1.** Positive regulation increases transcription when an activator binds and helps RNA polymerase bind or function. Negative regulation decreases transcription when a repressor binds and blocks or destabilises RNA polymerase.

**2.** LacI is a repressor protein. When lactose is absent, LacI binds near the lac operon and prevents transcription of lacZYA. When lactose is present, repression is relieved and transcription can occur.

**3.** Producing lactose-utilisation proteins is energetically costly. They are useful mainly when lactose is available as a substrate, so regulation avoids wasting resources.

**4.** Glucose is a preferred carbon source. When glucose is abundant, the cell has less need to strongly express lactose-utilisation genes; when glucose is absent, CAP activation can increase expression if lactose is present.

**5.** Combinatorial control occurs when expression depends on multiple regulators or signals. The lac operon depends on both lactose/LacI negative control and glucose/CAP positive control.

### 2. Yes/No Questions

**1.** Yes. Activators promote transcription.

**2.** Yes. Repressors reduce transcription.

**3.** Yes. lacZ encodes beta-galactosidase.

**4.** Yes. lacY encodes lactose permease.

**5.** No. The strongest expression is expected when lactose is present and glucose is absent.

### 3. Multiple-Choice Questions

**1.** A. A repressor.

**2.** A. Bind the operator/promoter region and block transcription.

**3.** A. It interacts with LacI and prevents effective repression.

**4.** A. Positive regulator/activator.

**5.** B. Lactose present, glucose absent.

**6.** A. Lactose absent, glucose present.

**7.** A. Expressed as a single mRNA and translated into separate polypeptides.

**8.** A. lacZ, lacY, lacA.

**9.** A. Galactose and glucose.

**10.** A. Multiple regulators or signals together determine expression output.

### 4. Biochemical / Genetic Application Questions

**1.** (i) Lactose absent, glucose present: no/very low expression because LacI represses and CAP is not activating. (ii) Lactose present, glucose absent: high expression because repression is relieved and CAP activates. (iii) Lactose present, glucose present: low to moderate expression because repression is relieved but CAP activation is weak/absent. (iv) Lactose absent, glucose absent: low expression because CAP may be active but LacI repression still blocks transcription in the simple model.

**2.** If LacI cannot bind DNA, negative repression is lost. With glucose absent, CAP activation is expected, so lac operon expression would be high even without lactose. This differs from wild type because wild-type LacI would repress the operon when lactose is absent.

**3.** The low activity with no lactose and glucose present indicates repression by LacI and lack of CAP activation. The high activity with lactose and no glucose indicates that lactose relieves repression and CAP activates. The intermediate activity with lactose and glucose present indicates repression is relieved but positive activation is reduced by glucose availability.

## Practice Set 3 - Experimental Analysis of Gene Expression

### 1. Short-Answer Questions

**1.** In situ hybridisation detects where a specific nucleic-acid sequence, usually an mRNA transcript, is located in a tissue, embryo, or cell sample.

**2.** A promoter can be fused to a reporter coding sequence such as GFP or luciferase. When the promoter is active, the reporter is transcribed and translated, producing fluorescence or luminescence that reveals promoter activity.

**3.** Microarrays compare labelled nucleic acids hybridised to known probes, usually for many genes at once. RNA sequencing sequences RNA-derived molecules and can provide more detailed, high-resolution transcriptome information, including abundance and transcript structure depending on the analysis.

**4.** Reverse transcription converts RNA transcripts into complementary DNA, which is more stable and can be fluorescently labelled and hybridised to DNA probes on the microarray.

**5.** RNA sequencing reads many RNA-derived fragments, allowing genome-wide quantification of transcript abundance and detection of detailed expression patterns across conditions.

### 2. Yes/No Questions

**1.** Yes. Spatial localisation is a key use of in situ hybridisation.

**2.** Yes. Reporter genes are used to monitor promoter activity.

**3.** Yes. The lecture shows comparison between healthy and pathological cells.

**4.** No. RNA sequencing analyses RNA-derived molecules/transcripts, not only protein abundance.

**5.** Yes. These methods can compare expression within and between samples.

### 3. Multiple-Choice Questions

**1.** A. Specific RNA/DNA sequences in their spatial context.

**2.** A. A promoter fused to a reporter gene.

**3.** A. Produce detectable signals.

**4.** A. Reverse transcription and fluorescent tagging.

**5.** A. Expression in both compared conditions.

**6.** A. RNA sequencing.

**7.** A. Where and when is this promoter active?

**8.** A. Genes expressed only or more strongly in one condition.

**9.** A. It sequences RNA-derived molecules to quantify and characterise transcripts.

**10.** A. In situ hybridisation.

### 4. Biochemical / Genetic Application Questions

**1.** Culture or collect healthy and pathological cells under comparable conditions. Isolate RNA from each sample. Convert RNA to labelled cDNA by reverse transcription, using different fluorescent labels for each condition. Hybridise the labelled cDNAs to a microarray containing gene probes. Spots with one colour indicate expression mainly in one condition; mixed colours indicate expression in both; no signal indicates little or no expression in either sample. Replicates and normalisation are needed for reliable interpretation.

**2.** The GFP reporter shows promoter activity in one tissue, while the microarray supports that the transcript is abundant in that tissue or condition. Together they suggest tissue-specific expression. A reporter may not perfectly reproduce all native regulatory context; a microarray loses spatial resolution if tissue samples are mixed and measures RNA rather than protein activity.

**3.** With lactose present and glucose absent, lacZYA should be highly expressed because LacI repression is relieved and CAP activation is strong. With glucose present and lactose absent, lacZYA should be low or absent because LacI represses and CAP activation is weak. RNA-seq would detect this as many lacZYA transcripts in the lactose/no-glucose condition and few in the glucose/no-lactose condition.
