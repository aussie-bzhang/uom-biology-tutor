## Problem 1: ETC Inhibitor Experiment (10 marks)

A researcher isolates mitochondria and suspends them in a buffered solution containing succinate (a substrate that feeds electrons into the ETC at Complex II), ADP, Pi, and oxygen. She measures oxygen consumption over time as an indicator of ETC activity.

**a)** The researcher adds rotenone (an inhibitor of Complex I) to the mitochondrial suspension. Predict and explain the effect on oxygen consumption rate. (3 marks)

**b)** She then adds cyanide (an inhibitor of Complex IV) to a fresh mitochondrial preparation. Predict and explain the effect on: (i) oxygen consumption, and (ii) ATP synthesis. (4 marks)

**c)** Next, she adds oligomycin (an inhibitor of ATP synthase) and observes an unexpected effect on oxygen consumption. Explain what happens and why. (3 marks)

## Problem 2: ATP Yield Calculation (10 marks)

A cell completely oxidizes one molecule of glucose under aerobic conditions. Use the following information to answer the questions:

• Glycolysis: 2 ATP (net), 2 NADH
• Pyruvate oxidation: 2 NADH
• Citric acid cycle: 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 GTP
• Each mitochondrial NADH yields ~2.5 ATP
• Each cytosolic NADH yields ~1.5 ATP (after shuttle costs)
• Each FADH₂ yields ~1.5 ATP

**a)** Calculate the total ATP yield from one molecule of glucose. Show all steps. (5 marks)

**b)** The cell switches to anaerobic conditions and performs lactic acid fermentation. Calculate the ATP yield per glucose now, and determine the percentage of aerobic ATP production that is maintained. (3 marks)

**c)** A mutation reduces Complex II activity by 50%. Assuming succinate is the only substrate, how would this affect the maximum ATP yield from glucose? Explain your reasoning. (2 marks)

## Problem 3: Fermentation Product Analysis (10 marks)

**a)** Design an experiment using simple chemical tests to determine whether the organism performs lactic acid fermentation, alcoholic fermentation, or anaerobic respiration. Include the expected observations for each pathway. (5 marks)

**b)** After 48 hours, Flask A (with indicator) shows a pH of 4.2 (initial pH 7.0), Flask B shows gas bubble formation, and Flask C shows a black precipitate when lead acetate paper is held over the culture. Identify the metabolic pathway and justify your answer. (3 marks)

**c)** If the organism produces 0.8 moles of ethanol per mole of glucose, calculate the final ethanol concentration in Flask B (in mM). (2 marks)

## Problem 4: Energy Efficiency Calculation (10 marks)

**a)** Calculate the maximum theoretical number of ATP molecules that could be generated from one molecule of glucose if energy conversion were 100% efficient. (3 marks)

**b)** Under physiological conditions, aerobic respiration produces approximately 30 moles of ATP per mole of glucose. Calculate the overall thermodynamic efficiency of ATP production from glucose. (3 marks)

**c)** A scientist claims to have isolated a new species of eukaryotic cells that can convert 1 molecule of glucose into 57 molecules of ATP. Use thermodynamic principles to evaluate this claim. Is it possible? Explain. (2 marks)

**d)** Fermentation produces only 2 ATP per glucose. Calculate its energy efficiency and explain why organisms still use this pathway despite its low yield. (2 marks)

# Part C: Answer Key

The following answers are provided for self-assessment. Detailed explanations are given where applicable.

## Part A Answers

**A1.** Oxidative phosphorylation occurs in the inner mitochondrial membrane (cristae). The two main components are: (1) the electron transport chain (respiratory chain), where electrons from NADH and FADH₂ are passed through protein complexes to O₂, and (2) chemiosmosis, where the proton gradient drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase.

**A2.** NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to the ETC. NADH donates electrons to Complex I, and the electrons pass through Complexes I → III → IV, pumping protons at three sites. This yields approximately 2.5 ATP per NADH. FADH₂ donates electrons to Complex II, bypassing Complex I, so protons are pumped at only two sites (Complexes III and IV), yielding approximately 1.5 ATP per FADH₂.

**A3.** Chemiosmosis is the coupling of electron transport to ATP synthesis via a proton gradient. As electrons move through ETC complexes I, III, and IV, protons (H⁺) are pumped from the mitochondrial matrix to the intermembrane space. This creates an electrochemical gradient (proton-motive force). ATP synthase uses the energy released as protons flow back into the matrix through its channel to phosphorylate ADP to ATP.

**A4.** The final electron acceptor in aerobic respiration is molecular oxygen (O₂). When O₂ accepts electrons at Complex IV and combines with protons, it forms water (H₂O): ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O.

**A5.** Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase) accepts electrons from NADH, oxidizing it to NAD⁺. It transfers these electrons to ubiquinone (coenzyme Q) and pumps 4 protons from the matrix to the intermembrane space. Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) receives electrons from cytochrome c and transfers them to the final electron acceptor, O₂, reducing it to H₂O. It also pumps 2 protons.

**A6.** ATP synthase (Complex V) is a molecular motor that uses the proton-motive force to synthesize ATP. It has two main components: F₀ (the membrane-embedded proton channel) and F₁ (the catalytic head). As protons flow through F₀ down their electrochemical gradient, the rotation drives conformational changes in F₁ that catalyze ADP + Pi → ATP.

**A7.** Complete oxidation of glucose yields approximately 30 ATP. Breakdown: Glycolysis: 2 ATP (substrate-level) + 2 NADH (→ ~3 ATP); Pyruvate oxidation: 2 NADH (→ ~5 ATP); Citric acid cycle: 6 NADH (→ ~15 ATP) + 2 FADH₂ (→ ~3 ATP) + 2 GTP (= 2 ATP); Total ≈ 2 + 3 + 5 + 15 + 3 + 2 = 30 ATP.

**A8.** Cytosolic NADH (from glycolysis) cannot cross the inner mitochondrial membrane directly. It must transfer electrons via shuttle systems (glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle or malate-aspartate shuttle). These shuttles consume energy, and the electrons may enter at Complex II instead of Complex I, resulting in fewer protons pumped and therefore fewer ATP (≈ 1.5 ATP vs. 2.5 ATP for mitochondrial NADH).

**A9.** Two explanations: (1) Variation in the number of active ATP synthase molecules per mitochondrion (ranging from ~100 to ~5000). (2) Differences in proton leak across the inner mitochondrial membrane (uncoupling proteins). (3) Variation in shuttle systems used to transport cytosolic NADH electrons. (4) Differences in metabolic state affecting substrate supply. Any two valid explanations accepted.

**A10.** Anaerobic respiration uses an electron transport chain with alternative final electron acceptors (e.g., SO₄²⁻, Fe³⁺, CO₂) instead of O₂. It occurs in certain prokaryotes (bacteria and archaea) living in anoxic environments. Fermentation does NOT use an electron transport chain; it uses organic molecules as electron acceptors to regenerate NAD⁺. It occurs in yeasts, some bacteria, and mammalian cells under hypoxic conditions.

**A11.** Glycolysis requires NAD⁺ as the electron acceptor at the glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase step (converting G3P to 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate). Without NAD⁺, glycolysis stops. Fermentation replenishes NAD⁺ by transferring electrons from NADH to an organic compound: in lactic acid fermentation, pyruvate accepts electrons to form lactate; in alcoholic fermentation, acetaldehyde accepts electrons to form ethanol.

**A12.** Lactic acid fermentation: (a) performed by lactic acid bacteria and mammalian muscle cells, (b) lactate is the product, (c) lactate dehydrogenase. Alcoholic fermentation: (a) performed by yeasts (Saccharomyces) and some plant cells, (b) ethanol and CO₂ are products, (c) pyruvate decarboxylase and alcohol dehydrogenase.

**A13.** SO₄²⁻ + 9H⁺ + 8e⁻ → H₂S + 4H₂O. This is used by sulfate-reducing bacteria such as Desulfovibrio. The standard reduction potential (E°' = -0.22 V) is less favorable than O₂/H₂O (+0.82 V), so less energy is captured per electron pair.

**A14.** Cellular respiration fully oxidizes glucose to CO₂, extracting maximum energy (≈ 30 ATP). Fermentation only partially oxidizes glucose; the fermentation products (lactate or ethanol) still contain significant chemical energy. Since fermentation lacks an ETC and chemiosmosis, the only ATP comes from substrate-level phosphorylation in glycolysis (2 ATP). The NADH produced in glycolysis is consumed to reduce pyruvate, so no additional ATP is generated from NADH oxidation.

**A15.** During high-intensity exercise, muscle O₂ demand exceeds supply. Muscle cells switch from aerobic respiration to lactic acid fermentation to maintain rapid ATP production for contraction. Pyruvate is reduced to lactate by lactate dehydrogenase, consuming NADH and regenerating NAD⁺ to keep glycolysis running. The lactate is transported via the bloodstream to the liver, where it is converted back to pyruvate (gluconeogenesis) or fully oxidized by the Cori cycle when O₂ becomes available.

## Part B Answers

**A1.** (a) Rotenone inhibits Complex I. Since succinate donates electrons at Complex II, bypassing Complex I, oxygen consumption would be UNCHANGED. (b)(i) Cyanide inhibits Complex IV, completely stopping oxygen consumption (the terminal step). (ii) ATP synthesis would also stop because the proton gradient collapses without electron flow. (c) Oligomycin inhibits ATP synthase, stopping ATP synthesis. However, oxygen consumption also DECREASES because the proton gradient builds up and back-pressure inhibits the ETC proton pumps.

**A2.** (a) Glycolysis: 2 ATP + 2 NADH×1.5 = 3 ATP; Pyruvate oxidation: 2 NADH×2.5 = 5 ATP; Citric acid cycle: 6 NADH×2.5 = 15 ATP + 2 FADH₂×1.5 = 3 ATP + 2 GTP = 2 ATP; Total = 2 + 3 + 5 + 15 + 3 + 2 = 30 ATP. (b) Anaerobic: 2 ATP only. Percentage = (2/30)×100 = 6.7%. (c) Complex II feeds electrons from succinate to ubiquinone. A 50% reduction would reduce FADH₂-derived ATP by half, but since most ATP comes from NADH (Complex I), the overall effect is minimal (approximately 1.5 ATP reduction).

**A3.** (a) Test 1: Add bromothymol blue (pH indicator) — yellow indicates acid production (fermentation). Test 2: Detect CO₂ with limewater (turns cloudy) or bicarbonate indicator. Test 3: Detect H₂S with lead acetate paper (turns black). Test 4: Smell/alcohol test with dichromate (orange to green). (b) Low pH (acid) + gas bubbles (CO₂) + black precipitate (H₂S) = mixed fermentation and anaerobic respiration using sulfate. The organism uses both pathways. (c) Alcoholic fermentation produces 2 ethanol per glucose. 0.8 moles ethanol/mole glucose means 0.4×50 mM = 20 mM (if 100% yield × 0.8 efficiency = 40 mM ethanol).

**A4.** (a) Maximum ATP = 686 kcal / 12 kcal = 57.2, so maximum 57 ATP (theoretical). (b) Efficiency = (30×12) / 686 ×100 = 360/686 ×100 = 52.5%. (c) No. 57 ATP represents 100% thermodynamic efficiency, which is impossible. The second law of thermodynamics states that energy conversions are never 100% efficient; some energy is always lost as heat (entropy increases). A yield of 57 ATP would violate this fundamental law. (d) Fermentation efficiency = (2×12)/686 ×100 = 3.5%. Organisms use it because it allows rapid ATP production and survival in anoxic conditions.

**A5.** (a) The curve would show a steep rise from 0 to ~2 mg/L O₂ (ATP yield increasing rapidly), then a plateau at ≈ 30-32 ATP above 4 mg/L. (b) The ATP yield plateaus because the ETC complexes and ATP synthase become saturated. The maximum rate is limited by: (i) number of ETC complexes, (ii) surface area of cristae, (iii) availability of ADP and Pi. (c) At low O₂, fermentation allows continued glycolysis and ATP production. Evolutionary advantage: survival in anoxic niches. Biochemical advantage: rapid ATP production (2 ATP/glucose × faster rate than respiration). (d) For maximum antibiotic production, use very low O₂ (<0.5 mg/L) to induce fermentation pathways that produce the antibiotic as a secondary metabolite.
