# Answer Key

## Part A Answers

### Question 1

a) Totipotent: Potential to produce all cell types of an organism including extraembryonic tissues (e.g., zygote).

b) Pluripotent: Can produce all cell types of the body but not extraembryonic tissues like the placenta (e.g., embryonic stem cells, iPSCs).

c) Multipotent: Can produce several (but not all) cell types within a particular lineage (e.g., haematopoietic stem cells).

d) Unipotent: Can produce only one cell type -- daughter cells of the same type (e.g., skin cells, spermatogonia).

### Question 2

Fertilisation -> Cleavage -> Blastula formation -> Gastrulation -> Organogenesis

### Question 3

Ectoderm: Outermost layer. Gives rise to the epidermis (skin) and the nervous system.

Mesoderm: Middle layer. Gives rise to muscles, bones, connective tissue, blood vessels, and the heart.

Endoderm: Innermost layer. Gives rise to the epithelial lining of the digestive tract, respiratory tract, and associated organs (e.g., liver, pancreas).

### Question 4

Diploblastic animals have two germ layers (ectoderm and endoderm), lack a mesoderm, and exhibit radial symmetry. Example: cnidarians (e.g., jellyfish).

Triploblastic animals have three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) and exhibit bilateral symmetry. Examples: frogs, humans, most animals.

### Question 5

Determination: The process by which a cell becomes committed to a particular developmental fate.

Differentiation: The process by which cells become structurally and functionally specialised.

Morphogenesis: The process by which cells and tissues are organised into body structures and shapes.

Growth: Increase in body size due to cell division and/or cell enlargement.

### Question 6

Cleavage is the rapid series of cell divisions of the zygote into smaller cells (blastomeres) without growth between divisions. Unlike normal mitosis, cleavage divisions lack G1 and G2 phases of the cell cycle. The overall embryo size remains constant while individual cells become smaller with each division.

### Question 7

The zona pellucida is a glycoprotein-rich protective layer surrounding the oocyte. During fertilisation, it undergoes the cortical reaction after sperm fusion, which modifies the zona pellucida to block polyspermy (entry of additional sperm). If polyspermy were not blocked, multiple sperm would fertilise the egg, resulting in polyploidy (abnormal chromosome number), which is typically lethal to the embryo.

### Question 8

The inner cell mass (ICM) is a cluster of cells inside the blastocyst that gives rise to the embryo proper. It is scientifically significant because ICM cells are pluripotent -- they can differentiate into all cell types of the body (but not extraembryonic tissues). This makes them valuable for stem cell research, disease modelling, and potential regenerative medicine applications.

### Question 9

Implantation occurs 6-7 days post-fertilisation. The trophoblast (outer layer of the blastocyst) develops into the placenta, which facilitates nutrient and gas exchange between mother and embryo.

### Question 10

Cell specialisation is unidirectional: as development proceeds, cells become progressively more restricted in their developmental potential (totipotent -> pluripotent -> multipotent -> unipotent -> terminally differentiated). This is generally irreversible in vivo because cells undergo epigenetic modifications that silence certain genes. The significance is that once specialised, cells cannot easily de-differentiate, which ensures tissue stability but limits regenerative capacity.

### Question 11

Two contexts are tissue maintenance and cancer. In tissue maintenance, stem cells continuously replace differentiated cells (e.g., intestinal epithelium replaced every 5-7 days). In cancer, developmental signalling pathways controlling proliferation and migration become dysregulated, leading to uncontrolled growth and metastasis.

### Question 12

Gurdon and Yamanaka demonstrated that mature, differentiated cells can be reprogrammed to a pluripotent state. Yamanaka identified four key transcription factors (Oct3/4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc) that, when introduced into somatic cells, convert them into induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). The significance is that iPSCs can be generated from a patient's own cells, avoiding ethical concerns with embryonic stem cells and enabling patient-specific disease modelling and cell therapy.

### Question 13

Tissue repair involves wound healing through fibrosis and scar tissue formation, often without restoration of original tissue structure or function. Tissue regeneration involves complete restoration of the original tissue architecture and function. Repair results in scar formation because fibroblasts deposit collagen without the precise patterning mechanisms seen in regeneration.

### Question 14

Intestinal stem cells reside in the crypts of the intestinal epithelium. They divide asymmetrically to produce one new stem cell (self-renewal) and one daughter cell that differentiates into specialised cells (enterocytes, goblet cells, enteroendocrine cells). The entire intestinal epithelium is replaced every 5-7 days, making it one of the most rapidly renewing tissues in the body.

### Question 15

Morphogenesis is the process by which cells and tissues acquire their shape and organisation to form functional body structures. During gastrulation, cell shape changes (such as flattening, elongation, and convergent extension) drive the inward movement of cells to form the three germ layers. These shape changes are powered by cytoskeletal reorganisation and cell adhesion molecule dynamics.

## Part B Answers

### Application Problem 1

**Procedure:**

Inject labelled cells from Population X and Population Y separately into blastocyst-stage embryos that carry a genetic marker (e.g., different coat colour). Allow the embryos to develop to term and analyse the contribution of injected cells to: (i) embryonic tissues, (ii) extraembryonic tissues (placenta), using the label to track contribution.

**Expected results:**

Population X (totipotent): Would contribute to both embryonic AND extraembryonic tissues (placenta), producing a chimeric offspring with donor cells in all tissues.

Population Y (pluripotent): Would contribute only to embryonic tissues, not to extraembryonic tissues.

**Control:**

Include un-injected blastocysts and blastocysts injected with known totipotent cells (e.g., early blastomeres) as positive controls to confirm the assay works.

### Application Problem 2

**(a) Average differentiated cells per day:**

10^9 cells / 6 days = 1.67 x 10^8 cells/day (approximately 167 million cells).

**(b) Number of stem cells needed:**

Each stem cell produces 1 differentiated cell per 12 hours = 2 differentiated cells per day.

Stem cells needed = (1.67 x 10^8) / 2 = 8.33 x 10^7 (approximately 83 million stem cells).

**(c) Prediction:**

With 18-hour divisions, each stem cell produces only 1.33 differentiated cells per day (24/18). The same number of stem cells would produce fewer differentiated cells (1.11 x 10^8 vs 1.67 x 10^8). This deficit could lead to epithelial thinning, impaired barrier function, and increased susceptibility to infection or inflammation. The stem cell pool would need to expand or division would need to accelerate to compensate.

### Application Problem 3

**(a) Most sensitive stage:**

Early gastrula is the most sensitive stage, with only 32% normal development and 58% showing abnormal gastrulation. This indicates the chemical specifically disrupts gastrulation processes.

**(b) Neurula stage explanation:**

By the neurula stage, gastrulation is already complete, so the chemical cannot cause abnormal gastrulation (0%). However, it can disrupt organogenesis (e.g., neural tube formation), leading to developmental failure (28% failed to hatch) due to defects in structures forming during this later stage.

**(c) Evaluation:**

The conclusion is partially supported: gastrulation does appear highly sensitive (58% abnormal at early gastrula). However, the data shows significant effects at cleavage (15% abnormal) and neurula (28% failure) stages too. A more accurate conclusion would be that different stages have different vulnerabilities -- early gastrula is most sensitive to gastrulation defects, while neurula stage is vulnerable to organogenesis failure.

### Application Problem 4

**(a) Key steps:**

1. Isolate skin fibroblasts from patient biopsy. 2. Culture fibroblasts in vitro. 3. Introduce reprogramming factors (using viral vectors, episomal plasmids, or mRNA). 4. Culture cells under conditions that select for iPSC colonies. 5. Verify pluripotency markers and differentiation potential. One Yamanaka factor is Oct3/4 (or Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc).

**(b) Benefits of autologous iPSCs:**

1. No immune rejection: Patient's own cells are genetically matched, eliminating need for immunosuppressive drugs. 2. Ethical acceptability: No destruction of embryos is required, addressing ethical concerns associated with embryonic stem cells.

**(c) Risk and mitigation:**

Risk: Teratoma formation -- undifferentiated iPSCs could form tumours after transplantation. Mitigation: Ensure complete differentiation before transplantation, implement purification protocols to remove residual pluripotent cells, and establish long-term monitoring of transplant recipients.

### Application Problem 5

**(a) Blastema:**

A blastema forms at the wound site -- it is a mass of proliferating, dedifferentiated cells that have reacquired developmental plasticity. The blastema is significant because it contains the progenitor cells that will proliferate, differentiate, and re-pattern to regenerate the missing structures.

**(b) Explanation for differential regenerative capacity:**

Salamanders retain the ability to form a blastema and reactivate developmental gene regulatory networks (e.g., Hox genes, FGF, Wnt, and Shh signalling) that pattern the limb. Mammals have lost this regenerative competence due to: (i) reduced blastema formation capacity, (ii) fibrotic/scarring response dominated by immune cells and fibroblasts, (iii) epigenetic silencing of key developmental genes in adult tissues, and (iv) differences in the extracellular matrix composition that promotes scarring over regeneration.

**(c) Molecular target proposal:**

Target: Wnt/beta-catenin signalling pathway. This pathway is essential for blastema formation and cell proliferation during regeneration in zebrafish and salamanders. A drug that activates Wnt signalling at the wound site could promote blastema formation, inhibit fibrosis, and stimulate regenerative growth. Supporting evidence includes studies showing that Wnt activation enhances regenerative capacity in normally non-regenerating animals.
