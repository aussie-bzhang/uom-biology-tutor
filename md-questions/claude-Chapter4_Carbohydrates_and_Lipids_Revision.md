## Revision Set 1 — Answers

### A. Short answer

**1.** Condensation: two monomers join by reaction of their hydroxyl (–OH) groups, forming a new covalent bond and releasing (producing) a molecule of water. Hydrolysis: water is added to a covalent bond to split it, breaking the polymer apart; this is the enzyme-assisted reaction. (Condensation produces water; hydrolysis consumes water.)

**2.** General formula (CH₂O)ₙ, where n is the number of carbon atoms (generally 3–8). Many sugars share the suffix “-ose”.

**3.** Monosaccharide (1 subunit) – e.g. glucose; Disaccharide (2) – e.g. sucrose; Oligosaccharide (3–10) – e.g. human milk oligosaccharide; Polysaccharide (100s–1000s) – e.g. starch / glycogen / cellulose.

**4.** They differ in the position (orientation) of the hydroxyl (–OH) group on carbon 1 of the glucose ring.

**5.** (a) Maltose = glucose + glucose; (b) lactose = galactose + glucose; (c) sucrose = glucose + fructose.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** No — hydrolysis breaks polymers apart; condensation builds them.

**3.** Yes — all are hexoses with the formula C₆H₁₂O₆ (they are isomers).

**4.** No — sucrose is a disaccharide.

**5.** Yes.

### C. MCQ

**.** 1-B, 2-B, 3-C, 4-B, 5-B, 6-C, 7-B, 8-A, 9-C, 10-C

### D. Calculation

**1.** (a) C₆H₁₂O₆ + C₆H₁₂O₆ → C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ + H₂O. (b) Maltose = C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁. (c) One (1) water molecule is released. (Check: left = C₁₂H₂₄O₁₂; right = C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ + H₂O = C₁₂H₂₄O₁₂.)

**2.** (a) Glyceraldehyde (triose): n = 3; ribose (pentose): n = 5. (b) (CH₂O)₃ = C₃H₆O₃ (glyceraldehyde) and (CH₂O)₅ = C₅H₁₀O₅ (ribose) — both fit the general formula.

## Revision Set 2 — Answers

### A. Short answer

**1.** Both are branched polymers of α-glucose joined by α-1,4 glycosidic bonds, with α-1,6 bonds creating branches. Starch is the primary energy-storage compound in plants and is easily degraded by amylases. Glycogen (“animal starch”) has essentially the same structure and is the primary energy store in animals, fungi and bacteria; in animals it is produced and stored mainly in the liver.

**2.** Cellulose is a linear (unbranched) polysaccharide of β-glucose joined by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds. Differences from starch: (i) it uses β-glucose rather than α-glucose; (ii) it is linear/unbranched rather than branched; (iii) it is more chemically stable (and serves a structural rather than storage role).

**3.** α-1,6 glycosidic bonds, which produce branching at carbon 6.

**4.** Cellulose is chemically stable, and its linear β-1,4 chains line up and hydrogen-bond to one another to form strong microfibrils (which bundle into fibrils). This gives the rigidity and strength needed for the plant cell wall.

**5.** Hydrogen bonding between neighbouring hydroxyl (–OH) groups on adjacent cellulose chains.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes.

**2.** Yes.

**3.** No — cellulose is more chemically stable and is not readily broken down by amylases as starch is.

**4.** Yes.

**5.** Yes.

### C. MCQ

**.** 1-B, 2-B, 3-C, 4-C, 5-C, 6-B, 7-B, 8-B, 9-C, 10-C

### D. Applied

**1.** Starch is built from α-glucose with α-1,4 (and α-1,6) bonds; this geometry produces helical/branched chains that enzymes (amylases) can readily access and hydrolyse, making it a good, accessible energy store. Cellulose is built from β-glucose with β-1,4 bonds; alternate glucose units are flipped, giving straight chains that pack tightly and hydrogen-bond into stable microfibrils that most enzymes cannot break — ideal for a structural material.

**2.** Hydrogen bonding between the many hydroxyl (–OH) groups on neighbouring straight cellulose chains holds them together as microfibrils. These hydrogen bonds are individually weak but collectively very strong, producing a stable, rigid fibre — providing the structural strength of the plant cell wall.

## Revision Set 3 — Answers

### A. Short answer

**1.** Any four: insoluble in water; non-polar; held together (between non-polar lipids) by weak but additive London dispersion forces only; dissolve readily in organic solvents; composed mainly of C, H and O; have less oxygen than carbohydrates; may contain other elements such as P and N.

**2.** A triglyceride consists of a glycerol backbone (an alcohol) joined to three fatty acid molecules. The fatty acids attach via three ester linkages, formed by condensation reactions (releasing water).

**3.** Saturated: no C=C double bonds, straight chains, pack tightly together. Unsaturated: one or more C=C double bonds that introduce kinks in the chain, which prevent close packing.

**4.** An amphipathic molecule has both a hydrophilic (water-loving) and a hydrophobic (water-fearing) part. In a phospholipid the hydrophilic “head” (phosphate/charged group) is attracted to water and can form ionic/ion-dipole interactions, while the two hydrophobic fatty-acid “tails” are not attracted to water and interact with other non-polar molecules via London dispersion forces.

**5.** In water the hydrophilic heads orient towards the water while the hydrophobic tails cluster away from it; this drives phospholipids to self-assemble into bilayers and closed vesicles (heads out, tails in). This spontaneous compartment-forming behaviour is thought to have ultimately led to the evolution of the cell membrane.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** No — lipids are insoluble in water.

**2.** Yes — non-polar lipids are held together only by (weak but additive) London dispersion forces.

**3.** Yes.

**4.** Yes — it is a condensation reaction that releases three water molecules.

**5.** Yes.

### C. MCQ

**.** 1-B, 2-B, 3-C, 4-B, 5-C, 6-B, 7-C, 8-B, 9-C, 10-B

### D. Calculation / applied

**1.** (a) 3 ester linkages. (b) 3 water molecules released. (c) A condensation reaction (one water lost per ester bond formed).

**2.** Saturated fats (butter) have no C=C bonds, so their straight fatty-acid chains pack tightly together; the many additive London dispersion forces hold them firmly, giving a solid at room temperature. Unsaturated fats (olive oil) contain C=C double bonds that create kinks in the chains; these kinks prevent close packing, so there are fewer/weaker dispersion contacts and the molecules slide past each other easily — giving a liquid at room temperature.
