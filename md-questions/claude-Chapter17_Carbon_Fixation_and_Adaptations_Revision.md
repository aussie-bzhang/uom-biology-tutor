# Answer Key

## Set 1 — The Calvin Cycle

### A. Short-answer

**1.** The Calvin cycle takes place in the stroma of the chloroplast. Inputs: CO₂, ATP and NADPH (from the light reactions), plus the acceptor RuBP. Outputs: sugar (G3P, used to build glucose/other carbohydrates) and the regenerated RuBP; ADP, Pᵢ and NADP⁺ are returned to the light reactions.

**2.** Stage 1 — Carbon fixation: CO₂ is attached to RuBP by RuBisCO, forming an unstable 6C intermediate that splits into two 3PG. Stage 2 — Reduction: 3PG is reduced to G3P using ATP and NADPH. Stage 3 — Regeneration of RuBP: most G3P is rearranged (using ATP) back into RuBP so the cycle can accept more CO₂; the remaining G3P leaves to make sugar.

**3.** CO₂ acceptor = RuBP (ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate, 5C); enzyme = RuBisCO; first stable product = 3PG (3-phosphoglycerate, 3C). Two 3PG form per CO₂ fixed.

**4.** It is 'light-independent' because it does not use light (photons) directly to drive its reactions; it uses the chemical products (ATP and NADPH) made by the light reactions. It usually runs in daylight only because that is when ATP/NADPH are being supplied — not because light powers the cycle itself.

**5.** Per glucose (6 CO₂ fixed): 18 ATP and 12 NADPH are consumed.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. Carbon fixation begins when CO₂ combines with RuBP (catalysed by RuBisCO).

**2.** Yes. RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) fixes CO₂.

**3.** Yes. The first stable product is the 3-carbon 3PG.

**4.** No. The Calvin cycle uses ATP and NADPH, not light directly.

**5.** Yes. ATP is used in the reduction step (3PG → G3P) and again in regenerating RuBP; NADPH is used only in the reduction step.

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-B, 2-B, 3-A, 4-C, 5-A, 6-B, 7-B, 8-B, 9-C, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) RuBP (5C) + CO₂ (1C) = a 6-carbon unstable intermediate, which immediately splits into TWO molecules of 3PG (each 3C). (b) Each G3P is 3 carbons; one G3P leaving the cycle represents 3 carbons, i.e. the fixation of 3 CO₂ (because for every 3 CO₂ the cycle nets one G3P out). (c) Glucose has 6 carbons = two G3P; making two G3P requires fixing 6 CO₂ over 6 turns of the cycle (one CO₂ per turn).

**2.** (a) Per 3 CO₂: reduction uses 6 ATP + 6 NADPH (to turn 6 3PG into 6 G3P); regeneration uses a further 3 ATP. Doubling for a whole glucose (6 CO₂): reduction 12 ATP + 12 NADPH, regeneration 6 ATP → totals 18 ATP and 12 NADPH. ✓ (b) ATP:NADPH used = 18:12 = 3:2. (c) Because the cycle needs more ATP than NADPH (3:2), the light reactions must supply extra ATP; cyclic electron flow (PSI only, making ATP but no NADPH) tops up ATP to meet this demand.

**3.** (a) 'The Calvin cycle begins when CO₂ reacts with RuBP' — catalysed by the enzyme RuBisCO. (b) RuBP (5-carbon) + CO₂ (1-carbon) → (6-carbon unstable intermediate) → two 3PG (each 3-carbon). (c) Experiment (Calvin's classic design): expose an illuminated suspension of algae (e.g. Chlorella) to ¹⁴CO₂ for only a few seconds, then kill the cells and separate the molecules (e.g. by chromatography) and detect radioactivity. Result confirming 3PG: after the shortest exposure, the radioactive label appears FIRST and predominantly in 3PG (the 3-carbon acid), showing it is the first stable product of carbon fixation.

## Set 2 — Photorespiration and Efficiency

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Photorespiration occurs when RuBisCO acts as an oxygenase instead of a carboxylase, adding O₂ (not CO₂) to RuBP: RuBP + O₂ → phosphoglycolate + 3PG. It fixes no useful carbon and wastes energy.

**2.** It is favoured when the leaf has relatively HIGH O₂ and LOW CO₂. This happens on hot, dry days when stomata close to prevent water loss: CO₂ cannot enter and O₂ (from the light reactions) builds up, so the O₂:CO₂ ratio rises and oxygenase activity increases.

**3.** The phosphoglycolate produced inhibits Calvin-cycle reactions and must be processed/eliminated at a cost (and some fixed carbon is released back as CO₂). Overall, photorespiration reduces the net carbon fixed by the Calvin cycle by about 25%.

**4.** RuBisCO normally has a much higher affinity for CO₂ than for O₂, so under normal CO₂ levels it mostly carboxylates (fixes CO₂) and photorespiration is limited.

**5.** Of 100% sunlight: ~50% is the wrong wavelength for the pigments (e.g. green light is reflected), ~30% is lost to poor leaf orientation/structure, ~10% to inefficiency of the light reactions, and ~5% to inefficiency of CO₂ fixation — leaving only about 5% stored as chemical-bond energy in carbohydrate.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. RuBisCO is dual-function: carboxylase (adds CO₂) and oxygenase (adds O₂).

**2.** Yes. It cuts net carbon fixation (by ~25%).

**3.** Yes. Closed stomata trap O₂ and exclude CO₂, raising the O₂:CO₂ ratio and favouring photorespiration.

**4.** No. RuBisCO normally has a higher affinity for CO₂, not O₂.

**5.** Yes. The ~50% lost to non-absorbed wavelengths (e.g. green light) is the single largest loss.

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-B, 2-B, 3-B, 4-B, 5-B, 6-B, 7-C, 8-A, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Carboxylase (productive): RuBP + CO₂ → 2 × 3PG → sugar (carbon gained). Oxygenase (photorespiration): RuBP + O₂ → phosphoglycolate + 3PG → (glycolate) → some CO₂ lost. (b) With stomata shut, CO₂ inside the leaf falls (used up by the Calvin cycle) and O₂ rises (made by the light reactions), so the high O₂:low CO₂ condition pushes RuBisCO toward the oxygenase reaction. (c) Net carbon = 100 − 25% = 100 − 25 = 75 units of carbon fixed per hour.

**2.** (a) 50 + 30 + 10 + 5 = 95% lost; 100 − 95 = 5% stored. ✓ (b) The biggest waste is the ~50% from wavelengths the pigments cannot absorb; green light (~500–550 nm) is reflected/transmitted rather than absorbed (which is why leaves look green), so that energy is not captured. (c) Reasonable strategies (target term in brackets): orienting/spacing crops or using sun-tracking to improve light interception (the ~30% orientation loss); breeding/engineering pigments or accessory pigments that absorb a wider spectrum incl. green (the ~50% wavelength loss); or engineering more efficient CO₂-fixation (the ~5% fixation loss, e.g. better RuBisCO or C4 traits).

**3.** (a) Plant A (normal air) will have MORE photorespiration, because its lower CO₂:O₂ ratio lets RuBisCO's oxygenase activity compete; Plant B's extra CO₂ keeps RuBisCO carboxylating. (b) Plant B will grow faster — more net carbon is fixed because photorespiration is suppressed. (c) Raising CO₂ (Plant B) shifts the competition at RuBisCO's active site toward carboxylation, cutting photorespiration — exactly the outcome C4 and CAM plants achieve biologically by concentrating CO₂ around RuBisCO.

## Set 3 — C3, C4 and CAM Adaptations

### A. Short-answer

**1.** C3 plants fix CO₂ directly with RuBisCO onto RuBP in the mesophyll cells, giving the 3-carbon first product 3PG (hence 'C3'). Because RuBisCO is exposed to the leaf's normal O₂, C3 plants suffer extensive photorespiration, especially in hot/dry conditions. Examples: rice, wheat, soybean, cannabis.

**2.** In C4 plants the mesophyll cells use PEP carboxylase to fix CO₂ onto PEP, forming the 4-carbon oxaloacetate (converted to malate) — hence 'C4'. The 4-carbon acid is pumped into bundle-sheath cells, where it is broken down to release CO₂ at high concentration around RuBisCO/the Calvin cycle. This 'CO₂ pump' minimises photorespiration.

**3.** In CAM plants the stomata open at NIGHT (and close by day) to conserve water. At night CO₂ is fixed by PEP carboxylase and stored as malate (an organic acid) in the vacuole. During the DAY the malate is decarboxylated and the released CO₂ enters the Calvin cycle — all in the same cells but at different times.

**4.** C4 = SPATIAL separation: initial CO₂ capture (PEP carboxylase) in mesophyll cells, Calvin cycle (RuBisCO) in bundle-sheath cells. CAM = TEMPORAL separation: initial CO₂ capture at night, Calvin cycle during the day — both in the same cell.

**5.** RuBisCO is present in ALL three (C3, C4, CAM) — every one ultimately runs the Calvin cycle. PEP carboxylase is present in C4 and CAM (for the initial CO₂-concentrating step) but is not a significant part of the C3 pathway.

### B. Yes / No

**1.** Yes. C4 plants separate the steps spatially (mesophyll vs bundle-sheath cells).

**2.** Yes. CAM plants separate them temporally (night vs day).

**3.** Yes. PEP is the primary CO₂ acceptor in both C4 and CAM (RuBP is the C3 acceptor).

**4.** Yes. CAM plants open stomata at night to reduce water loss.

**5.** Yes. All three use RuBisCO and the Calvin cycle (C4/CAM just feed it concentrated CO₂).

### C. MCQ

**1–10.** 1-B, 2-B, 3-B, 4-B, 5-B, 6-B, 7-B, 8-C, 9-B, 10-B.

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) 'In C4 plants the C4 and C3 pathways occur at different LOCATIONS / CELLS (mesophyll vs bundle-sheath); whereas in CAM plants the CAM and C3 pathways occur at different TIMES (night vs day).' (b) Table — C3 / C4 / CAM: primary CO₂ acceptor = RuBP / PEP / PEP; primary fixing enzyme = RuBisCO / PEP carboxylase / PEP carboxylase; first product = 3PG (3C) / oxaloacetate (4C) / oxaloacetate (4C); photosynthetic cells = mesophyll / mesophyll + bundle-sheath / mesophyll with large vacuoles; photorespiration = extensive / minimal / minimal. (c) In hot, sunny climates C3 plants must keep stomata partly closed to save water, which lowers CO₂ and raises photorespiration; C4 plants use PEP carboxylase to pump and concentrate CO₂ around RuBisCO, so they keep fixing carbon efficiently with little photorespiration and less water loss — giving corn higher yields than rice under those conditions.

**2.** (a) Yes — all three have RuBisCO, because all three ultimately use the Calvin cycle to build sugar; RuBisCO is the only enzyme that incorporates CO₂ into RuBP. (b) C4 and CAM plants also have PEP carboxylase (for the initial CO₂-capturing step); C3 plants generally do not rely on it because they fix CO₂ directly with RuBisCO and have no CO₂-concentrating step. (c) In a C4 leaf, PEP carboxylase works in the MESOPHYLL cells (capturing CO₂ as a 4C acid even at low CO₂, and it is not confused by O₂), while RuBisCO works in the BUNDLE-SHEATH cells, where the 4C acid is broken down to flood RuBisCO with CO₂. Keeping RuBisCO in the inner bundle-sheath cells, supplied with concentrated CO₂, shields it from O₂ and suppresses photorespiration.

**3.** (a) Useful because C4/CAM crops use water more efficiently, photorespire less and yield more in hot, dry, high-light climates — valuable for food security as the climate warms and water becomes scarcer. (b) You would need to introduce (at least) the gene for PEP carboxylase (and the other C4-cycle enzymes such as those making/decarboxylating malate), PLUS the anatomical change — 'Kranz' anatomy with specialised bundle-sheath cells and the correct mesophyll/bundle-sheath arrangement — so CO₂ can be concentrated around RuBisCO. (c) A key difficulty: C4 photosynthesis is not a single enzyme but a coordinated SYSTEM — it needs the right leaf anatomy, the correct cell types, transporters and regulation all working together. Adding PEP carboxylase alone, without the bundle-sheath structure and supporting enzymes, will not produce a functioning CO₂-concentrating mechanism.
