# Answer Key

## Set 1 - Short-answer questions

**1.** Electrons from NADH and FADH2 pass through electron carriers in the inner mitochondrial membrane. The released energy pumps H+ into the intermembrane space. H+ then flows back through ATP synthase, and this chemiosmotic flow drives ATP formation from ADP and Pi.

**2.** Oxygen is the final electron acceptor. Without oxygen, electron flow stops, NADH/FADH2 cannot be efficiently reoxidized, the proton gradient cannot be maintained, and high-yield ATP production by oxidative phosphorylation fails.

**3.** NADH and FADH2 carry high-energy electrons from glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, and the TCA cycle to the electron transport chain. Their reoxidation provides energy for proton pumping.

**4.** Fermentation does not use an electron transport chain and does not fully oxidize glucose to CO2. It mainly allows glycolysis to continue by regenerating NAD+, so only the 2 ATP from glycolysis are gained.

**5.** Lactic acid fermentation uses pyruvate as the electron acceptor and produces lactate; it occurs in some microorganisms and animal cells. Alcoholic fermentation converts pyruvate to ethanol and CO2; it occurs in yeasts and some plant cells.

## Set 1 - Application/calculation questions

**1.** Maximum theoretical ATP = 686/12 = 57.2, so about 57 ATP. Actual efficiency with 30 ATP = 30/57.2 x 100% = about 52%. The remaining energy is lost or unavailable, consistent with the second law of thermodynamics.

**2.** NADH would accumulate and NAD+ would become depleted. Since glycolysis requires NAD+ to oxidize glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, glycolysis would slow or stop. ATP production would fall because fermentation is needed to regenerate NAD+ when oxygen is absent.

**3.** H+ flowing down its electrochemical gradient through ATP synthase provides the energy for rotation/conformational change in ATP synthase, allowing ADP and Pi to form ATP.

## Set 2 - Short-answer questions

**1.** Aerobic respiration uses O2 as final electron acceptor and makes ATP mainly by oxidative phosphorylation. Anaerobic respiration uses an ETC but uses alternative terminal electron acceptors such as sulfate, ferric iron, or CO2. Fermentation uses no ETC and makes ATP only through substrate-level phosphorylation in glycolysis.

**2.** Anaerobic respiration can use electron transport with non-oxygen electron acceptors. Fermentation instead transfers electrons from NADH to organic molecules derived from the nutrient, mainly to regenerate NAD+.

**3.** As electrons move through ETC complexes, energy is released and used to pump H+ from the matrix to the intermembrane space. This creates a proton gradient and membrane potential.

**4.** NAD+ is required in glycolysis to accept electrons. Without NAD+, the oxidation step in glycolysis cannot continue, so ATP production from glycolysis stops.

**5.** A yield of 57 ATP would represent nearly all the free energy of glucose captured as ATP if each ATP stores 12 kcal. Real cells produce about 30 ATP and lose some energy as heat/unusable energy, so 57 ATP is not realistic under normal biological thermodynamics.

## Set 2 - Application/calculation questions

**1.** Aerobic respiration: electrons go through ETC to O2; reduced product is water; ATP is made by oxidative phosphorylation plus substrate-level phosphorylation. Anaerobic respiration: electrons go through ETC to non-O2 acceptors such as sulfate/ferric iron/CO2; ATP is still made via chemiosmosis. Fermentation: electrons are transferred to organic molecules; no ETC; ATP comes only from glycolysis.

**2.** 30/2 = 15. Aerobic respiration produces about 15 times more ATP per glucose than fermentation.

**3.** ATP production by oxidative phosphorylation would fall sharply or stop because ATP synthase requires the H+ gradient as its energy source. Even if ATP synthase is intact, without the gradient it cannot efficiently synthesize ATP.
