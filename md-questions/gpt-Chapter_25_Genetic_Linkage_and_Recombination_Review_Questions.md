# Reference Answers

## Set 1

### 1. Short-Answer Questions

**1.** Genetic linkage occurs when loci are on the same chromosome and therefore tend to be inherited together. The 1:1:1:1 test-cross ratio is disrupted because parental allele combinations are usually more frequent than recombinant combinations.

**2.** During prophase I, homologous chromosomes pair and exchange segments at chiasmata. If a crossover occurs between two loci, alleles originally on different homologues can be recombined onto the same chromatid.

**3.** Recombination frequency is the percentage of recombinant gametes or recombinant offspring among the total. It is used as a proxy for distance because genes farther apart are more likely to have a crossover between them.

**4.** Parental gametes carry the allele combinations present in the heterozygous parent. Recombinant gametes carry new combinations produced by crossing over.

**5.** In a test cross with a homozygous recessive tester, each offspring phenotype/genotype reflects the gamete contributed by the heterozygous parent, making parental and recombinant classes easy to count.

### 2. Yes/No Questions

**1.** Yes. Genes on different chromosomes assort independently, giving equal gamete classes in the standard model.

**2.** Yes. Crossing over between homologous chromosomes can recombine linked genes.

**3.** No. A value near 50% suggests independent assortment or very distant loci, not tight linkage.

**4.** Yes. This is the main reason test crosses are useful for linkage analysis.

**5.** Yes. Genetic map distances are expressed as map units or centimorgans.

**2.** A. 12 map units.

### 4. Biochemical / Genetic Application Questions

**1.** Parental classes are AaBb and aabb, because they are most frequent. Recombinant classes are Aabb and aaBb. RF = (75 + 75) / (430 + 420 + 75 + 75) x 100 = 150/1000 x 100 = 15%. Estimated distance = 15 cM.

**2.** Recombinant classes = 105 + 4 = 109. Total male offspring = 316 + 105 + 33 + 4 = 458. RF = 109/458 x 100 = 23.8%. Viability can complicate the estimate because some phenotypes, such as rudimentary-wing flies, may survive or be recovered at lower frequency than expected.

**3.** Cross pure-breeding parents with alternative allele combinations, such as AABB x aabb or AAbb x aaBB, to generate F1 dihybrids. Test-cross F1 individuals to aabb testers. Count offspring phenotypes. If the four classes fit about 1:1:1:1, independent assortment is supported; if two parental classes greatly exceed the two recombinant classes, the loci are linked. Calculate RF from recombinant offspring / total offspring x 100.

## Set 2

### 1. Short-Answer Questions

**1.** In cis arrangement, the two dominant alleles are on one homologue and the two recessive alleles are on the other, written AB/ab. In trans arrangement, each homologue carries one dominant and one recessive allele, written Ab/aB.

**2.** The most common classes are usually parental because linked loci are transmitted together more often than they are separated by crossing over.

**3.** A recombination frequency close to 0% means very few recombinant gametes are produced; the loci are very tightly linked or extremely close on the chromosome.

**4.** If two genes are far apart on the same chromosome, crossovers between them are frequent enough that the observed recombination frequency approaches 50%, resembling independent assortment.

**5.** A linkage map gives approximate genetic distances. These distances can be converted into expected parental and recombinant gamete frequencies, allowing prediction of offspring classes in test crosses.

### 2. Yes/No Questions

**1.** Yes. Cis is usually represented as AB/ab.

**2.** Yes. Trans is represented as Ab/aB, so Ab and aB are parental.

**3.** Yes. Adjacent intervals can be added to estimate distances across a map, while remembering that observed RF can be approximate.

**4.** No. A single crossover involves two of the four chromatids, producing two recombinant and two nonrecombinant chromatids.

**5.** Yes. Rare recombinants generally indicate close linkage.

**1.** The loci are linked because the four classes are far from equal. The large classes are normal-flaky (56) and droopy-normal (57), so these are parental. ## Set 3

### 1. Short-Answer Questions

**1.** Sturtevant observed that some trait combinations appeared much more often than expected under independent assortment, while other combinations were rare. This showed that certain loci were associated because they were located on the same chromosome.

**2.** RF is calculated as a percentage of recombinant offspring or gametes. Empirically, 1% recombination is treated as about 1 map unit, or 1 cM, for mapping over short intervals.

**3.** You must know the allele arrangement in the heterozygous parent, or infer it from the most common offspring classes. Only then can you decide which classes are parental and which are recombinant.

**4.** Linked genes can be separated by crossing over. Therefore, they are inherited together more often than unlinked genes, but not necessarily always together.

**5.** Identify the two most frequent offspring classes; these usually represent the parental gametes from the heterozygote. Their allele combinations reveal whether the heterozygote was in cis or trans arrangement.

### 2. Yes/No Questions

**1.** Yes. In a test cross, offspring classes usually reflect gametes produced by the heterozygote.

**2.** Yes. AB and ab as the frequent classes indicate cis arrangement.

**3.** Yes. For a two-point map, 25 cM corresponds to about 25% total recombinants.

**4.** No. Recombinant status depends on allele combination, not simply dominance.

**5.** Yes. By comparing distances among loci, linkage maps can infer relative gene order.
