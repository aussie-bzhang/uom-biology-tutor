# Application Problems

*This section contains 5 application problems covering genotype-phenotype relationships, dominance patterns, multiple alleles, and X-linked inheritance. Show all working and explain your reasoning.*

## Question 1: Drosophila Eye Colour - Monohybrid Cross Analysis

In Drosophila melanogaster, wild-type red eye colour (allele R) is dominant to brown eye colour (allele r). A pure-breeding red-eyed female is crossed with a brown-eyed male.

1. **(a)** Write the genotypes of the parental (P) generation. (2 marks)
2. **(b)** Determine the genotypes and phenotypes of the F1 generation, showing the Punnett square. (4 marks)
3. **(c)** If two F1 individuals are crossed, what is the expected phenotypic ratio in the F2 generation? Provide the Punnett square and explain your answer. (4 marks)
4. **(d)** In the F2 generation, what proportion of individuals with red eyes are expected to be homozygous? Explain your calculation. (3 marks)
5. **(e)** A researcher observes a 3:1 phenotypic ratio in the F2. Explain what this ratio reveals about the molecular basis of dominance for this trait. (2 marks)

## Question 2: Snapdragon Flower Colour - Incomplete Dominance

In snapdragons (Antirrhinum majus), flower colour shows incomplete dominance. The allele for red flowers (CR) and the allele for white flowers (CW) produce pink flowers in the heterozygous condition.

1. **(a)** Explain why this is an example of incomplete dominance at the molecular level, using the concept of allele "dose". (3 marks)
2. **(b)** A plant breeder crosses a red-flowered snapdragon with a white-flowered snapdragon. Show the expected genotypic and phenotypic ratios of the F1 and F2 generations using Punnett squares. (5 marks)
3. **(c)** Design an experiment to distinguish whether a pink-flowered plant is homozygous or heterozygous using a test cross. Describe the expected results for each possible genotype. (4 marks)
4. **(d)** If 160 offspring are produced from a cross between two pink-flowered snapdragons, calculate the expected number of each phenotype. Show your working. (3 marks)
5. **(e)** Explain how the phenotypic ratio in the F2 generation of incomplete dominance differs from that of complete dominance, and why this difference occurs at the molecular level. (2 marks)

## Question 3: Human ABO Blood Groups - Multiple Alleles & Codominance

The ABO blood group system in humans is controlled by three alleles: IA, IB, and i. Alleles IA and IB are codominant to each other, and both are dominant to allele i.

1. **(a)** Complete the following table showing all possible genotypes for each blood group phenotype. (3 marks)

| **Blood Group** | **Possible Genotypes** | **Dominance Relationship** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| A | IA/IA or IA/i | IA dominant to i |
| B | IB/IB or IB/i | IB dominant to i |
| AB | IA/IB | IA and IB codominant |
| O | i/i | Recessive |

1. **(b)** A woman with blood group A and a man with blood group B have a child with blood group O. Determine the genotypes of both parents and show how this child is possible using a genetic diagram. (4 marks)
2. **(c)** A woman with blood group AB is expecting a child. The alleged father has blood group O. Using a genetic diagram, determine whether this man could be the biological father. Explain your reasoning. (4 marks)
3. **(d)** In a paternity dispute, a mother has blood group B, her child has blood group AB, and the alleged father has blood group A. Show all possible parental genotypes and explain whether the alleged father could be the biological father. (4 marks)

## Question 4: Guinea Pig Coat Colour - Multiple Allele Series

In guinea pigs, coat colour is controlled by a series of four alleles with the following dominance hierarchy: C (black) > ck (sepia) > cd (cream) > ca (albino).

1. **(a)** How many different genotypes are possible in total for this gene? Show your calculation. (2 marks)
2. **(b)** Determine the phenotypes resulting from the following crosses. Show your working for each: (6 marks)

**(i)** ck cd x ck cd

**(ii)** C ca x cd ca

**(iii)** ck cd x cd ca

1. **(c)** Two sepia guinea pigs are crossed and produce offspring with three different phenotypes. Deduce the genotypes of the parents and calculate the expected phenotypic ratio of the offspring. (4 marks)
2. **(d)** A breeder wants to produce only black offspring. What genotype(s) should the parents have? Justify your answer. (2 marks)

## Question 5: X-linked Colour Blindness - Pedigree & Probability Analysis

Red-green colour blindness is an X-linked recessive disorder in humans. The normal vision allele (XN) is dominant to the colour-blind allele (Xn).

1. **(a)** A man with normal vision marries a woman who is a carrier for colour blindness. Using a genetic diagram, determine the probability that: (6 marks)

**(i)** Their first son will be colour blind

**(ii)** Their first daughter will be a carrier

**(iii)** Their second child, regardless of sex, will have normal vision

1. **(b)** A colour-blind man marries a woman with normal vision whose father was colour blind. What is the probability that their first child will be: (6 marks)

**(i)** A colour-blind son?

**(ii)** A carrier daughter?

**(iii)** A daughter with normal vision who is not a carrier?

1. **(c)** Explain why X-linked recessive disorders are more commonly observed in males than in females. Use the terms hemizygous and homozygous in your answer. (3 marks)
2. **(d)** A genetic counsellor is advising a couple where the woman is a known carrier for an X-linked recessive disorder and the man is unaffected. They want to know the probability of having an affected child. Calculate this probability and explain your reasoning. (2 marks)

# Answer Key

*Answers and marking guidelines for all application problems.*

## Question 1: Drosophila Eye Colour

**(a)** Parental genotypes: Red-eyed female = RR (homozygous dominant); Brown-eyed male = rr (homozygous recessive). (1 mark each)

**(b)** F1 generation: All offspring will be Rr (heterozygous) with red eyes. Punnett square: RR x rr produces all Rr offspring. The dominant R allele masks the recessive r allele, so all F1 individuals show the red-eyed phenotype. (2 marks genotype, 2 marks phenotype explanation)

**(c)** F2 phenotypic ratio: 3 red-eyed : 1 brown-eyed. Punnett square for Rr x Rr: RR (1/4, red), Rr (2/4, red), rr (1/4, brown). The 3:1 ratio occurs because the dominant allele R is fully expressed in both homozygous (RR) and heterozygous (Rr) conditions. (2 marks Punnett, 2 marks explanation)

**(d)** Of the red-eyed individuals (3/4 of total), 1/3 are RR and 2/3 are Rr. Therefore 1/3 of red-eyed F2 individuals are homozygous. Calculation: RR = 1/4 of total; red-eyed = 3/4 of total; (1/4)/(3/4) = 1/3. (2 marks calculation, 1 mark explanation)

**(e)** The 3:1 ratio reveals that the R allele produces a functional protein product (e.g., an enzyme in the pigment synthesis pathway), and one copy of the functional allele is sufficient to produce the wild-type phenotype. The r allele is non-functional, and the brown phenotype only appears when no functional copies are present (rr). This demonstrates complete dominance at the molecular level. (2 marks)

## Question 2: Snapdragon Flower Colour

**(a)** Incomplete dominance occurs because neither allele is fully dominant over the other. At the molecular level, the CR allele produces a full dose of red pigment, while CW produces no pigment. CR/CR = high pigment (red), CR/CW = intermediate pigment (pink), CW/CW = no pigment (white). The heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype because one functional allele produces half the amount of pigment compared to two functional alleles. (3 marks)

**(b)** P cross: CR/CR (red) x CW/CW (white). F1: All CR/CW (pink). F2 from CR/CW x CR/CW: 1 CR/CR (red) : 2 CR/CW (pink) : 1 CW/CW (white). Genotypic ratio = 1:2:1; Phenotypic ratio = 1:2:1 (red:pink:white). (3 marks Punnett squares, 2 marks ratios)

**(c)** Test cross: Cross the pink-flowered plant with a homozygous white-flowered plant (CW/CW). If the pink plant is CR/CW: offspring will be 1/2 pink (CR/CW) and 1/2 white (CW/CW). If the pink plant were somehow homozygous (not possible for pink in true incomplete dominance, but if testing a red plant): all offspring would be pink (CR/CW). (2 marks design, 2 marks expected results)

**(d)** From CR/CW x CR/CW: expected ratio 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white. Of 160 offspring: Red = 160 x 1/4 = 40; Pink = 160 x 2/4 = 80; White = 160 x 1/4 = 40. (2 marks ratio, 1 mark calculation)

**(e)** In complete dominance, the F2 ratio is 3:1 (two phenotypes), while in incomplete dominance it is 1:2:1 (three phenotypes). This difference occurs because in complete dominance, the heterozygote expresses only the dominant phenotype, while in incomplete dominance, the heterozygote expresses an intermediate phenotype due to dosage effects - one functional allele produces less gene product than two functional alleles. (2 marks)

## Question 3: ABO Blood Groups

**(a)** Blood group A: IA/IA or IA/i; Blood group B: IB/IB or IB/i; Blood group AB: IA/IB; Blood group O: i/i. (3 marks - 1 mark per correct row or equivalent partial credit)

**(b)** For a group A mother and group B father to have a group O child, both parents must be heterozygous: Mother = IA/i, Father = IB/i. Genetic diagram: IA/i x IB/i produces offspring with genotypes IA/IB (AB), IA/i (A), IB/i (B), and i/i (O), each with 25% probability. The i/i child has blood group O because neither IA nor IB alleles are present. (2 marks parental genotypes, 2 marks genetic diagram)

**(c)** Woman = IA/IB (blood group AB), Man = i/i (blood group O). Cross: IA/IB x i/i produces 50% IA/i (blood group A) and 50% IB/i (blood group B). It is impossible for this man to father a child with blood group AB, O, or any phenotype matching alleles he does not possess. He could only be the father if the child has blood group A or B. (2 marks diagram, 2 marks explanation)

**(d)** Mother (group B): could be IB/IB or IB/i. Child (group AB): must be IA/IB. Alleged father (group A): could be IA/IA or IA/i. For the child to be IA/IB, the child must receive IB from mother and IA from father. Father could be IA/IA or IA/i; either can donate IA. Mother must be IB/i to donate IB. Yes, the alleged father could be the father if his genotype is IA/IA or IA/i, and the mother's genotype is IB/i. (2 marks genotypes, 2 marks explanation)

## Question 4: Guinea Pig Coat Colour

**(a)** With 4 alleles, the number of possible genotypes in a diploid organism is calculated by n(n+1)/2 where n = number of alleles. For 4 alleles: 4 x 5 / 2 = 10 genotypes. These are: CC, Cck, Ccd, Cca, ckck, ckcd, ckca, cdcd, cdca, caca. (2 marks)

**(b)** (i) ck cd x ck cd: Both parents are sepia. Offspring: 1/4 ck/ck (sepia), 2/4 ck/cd (sepia), 1/4 cd/cd (cream). Phenotypic ratio: 3 sepia : 1 cream. (ii) C ca x cd ca: Parent 1 is black (C is dominant), Parent 2 is cream. Offspring: 1/4 C/cd (black), 1/4 C/ca (black), 1/4 cd/ca (cream), 1/4 ca/ca (albino). Phenotypic ratio: 2 black : 1 cream : 1 albino. (iii) ck cd x cd ca: Parent 1 is sepia, Parent 2 is cream. Offspring: 1/4 ck/cd (sepia), 1/4 ck/ca (sepia), 1/4 cd/cd (cream), 1/4 cd/ca (cream). Phenotypic ratio: 2 sepia : 2 cream = 1 sepia : 1 cream. (2 marks each)

**(c)** Two sepia guinea pigs cannot produce offspring with three different phenotypes. Since both parents lack the dominant C allele, black offspring are impossible. The maximum number of phenotypes from two sepia parents is two: (i) ck/ca x ck/ca gives 3 sepia : 1 albino; (ii) ck/cd x ck/ca gives 3 sepia : 1 cream; (iii) ck/cd x ck/cd gives 3 sepia : 1 cream. To obtain three phenotypes (sepia, cream, albino), one parent must carry ca and another must carry cd, but both being sepia limits the possible combinations. (2 marks deduction, 2 marks explanation)

**(d)** To produce only black offspring, at least one parent must be homozygous dominant CC. The cross CC x any genotype will produce all offspring with at least one C allele, resulting in all black offspring. For example, CC x caca produces all C/ca (black). Both parents being CC also works. (2 marks)

## Question 5: X-linked Colour Blindness

**(a)** Man = XN/Y (normal vision), Woman = XN/Xn (carrier). Cross: XN/Y x XN/Xn. Offspring genotypes: 1/4 XN/XN (normal female), 1/4 XN/Xn (carrier female), 1/4 XN/Y (normal male), 1/4 Xn/Y (colour-blind male). (i) P(colour-blind son) = 1/4 or 25%. (ii) P(carrier daughter) = 1/4 or 25% (of all offspring) or 1/2 of daughters. (iii) P(normal vision) = 3/4 or 75% (all except Xn/Y). (2 marks each)

**(b)** Man = Xn/Y (colour blind), Woman: her father was colour blind (Xn/Y), so she must be XN/Xn (carrier, since she has normal vision). Cross: Xn/Y x XN/Xn. Offspring: 1/4 XN/Xn (carrier female), 1/4 Xn/Xn (colour-blind female), 1/4 XN/Y (normal male), 1/4 Xn/Y (colour-blind male). (i) P(colour-blind son) = 1/4 or 25%. (ii) P(carrier daughter) = 1/4 or 25% of all offspring, or 1/2 of daughters. (iii) P(normal vision daughter, not carrier) = 0%, because all daughters receive Xn from father, so all daughters are either carriers (XN/Xn) or colour blind (Xn/Xn). (2 marks each)

**(c)** X-linked recessive disorders are more common in males because males are hemizygous for X-linked genes - they have only one X chromosome. A male needs only one copy of the recessive allele (Xn/Y) to express the disorder. Females have two X chromosomes, so they must be homozygous recessive (Xn/Xn) to express the disorder. A female with one normal allele and one recessive allele (XN/Xn) is a carrier with normal phenotype. (3 marks)

**(d)** Woman = XN/Xn (carrier), Man = XN/Y (unaffected). Cross produces: 1/4 XN/XN, 1/4 XN/Xn, 1/4 XN/Y, 1/4 Xn/Y. Only Xn/Y offspring are affected. Probability of an affected child = 1/4 or 25%. This is because there is a 50% chance of a male child, and a 50% chance that male inherits Xn: 1/2 x 1/2 = 1/4. (2 marks)
