## Revision Set 1 — The Cytosol and the Cytoskeleton

### A. Short-answer

**1.** The cytosol is the fluid component of the cytoplasm: the space between the organelles and the endomembrane system. It is the site of numerous biochemical processes; biochemical intermediates are shuttled and converted there. Two processes that occur there: protein biosynthesis and the reactions of (cytosolic) metabolism, e.g. glycolysis. (The cytoskeleton is also located there.)

**2.** Cytoskeletal components are composed of PROTEIN, NOT membrane. Functions: they act as scaffolding/structural elements within the cytoplasm; they maintain cell shape; they organise cellular structures and activities and anchor organelles; they facilitate cell movements. (Any two.)

**3.** Microfilaments (actin filaments) ~7 nm; intermediate filaments ~8-12 nm; microtubules ~25 nm (with a ~15 nm lumen).

**4.** Subunit: actin (two intertwined strands of actin subunits). Functions (any three): maintenance of cell shape as tension-bearing elements; changes in cell shape; muscle contraction; cytoplasmic streaming; cell motility (e.g. pseudopodia); cell division (cleavage-furrow formation).

**5.** Intermediate filaments are built from fibrous proteins of the keratin family (which protein depends on cell type), supercoiled into thicker cables; distinctive functions: anchorage of the nucleus and certain organelles and formation of the nuclear lamina. Microtubules are built from tubulin (a dimer of alpha- and beta-tubulin); distinctive functions: cell motility via cilia/flagella, chromosome movement in cell division, and organelle movement.

### B. Yes/No

**1.** No — they are made of protein, not membrane.

**2.** Yes — a microtubule is a hollow tube whose wall consists of 13 columns of tubulin molecules.

**3.** No — the cytoplasm is made up of the cytosol, the cytoskeleton AND the organelles; the cytosol is only the fluid part.

**4.** Yes — microfilaments (actin) are involved in muscle contraction and cytoplasmic streaming.

**5.** Yes — intermediate filaments help form the nuclear lamina (and anchor the nucleus).

### C. MCQ

**1.** A (7 nm).

**2.** C (tubulin).

**3.** C (microtubule).

**4.** C (keratin family).

**5.** A (microfilaments).

**6.** B (microtubules).

**7.** C (microfilaments).

**8.** B (microtubules).

**9.** B (protein biosynthesis).

**10.** C (intermediate filaments).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Combined wall thickness = outer diameter - lumen = 25 - 15 = 10 nm; therefore one wall = 10 / 2 = 5 nm. (b) 25 nm / 7 nm ~= 3.6, so about 3-4 microfilaments side by side would span one microtubule's outer diameter.

**2.** A microtubule is a wide hollow cylinder built from 13 longitudinal columns of tubulin; a hollow tube has a high resistance to bending and buckling for relatively little material, so it acts like a rigid 'girder' that resists being compressed/pushed. A microfilament is just two thin actin strands twisted together; such a thin fibre is easily bent under compression but is strong when pulled along its length, so it works well as a tension-bearing cable. Hence microtubules resist compression while microfilaments bear tension.

## Revision Set 2 — DNA Replication

### A. Short-answer

**1.** Semi-conservative means that each of the two daughter DNA molecules contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesised strand. Because the original strand is retained, it can serve as an intact template, which is what allows damage/errors to be detected and corrected (repair).

**2.** Nucleotides are added in the 5' -> 3' direction. 'Antiparallel' means the two strands of the double helix have opposite polarity/directionality: one runs 5' -> 3' while its partner runs 3' -> 5'.

**3.** DNA helicase unwinds the double helix; single-stranded binding proteins keep the separated template strands apart; primase synthesises a (RNA) primer to start synthesis; DNA polymerase III is the enzyme that synthesises DNA, adding nucleotides to the 3' end (it elongates both strands and also proofreads).

**4.** The leading strand is synthesised continuously toward the advancing replication fork. The lagging strand runs in the opposite direction, so it is made discontinuously, in short pieces called Okazaki fragments, which are later joined together.

**5.** Prokaryotes have a SINGLE origin of replication on their (usually circular) DNA, whereas eukaryotes have MULTIPLE origins along each chromosome. Multiple origins let a large eukaryotic genome be copied from many points at once, so replication finishes in a reasonable time.

### B. Yes/No

**1.** No — DNA polymerase III adds nucleotides to the 3' end (synthesis is 5' -> 3').

**2.** Yes — synthesis of the leading strand is continuous.

**3.** No — prokaryotes have a single origin of replication.

**4.** Yes — primase synthesises a primer (an RNA primer).

**5.** No — DNA replication is semi-conservative, not conservative.

### C. MCQ

**1.** C (one parental strand and one new strand).

**2.** B (DNA helicase).

**3.** B (keep the separated template strands apart).

**4.** B (Okazaki fragments).

**5.** B (5' -> 3').

**6.** B (3' end).

**7.** B (one).

**8.** C (multiple origins of replication).

**9.** B (RNA).

**10.** B (the two strands run in opposite 5'/3' directions).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) After one round, EVERY molecule is of INTERMEDIATE density. Each daughter molecule has one old (heavy, 15N) parental strand paired with one new (light, 14N) strand, so all molecules are half-heavy/half-light. (b) After two rounds you get a MIX: half the molecules are intermediate (old heavy strand + new light strand) and half are fully light (both strands made in 14N). (c) Conservative replication would have kept the two heavy parental strands together, giving one heavy molecule plus light molecules after the first round (no intermediate band) — that is NOT seen. The single intermediate band after round one, splitting into intermediate + light after round two, is exactly what semi-conservative replication predicts. (This is the Meselson-Stahl experiment.)

**2.** (a) One origin -> two forks, so each fork copies half the segment = 1.5 x 10^7 bp. Time = 1.5 x 10^7 / 50 = 3.0 x 10^5 s = 300,000 s ~= 83.3 hours (about 3.5 days). (b) 100 origins -> 200 forks; total work 3.0 x 10^7 bp shared over 200 forks = 1.5 x 10^5 bp per fork. Time = 1.5 x 10^5 / 50 = 3,000 s = 50 minutes. (c) A single origin would take days to copy a large chromosome, which is far too slow; using many origins at once cuts replication time dramatically, so eukaryotes (with very large genomes) rely on multiple origins.

## Revision Set 3 — The Cell Cycle, Mitosis and Binary Fission

### A. Short-answer

**1.** The two broad parts are interphase and the mitotic (M) phase. The M phase is usually the shorter (shortest) part. Interphase accounts for about 90% of the cycle.

**2.** G1 ('first gap') — metabolic activity and growth; S ('synthesis') — DNA replication (also growth and metabolic activity); G2 ('second gap') — metabolic activity, growth and preparation for mitosis (spindle synthesis begins).

**3.** CDKs (cyclin-dependent kinases) are always present but inactive on their own; cyclins are made and destroyed so their levels rise and fall ('cycle') through the cell cycle. When a cyclin binds its CDK it forms an activated complex that phosphorylates target proteins, driving the cell forward. Different cyclin-CDK complexes (G1, S, G2-M, M) act at different points, and they work with checkpoint signals (restriction points) to allow or block progression.

**4.** Prophase — chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes (paired sister chromatids) and the spindle begins to assemble; Prometaphase — the nuclear envelope breaks down and kinetochore microtubules attach chromosomes to the spindle; Metaphase — chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (the cell's equator); Anaphase — sister chromatids separate and the daughter chromosomes move to opposite poles; Telophase — chromosomes reach the poles, nuclear envelopes and nucleoli re-form and chromatin decondenses (followed by cytokinesis).

**5.** Mitosis is the division of a eukaryotic nucleus to give two genetically identical nuclei; binary fission ('division in half') is the division of a prokaryotic cell and does NOT involve mitosis (the term is also used for asexual reproduction of single-celled eukaryotes such as amoeba). The constricting protein in prokaryotes is FtsZ, which forms protein fibres (the Z ring) that pinch the cell; FtsZ is a homologue of the eukaryotic cytoskeletal protein tubulin.

### B. Yes/No

**1.** No — the M phase is usually the SHORTEST part of the cell cycle.

**2.** Yes — mitosis produces two genetically identical nuclei.

**3.** No — prokaryotic cell division is by binary fission and does not involve mitosis.

**4.** Yes — FtsZ is a homologue of tubulin.

**5.** No — plant cells form a cell plate between the two daughter cells; the contractile ring (cleavage furrow) is used by animal cells.

### C. MCQ

**1.** B (S phase).

**2.** D (90%).

**3.** B (metaphase).

**4.** C (anaphase).

**5.** B (prometaphase).

**6.** B (CDKs are always present while cyclin levels rise and fall).

**7.** C (a chromosome is unattached to the spindle).

**8.** B (a contractile ring forming a cleavage furrow).

**9.** C (FtsZ).

**10.** B (two genetically identical nuclei).

### D. Applied

**1.** (a) Interphase = 90% of 24 h = 0.90 x 24 = 21.6 hours; M phase = 10% of 24 h = 2.4 hours. (b) G1 + S + G2 = 11 + 8 + 4 = 23 h of interphase-style activity; since the whole cycle is 24 h, that leaves 24 - 23 = 1 hour for the M phase. (Note: the exact split depends on the numbers given; the 'about 90% interphase / 10% M' figure and these specific G1/S/G2 values are two different ways of approximating the same cycle, so the M phase comes out as roughly 1-2.4 h.) (c) Most of the cycle is spent growing, carrying out normal metabolism and accurately copying the DNA (interphase); the actual separation of chromosomes and the cell into two (mitosis + cytokinesis) is mechanically quick, so the M phase is brief.

**2.** (a) End of S phase: 4C (DNA has been replicated, doubling the amount). During G2: 4C (stays doubled until division). Each daughter cell immediately after mitosis: 2C (the 4C is shared equally between the two cells). (b) During S phase the chromosome NUMBER stays the same (still 2n) but each chromosome now has two identical sister chromatids, so the number of chromatids doubles. At anaphase the sister chromatids separate, so each becomes a full chromosome moving to a pole; once the two nuclei form, each again has the original 2n chromosome number with one chromatid each. (c) Because the DNA was copied semi-conservatively in S phase, the two sister chromatids of each chromosome are identical; mitosis separates one chromatid of each pair into each daughter nucleus, so both daughters receive an identical, complete set of chromosomes — genetically identical to each other and to the parent.
