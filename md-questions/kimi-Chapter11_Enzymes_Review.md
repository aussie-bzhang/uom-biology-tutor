## Part A Answers

Question 1

C) Activation energy (Delta G double-dagger) of the reaction. Enzymes are biological catalysts that increase the rate of reaction by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. They do not affect the equilibrium constant, the overall free energy change, or the enthalpy change of the reaction.

Question 2

B) Substrates. The substrate is the reactant molecule that binds to the enzyme's active site and is converted into product(s). Products are the molecules formed after the reaction; cofactors are non-protein helpers; and apoenzymes refer to the protein portion of an enzyme without its cofactor.

Question 3

Enzymes are critical for metabolism because over 1,000 different reactions can occur in a living cell, and a specific enzyme controls each individual reaction. This specificity allows cells to regulate complex metabolic pathways through gentle, step-by-step control. Without enzymes, most biochemical reactions would proceed too slowly to sustain life. Furthermore, enzymes not only speed up reactions but also control which reactions occur and at what rate, enabling precise metabolic regulation in response to cellular needs.

Question 4

The lock-and-key model proposes that the enzyme is a rigid three-dimensional structure with a pre-formed active site that exactly fits the substrate, much like a key fits a lock. The induced-fit model proposes that the active site is flexible and undergoes conformational changes upon substrate binding to better accommodate and orient the substrate. The induced-fit model is considered more accurate for most enzymes because it accounts for the dynamic nature of protein structures and explains how binding energy can be used to distort the substrate and facilitate catalysis.

Question 5

The five most common amino acids in enzyme active sites are: Histidine (His) > Cysteine (Cys) > Aspartate (Asp) > Arginine (Arg) > Glutamate (Glu). Four of these have acidic or basic side chains (Asp, Glu are acidic; His, Arg are basic), while Cysteine has a sulfhydryl group (-SH). These side chains are crucial because they can: (1) act as general acids or bases in proton transfer, (2) form ionic bonds with substrates, (3) participate in covalent catalysis, and (4) stabilise charged transition states through electrostatic interactions.

Question 6

The enzyme is trypsin. Trypsin catalyses the hydrolysis of peptide bonds specifically formed by the carboxyl groups of lysine (Lys) and arginine (Arg). Its specificity arises from a deep, negatively charged binding pocket (S1 pocket) in the active site that accommodates and binds the positively charged side chains of Lys and Arg through electrostatic interactions. This structural complementarity ensures that trypsin only cleaves after these specific residues.

Question 7

a) Oxidoreductase; b) Transferase; c) Hydrolase; d) Isomerase.

Question 8

Zymogens (or proenzymes) are inactive precursors of enzymes. They are biologically significant because they allow enzymes to be safely stored and transported without causing unwanted proteolytic damage to the cells that produce them. For example, digestive enzymes like trypsin are produced as trypsinogen in the pancreas to prevent autodigestion. During autoproteolysis, the zymogen catalyses its own cleavage at a specific peptide bond (between residues 45 and 46 in the example from lectures), releasing an N-terminal fragment and exposing or forming the active site of the catalytic domain. This irreversible activation step ensures protease activity only occurs at the appropriate location and time.

## Part B Answers

Question 9

Carbonic anhydrase achieves its remarkable rate enhancement through several mechanisms: (1) The zinc ion (Zn2+) in the active site polarises the water molecule, generating a nucleophilic hydroxide ion at physiological pH. (2) The enzyme properly orients CO2 and OH- for reaction. (3) A network of hydrogen-bonding residues (including His64) facilitates proton transfer. (4) The enzyme stabilises the negatively charged transition state through electrostatic interactions. Together, these mechanisms lower the activation energy (Delta G double-dagger) from approximately 50 kJ/mol (uncatalysed) to about 20 kJ/mol, corresponding to the observed 10^7-fold rate enhancement.

Question 10

When an enzyme is added: (1) Delta G degree remains unchanged because enzymes cannot alter the free energy difference between reactants and products. (2) The equilibrium constant (Keq = 0.95) remains unchanged for the same reason. (3) Delta G double-dagger is lowered, providing an alternative reaction pathway. (4) The reaction will reach the same equilibrium position more rapidly. Both the forward and reverse reactions are accelerated equally, so the equilibrium concentrations are unchanged, but the system reaches equilibrium faster.

Question 11

The four mechanisms are: (1) Correct orientation of substrates - the active site binds substrates in precise geometries that promote reaction, equivalent to increasing the effective concentration. (2) Straining substrate bonds - binding energy distorts substrate geometry toward the transition state (transition state stabilisation). (3) Providing a favourable microenvironment - the active site may have a different polarity or charge distribution than the surrounding solvent. For example, acetoacetate decarboxylase has a hydrophobic pocket that destabilises the carboxylate group. (4) Covalent bonding to the substrate - some enzymes form transient covalent intermediates with substrates (e.g., serine proteases form acyl-enzyme intermediates).

Question 12

a) The graph shows a rectangular hyperbola: v0 increases with [S] at low concentrations, then plateaus at Vmax at high [S]. Km is the [S] at which v0 = Vmax/2. b) Km (Michaelis constant) is the substrate concentration at which the reaction rate is half of Vmax. It approximates the dissociation constant of the enzyme-substrate complex; a low Km indicates high affinity. c) At saturation, all enzyme molecules are bound to substrate (ES complex), and the enzyme is working at its maximum catalytic rate (Vmax). Adding more substrate cannot increase the rate further.

Question 13

a) While shape complementarity is necessary for substrate binding, the chemistry of the active site determines catalytic efficiency. Specific amino acid side chains provide acid-base catalysis, covalent catalysis, metal ion catalysis, and transition state stabilisation. Without the correct chemical functionality, an enzyme cannot lower the activation energy effectively, regardless of how well the substrate fits geometrically. b) Enzymes are catalysts and catalysts affect only the kinetics (rate) of a reaction, not the thermodynamics. Delta G degree depends only on the free energy difference between reactants and products, which is an intrinsic property of the molecules themselves. Enzymes accelerate both the forward and reverse reactions equally, leaving the equilibrium position unchanged.

Question 14

a) Initial rates are measured because as the reaction proceeds, substrate concentration decreases, product concentration increases (possibly causing product inhibition), and the enzyme may lose activity. The initial rate provides a linear approximation where [S] is approximately constant. b) Step 1: delta A = 0.45, epsilon = 6220 M^-1 cm^-1, l = 1 cm. Using Beer-Lambert: [NADH] = A/(epsilon\*l) = 0.45/6220 = 7.23 x 10^-5 M. Step 2: Rate = 7.23 x 10^-5 M / 3 min = 2.41 x 10^-5 M/min = 2.41 x 10^-5 mmol/min/mL = 0.0241 micromol/min in 1 mL. Step 3: Specific activity = (0.0241 micromol/min) / (0.5 micromol / 50000 g/mol = 0.01 mg) = 2.41 micromol/min/mg.

Question 15

a) Lineweaver-Burk: 1/v0 = (Km/Vmax)(1/[S]) + 1/Vmax. Plotting 1/[S] vs 1/v0: Slope = Km/Vmax, y-intercept = 1/Vmax. From the data: 1/Vmax approximately 0.01 (micromol/min)^-1, so Vmax approximately 100 micromol/min. Km/Vmax from slope approximately 0.5 mM/(100 micromol/min) = 0.005 mM.min/micromol, giving Km approximately 1.0 mM. b) A competitive inhibitor would increase the apparent Km (Km,app > Km) because more substrate is needed to achieve half-maximal velocity, while Vmax remains unchanged because at infinite [S], the substrate can outcompete the inhibitor.

## Part C Answers

Question 16

a) At low temperatures, enzyme activity is low because molecules have less kinetic energy, resulting in fewer collisions between enzyme and substrate. Additionally, the flexibility of the enzyme is reduced. b) The optimum temperature is the temperature at which enzyme activity is maximal, balancing kinetic energy benefits against thermal denaturation. c) Above 50 degree C, non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds) that maintain the tertiary and quaternary structure are disrupted. This causes the active site to lose its precise geometry (denaturation), leading to irreversible loss of activity. d) 37 degree C is human body temperature; enzymes have evolved to function optimally at this temperature to match physiological conditions.

Question 17

a) pH affects enzyme activity because the ionisation states of acidic (Asp, Glu) and basic (His, Lys, Arg) amino acid side chains in the active site depend on pH. Only at the optimum pH are these groups in the correct protonation states for substrate binding and catalysis. Deviations from optimum pH alter charge distributions, disrupting both substrate binding and catalytic mechanisms. b) Pepsin functions in the stomach (pH approximately 1.5-2), so it has evolved an acidic optimum. Trypsin functions in the small intestine (pH approximately 8), so it has a basic optimum. These adaptations ensure each enzyme is maximally active in its physiological environment. c) At pH 2, excess H+ would protonate carboxylate groups and disrupt ionic bonds, causing unfolding of the tertiary structure (denaturation). The active site geometry would be lost, resulting in irreversible inactivation.

Question 18

a) Activators are inorganic ions (often metal ions such as Mg2+, Zn2+, or Ca2+) that bind to enzymes and are required for activity. Example: Mg2+ is required by many kinases, including hexokinase. b) Coenzymes are complex organic molecules that transiently bind to enzymes and transfer chemical groups between reactions. Example: NAD+ acts as an electron acceptor in dehydrogenase reactions. c) Prosthetic groups are organic coenzymes that are permanently (tightly) bound to the enzyme protein. Example: Pyridoxal phosphate (PLP) in AST and ALT; haem in cytochromes and haemoglobin.

Question 19

a) PLP is classified as a prosthetic group because it is permanently/covalently bound to the enzyme (through a Schiff base linkage to a lysine residue in the active site). Unlike coenzymes such as NAD+, which dissociate after each catalytic cycle, prosthetic groups remain attached to the enzyme. b) AST and ALT are normally intracellular enzymes found at high concentrations in hepatocytes. When liver cells are damaged (e.g., by hepatitis, alcohol, or drugs), the cell membrane becomes permeable and these enzymes leak into the bloodstream. Elevated serum levels therefore indicate hepatocellular damage. The degree of elevation correlates with the extent of tissue damage.

Question 20

a) Replacing histidine with alanine would likely abolish or severely reduce enzyme activity. Histidine has an imidazole side chain (pKa approximately 6.0) that can act as both acid and base at physiological pH. Alanine has a non-reactive methyl group that cannot participate in acid-base catalysis, ionic bonding, or hydrogen bonding. b) Histidine is prevalent because its imidazole ring has a pKa near physiological pH (6.0), allowing it to readily donate and accept protons. This makes it ideal for general acid-base catalysis. It can also form hydrogen bonds and coordinate metal ions. c) Not necessarily. A distant mutation might have little effect, or it could affect activity through allosteric mechanisms, protein folding, or subunit interactions. Without knowing the tertiary structure and the residue's functional role, the effect cannot be predicted.

Application Problem 2

a) Lineweaver-Burk analysis: Transform data to 1/[S] and 1/v0. From the uninhibited data, the y-intercept (1/Vmax) is approximately 0.02, so Vmax = 50 micromol/min. The slope is Km/Vmax = 0.08 mM.min/micromol, so Km = 4.0 mM. b) With inhibitor: Vmax,app remains approximately 50 micromol/min (same y-intercept), but the slope increases to approximately 0.16, giving Km,app = 8.0 mM. Since Vmax is unchanged but Km is doubled, this is competitive inhibition. This is consistent with glucose-6-phosphate competing with glucose for the active site. c) For competitive inhibition: Km,app = Km(1 + [I]/Ki). Rearranging: Ki = [I] / (Km,app/Km - 1) = 0.5 / (8/4 - 1) = 0.5 / 1 = 0.5 mM.
