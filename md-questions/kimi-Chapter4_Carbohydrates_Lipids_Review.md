## Part A: Short Answer Questions

**Question 1 - Carbohydrate Classification**

Monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrates, consisting of a single sugar unit. Example: glucose (C6H12O6). Disaccharides are formed from two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond. Example: sucrose (C12H22O11), composed of glucose and fructose. Polysaccharides are large polymers containing hundreds to thousands of monosaccharide units. Example: starch [(C6H10O5)n], a polymer of glucose. The general formula for carbohydrates is (CH2O)n, where n represents the number of carbon atoms.

**Question 2 - Glucose Isomers**

Alpha-glucose and beta-glucose are stereoisomers (anomers) that differ in the position of the hydroxyl (-OH) group on carbon 1. In alpha-glucose, the -OH group is positioned below the ring plane (trans to the CH2OH group on C6), while in beta-glucose, the -OH group is above the ring plane (cis to the CH2OH group). This seemingly minor difference results in starch being composed of alpha-glucose with alpha-1,4 and alpha-1,6 glycosidic bonds, forming helical structures that are easily degraded by amylase enzymes. Cellulose is composed of beta-glucose with beta-1,4 glycosidic bonds, forming straight linear chains that hydrogen-bond with neighboring chains to create rigid microfibrils. Humans lack the enzyme cellulase needed to break beta-1,4 bonds, making cellulose indigestible.

**Question 3 - Glycosidic Bonds**

A glycosidic bond (also called an ether linkage) is a covalent bond formed between the hydroxyl group of one monosaccharide and the anomeric carbon of another. This is a condensation (dehydration) reaction, meaning a water molecule is eliminated during bond formation. The reaction for maltose formation: Two alpha-glucose molecules undergo condensation, with the -OH on C1 of one glucose reacting with the -OH on C4 of another, eliminating H2O and forming an alpha-1,4 glycosidic linkage. The reverse reaction (adding water to break the bond) is hydrolysis, which requires enzymatic assistance.

**Question 4 - Starch vs. Glycogen**

(a) Both starch and glycogen contain alpha-1,4 and alpha-1,6 glycosidic bonds. (b) Starch consists of amylose (unbranched, helical) and amylopectin (branched every 24-30 residues). Glycogen is more highly branched, with branches every 8-12 residues. (c) Starch is produced by plants as the primary energy storage compound; glycogen ("animal starch") is produced and stored in the liver and muscles of animals, fungi, and bacteria. Both can be easily degraded by amylase enzymes due to their alpha-linkages. Glycogen's more extensive branching provides more free ends for rapid glucose release.

**Question 5 - Cellulose Structure**

Cellulose is the most abundant polymer on Earth because it is the primary structural component of plant cell walls, and plants are the most abundant organisms globally. Cellulose consists of linear chains of beta-glucose connected by beta-1,4 glycosidic bonds. These linear chains can pack closely together, and the numerous hydroxyl groups on adjacent chains form extensive hydrogen bonds between neighboring strands. These hydrogen bonds bundle the chains into microfibrils (containing about 40 cellulose chains each), which further bundle into larger fibrils. This extensive hydrogen bonding network gives cellulose exceptional tensile strength and chemical stability, making it ideal for its structural role.

**Question 6 - Lipid Properties**

Lipids are predominantly nonpolar molecules composed mainly of carbon and hydrogen atoms with very few oxygen atoms. The C-H and C-C bonds have minimal electronegativity differences, resulting in no significant charge separation (no dipole moment). Water is a highly polar molecule that forms strong hydrogen bonds with other polar molecules. Since lipids are nonpolar, they cannot form hydrogen bonds with water molecules. The interactions between lipids and water are unfavorable because water molecules would need to form ordered "cages" (clathrate structures) around nonpolar molecules, which is entropically unfavorable. In contrast, organic solvents are typically nonpolar or weakly polar, allowing lipids to dissolve readily through London dispersion forces (weak but additive van der Waals interactions).

**Question 7 - Fatty Acid Types**

Saturated fatty acids contain only single bonds between carbon atoms, with each carbon bonded to the maximum number of hydrogen atoms. Their hydrocarbon chains are straight, allowing them to pack closely together with strong London dispersion forces between chains. This results in solid fats at room temperature (e.g., butter, lard). Unsaturated fatty acids contain one or more carbon-carbon double bonds (C=C), which create kinks or bends in the hydrocarbon chain due to the cis configuration. These kinks prevent close packing of the chains, resulting in weaker intermolecular forces and liquid oils at room temperature (e.g., olive oil, fish oil). Saturated fats are typically found in animal sources, while unsaturated fats are more common in plant and fish sources.

**Question 8 - Phospholipid Structure**

An amphipathic molecule has both hydrophilic (water-loving, polar) and hydrophobic (water-fearing, nonpolar) regions. A phospholipid consists of a glycerol backbone, two nonpolar fatty acid tails, and a polar phosphate head group (often with an additional charged group like choline or ethanolamine). The phosphate head can form ionic and ion-dipole interactions with water molecules, making it hydrophilic. The fatty acid tails are nonpolar hydrocarbon chains that interact with water unfavorably. When placed in an aqueous environment, phospholipids arrange themselves so that the polar heads face the water and the nonpolar tails are shielded from it. This drives the spontaneous formation of phospholipid bilayers, where two layers of phospholipids arrange tail-to-tail with heads facing outward on both sides - the fundamental structure of all biological cell membranes.

**Question 9 - Steroid Functions**

Three important steroids: (1) Cholesterol - a component of animal cell membranes that modulates membrane fluidity and serves as a precursor for other steroids. (2) Estradiol - a primary estrogen (female sex hormone) involved in reproductive system development and regulation. (3) Testosterone - a primary androgen (male sex hormone) responsible for male reproductive tissue development and secondary sexual characteristics. The basic steroid structure consists of four fused carbon rings (three six-membered cyclohexane rings and one five-membered cyclopentane ring), quite different from triglycerides which have a glycerol backbone esterified to three fatty acid chains. All steroid hormones are derived from cholesterol through enzymatic modifications.

**Question 10 - Energy Storage Comparison**

Animals use both glycogen and lipids because each serves different physiological needs. Glycogen provides approximately 16 kJ/g (anhydrous), but because it is highly hydrophilic, it binds about 2 grams of water per gram of glycogen, reducing the effective energy density to about 5.3 kJ/g of wet weight. Triglycerides yield about 37 kJ/g and are hydrophobic, so they store energy without associated water weight. However, glycogen can be broken down to glucose much more rapidly than lipids can be mobilized, making glycogen ideal for short-term, high-intensity energy needs (e.g., muscle contraction). Lipids are better suited for long-term energy storage due to their higher energy density. Additionally, glucose from glycogen can enter glycolysis without oxygen in anaerobic conditions, whereas fatty acid oxidation requires oxygen.

## Part B: Application Problems

**Problem 1 - Biochemical Testing of Carbohydrates**

(a) Procedure: First, add iodine solution to samples A, B, and C separately. The sample that turns blue-black contains starch (amylose forms a helical complex with iodine). The other two samples (glycogen and glucose) will show no color change or a slight reddish-brown color (glycogen may give a reddish-brown due to its branched structure). Next, heat the remaining two samples with Benedict's reagent. The sample that forms a brick-red precipitate contains glucose (a reducing sugar that reduces Cu2+ to Cu+). The sample with no color change (remains blue) contains glycogen (non-reducing unless hydrolyzed first). Benedict's test detects free aldehyde groups; glucose has a free anomeric carbon, while in glycogen all anomeric carbons are involved in glycosidic bonds.

(b) Iodine solution cannot distinguish between glycogen and glucose because neither forms the characteristic blue-black complex with iodine. Both would show negative results (no color change), making them indistinguishable. Iodine specifically detects the helical structure of amylose in starch.

(c) Add amylase to the starch solution and incubate at 37 degrees C. Amylase hydrolyzes the alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds in starch, producing maltose (a reducing sugar). After incubation, test with Benedict's reagent - a positive result (brick-red precipitate) confirms starch was present, as the hydrolysis products (maltose) are reducing sugars.

**Problem 2 - Molecular Calculations for Polysaccharides**

(a) Number of water molecules lost = number of glycosidic bonds = number of glucose monomers - 1 = 2,400 - 1 = 2,399 water molecules. Each glycosidic bond formation involves the loss of one water molecule.

(b) Molecular weight = (2,400 x 180) - (2,399 x 18) = 432,000 - 43,182 = 388,818 g/mol. Alternatively: 2,400 x 162 = 388,800 g/mol (using the anhydrous glucose unit mass of 162 g/mol).

(c) Number of alpha-1,6 linkages (branch points) = 2,400 / 12 - 1 = 200 - 1 = 199 branch points. (Note: The main chain has approximately 2,400/12 = 200 segments, with a branch point at the start of each segment except the first.)

(d) Branching creates numerous free ends (non-reducing ends) in the glycogen molecule. Glycogen phosphorylase and other degradative enzymes can simultaneously attack these multiple ends, allowing rapid mobilization of many glucose molecules at once. This is essential during muscle activity when ATP demand increases suddenly.

**Problem 3 - Lipid Extraction and Analysis**

(a) Triglycerides are entirely nonpolar (three fatty acid chains with no charged groups), making them highly soluble in the nonpolar solvent system and causing them to migrate farthest up the TLC plate. Phospholipids have a charged phosphate head group, making them significantly more polar. They interact strongly with the polar stationary phase (silica gel) through ionic and hydrogen bonding interactions, causing them to remain near the origin. The principle is "like dissolves like" - nonpolar compounds move faster in nonpolar solvents.

(b) Rf 0.85: Triglycerides (most nonpolar, greatest mobility). Rf 0.42: Free fatty acids or cholesterol (moderate polarity due to the carboxyl or hydroxyl group). Rf 0.08: Phospholipids (most polar due to charged phosphate head, strongest interaction with stationary phase).

(c) Saponification: Triglyceride + 3 NaOH -> Glycerol + 3 Soap molecules (sodium salts of fatty acids). The products are glycerol (a trihydroxy alcohol) and fatty acid salts (soap).

(d) Free fatty acids in blubber serve multiple functions: (1) They can be rapidly mobilized for energy via beta-oxidation without requiring enzymatic hydrolysis. (2) They contribute to the insulating properties of blubber by creating a thicker, less fluid lipid layer. (3) The fatty acid composition can be adjusted seasonally to maintain optimal membrane fluidity in varying water temperatures. This is crucial for marine mammals that need both energy reserves and thermal insulation in cold aquatic environments.

**Problem 4 - Energy Yield Calculations**

(a) Energy ratio = 37 kJ/g / 16 kJ/g = 2.31 times more energy per gram in triglycerides. However, considering hydrated glycogen (with 2g water per g glycogen), the effective ratio is 37 / 5.3 = 7.0 times more energy per gram of storage weight.

(b) Energy from glycogen: 400 g x 16 kJ/g = 6,400 kJ. Energy from triglycerides: 10,000 g x 37 kJ/g = 370,000 kJ. Total energy reserves = 376,400 kJ.

(c) Days from lipid reserves alone = 370,000 kJ / 8,400 kJ/day = 44.0 days. (In reality, the brain requires glucose, so complete lipid-only metabolism would require gluconeogenesis or ketone body utilization.)

(d) Despite lower energy density, glycogen remains important because: (1) Rapid mobilization: Glycogen phosphorylase can release glucose-1-phosphate quickly for glycolysis, essential for high-intensity exercise. (2) Anaerobic glycolysis: Glucose from glycogen can produce ATP without oxygen, unlike fatty acid oxidation which requires oxygen. (3) Brain fuel: The brain preferentially uses glucose as an energy source; glycogen stores help maintain blood glucose levels. (4) Solubility: Glycogen's hydrophilic nature allows it to be stored in aqueous cytoplasm, while lipids require specialized adipocytes.

**Problem 5 - Membrane Formation and Cell Evolution**

(a) (i) In nonpolar solvent: Phospholipids would orient with their nonpolar tails facing outward into the solvent and their polar heads clustered together inward, forming inverted micelles. (ii) In aqueous solution: Phospholipids form micelles (spherical structures with heads facing water outward and tails shielded inward) or bilayer vesicles (liposomes). (iii) At oil-water interface: Phospholipids orient with heads in the water phase and tails in the oil phase, forming a monolayer.

(b) Individual phospholipid molecules in water have their hydrophobic tails exposed to water, which forces surrounding water molecules to form ordered "cages" (clathrate structures). This ordering of water molecules represents a decrease in entropy (increase in order), which is thermodynamically unfavorable (Delta G > 0). When phospholipids aggregate into a closed vesicle, the hydrophobic tails are shielded from water, and the water molecules are released from their ordered cages, resulting in a large increase in entropy of the system. This entropic driving force (hydrophobic effect) makes vesicle formation spontaneous. The closed spherical shape minimizes the exposure of hydrophobic tails to water while maximizing head group interactions with the aqueous environment.

(c) The saturated 16-carbon phospholipid membrane would have a HIGHER melting temperature (Tm) than the membrane with 18-carbon oleic acid tails. This is because: (1) The saturated palmitic acid tails are straight, allowing tight packing of the hydrocarbon chains and maximizing London dispersion forces between adjacent phospholipids. (2) The cis-double bond in oleic acid creates a kink in the hydrocarbon tail, disrupting the orderly packing of the membrane and reducing the strength of London dispersion forces. (3) Weaker intermolecular forces in the unsaturated membrane require less thermal energy to overcome, resulting in a lower Tm and greater fluidity at any given temperature.

(d) Organisms regulate membrane fluidity through several mechanisms: (1) Temperature adaptation: Cold-adapted organisms increase the proportion of unsaturated fatty acids in their membranes to maintain fluidity at low temperatures. Warm-adapted organisms use more saturated fatty acids to prevent excessive fluidity. (2) Cholesterol modulation: In animals, cholesterol acts as a fluidity buffer - at high temperatures it restricts phospholipid movement (reducing fluidity), while at low temperatures it prevents tight packing (maintaining fluidity). (3) Chain length adjustment: Shorter fatty acid chains reduce the strength of London dispersion forces, increasing fluidity. These adaptations ensure that membranes maintain optimal fluidity for proper functioning of membrane proteins and transport processes across a range of environmental temperatures.
